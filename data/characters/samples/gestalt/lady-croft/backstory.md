# Lady Lara Croft — Backstory

[← Back to Sheet](sheet.md)

---

## The House of Croft

The Crofts are minor nobility in Cheliax — old enough to be respected, not old enough to be feared. Their ancestral estate sits in the Archduchy of Longmarch, two days' ride from Egorian: a sprawling manor of pale stone and darker history, surrounded by a hundred acres of formal gardens, a hedge maze nobody has mapped in three generations, and a library that is arguably the finest private collection of pre-Imperial Thassilonian scholarship anywhere in the Inner Sea.

Lord Richard Croft, the current Lord's father, was an explorer-scholar of some renown before his death. He published monographs on Osirian funerary architecture and corresponded with the Arcanamirium in Absalom. He was, by the standards of Chelaxian nobility, an *oddity* — a man more interested in unearthing the past than in participating in the present. His wife, Lady Amelia Croft, was of old Taldan blood and kept the accounts. She was the one who disapproved, gently, of her husband's obsessions, and then, less gently, of her daughter's.

Lara was the only child.

## The Disappearance

Lara was eleven years old when her parents did not come home from an expedition to the ruins of Nex. The official report, delivered six months later by a representative of the Chelaxian embassy in Quantium, listed the cause of death as "exposure and desert hazards." No bodies were recovered. The expedition's notes were lost. The guide they had hired refused to speak to investigators.

Lara did not believe the report. She was eleven and she did not understand desert hazards, but she understood her father's field journals — she had read them all — and she understood that a man who had survived three expeditions to the Mwangi Expanse was not killed by "exposure" on what was supposed to be a routine survey. She also understood, because she had watched her mother's face in the weeks before the expedition, that her mother had not wanted to go.

Her parents' estate passed to her in trust, managed by a family solicitor in Egorian. Her childhood, after the disappearance, was supervised by governesses and tutors and a succession of Chelaxian noble aunts who regarded her as a project. She was taught the sword (she was good), the pistol (she was better), the etiquette of the Egorian court (she could perform it on command), and the recitation of House Thrune's genealogy (she memorized it and then spoke of it never again). She was sent to the most prestigious boarding school in Westcrown. At fourteen she broke a boy's nose for mocking her father's publications. The school contacted her guardian. Her guardian, quietly amused, paid the family a settlement and told her only *"do not get caught next time."*

At sixteen she ran away from school for the first time. She returned three days later with a set of Garundi ruin-sketches she had purchased from a dockside merchant in Corentyn. She was beaten, grounded, and then — at her own insistence — transferred to the more permissive tutelage of a retired Pathfinder Society agent named Mistress Dorovan, who lived in an unfashionable house in Westcrown and had once, allegedly, killed a lich with a letter opener.

Mistress Dorovan recognized what the aunts had not. Lara was not a difficult child. She was a *field operative* misclassified as a debutante. The old woman rewrote the curriculum from scratch. Rope work. Map-reading. Ancient Osiriani. Thassilonian glyphs. Firearms maintenance. Rapid reload drills in the garden. Dead languages before breakfast, pistol drills after. Lara thrived.

By nineteen, when she came of age and legally took possession of the Croft estate, she had the skills of a second-year Pathfinder and the library of a senior scholar. She dismissed the aunts with courteous letters, moved the governess's furniture out of her father's study, and began — methodically, patiently, without haste — to reconstruct her parents' final expedition from the scraps that remained in the family archive.

It took her eight years.

## What She Found

She does not speak of what she found.

She returned to Nex once, accompanied by three Pathfinder Society associates and a hired Garundi guide. Two of the Society associates died in the ruin. The third retired from adventuring permanently and now runs a bookshop in Magnimar where he does not discuss Lara Croft under any circumstances. The guide refused payment and walked into the desert alone. Lara came out of the ruin with a small leather-wrapped parcel, a pistol wound in her left shoulder that did not entirely heal, and a look in her eyes that had not been there when she went in.

The parcel is in a locked drawer of her desk at the Croft estate. She does not look at it. She knows what it is. She knows who killed her parents. She is not yet ready to do anything about it. She is, instead, preparing — expedition by expedition, artifact by artifact, tomb by tomb — until she is ready.

## The Professional Approach

Lara has been a working archaeologist for twelve years now. She does not consider herself an adventurer. Adventurers, in her experience, are loud, untrained, and dead within five years. She considers herself a **professional** — a person with a trade, who has developed a specific competence for extracting historical objects from dangerous locations and returning them to places where they can be studied, preserved, or sold to fund the next expedition.

She has published four monographs (two in Academae Arcanamiriana, one in the Pathfinder Chronicles, one privately for a collector who paid extremely well). She is on cordial terms with the Pathfinder Society but has never joined — she refuses the constraint of having a commanding officer. She is on decidedly less cordial terms with the Aspis Consortium, whose agents have attempted to intercept her shipments on three separate occasions; all three attempts ended with Consortium agents in a hospital and Lara writing polite letters of complaint to their regional director.

Her gear is methodical. Her pistols are well-maintained and carried low on the thigh for fast draw. Her climbing equipment is inspected weekly. Her cartridge belt carries 200 rounds of powder-and-ball, distributed in three types: standard, incendiary, and a specially-commissioned salt-and-silver load for undead. She keeps six backup pistols because she knows — from hard experience — that the misfire chance on standard firearms is low but not zero, and at a certain point in a Boneyards cult encounter she does not want to spend a standard action clearing a jam.

She travels light. One horse, or a hired guide, or a commissioned passage on a coastal trader. She sleeps in the open when she has to and in a Chelaxian embassy when she can. She drinks Varisian coffee in the morning, Qadiran tea in the afternoon, Andoran whiskey in the evening. She reads every night before sleeping — usually whatever primary sources relate to her next expedition — and she writes in her field journal every day without exception.

## The Pharasma Question

She is not religious, but she is not *not* religious.

Her mother's family had a tradition of Pharasma worship — the Chelaxian nobility rarely does, preferring Asmodeus for political reasons and none of the goddesses for social reasons, but the Taldan branch of her mother's line kept a small shrine to the Lady of Graves in the manor's family chapel, and Lara grew up putting a coin on that shrine every morning. It was a habit before it was a belief. It remains, to this day, mostly a habit.

But — and she does not tell anyone this — she has never had a dream that told her to turn back from a ruin. She has had dreams that told her to *turn left* when she thought she should turn right, and she has obeyed those dreams, and twice it has saved her life. She does not know whether the dreams are from Pharasma or from her own subconscious processing of subtle cues she did not consciously notice. She does not particularly need to know. Either way, the dreams are useful, and she is grateful.

She leaves a coin at every Pharasma shrine she passes. It is a small expense. It is professional courtesy. The Lady of Graves is, after all, the patron of history, and Lara is in the business of returning history to the living. They have, in Lara's private theology, a working relationship.

She also leaves flowers at graves — when she has to enter one, when she has to disturb the remains of someone who was once a person. She apologizes, in whatever language she thinks they would have understood, for the intrusion. She takes nothing from a burial chamber that she does not believe the dead would have *wanted* to be taken. She does not rob the dead. She *recovers* what the dead no longer need.

The distinction matters to her. It is, perhaps, the closest thing she has to a moral code.

## What She Carries

Inside the locked drawer of her desk at the Croft estate:

- Her father's last field journal, recovered from a Nex ruin eight years ago, written in a cipher Lara broke in three months
- A small stone disc inscribed with Thassilonian runes her father had been translating at the time of his death
- A calling card from the person — the thing — that killed her parents
- A single pressed desert flower, the kind that blooms only at the entrance of Osirian tombs

She does not open the drawer often. She does not need to. She remembers what is in it. She is preparing.

## On the Road

The campaign begins when Lara leaves the Croft estate on what she is calling "routine preparatory work" — three expeditions she has been planning for years, each of which will, she believes, give her a piece of a larger puzzle. The first is to a recently-discovered Thassilonian runewell in the Sandpoint hinterlands. The second is to an Osirian tomb complex beneath the dunes east of Wati. The third — she has not yet decided where the third will take her, because it will depend on what she finds in the first two.

She does not travel with a party, traditionally. She does not trust easily. But the first expedition has gone sideways — the runewell was occupied — and she has found herself, against her preference, working alongside a set of other people who are competent enough that she has not yet insisted on working alone. She is cautiously willing to continue the arrangement. For now.

She carries her pistols. She carries her wits. She carries her father's field notebook and her mother's old journal and her own unfinished monograph on pre-Imperial Thassilonian burial customs.

She carries the weight of eight years of preparation, and she is finally, finally starting to feel ready.

*Blessed be Pharasma, keeper of the past. Lara Croft is coming.*
