# The Shwarma Master — Backstory

## The Meat Has a Soul

His name, at birth, was Yusuf al-Mahzi, and he was born above a spice market in Katheer, capital of Qadira. His mother was a baker. His father was a lamb-seller who argued with everyone, including the lambs. His grandmother, **Aisha**, ran the shwarma stand on the corner of the Avenue of Small Quarrels, and she was the most feared woman on the street — not because she was cruel, but because she was *correct*, always, about everything, and she had a scimitar.

Aisha taught him that meat has a soul. "Every animal," she would say, turning the spit, "carries a life. When you cook it, you are not destroying that life. You are *translating* it. Into joy. Into memory. Into the fat that makes the bread sing. You must be very careful. Translation is sacred work."

He was six years old. He believed her completely. He never stopped believing her.

## Nethys in the Spice Pouch

Yusuf was a quiet, watchful child. He showed an early aptitude for the subtler arts — he could tell when bread had risen without touching it, when oil was a heartbeat from smoking, when the cumin in a clay jar had lost its temper. At twelve, a travelling scholar of Nethys stopped at the stand, ate three wraps in complete silence, and then told Aisha that her grandson had a talent for *transformations*. Every cook did, the scholar explained. But this one had the fingers for the other kind of transformation too.

Aisha considered this over a long drag on her pipe. Then she took the scholar's remaining fee and paid half of it toward Yusuf's first year of study at the College of Mysteries in Sothis. "Go learn," she told him. "But promise me you will come back, and promise me you will learn something *useful*."

He studied for nine years. He read the works of Nex and Arazni. He learned to scribe evocation in the old Thassilonian forms and divination in the newer Osirian schools. He learned Draconic, Aklo, Celestial, and one of the dialects of the Elemental Planes. His masters called him a promising evoker with an unusual meditative discipline. They did not understand why, at the end of every practicum, he would ask the same questions:

- *Does Intensified Spell increase the yield of cooked meat?*
- *Can Prestidigitation remove the taste of bad vinegar?*
- *What is the correct caster level at which to sear a lamb?*

They thought it was a joke. It was not a joke. He was taking notes.

## Aisha's Knife

He returned to Katheer the year his grandmother died. He was twenty-two. She left him three things: the shwarma stand, a small, quiet library of cookbooks written in a script no living person could read, and her scimitar — plain steel, razor-edged, the handle worn smooth by forty years of her grip.

He named the scimitar *Aisha*, because a blade that has cut that much meat deserves a name, and because his grandmother had been, in her own way, the sharpest thing in the Inner Sea.

He refused the gods most of his classmates took. He refused the merchant prosperity of Abadar. He refused the hospitality of Cayden Cailean. He refused the sun of Sarenrae. He chose instead to inscribe Nethys's sigil over the doorway of the stand, because Nethys — the god of magic's dual face, preservation and destruction, the whole and the fractured — understood something important that the other gods did not. Cooking *is* a theological act. Fire transforms. Ice preserves. Salt destroys and immortalizes. Every pot on the stove is a prayer, and every prayer eventually meets a knife.

He spent the next decade turning the stand into a temple.

## The Dance of the Spit

By thirty he had mastered the dance — the one he performs now, every morning, without variation. It begins with the scimitar at rest across his shoulders. The meat is rotating on the spit. He draws in one motion, never looking, and shaves a single slice that lands exactly where he means it to land. He adjusts the flame with two fingers and a whispered cantrip. He slaps the bread on the heated stone. He smears tahini with the back of his thumb, sprinkles sumac from a pouch that never runs out, arranges brined turnips with the gravity of a jeweler setting a ring. He wraps, folds, hands over.

And he smiles — small, polite, a chef's professional courtesy — and takes the next order.

This is what he does. This is what he has done for twenty years. This is what, in his private theology, he believes he was made to do.

He has also, in those twenty years, been obliged — perhaps six or seven times — to kill a man over shwarma.

## The Incidents

He does not talk about the incidents.

The first was a drunken mercenary who spat out a mouthful of spiced lamb and complained it was "dog." Yusuf explained that it was not dog. The mercenary drew a blade. Yusuf drew *Aisha*. The fight lasted approximately one second, long enough for Yusuf to cast a spell he had been refining for weeks specifically in case of this situation. Afterward, he wiped the blade on his apron, cleaned the floor with Prestidigitation, and served the next customer. The next customer, who had witnessed the entire event, tipped generously and left without speaking.

The second was a tax collector, but the tax collector had been *rude* about the tahini.

The others he does not count.

## Why He Travels Now

Something has happened. He will not say what.

It involves a shipment of rare saffron from Osirion that never arrived. It involves a rival chef — or possibly a false chef, a demon wearing the face of a chef — who has been copying his recipes without his permission and selling them in the markets of Sothis under a name that is almost, but not quite, his own. It involves his grandmother's cookbooks, the ones written in the unreadable script, because he has begun to suspect that script is not any mortal language. And it involves a message — delivered in the form of a single perfectly grilled lamb skewer, left on his doorstep, still warm, with a sigil of Nethys burned into the meat in a way that implies the sender has access to spells Yusuf has never seen.

So he has packed his spice belt. He has tied *Aisha* at his hip. He has rolled his cooking stones into his travel pack. He has locked the door of the stand on the Avenue of Small Quarrels and pinned a note to it that reads, in elegant Kelish, *"Closed for investigation. Will return. Do not touch the jars."*

And he has begun to walk.

## The Cooking Never Stops

Wherever the road takes him, the dance continues. He sets up his portable spit in inns, on riverbanks, in the courtyards of ruined temples. He feeds the party first, always, before any of them have thought to ask. He feeds strangers who look hungry and takes no coin for it. He feeds enemies, on the rare occasions this seems likely to de-escalate a situation, because the first principle of hospitality is that food is the oldest peace treaty.

When it fails — when the meal ends and the blade is required — he fights exactly as he cooks. Calm. Precise. Reading the rhythm of the moment. *Aisha* in one hand, a spell forming in the other, his mustache perfectly composed. He does not rage, he does not fear, he does not hesitate. He translates.

He will, eventually, go home. When he does, there will be a pot of lamb stewing on the stove, because he set it on the Unseen Servant timer before leaving, and Prestidigitation keeps it fresh indefinitely. He has planned for this. He has planned for everything. Cooking is a calling, and a calling requires foresight.

The spit is always turning. The meat is always translating. The apron abides.

*Blessed be Nethys, who teaches that the fire and the ice are one.*
