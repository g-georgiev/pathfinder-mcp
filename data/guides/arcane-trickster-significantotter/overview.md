# Introduction

The Arcane Trickster is a fun, flavorful, and powerful prestige class that allows for a seamless merging of rogue and caster playstyles. You won’t be a poisoner, you won’t be useful with any martial weapons, but you’ll zap your enemies for tremendous damage in their weak spots, and then vanish as if you were never there in the first place. You’ll infiltrate keeps, expertly pick locks with magically enhanced technique, and hide as well as the best rogue. At the same time, you’ll be creating demiplanes, constructing simulacrums, and breaking the very fabric of reality with your magical prowess.

## Why only full casters?

The arcane trickster offers a huge boost that many other prestige classes don’t. It provides spellcasting progression at every level, including the first level. Many spellcasting prestige classes are hampered by the first level of the prestige class not progressing prior spellcasting.

The full spell progression naturally synergizes with full casters. Full casting classes receive few class features, compensating for their lack of class abilities with pure spellpower. So when a prestige class offers spellcasting advancement and a different set of class features, a full caster doesn’t actually have to trade away all that much to take advantage of it.

A 6th level or 4th level spellcaster, on the other hand, takes much of their benefit from their better Base Attack Bonus alongside stronger and more frequent class features. An arcane trickster bard or magus, for example, is fully possible, but gains less from the spellcasting advancement and gives up more in class features than an arcane trickster wizard or arcanist.

There are still very cool and fun builds that can be made with arcane trickster maguses, bards, or others, but they won’t be what this guide focuses on. I’m going to showcase what the arcane trickster is really good at, and that’s creating a full caster that excels at blasting, spellcasting, and out of combat rogue-ish utility.

## What questions do you want to answer when building an arcane trickster?

### How am I going to enter arcane trickster?

The first question you’ll want to focus on when building an arcane trickster is “What class do I use to get 2nd level spells, and what class do I use to give me an initial 1d6 of sneak attack?”

Once you know that, the character building can start! It’s almost always more effective to enter your sneak attack class first, as they usually have higher hit dice than full casters, and so you’ll end up with +2 or so HP at first level. But you may opt to start as a wizard for flavor if you begin with level 1 characters. +2 hp is a small bonus that becomes less important as your level goes up. It can be worth the sacrifice for flavor!

### How will my build function in the early levels?

Don’t build a character that only becomes functional at level 5, or even level 8! The arcane trickster actually has a surprising amount of early game edge over other wizards. You can remain competitive with damage while casting with cantrips. We also have a major boon in the form of Alchemical Reagents for an extra little damage boost even at the first few levels.

At second level, a 1 admixture wizard/1 unchained rogue can do 1d3 + 1d6 + 2 damage, using liquid ice as an alchemical focus to add 1 damage, and gaining another damage from the admixture evocation school’s Intense Spells ability. That’s an average of 7.5 damage! Not shabby for a level 2 wizard with a cantrip. If they started as a rogue and took accomplished sneak attacker, then deadly aim at level 3, they’re doing 1d3 + 2d6 + 4 damage. We just went up to an average of 13 damage!

And absolutely do not forget that Ray spells are “weapon attacks”. You can apply all sorts of damage buffs to your ray spells. A bard’s inspire courage effects rays.

### What am I going to get out of my spellcasting class that matters for the rest of my levels?

This section could also be called “Witches are bad arcane tricksters.” You want to choose your spellcasting class because it does something helpful for you as an arcane trickster. For example, you may have chosen Arcanist so that you can pile up pearls of power and use Quick Study to switch around between your repertoire of out of combat utility spells, while leveraging Potent Spell to give your blasts an extra kick. You may have picked Admixture Wizard for incredible combat flexibility with your elemental attacks, while still keeping the prepared spellcaster out-of-combat flexibility. Or you might choose Sorcerer for Blood Havoc, and a bloodline arcana that provides +2 damage per spell dice. That’s a formula for a blaster arcane trickster with less utility, but brutal, unparalleled spell damage. Witches give you unsynergystic hexes with DC’s and abilities that don’t scale as you level outside the class.

### How do I consistently maintain sneak attacks? How do I keep up with damage early on and stay useful in combat?

Your early feat selection and/or item selection should be focused on making sure that you can keep up your sneak attacking in combat. You need to understand how stealth works in pathfinder. You need to make sure your GM understands how stealth works in pathfinder! And you need to figure out how to minimize the brutal early -10 to -20 stealth penalties you’ll take moving from cover to cover for your stealthing action economy.

### How am I going to fill a rogue’s role out of combat?

Stealth isn’t the only roguish thing you should be doing.

If you’re playing an arcane trickster, don’t expect your allies to handle traps for you. You will need to be competent with disable device, and figure out how to disable magic traps. This may be through a dip in Unchained Rogue or through the second level spell Aram Zey’s Focus.

Perception is pivotal. What use is breaking into the lord’s manor if you never spot the safe, or the hidden drawer with all the important documents? You have to notice traps before you can disable them!

You’ll potentially want acrobatics or escape artist to climb through vents, slip through tight spaces, or otherwise infiltrate areas. Sleight of hand to pickpocket or slip important documents off the table without anyone noticing.

These aspects can be accomplished through pure skill ranks, through utility spells, through feats, and also through magic items.

Or you can be a sorcerer who hits blasts things hard, hides, and looks very pretty while doing so!

### Am I going to be a face?

An int caster Wizard or Arcanist has plenty of skill ranks, but they aren’t infinite! You can’t do everything. There’s no shame in letting the party oracle or bard be the face while you do all the sneaky stuff as well as pick up knowledge skills.

The Sorcerer or Arcanist arcane trickster have synergy with charisma based class features, and can confidently put ranks in social skills. Bluff also helps if you want to use feinting to make enemies flat footed for sneak attacks or distract them to stealth. Plus, if you’re gonna do some breaking and entering, it helps to be able to lie about it!

## How does stealth even work in pathfinder?

People don’t understand stealth in pathfinder. Everyone has their own idea of how it should work, and that often ends up either letting roguish characters sneak attack too much. Or, much more commonly, completely fucking over rogue characters and almost never letting them sneak attack at all.

So, as a public service to all players who want to play an arcane trickster, I’m going to go through the basic rules involved in pathfinder stealth, and how you interact with these rules as an Arcane Trickster.

Let’s start off by pasting the full stealth passage from the Aonprd rules page. Then we’ll break it down into the simpler rules.

### The Rules as Written of Stealth

Your Stealth check is opposed by the Perception check of anyone who might notice you. Creatures that fail to beat your Stealth check are not aware of you and treat you as if you had total concealment. You can move up to half your normal speed and use Stealth at no penalty. When moving at a speed greater than half but less than your normal speed, you take a -5 penalty. It’s impossible to use Stealth while attacking, running, or charging.

Creatures gain a bonus or penalty on Stealth checks based on their size: Fine +16, Diminutive +12, Tiny +8, Small +4, Medium +0, Large -4, Huge -8, Gargantuan -12, Colossal -16.

If people are observing you using any of their senses (but typically sight), you can’t use Stealth. Against most creatures, finding cover or concealment allows you to use Stealth. If your observers are momentarily distracted (such as by a Bluff check), you can attempt to use Stealth. While the others turn their attention from you, you can attempt a Stealth check if you can get to an unobserved place of some kind. This check, however, is made at a –10 penalty because you have to move fast.

Breaking Stealth: When you start your turn using Stealth, you can leave cover or concealment and remain unobserved as long as you succeed at a Stealth check and end your turn in cover or concealment. Your Stealth immediately ends after you make an attack roll, whether or not the attack is successful (except when sniping as noted below).

Sniping: If you’ve already successfully used Stealth at least 10 feet from your target, you can make one ranged attack and then immediately use Stealth again. You take a –20 penalty on your Stealth check to maintain your obscured location.

Creating a Diversion to Hide: You can use Bluff to allow you to use Stealth. A successful Bluff check can give you the momentary diversion you need to attempt a Stealth check while people are aware of you.

Action: Usually none. Normally, you make a Stealth check as part of movement, so it doesn’t take a separate action. However, using Stealth immediately after a ranged attack (see Sniping, above) is a move action.

Special: If you are invisible, you gain a +40 bonus on Stealth checks if you are immobile, or a +20 bonus on Stealth checks if you’re moving.

### What Do We Learn From This?

You may not stealth when you are Observed by any of a creature’s senses. This includes the Scent ability!

Finding Cover or Concealment allows you to stealth, because it breaks Observation (in most cases).

You can use a bluff check to use stealth in a location where otherwise Observed, but you need to find a place where you aren’t Observed before the turn ends to maintain stealth.

Stealth is made as part of a move action. To stealth without moving (after a ranged attack while taking the sniping action) is a move action.

Moving while stealthed is normally at half speed, but you can do so at full speed with a -5 penalty.

If you move to an unobserved location (behind cover or gaining total concealment) while observed, you take a -10 on your stealth check due to your quick movement.

If you start a turn stealthed, you must make another stealth check if you move through an area where you could be observed to another position with cover or concealment.

If you succeed on stealth, you are treated as having total concealment against your enemy. And they are not aware of you.

### What does it mean to ‘Be Observed?’

Observation is the imperative word for the stealth rules. If you are observed, you can’t stealth. If you break observation, you are allowed to stealth–though the way in which you broke observation may impose penalties on your stealth check. -10 if you moved to get there, another -5 if you moved at full speed to get there, -10 if you’re ducking back into cover while sniping.

But what does observation mean? If an enemy uses their senses to determine with 100% accuracy what your location on the map is, then you’re observed. If the knowledge of your location comes from a source other than senses, you are not being observed. They can’t say “After you ducked behind cover and I lost sight of you, you can’t have moved out of that square.” That isn’t using only your senses to determine your location. An enemy can’t walk through every square except for the one with your invisible self in it, and have that count as “observing you”. Process of elimination is not a “sense”. If an enemy casts commune and asks their god which square you’re in, that is not observing you. The answer from their god is not a “sense”.

A creature with scent can spend a move action to identify the direction of a target within range of their scent, or to ‘pinpoint’ a hidden target within 5ft. I would argue that identifying the direction is not observation of the individual, but pinpointing is a form of observation. Pinpointing an individual with your senses breaks stealth because it inherently observes. Pinpointing is additionally a keyword that ties into invisibility rules, with the passage:

Even once a character has pinpointed the square that contains an invisible creature, the creature still benefits from total concealment (50% miss chance).

The implication in that passage is that “[The invisible creature is no longer hidden, but] still benefits from concealment.” Yet, remember that your sneak attack doesn’t require stealth! Stealth is just a way of gaining total concealment to deny a dex bonus to AC and trigger your sneak attack. If you have total concealment, you get your sneak attack even if your stealth is broken!

What about blindsight, blindsense, tremorsense, lifesense, etc?

Blindsight is the most powerful of these. Concealment is now irrelevant to the creature, and cover is often irrelevant because it just serves to provide you concealment from observation. If you don’t have a way to gain abilities that specifically circumvent this, then the only way to stealth around blindsight is simply to stay out of the blindsight’s range.

The ‘senses’ blindsense/tremorsense/lifesense allow you to “pinpoint” a target in range. This is a very different language from blindsight! Remember the “pinpointing” language from invisibility. You can still benefit from concealment against their normal senses. This can allow you to sneak attack with total concealment. However, you cannot benefit from stealth while pinpointed by a sense like this.

As with all rules in pathfinder, the specific trumps the general. The ability Hide in Plain Sight, and some others similar to it, allow a character to stealth while being observed. If that is the case, then none of these considerations matter. As long as the conditions that trigger the hide in plain sight ability (such as dim light or darkness in the shadow dancer’s case) are met, no amount of senses or observation can stop you from making a stealth check and gaining the benefits of total concealment and other creatures not being aware of you.

One last note. There is no “being observed by some enemies and stealthing against others.” The rules are very clear. “If enemies are observing you using any of their senses (but typically sight), you can’t use Stealth.” If any enemy is observing you, you can’t use stealth. Period. If there’s a dragon with blindsense and a bunch of kobolds who are blind, deaf, and have stuffy noses, you straight up can’t stealth from the kobolds while in range of the dragon’s blindsense. Maybe your GM will make a common sense house ruling that goes differently, but this is how the rules work.

### Notes on Cover and Concealment

Soft Cover does not allow for stealth outside of specific rules such as the Halfling’s Human Shadow trait.

Based on context clues, sans cover, total Concealment is required for stealth, not just partial concealment. With partial concealment, you are still partially observed. Total concealment implies that you aren’t being observed.

The spell Blur does not prevent you from being observed, even though it grants you concealment and a miss chance. Being observed always means you can’t stealth, no matter the other rules. The other rules are ways to avoid being observed. For example, the total concealment from being 10ft away from an enemy in Obscuring Mist grants total concealment, and thus the ability to stealth.

When we look at the spell Displacement, which seems as if it might offer total concealment, it carefully states “The creature benefits from a 50% miss chance as if it had total concealment. Unlike actual total concealment, displacement does not prevent enemies from targeting the creature normally.” We can understand from this language that we are not actually granted total concealment. And because enemies can target us normally, we are able to be observed by them. Displacement, on its own, does not allow us to stealth.

### How do I use stealth in combat as an Arcane Trickster?

Your primary concern as an arcane trickster is consistently gaining total concealment to enable sneak attacks while maintaining a good action economy that allows you to cast spells on most turns. You will either focus on sneak attacking through non-stealth total concealment, or through gaining total concealment through stealth while jumping between locations with cover, or stealth while sniping from a single point of cover.

#### Stealth and Cover

I’m going to talk about this method first, as it will likely be the first method an arcane trickster uses to gain steak attacks. You don’t have invisibility, and you don’t have other fancy magical effects to accomplish similar things. We’re going to want a very high stealth, because we’ll be taking steep penalties.

This tactic normally involves moving from cover to cover and shooting out sneak attacks in between. You stealth as part of a move action to reach cover. You are now stealthed which grants you total concealment. You then attack with a spell. That spell gains sneak attack due to you denying your target their dex bonus. Rinse and repeat.

Stealth isn’t just good for sneak attacking, it’s also an excellent defensive condition. The enemy, unaware of your location, can’t specifically target you, they can only lob area of effect abilities towards where they suspect you are, or hunt for you. But if you have a good frontline, it should be difficult to get past the frontline to bypass the cover and observe you.

So, obviously, it’s better to make your spell attack at the start of the turn, and then move to cover and stealth for the end of your turn. It’s hard to miss a turn, though. If you start your combat exposed and observed, your options are to cast a non-sneak-attack spell and then go to cover to stealth, or you can go to cover for stealth, and immediately break that stealth by attacking. Use your discretion. How exposed are you? How much danger will it put you in to end your turn without stealth? Of course, even if you do stealth, at -10 or -15 to the check, it can be very difficult to succeed sometimes.

An enemy pushing through to the backline is always a possibility. And there will be cases when there is no good cover or where the cover is compromised. Your group may be surrounded. This is why spells like vanish and invisibility are important. They let you hard reset your observed status (against enemies with typical senses), make your way to cover, and get an ensured sneak attack before restarting your typical cycle.

From levels 1-2, when you just can’t get sneak attacks off, it’s usually not worth casting vanish as a standard action every other turn just to get your sneak attack. Your rays of frost should be doing 1d3+1 damage at least for an average of 3 damage. 1d3+1d6+1 is an average of 6.5 damage. You’re spending a spell slot every other round for an extra .25 damage a round. Instead, you could be casting snowballs for 2-3d6 extra damage on the off rounds. If you have a bard or other buff spells for your damage, you’ll be doing even more than that.

At higher levels, you get better tools to make this more consistent. You stop having to find cover or concealment, because you gain effects that give you cover or concealment permanently. For example, you may be casting Ashen Path on your whole team, then dropping obscuring mist to give you total concealment against anything further than 10ft from you without Ashen Path. Or you might use Fog-Cutting Lenses to avoid even having to cast ashen path. Or the Cloak of Eternal Mist to generate the concealment automatically instead of fog cloud.

As you gain levels, you’ll be hunting for methods to obtain the holy grail of stealth, Hide in Plain Sight. There are a few ways to accomplish this. Hellcat Stealth and Shadow’s Shroud are both ways of obtaining some form of this ability. And once you have it, oh boy does it get better. Now, even when you’re surrounded and stared at by creatures with a mix of blindsight, blindsense, scent, or whatever, you can stealth. Because Hide in Plain Sight allows you to stealth while being observed, so none of these types of perception prevent you from stealthing anymore.

At that point, you can stealth as a 5ft step, and do whatever swift, move, and standard action you want on your turn.

#### Sniping

Sniping is another great way to gain sneak attack early on. You don’t need to suffer the up to -15 penalty for moving at full speed to a new source of cover, but you will suffer a -20 penalty for attempting the sniping action.

So why would you ever do this? Well, you no longer have to constantly hunt for cover. Once you find cover, you’re good until that cover is compromised or your enemy moves out of range. Furthermore, the feats that help with sniping give you a drastically higher reduction in its penalty than feats which help with stealth in general. See Expert Sniper which grants a whopping reduction of 10 points to that penalty. That’s huge at low levels!

Furthermore, you have a level 1 spell that gives you +20 stealth and hide in plain sight when you’re staying still. This requires you to take 1 turn to set up, but it’s fantastic for getting sneak attacks off. See Blend with Surroundings.

However, this method is much harder to consistently pull off. Most of your early ray attacks have a hard range limit of close. That’s only 25ft + 5ft/2 levels. Even if you have a spell that targets a longer range, you have a hard limit of 30ft for your sneak attack until you manage to pick up sniper’s goggles. Once you have sniper’s goggles, this method becomes much more effective. But it’s also always going to be more effective when you have feats and abilities that help your stealth and ability to hide in plain sight in the first place.

Furthermore, when you fail to snipe, you now need to find a new source of cover or concealment just as the first method does.

Sniping, in summary, can be useful, but it can be hard to invest the feats specifically for it when you also need feats or items to benefit stealth in general. And those benefits will automatically make the first method more effective as well. So why put extra investment in sniping?

#### Non-Stealth Total Concealment

You can start getting consistent concealment as soon as level 4, though you’ll want to balance utility magic with powerful offensive magic like scorching ray. In either case, as soon as you have level 2 spells, you have access to ashen path. Ashen path lets you see through all magical fogs, smokes, ash, and similar forms of concealment within 60ft. Even better, you can split the duration across your party, so you aren’t making it harder for them to hit through your fogs. Then, you lay down obscuring mist to give you total concealment against any enemy more than 10ft away.

Of course, that method takes two spells. You can prebuff ashen path, but it’s often a bad idea to spend more than one round setting up in a combat. This is where your excellence as a scout comes into play. Try your best to anticipate combats, and cast ashen path before you engage. Then it’s only a single turn to lay down your obscuring mist before blasting out sneak attack rays without needing any stealth check.

At 4th level spells, you get the major upgrade to this method with the spell greater invisibility.

Different ‘senses’ won’t remove your concealment, but unfortunately, you still have blindsight to frustrate you. For that, you’ll either need to have Sniper’s Goggles and keep out of the sensory range, or you’ll need to resort to Hide in Plain Sight stealth abilities like Hellcat Stealth and Shadow’s Shroud.

## Rating System

Red Typically a trap option. These sorts of abilities were usually developed as part of a splat book aimed at hyper-specific scenarios, or for NPCs to utilize. Take it only if you’re sure that the scenario it was made for will happen often.

Orange An option that may work for certain specific scenarios. Not necessarily a trap option, but one that may sit unused on your character sheet for the majority of your game, or which may be more commonly used but underpowered in effectiveness relative to most options. Take a few of these as flavorful options, but be careful of choosing too many or you might feel less useful than other party members.

Green A typical good choice for a character. Typically some balance is frequently usable and well-balanced. Or perhaps less frequently usable, but powerful in those circumstances.

Blue A strong choice for your level. Rises above the typical level of your average options. These are strong picks for your character.

Purple A fantastic choice. I might use this to denote an overpowered option, or an option that’s just an essential pick to make the class work right. You don’t have to pick these options, but you will notice a serious difference in how your character plays when you do.

Throughout this guide, wherever possible, I accompany the names of game options with links to the source material. When you see Arcane Trickster, you can often click on that, and reference the content directly.
