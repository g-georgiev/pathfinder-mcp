# Equipment

Equipment
Here’s where you come to look for what toys you need to optimize your characters. We’ll cover mundane gear, magic gear, magic armor, and magic weapons in depth. Next to each entry, I’ll include the book cost of the item, and its weight for carrying capacity, so you have a guess at about what level you can afford it and when you can expect to see it.
Mundane Gear
This is going to cover anything that doesn’t have magic on it, so expect a lot of these to be early level choices, though some will be useful for your entire career so be sure to know what is and isn’t important.
Adventuring Gear
Fighter’s Kit [9 gp, 29 lbs]: for mundane gear, the fighter’s kit has about everything you could want; 9gp is well worth the coin at first level.
 
Chalk [1 cp]: your kit doesn’t include it, but this is never useless, especially in dungeon crawls and you don’t want a whole kit for it. Mark your path, draw symbols or messages. Always useful.
 
Armor Truss [30 gp, 10 lbs] (Melee Tactics Toolbox): If your DM cares about donning armor, one of this is essential for self sustaining fighters.
 
Healer’s Kit [50 gp, 1 lb]: it’s really expensive for what it does, I blame big Pharma. At earlier levels you’ll need one of these in the party to keep the healer alive if (s)he goes down, after that first little while NEVER AGAIN.
 
Masterwork Backpack [50 gp, 4 lbs]: Not only does it have more pockets for you to BS your way into carrying more stuff around to your DM, but it treats your STR as +1 higher for carrying capacity.
 
Explorer’s Outfit [10 gp, 8 lbs]: clothing matters, and the explorer’s outfit is one you can start with for free due to its cost, so it’s basically the best one. It’s heavy, but it has everything you’ll need, if it ever comes up.
 
Mirror [10 gp, ½ lb]: these come in handy for reflecting light in dungeons to being able to look around corners more safely.
 
Satchel, Familiar [25 gp, 6 lbs]: if you really need to keep your familiar safe….
 
Weapons
This will come down to a number of choices, the main choice being your primary role in combat. The second will be you selection of weapon groups, which can determine certain feat allowances on your build. We’ll only go over the ones that stand out as being notable. Remember when picking weapons to use as a primary weapon that crit range is king, multiplier is an afterthought.
Simple Weapons (Melee):
[light] Gauntlet/Spiked Gauntlets- these weapons really come in handy since they are easily worn and carried. You’re always wielding them, so consider them for those weapon enhancements you want always on, like Training, Dueling, and others. It’s also always smart to carry a light weapon in case you get swallowed/grappled.
 
[light] Cestus- there’s very little difference between the two types of gauntlets and the cestus. The cestus is a little bit more versatile since it can do different types of damage, but it gives you a -2 on anything that requires handywork.
 
[light, thrown] Dagger/Punching Dagger- generally speaking, daggers are go to weapons to have on your side. They have a range and can be thrown, as well as being reliable in case you find yourself in need of a light weapon.
 
[one-handed] Morningstar- accept no substitutes, this does B and P damage at the same time, if you feel that will matter. It’s a simple weapon so its criticals suck, but it has a decent damage die.
 
[one-handed, thrown] Short Spear- it falls into several weapon groups, including thrown and spears meaning you can build around having access to certain things like gaining Perception through Versatile Training, and using a shield and wielding this two-handed with Shield Brace.
 
[two-handed] Longspear- spears are good for the same reasons, this one has reach.
 
[two-handed, thrown] Spear- the main issue here is that it’s a two-handed weapon that has only slightly better damage dice than the shortspear. Still though, spears are good.
Simple Weapons (Ranged):
[two-handed] Crossbows- all variations of this weapon are weak, since you rely solely on damage dice and bonuses from feats and class features for damage with it. Plus reloading, which sucks. Decent crit range.
 
[two-handed] Stonebow- it’s exactly the same as a crossbow, but with bullets.
Martial Weapons (Melee):
[light] Kukri- your best option for light weapons, it has the best crit range you will find.
 
[light] Armor Spikes- another great option for when you get grappled, like the spiked gauntlets these are always on and another great spot to put on more “always on” enhancements.
 
[light] Short Sword/ Machete- these two weapons are mechanically identical, but the short sword has more crunch for it. Super generic light slashing weapon, use only if and when kukris aren’t around.
 
[light, thrown] Starknife- James Jacobs invented this weapon, and this weapon has the most support as far as splatbooks go. You can get DEX to damage with these, and they work as light weapons and as thrown weapons. You can also get CHA to hit and damage with them. It’s got a bad crit range, but a x3 multiplier. Most of that can be ignored if you’re building for damage and bypassing STR.
 
[one-handed] Combat Scabbard- if you’re using a sword, consider one of these in case you need access to another weapon. It’s also cool, but not necessary.
 
[one-handed] Cutlass/Scimitar- the go-to for one handed weapons. Decent damage die for the size, and the best crit range in the game. The Scimitar is wildly popular thanks to Dervish Dance enabling DEX builds, though Slashing Grace and Fighter’s Finesse has opened up more weapons to this option. A cutlass is treated as a scimitar for feats, thanks to Adventurer's Armory 2.
 
[one-handed] Klar- it counts as a shield and has better damage dice than a shield.
 
[one-handed] Longsword- there’s a great iconography that comes from the longsword, and many exotic weapons tend to fall into “longsword that does X” by design. All weapons are compared to this one, if it’s not better than this then it’s not worth mentioning. It has okay damage and a decent crit range.
 
[one-handed] Rapier- this is the weapon designed for duelists, good dice, good crit range, finessable, and it has it’s own DEX to damage feat.
 
[one-handed] Warhammer- the longsword of bludgeoning damage, not a great crit machine so I avoid most bludgeoning weapons.
 
[two-handed] Bardiche- best crit range you’ll find on a martial reach weapon.
 
[two-handed] Earthbreaker- I don’t like a lot of Bludgeoning weapons because I don’t like their crit ranges, but the Earthbreaker is the greatsword of bludgeoners.
 
[two-handed] Falchion- the damage dice are a bit weak, being just a hair better than what you could get with a one-handed weapon (d8), but that 18-20 crit range on a two-handed weapon is nice, and it’s almost always better to roll two dice than one. Almost, until you get to eastern weapons.
 
[two-handed] Heavy Flail- if you’re considering a two-handed bludgeoning weapon (or really any bludgeoning weapon) let it be this one. One of the only B weapons you will find with a 19-20 crit range.
 
[two-handed] Glaive- it’s here because Sheyln worshipers got a feat (Bladed Brush) that more or less enables DEX to damage for it. Be sure to go over it with your DM before building this way. It’s unclear as to how said feat really interacts with certain game mechanics like two-handed damage with DEX or Power Attack.
 
[two-handed] Greataxe- d12 x3, it’s impressive and unique, but underwhelming in performance.
 
[two-handed] Greatsword- it has better damage dice than the falchion which leads to a lot of people choosing this weapon over it, and I can’t really blame people. Decent crit range, it’s a classic for a reason and it will be seen often (including in loots) so it’s usually safe to build around.
 
[two-handed] Lance- small damage dice, but it has a big crit multiplier. Thing is, this is the weapon on mounted builds. Other thing is, mounted builds are best for other classes.
 
*-*[two-handed] Nodachi*-*- of all the eastern weapons, this is the one that makes DMs ban them. It’s a martial weapon to start, and it has a d10 and the 18-20 crit range. It’s better than the falchion in almost every way, and when compared to the greatsword you’ll see this taken more times than not. It also counts as a polearm for some stupid reason meaning you can use one of these with Shield Brace. Good weapon is good.
 
[two-handed] Scythe- small damage dice, but x4 crit multiplier. Often you will never get to use it sadly.
Martial Weapons (Ranged):
[thrown] Chakram/Hurlbat- these weapons serve the same mechanical purpose, that is to be worse than most other throwing weapons. You need either ricochet toss or a lot of money to build around these.
 
[two-handed] Longbow- no one uses a longbow, because like the crossbow you get no bonus to damage aside from those you get from feats and your class. But if you find one, it’s not useless since all the same feats work for it and its upgrade:
 
[two-handed] Composite Longbow- this is the ranged weapon that you probably wanna use. Eventually you can skip out on upgrading your bow to your relevant STR modifier and instead can just dump 1,000 gp on an adaptive bow.
 
[two-handed] Shortbow- only bards use short bows, because that’s what they’re proficient with. Worse damage than the longbow, worse range.
 
[two-handed] Composite Shortbow- ONLY BARDS USE SHORT BOWS!
 
[two-handed] Spear Sling- this is a really cool weapon, since it has a STR modifier like the composite bows, but it doesn’t qualify for Adaptive and even with Rapid Reload you won’t be getting off full attacks. Shame.
Exotic Weapons (Melee):
[light] Gnome Pincher- a bludgeoning weapon that doubles as something you can use to pick things up. Gnomes get it for free, too bad it sucks mechanically.
 
[light] Halfling Rope Shot- a fun little weapon that you can swing around like a flail. Not very effective, but it’s something halflings get.
 
[light] Dwarven Boulder Helm- without needing to take Steel Headbutt, dwarves actually get something really awesome. Since dwarves are proficient with it, it’s another great way to have an “always on” slot.
 
[light] Dwarven Maulaxe- it’s the light axe weapon you’ve been waiting for, but generally 20/x3 weapons aren’t good to build around.
 
[light] Scorpion Whip- better than the whip in almost every way, though whips still aren’t great especially if you have to waste a feat on it. Ioun stones can help with that.
 
[light] Wakizashi- this is the upgrade to the kukri, big time. Best light weapon in the game, bar none.
 
[one-handed] Falcata- this is an amazing weapon, and the first in our “better longsword” weapons. This time, it just has a better crit multiplier, which means this weapon has the best crit range to multiplier ratio in the game.
 
[one-handed] Rhoka- longsword with a better crit range. (18-20)
 
[one-handed] Sawtooth Sabre- longsword that counts as light for TWF, can be used without proficiency as a longsword.
 
[one-handed] Dueling Sword- longsword that can be used with Weapon Finesse, can be used without proficiency.
 
[one-handed] Bastard Sword- strictly inferior to the longsword unless you can get free proficiency, it’s half-way between the longsword and the greatsword. Can be used as a two-handed weapon without proficiency if you’re into that (can be worth it if you like the d10 better than 2d6 for whatever reason).
 
[one-handed] Dwarven Waraxe- getting a d10 weapon with a x3 multiplier is cool for dwarves, but still better off with something with a better crit range. The double one is the same, but better for cleaving.
 
[one-handed] Katana- it’s the eastern bastard sword, and it can be used as a martial weapon 2-handed. Comparable directly to the falchion, this is okay but better if you can get free proficiency.
 
[one-handed] Temple Sword- longsword that can be used by monks.
 
[one-handed] Urumi- mechanically identical to the Rhoka, save for the price. I remember that one guy in Rurouni Kenshin used one, it’s decent but not great considering the feat.
 
[one-handed] Whip- terrible weapon that requires a lot of investment to make work as well as martial weapons with a few extra abilities.
 
[two-handed] Orc Double Axe- orcs get a double weapon, it’s not that good.
 
[two-handed] Spiked Chain- it got nerfed hard from it’s 3.5 glory, no longer worth looking into any more than any other weapon. Finessable.
 
[two-handed] Elven Curve Blade- this weapon is great for elves who don’t want to build around STR, since they get free proficiency with it they can build into Elven Battle Style and use DEX to hit and INT x1.5 for damage. Plus a great crit range.
 
[two-handed] Dwarven Dorn Dergar- bland reach weapon for dwarves with nothing special as far as crits go.; Well, that changed. We got some old gems from the 3.5 days back with Adventurer’s Armory 2, and now this weapon has some great versatility. Dwarves get nice things.
 
[two-handed] Elven Branched Spear- much like the curve blade, you can build around this with Weapon Finesse and Elven Battle style for a reach build that gets around the need for STR.
 
[two-handed] Fauchard- best crit range for a reach weapon.
 
[two-handed] Flambard- it’s a bastard sword unless you are proficient with it, in which case sunder away at wooden things.
 
[two-handed] Gnome Hooked Hammer- this weapon offers damage versatility to gnomes. Gnomes would probably rather use starknives….
 
[two-handed] Dwarven Longaxe- reach slashing weapon with a d12 and x3. Meh.
 
[two-handed] Dwarven Longhammer- reach earthbreaker for dwarves.
 
[two-handed] Orc Skull Ram- reach Bludgeoning weapon for orcs.
 
[two-handed] Two-Bladed Sword- longsword at both ends.
 
[two-handed] Totem Spear- a spear that gives you +2 on acrobatics, and can do a d10 slashing or piercing. But the feat….
 
[two-handed] Dwarven Urgrosh- a neat double weapon for dwarves that offers some versatility.
Exotic Weapons (Ranged):
[thrown] Crystal Chakram- good crit range and they’re treated as ammunition for crafting purposes. I guess this is what you want from a thrown weapon if only it wasn’t exotic. They have a 50% chance to break if you miss, but they are meant to break anyway if you hit.
 
[two-handed] Crank Crossbow- it doubles as a grappling hook, batman style. Points for being cool, but it still falls into the same issues we had with crossbows and damage.
 
[two-handed] Double Crossbow- the action economy here to reload is atrocious and weak. You don’t add an ability modifier to damage. Good news, this actually does replace Manyshot for regular archery, too bad crossbows still suck.
 
[one-handed] Hand Crossbow- if you could manage to find a way to add damage to these guys, I would love them. This is almost a good crossbow.
 
[two-handed] Launching Crossbow- that one ratfolk build that can use alchemical weapons like an alchemist finally gets a weapon, and ironically he can make crossbows work. Nice!
 
[two-handed] Orc Hornbow (Adventurer's Armory 2): it has a shorter range than the composite longbow, but does 2d6 base damage. If you’re in PBS range, it won’t matter.
 
[two-handed?] Repeating Crossbow- you can manage to get around the reloading issue with these for a little bit, but the problems with crossbows damage persist.
 
[two-handed?] Flask Thrower- another weapon intended to make splash weapons better by extending the reach. I think the crossbow is actually better for this.
 
[thrown-ish] Halfling Sling Staff- halflings get a free weapon that is actually quite nice. When using the sling, you get to add STR to damage like with thrown weapons. Too bad halflings have that STR penalty.
Weapon Modifications (Adventurer’s Armory 2)
[bludgeoning melee] Brutally Weighted: since the game expects you to waste an extra feat to use this, unless you really wanna go with the tetsubo, I would not bother since the weapons this affects are not really in your ballpark for optimization.
 
[melee] Dual-Balanced: if you’re going two-weapon fighting, there’s yet ANOTHER feat you need, unless you plan on using simple, light weapons. Still, if you need it, you need it.
 
[piercing or slashing melee] Serrated Edge: this lets you add bleed damage, and it works on a couple exotic weapons without needing an EXTRA feat to use them. The magical enhancement is better, but this works in an antimagic field.
 
[melee] Tactically Adapted: are you going Maneuver Monkey and really wanna use a different weapon? This can help you with that, immensely.
 
Versatile Design: if you ever need to change weapon groups on the fly, this modification can help you out a lot. This means there are some weapon group specific things that you can now transcend like the Versatile Training option for AWT. Consider it if it tickles your fancy to have more options.


 
Armor & Shields
The armor that you wear is almost as important as the weapons you wield. AC is a very important statistic because it controls how often you get hit vs. how often you should get hit. You can build for monster AC, or you can build more moderately to get into that sweet spot range where enemies might still try to hit you instead of someone else they can actually hit and do damage to. Armor Training is a factor here, and you’re almost always gonna end up with max DEX +1 thanks to it. Thanks to that, plus the ability to move at full speed regardless of armor at lvl 7, you will see a lot of heavy armor choices being highlighted as the best. Remember certain choices in your build require a specific type of armor so be considerate of that.
Light Armor:
Armored Kilt (Adventurer’s Armory): this is an odd piece of equipment from Adventurer’s Armory, as it simply increases the weight category of the armor and trades +1 DEX for +1 armor to AC. There’s no word on how this interacts with mithral or darkleaf cloth, so if you’re running around in medium armor for some reason and don’t care about DEX here you go.
 
Leaf Armor (Inner Sea World Guide): this armor is nasty expensive, 500 gp for what comes down to the same thing basically as studded leather. Since you have armor training, the fact that this thing doesn’t have an Armor Check Penalty really doesn’t matter. Save your money.
 
Parade Armor/Studded Leather (Adventurer's Armory & CRB): these two armors have the exact same statistics and price. They start with a 5 max DEX, so this is probably the armor to go with if you intend on building a DEX monster. Can be made with Darkleaf for even higher max DEX and nonexistent ACP. Also not metal for when that matters once in awhile.
 
Leather Lamellar: of all the eastern armors, this is the one that makes the list on the light category. Leather lamellar is much cheaper than a chain shirt (by a first level character’s perspective) but it does suffer from that lower DEX by 1. This can also be made from Darkleaf, which is cheaper than mithral for DEX builds that don’t plan on having monstrous stats. Overall it’s better than studded leather in the long run unless you have that DEX, so buy accordingly.
 
Chain Shirt: this is the best light armor for a reason. You will not find a better ratio of DEX/armor bonus. Classically, these are often made mithral for DEX builds to exploit the 6 max DEX on them. For early levels before you even get Armor Training 1, this or the leather lamellar are your best choices.
 
Spider-Silk Bodysuit (Adventurer’s Armory 2): you will find this superior to studded leather in almost every way but the price. Don’t count on benefitting from darkleaf for this, but you can still rock up to an 8 DEX mod with your drow weapon familiarity...
Medium Armor:
Hide: for medium armors, hide is pretty bad. It has slightly worse stats than chainmail, but slightly better than leather lamellar. That said, it is one of the very few non metal medium armors out there and it can be made of Darkleaf which creates statistically the best non metal “light” armor out there which makes it worth mentioning. (Darkleaf actually doesn’t treat it as light.)
 
Horn Lamellar: it isn’t really clear what this is made out of (game mechanics wise) so it seems impossible to consider a special material for this armor. To that end it’s just scale mail that costs twice as much.
 
Chain Coat (Adventurer’s Armory 2): perhaps this has some uses, perhaps not. It has the best max DEX of any armor you can make from mithral, so when rocking that you can boast some impressive DEX.
 
Chainmail: it’s iconic but often not used since a breastplate is better in almost every way. The few times you do see chainmail used, is often because it comes prepackaged as part of a specific item that has amazing benefits like Elven Chain counting as light armor for prerequisites (Lore Wardens-that’s you!) or as part of the base Celestial Armor. It can save you on money if you don’t wanna invest in DEX, but not much.
 
Kikko: this is a very famous armor for its goofy stats. On it’s own it actually can compare to the breastplate in terms of max AC. If you make it out of mithral you get the best light armor in the game since it has no ACP and has no effect on your attack rolls or skills anyway even if you aren’t proficient in it. Compare it to Elven Chain for builds who care about light armor and DEX.
 
Breastplate/Agile Breastplate: this is the end all for medium armor. It has the best stats, though you might note how expensive it is-especially the agile variant. Agile breastplates lessen your ACP on climb and jump checks for games where that matters. Mountain Pattern armor from the eastern armors has the same stats as regular breastplate, but its price falls in between the breastplate and the agile version. Naturally mithral is an option here.
 
Heavy Armor:
Banded Mail: it makes the list because of the price. You end up with 2 less armor bonus to AC than plate, but at one sixth of the price. For early levels this matters if you really want that AC boost. Don’t build your character around it though.
 
Tatami-Do: the best heavy armor for DEX based builds. It’s a little bit better than a breastplate, but for fighters who can move at full speed in it anyway that could make the difference for you if you’re crunching numbers. Mithral heavy armor that still gets 5 max DEX is amazing, plus it gets the full benefits of Armor Specialization. Combined with armor training you can still rock up to a 28 DEX and benefit from it fully in heavy (well, medium) armor.
 
O-Yoroi: this falls between tatami-do and full plate. It’s super expensive, but it does offer you some different DEX/armor bonus ratio for builds who are crunching numbers with how high your DEX is gonna end up.
 
Full Plate: the iconic and plain armor you expect to see on most heavy armored builds. Mithral is generally a must on this build and you’ll never find an armor with better bonus than this. You may find other materials that suit your needs like the Dwarven Plate which like Elven Chain is just the same item as the base made from a special material, only this one doesn’t play with proficiency.
 
Hellknight Plate (Inner Sea World Guide): more expensive than full plate and the Hellknight prestige class is not a fighter exclusive thing. It’s getting mentioned because it has one fewer ACP than regular old plate and that might matter to you. Technically it is the best armor, but you’ll have to deal with being dressed like a Hellknight for better or worse depending on your game.
Shields:
Buckler: normally people scoff at these for being useless compared to heavier shields for AC, but the buckler has gotten some love in recent supplements. You can use one with a bow or other weapons, and there are ways to mitigate the penalties so you don’t have to take on the silly -1 to attack rolls. It’s a tie between this and building for Shield Gauntlet Style. Both cost feats and money.
 
Light Shield (metal or wood): don’t bother, it’s worse than the buckler in almost every way possible.
 
Quickdraw Light Shield: if you intend on switching between using a shield and not, I’d probably still tell you to take Shield Gauntlet Style or a Buckler, but this item can be seen as a cheaper way to deal with the issue.
 
Heavy Shield (metal or wood): this is your mainstay for shields, since nothing really is better than it without building for it specifically. It’s the best one to use as a weapon, and it’s the best one to use for defense. There’s a specific item, the Mithral Heavy Shield that comes from Ultimate Equipment and answers the question on how to make one of these mithral, which I recommend unless you’re staying away from metal in which case look at Darkwood.
 
Tower Shield: you’re the only class that is proficient with these, rejoice. Thanks to that, you may actually consider building around one of these. Great defensive bonuses, but you still need feats to be able to function in combat properly when using one of these. Consider it for power turtles.
Throwing Shield: you can spend the money to add this to unclasp it as a free action. Quickdraw shields just became obsolete. Still, I wouldn’t consider it for an actual weapon as there is a class built for that.
 
Boss, Breakaway: for that one ratfolk build? It enables melee damage with splash weapons so you can consider it for anyone who wants to shield bash as well, adding some nice damage options for certain enemies with weaknesses.
 
Boss, Hooked: this allows you to do a trip attack to cause your enemy to become flat-footed (not prone). If you’re building a maneuver monkey and already don’t provoke when you trip this can be an amazing addition to your options.
 
Boss, Illuminating: generally you will not want to spend money on this, since light is a cantrip and an orison you’ll probably have someone on your team to deal with this anyway. That said, a sunrod does light better than most other ways so if it’s a necessity to your team you should consider it.
 
Boss, Reinforcing: if you are concerned about you shield being sundered or broken, one of these can help, though they are mostly extraneous.
Armor Modifications (Adventurer’s Armory 2)
[metal] Burnished: dazzled sucks.
 
Deflecting: I wouldn’t bother wasting a feat for a situational +1, but that’s just me. There are better options.
 
Double-Plated: if you’re running one of those builds that really doesn’t care about adding DEX to your AC, then this is for you. You will have to waste a feat though, if you’re using heavy armor as a base.
 
Jarring: the DC scales at a pretty decent rate, but sickened is a terribly weak condition to waste your money on.
 
Nimble: I was wondering when and how we’d get an equivalent to the 3.5 version of this. Only thing is, this is better! Enjoy having higher DEX, and if you want better armor too take the feat. Great for anyone who cares about maxing out that DEX/AC ratio.
 
Razored: you get better armor spikes, but you lose speed. Unless you’re a dwarf. Dwarves continue to get nice things. They use your armor’s enhancement bonus for their weapon enhancement bonus. If you take nothing else, take this.
 
Slumbering: you can now buy the Endurance feat, or improve you’re already existing Endurance feat. If your DM cares about this, take it.
 
Vitalguard: gives you +2 AC to enemy confirmation rolls. Not bad, probably worth losing 5ft of speed. Again, dwarves need not give a damn.
Special Materials
Abyssium (AP 61- Shards of Sin): immune to poison? Then avoid this at all costs. If you are immune to poison, go wild.
 
Adamantine: holy expensive! Weapons made from this have great uses, and I pretty much always make sure to pick one up. Armor grants you DR, which can stack with armored juggernaut. Are you building for DR? Get heavy armor made from this. Figure out the money later, it’s worth it. Also crafting technically reduces the cost to a third, as generally that’s considered the cost of the raw materials.
 
Angelskin: if you want to make one of those non-metal armors, this can help mask an evil alignment and protect you from things like Smite or Detect Evil. Niche utility, but an important one when it comes up.
 
Blood Crystal: probably one of the most metal materials on here, ironically it isn't even metal. If you’re building around a bleed build, like with Bleeding Critical and what not, then this is the material for you. Everyone else can skip it.
 
Darkleaf Cloth: I’m really glad this carried over from 3.5. It’s mithral for non-metal armors, so you can get some nice things going. It’s also relatively cheap as far as special materials go. It doesn’t reduce movement classification, but you shouldn’t really care because fighter.
 
Darkwood: this material is great for wooden shields, as it also functions just like mithral. There’s no rules for making armor out of this material, but I did have some fun with the idea a while ago, feel free to check that out.
 
Dragonhide (CRB & Dragon Slayer’s Handbook): I really wish I could cite the cool stuff from 3.5 that one could do with dragon corpses, but alas I cannot in this guide. In Dragon Slayer’s, it got a buff in being able to reduce the cost of energy resistance by 25%. Not as good as Dragoncraft Armor, but then again it really can’t be. It’s better for druids tbh.
 
Dragonskin (Dragon Slayer’s Handbook): this cannot make any armor, but it can make a decent grip for a weapon to help with being disarmed. Seems cool enough to add onto your main weapon, but is nowhere near essential.
 
Eel Hide: this material isn’t as good as Darkleaf, but it has some interesting properties. You can make all the leather armors out of it, so you can get some studded leather with reduced ACP and a slightly higher max DEX. You also randomly get electricity resistance 2, for games where that matters a lot.
 
Elysian Bronze: Adamantine that is specialized for monstrous humanoids and magical beasts to be your enemy. If you’re in a game where that’s the case, buy this over Adamantine every time, otherwise it’s just plain inferior.
 
Fire/Frost- Forged Steel: small little boosts like these are really nice in random loot situations. A suit of armor made from one of these grants energy resistance 2, which I believe could be enough to mitigate the damage from environments that Endure Elements otherwise covers. Fire is better thanks to the lack of nonmagical cold damage in enough bursts to gain damage, but otherwise a set of weapons and armor of these can be great in games where the environment matters. The rules are iffy, so check with your DM before assuming this material will protect you.
 
Greenwood: this is great for bows or other mostly wooden weapons that you want to make sure don’t break. Granted, most of the time this is a rare bookkeeping chore that most DMs don't enforce so it may be useless aside from the cool factor.
 
Griffon Mane: getting a cloak or something made of this can give you a small bonus on fly checks, which may or may not be relevant to you at later levels. It’s fairly cheap so it’s worth mentioning, but not essential.
 
Horacalcum (AP 61- Shards of Sin): this material is insane. Mad expensive, but it gives you a +1 circumstance to hit with weapons and a scaling bonus to initiative based on the size category of the armor. The cost is enough to dock it though, at least on armor.
 
Inubrix (AP 61- Shards of Sin): what an interesting material! You’ll have to figure out something for the damage dice, but you get to bypass certain metal armors completely. Worth it for certain scenarios.
 
Cold Iron: It’s here because it bypasses some DR, but i has no benefits for armor and it just adds costs to weapons. Maybe grab a weapon like this in games where fey and demons show up, but otherwise pass.
 
Living Steel: much like Greenwood, this material may or may not be entirely useful depending on your games. Armor made of this material gives enemies a reason to fear Nat 1’s which can be cool, but rare.
 
Mithral: you should already know what this is, as it’s ripped pretty much straight from Tolkein. Most people who aren’t sure about what to get their armor made from go for this, as it improves your DEX, speed for anyone that doesn’t have armor training 2 yet, and reduces ACP. Cost is high, but worth it. It also counts as silver for DR bypassing, nice!
 
Noqual (AP 61- Shards of Sin): super mithral for you. This stuff includes a free +2 resistance bonus against spells for armor, and gains an additional bonus against constructs and undead created by magic. You can’t cast spells, but you really don’t care. Generally though, you’re gonna be better off just paying for mithral because of the price point.
 
Siccatite (AP 61- Shards of Sin): this seems like an upgrade to Fire/Frost- Forged Steel, but it has a few differences. You get to just use the energy all the time, but you also take damage all the time. If you plan on using this (which you might) make sure you are playing a race that has or otherwise have the ability to gain energy resistance to the type of energy this armor gives off. It’s fairly cheap as well, which is nice. It also includes energy resistance 5 of the other type.
 
Alchemical Silver: for special materials, this one is a cheap way to get past DR. The weapons themselves aren’t that great because you take a -1 on damage rolls anyway, so only grab it if you absolutely need it now, or use some Silversheen (Qadira, Gateway to the East) instead.
 
Glaucite (AP 61- Shards of Sin): an alloy of iron and Adamantine, this metal really only offers better hardness and hp/inch than iron. I also can’t find a price for it, so probably don’t use it.
 
Viridium: gross, this stuff is poisonous to carry and the benefits of using it as a weapon don’t really matter for a DC 12 Fort save.
 
Whipwood: the Vanara race has a cool unique material to them which enhances wooden weapons like bows. It’s great to have the added HP and resistance to Sunder, though it may not come up in your games much.
 
Wyroot: this only works on builds that use an arcane pool or ki, which isn’t you. Even then it’s mediocre.


 
Alchemical Items
You will find these among the most useful of the mundane and nonmagical equipment you can pick up. They cover a large variety of uses, from utility to damage so be sure to note what you need on your character specifically.
 
[light] Liquid Blade: not a fan, of poisons or this weapon. Better on a different build.
 
[one-handed] Flame Fountain Firework: this is a really cool item, and if you fight things a lot that you need fire damage against (like trolls, or specific colored dragons) then carrying one of these can be useful, but they are mad expensive for the level(s) at which they are useful.
 
[splash] Acid: the most generic alchemical weapon you will find, and it’s pretty solid. You may want some to deal with trolls, or need some to deal with other enemies that need to be hit with acid to die. Fairly cheap as well.
 
[splash] Alchemist’s Fire: this costs twice as much as acid, but oftens serves the same purposes, and is a little better. If you have to pick between this and a flask of acid, pick this. For any alchemical weapon user this is your go-to weapon for the best price to effect ratio. Lighting things on fire is an important ability.
 
[splash] Alkali Flask: a straight upgrade to a regular flask of acid, this also greatly helps get rid of oozes, which you hate. This is the other go-to alchemical weapon.
 
[ranged touch] Bottled Lightning: very few other ways to get electricity damage, and this seems cool, but your abilities on your builds tend to focus more on splash weapons. If you need electricity (you probably won’t, but who knows) then pick some up, but stick to the better splash weapons if you need alchemical weapons.
 
[splash] Burst Jar: take away the caster’s turn(s)! Always take away the caster’s turn(s)! The damage is only secondary to a guaranteed deafen for at least 1 round, a possible stun, or forcing them to spend a whole turn avoiding it, thus a turn not casting.
 
[splash] Fuse Grenade: time lapse weapons are bad, you gotta hold onto it for 2 rounds then throw it. Ewwwwwwww.
 
[splash] Holy Water: fighting undead? This can help, a little. It’s better at lower levels when the undead can actually die from low amounts of area damage.
 
 
 
[balm] Holy Weapon Balm: fighting undead? This can help, A LOT. Each one of these is expensive (30gp a shot), but they can really add to your ability to land hits since they can treat your weapon as magical if it isn't already, and it can add 2d4 to your weapon’s attack. They are once and done kind of things on melee weapons, which means you waste a lot of time with them (standard action) but it’s better than the alternative when dealing with enemies that you need this for. Incorporeal enemies are game enders. Amazing on ammunition. Use at low levels.
 
[ranged touch] Keros Oil: do not swallow oil, do not try and spit out oil with a terrible range increment that does less damage than Alchemist’s Fire.
 
[splash] Liquid Ice: like Alchemist’s Fire, but twice as much and it does cold damage. Solid for what it does, but expensive.
 
[splash] Pellet Grenade: a very nice (and somehow cheaper?) upgrade to the fuse grenade, but it’s still a fuse grenade so you have the timing issue. Price is better too, and you have options to get over DR.
 
[splash] Flash Powder: Fort 13 is not that reliable, even at low levels.
 
[splash] Itching Powder: it stacks with debuffs like shaken or sickened, but the DC is only 12. Don’t bother.
 
[splash] Sneezing Powder: see itching powder.
 
[splash] Shard Gel: piercing damage with a splash weapon is pretty cool. Great if you need generic damage because things become immune to energy damage at later levels.
 
[splash] Tangleburn Bag: these are REALLLYYY expensive for what they do, but what they do is amazing, not only can they entangle an enemy with an amazing Reflex DC (25) but they also do fire damage. If going with alchemical weapons this is not one to miss.
 
[splash] Tanglefoot Bag: super useful if you ever want to hinder an enemy and not kill them, great for intrigue campaigns or intrigue like moments where you need to keep someone alive. It can also slow down spellcasters.
 
[ranged touch] Thunderstone: creates a deafening bang. DC is only 15, so it becomes pretty not useful once you get a few levels in.


 
Magic Items
There’s a great number of magic items in the game to consider, and a great number of magical abilities to attain. I can’t pretend to own every book or that I am all knowing when it comes to certain items being useful, there’s also the issues with errata coming out that changes certain things. Be aware of these and be careful when shopping with your DM as “Magic Mart” is not always available and choosing to build around certain magic items may hurt you more than it helps.
Magic Weapons
Remember that with all the abilities listed here, if you have the Warrior Spirit Advanced Weapon Training, then any and all of these are available to you on the fly, regardless of money or time. Consider this when you look into what abilities are worth investing into, and what abilities are better to grab mid combat. I’ll try and make a note of any situational differences in the description text, expect the rating to be based on its value to invest into, and any change in its rating based on Warrior Spirit to be listed in the description.
+1 Abilities
Agile (AP 100: A song of Silver): DERP. This is awesome, because slashing/fencing/starry grace isn’t. This ability is amazing, because DEX to damage.
 
Allying: I only really see one use of this, and it’s munchkin city. If you can manage to get a single weapon (like a gauntlet or helmet) that you will always have on you to +5 (or however high you want to get) you can keep that weapon equipped and target yourself as the ally to use this weapon with, meaning you can golfbag weapons without fear. Think of it as a really expensive way to cast Greater Magic Weapon.
 
Bane: this depends on your campaign a lot. If you find yourself fighting one type of enemy a lot, like undead or dragons, then nab this early as it’s essential to pumping those numbers up. Otherwise it is really high on the shortlist of Warrior Spirit options, if neither of those things are true, then this becomes rather situational.
 
Beaming (Blood of Shadows): WOW, once per day you get to shoot Searing Light at things, cool thing is it’s already CL 10th so it’s maxed out on damage and it only gets better as you increase your weapon’s enhancement bonus. Nice.
 
Benevolent: if you are using the familiar or teamwork builds that abuse aid another then pick this up, otherwise avoid it.
 
Bewildering (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): this is okay, DC 17 isn’t that bad but Will is not the save to target.
Blood-Hunting: it’s not Magebane. It can be a nice alternative if you are in a game where there’s a lot of sorcerers, but that’s really niche and since we’re talking niche this is gonna most likely fall into Warrior Spirit realms of usefulness.
 
Bloodsong: you’re not a bard, skald, or barbarian, just get keen.
 
Brawling: on its own it’s not that amazing, but remember when I talked keeping a gauntlet on? This is a great ‘always on’ ability for said gauntlet, but it’s redundant with Defending and not as strong.
 
Breaking (Dungeoneer’s Handbook): cool when fighting the one thing that it works against….
 
Called: I love this ability, I love it a lot. If you want to avoid Quickdraw as a feat, or end up in a situation where you need to get your weapon back from somewhere across the plane, then accept no other options.
 
Compassionate (Champions of Purity): this one is actually the best option for bringing someone back alive. You get to control it with a free action, and you can automatically stabilize someone with your weapon so long as they aren’t “dead, dead”. Great to pick up situationally.
 
Conductive: the fighter class has nothing to use this with, so unless you are multiclassing or have a racial ability, pass on this.
 
[ranged] Conserving: I actually really like this, though it seems a bit less than stellar compared to Endless Ammunition, even at the lower (and much more desirable) bonus cost.
 
Corrosive: the first of many elemental damages available to you. All of these are just okay, but some are more important than others, and this is one of them since there is less acid resistance in the books than there is of any other element, and there are some enemies that need acid or fire to kill them this one stands out as one of the best elements for damage.
CHEESE DISCLAIMER: I’ve seen players cheese their way into putting multiple elements on their weapons at the same time, arguing with the DM that as long as they all trigger off the same command word that they can all be active at the same time and you can stack the damage up. Doesn’t work in my games, but for the sake of the guide I must mention this ‘strategy’ to you.
 
[melee] Countering: this is a very situational one, as you often will not find too many enemies that want to disarm or sunder you in most books. Thanks to that Warrior Spirit though, it can have situational usefulness.
 
Courageous: fear saves, ughhhh. The other ability I believe has been clarified to only enhance the morale bonus on fear effects, but if you go by RAW then it works on all of them, like Flagbearer which makes it worth mentioning.
 
Cruel: not sure how this works on ranged weapons, but it does. Great for intimidate monsters that want that little extra umph, but hardly necessary.
 
Cunning: this one has the same situational usefulness as bane, but gated by skill access. But eventually getting a +6 untyped bonus to confirm crits that stacks with Critical Focus is good.
 
Dazzling Radiance (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): Dazzling Display is awesome and all, and a DC 17 Fort save seems okay at lower levels. This ability tatters out in usefulness, though granted combining dazzled with shaken can be a pretty nice debuff (-3 on attacks) at those lower levels.
 
[melee] Deadly: avoid whips, avoid saps, avoid this.
 
Debilitating (Spymaster’s Handbook): it’s not useless, but it’s definitely better on an Unchained Rogue.
 
Deceptive (Pathfinder Society Primer): if you’re building a bluff-feint build then this is a no-brainer choice otherwise skip it.

By ‘sacrificing’ your last iterative attack each round, you can choose to attack with whatever weapon you have currently available, and you can spend your bottom attack (that usually isn’t going to hit anyway) to instead boost your AC with a weapon that you don’t care about losing that attack bonus. This works wonders for Power Turtles.
 
Dispelling: are you building the Anti-Mage? This helps you do that better, since Shatter Spell and the like all function similarly (if not directly referenced to) Spell Sunder. A caster friend dumping scrolls of Dispel Magic into your weapon that you pay for can be an awesome way to build up your anti-casting repertoire.
 
[ranged] Distance: on its own, I feel this works better on thrown ranged weapons that you intend to have come back to you. Most bows have decent range on their own anyway.
 
[ranged] Driving (Weapon Master’s Handbook): Bull Rush or Trip with your ranged weapons. Seems cool, too bad both maneuvers aren’t that great for ranged style.
 
Fervent (Quests & Campaigns): like worshipping oddball gods? This is a cool little ability that can add to improve your critical confirmations against anyone who doesn’t worship your god. Most of the other stuff won’t come up.
 
Flaming: it’s just as useful as acid, but a LOT more things are fire resistant (hence immune to this) which causes this to have a lower rating than acid.
 
[melee] Fortuitous: !!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!! Extra attacks of opportunity are awesome!!!!!!!! Aptly named.
 
Frost: while it doesn’t have as many uses as fire or acid, it still has uses.
 
Ghost Touch: if you will fight incorporeal enemies a lot, then buy the crap out of this! If not, then make a note of it for when you might need it with Warrior Spirit. Otherwise, they still have a 50% miss chance just because.
 
Grayflame: you don’t channel, do you?
 
[melee] Grounding: it’s like a worse bane against air-subtypes. Even with Warrior Spirit there are better choices.
 
[melee] Growing (Melee Tactics Toolbox): I like this a lot, but it’s only 1/day. [melee] Heartseeker: super situational, but in those situations man is it good. Grab with Warrior Spirit, if anything.

[bladed] Injecting (Agents of Evil): this works better on rogues or other classes that focus on poisons.

Magic Armor
Overall, you can expect a lot of these to be secondary or tertiary priorities as far as your money goes, most of the time there isn’t much to improve your armor with at earlier levels until you get your armor to +5. Other than genuinely improving your AC, you’ll be looking at different kinds of utility options to improve your narrative power. You don't have a Warrior Spirit like ability for this, but there is Master Armorer, which allows you to craft your own sets of armor. I would consider this option if you really plan on maxing out what your armor can do, otherwise this could get really expensive. Shields however, are another animal and there’s plenty of added options that make shields unique items to build for, including options that can improve their functionality as weapons.
+1 Abilities
[heavy] Advancing (Armor Master’s Handbook): you will most likely benefit from heavy armor. Heavy armor melee guys will love this, it’s fantastic and it helps you get around the movement problem elegantly.
 
[shield] Arrow Catching: this one’s kinda weird. It gives you a +1 deflection bonus against ranged attacks, which is pretty cool, but it’s overall worse than just investing in a ring. You can divert arrows to hit your AC instead of your allies. This is a decent way to tank for your allies, as long as they stay within 5 feet of you. It’s a must for power turtles who want to help the party.
 
[shield] Assiduous (Armor Master’s Handbook): if you ever think you might go against gaze attacks, then take this. If you ever get to use it, you’ll never regret the money.
 
[medium] Balanced (Armor Master’s Handbook): I actually love this, its an immediate action that transfers some of your enhancement bonus to a single type of saving throw. My money says investing in Will saves is your best bet here, but a case can be made for Reflex. This needs to be placed on medium armor, but there are ways around that with special materials and feats and whatnot.
 
[light or medium] Balanced (Ultimate Equipment): if falling down is your greatest fear ever, this helps you against attempts to trip or knock you down. It also helps in environments to maintain your balance with a +4, which means the base save (DC 5) you’ll pass automatically. You may be better off with taking a different option to just train Acrobatics properly, but this is a decent alternative.
 
[light or heavy shields] Bashing: up your damage dice by 2 steps, and treat it as a +1 weapon. Figure out from your DM if that makes it a +1 weapon for enchanting, but it would make it a viable option if it did.
 
[armor] Benevolent: if you are making the Aid Another build, or are looking to equip a Protector familiar with something, this ability is where it’s at. Otherwise, skip it.
 
[armor] Bitter: DC 14 fort sucks, don’t bother reading it.
 
[shield] Blinding: DC 14 Reflex save. Since Reflex is supposedly the worst save, you could use this for a while before it becomes useless. Blinded is a great debuff.
 
[armor] Bolstering: if you get hit by an enemy or BBEG, you’ll get +2 on saves vs them for the rest of their turn. If you’re feeling a bit weak on saves, this isn’t as bad as it seems, given the duration. A lot of abilities require you to be hit before they trigger, so a +2 on those could be beneficial.
 
[armor] Calming: this seems like it's useful, but it only works on enemies who have rage, and at that a DC 15 Will won’t be effective.
 
[armor] Champion: this is listed solely for the fact that I’ve already mentioned VMC cavalier as a good choice, this gives you an extra +2 sacred bonus on AC when using smite/challenge. Sacred stacks with your enhancement bonuses and it applies to touch.
[shield] Clangorous: not worth the money, the DCs are garbage.
 
[armor] Crusading (Advanced Class Origins): this is actually better than the Champion ability, but it does require you invest into the ability to get Lay on Hands, which may be off limits to you otherwise. Still worth mentioning since +2 sacred bonus to AC is an amazing buff for a +1 ability.
 
[armor] Dastard: Champion ability for evil characters, nice.
 
[armor] Deathless: campaign dependent, this is useful almost exclusively against undead or evil clerics. Good thing for us they happen to cover a lot of common enemies. Still, this is campaign dependent, but if you are in any way considering it based on the game you’re playing, then you won't regret it.
 
Defiant: bane backwards. You can actually increase your enhancement bonus on your armor up to +7 with this, but you have to pick the exact enemy to use this against and you have to invest in +5 armor first. You probably won’t get enough mileage from this unless you’re in a specific game where one type of enemy is expected.
 
 
[armor] Evolving (Melee Tactics Toolbox): so, the armor melds into you reducing your AC. Bad start, but for 30 minutes it can give you a bunch of utility like abilities from the Summoner’s Evolution list. Stuff like improved natural armor seems completely pointless, but gaining a swim speed and gills while merging your big heavy full plate into your body may not be outside the realm of useful. Scent is also really useful for tracking down enemies etc. Consider it for out of combat applications.
 
[shield] Folding (Armor Master’s Handbook): I love this actually, for builds that don’t know whether they want to invest in one combat style vs. another. You can change your shield into any other kind of shield. I guess you can get metal tower shields this way, neat. You can also potentially mix and match different shield abilities, and change into something you could use on a two-handed weapon.
 
Light Fortification: 25% chance to negate a crit or a sneak attack. The odds are low, but the effect is good. If you’re considering spending the money, it may be better for you to go with the better versions though, that said you’ll never feel bad about having this, unless you roll a variable in that in-between range.
 
Frosted (Melee Tactics Toolbox): this helps you get out of grapples, and does damage to things that constrict, pin, or eat you. As long as they aren’t immune to cold. Could be worse, but it could be better too.
 
Grinding: this works better as a defense for grapples, they take damage every single attempt they make, and this counts as armor spikes on your turn, using your armor’s enhancement bonus for the weapon damage. Not bad.
[heavy or tower shields] Heraldic (Armor Master’s Handbook): you can treat your shield as a holy symbol or a banner if you somehow got one of those class features….
 
Impervious: you won't know how much this is worth until you need it. Like the weapon ability, this works great against opposed sunder attempts and helps out against oozes and rust attacks.
 
[metallic armor or shield] Mirrored: if you think you’re going up against gaze attacks often this can help, maybe? Probably not.
 
[armor] Poisoning (Magical Marketplace): if you’re one of those builds that’s all about making poison work, this is a must. That role however, is most likely going to another class. It’s here in case that one ratfolk build cares about it, which they most likely do not.
 
[shield] Ramming: if and when you make a charge attack with the intention of using your shield bash attack, you get to use the armor’s enhancement bonus in place of the already existing (if any) weapon enhancement bonus. Very situational, and I wouldn’t bother with it unless you plan on exclusively charging with your shield as your entire character build.
 
[shield] Rebounding (Magical Marketplace): you’ll get an extra +2 AC against thrown weapons, which are exactly how common? It can do extra stuff against alchemists. Meh.
 
[shield] Singing (Melee Tactics Toolbox): you can counter bardic abilities, essentially giving you 10 rounds of countersong per day with a +10 for the d20 check. Doesn’t seem that bad if you’re in a game where Countersong is something you could use and don't have a bard, which is very unlikely anyway.
 
[armor] Spell Storing: you can have up to a 3rd level touch spell that will discharge as an immediate action when someone hits or touches you. There may be some interesting uses for this, but they all rely on you having spellcaster buddies willing to put their spells into this thing.
 
[shield] Spellrending (Armor Master’s Handbook): I really want this to be good, but paying for that SR on your shield really isn’t worth it because it’s not that great for the price.
 
Spellsink (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): I think I’m gonna cry a little. This ability is so good, it makes me want to make it purple just on principle, however for the ability to work, the enemy has to fail a meager 14 Will save, and it only works once per day.
 
[armor] Stanching: this is great if you think you’re going to be facing Bleed effects often enough. It also has this nice ability to add it’s enhancement bonus on checks to stabilize when dying, a scenario you do not want to plan around.
 
 
[medium] Terrain-Striding (Armor Master’s Handbook): pick a favored terrain, you gain a +10ft enhancement bonus to movespeed and ignore the ACP on a bunch of skills in that terrain. Some games take place almost entirely in a single terrain. Consider this if you’re in one of those games, or if there’s one that is predominant where you fight, like in cities or underground, etc.
 
[armor] Vouchsafing: the only time you can use it is when you can do nothing.
 
[armor] Warding: plan to face a lot of paladins, antipaladins, hellknights, inquisitors, or cavaliers? This can force resource drain on them, and can even be bolstered if you went with the VMC cavalier build. Consider it then and in games where these types of enemies are very common.
 
[medium] Withstanding (Armor Master’s Handbook): it’s like defiant, but it gives you moderate fortification against that type of enemy. Bonus, it also gives you a +2 on knowledge checks to identify them. If you’re in a game where defiant is a good choice, so is this.
+2 Abilities
[heavy] Adamant (Armor Master’s Handbook): you can turn your armor into effectively having Adamantine armor for 9 rounds. DR 3/- is low, and I’d rather build around better DR. By the time you can afford this, it’s nigh useless.
 
[shield] Animated: this functions well for anyone who plans on using two hands. You’ll get 4 rounds of a shield that follows you around and can keep using passive abilities. A must for anyone planning on using a shield with a two-handed weapon that doesn’t wanna waste feats.
 
[shield] Arrow Deflection: you get Deflect Arrows on your shield. You gotta make a DC 20 Reflex to do it, but that might not be a problem on some builds. Still, it’s only once per round and only blocks one attack. Seems weak for +2.
 
[light or medium] Bloodthirsty (Agents of Evil): it’s a 2 for 1 exchange on improving your enhancement bonus; unless you picked up rage, it’s not worth it.
 
[armor] Ghost Spike (Dirty Tactics Toolbox): you get to add +1 ghost touch armor spikes to your armor for up to 1 minute, once per day. I’d rather just invest in them.
 
Jarring: Dazed is such a good debuff, and the activation time is right, and you even get 3 of them per day. But that 16 Fort save will not serve you well.
 
[shield] Mastering (Armor Master’s Handbook): if you ever wanted to use a shield in conjunction with TWF, this ability lets you treat your shield as if it was the same weapon for the purpose of using your weapon group for damage. If you’re already using the close weapon group, then you can use Weapon Focus/Specialization and their Greater versions on your shield if you already have them for another weapon. This is great for sword and board, and it’s a fighter exclusive thing. Even if you aren't using the shield as a primary weapon, you can use it like it is in a pinch.
 
[shield] Merging: just why? Why? Trap option, don't even waste the time to read it.
 
[medium or heavy] Mind Buttressing (Magical Marketplace): +2 resistance bonus on Will saves, boring. Immunity to Possession and Mental Control: NOT boring.
 
[light] Phantasmal (Armor Master’s Handbook): you can create an illusory afterimage which can flank with you. Designed for rogues imo.
 
[heavy] Rampaging (Armor Master’s Handbook): charge through difficult terrain, do some mediocre stuff with Bull Rush.
 
[armor] Spell Dodging: +4 dodge bonus against spells that require attack rolls, and against summoned creatures. Campaign dependent, but in the right game it’s amazing.
 
Spell Resistance [13]: 13 is so low for SR, by the time you can afford this, barely any enemy will even have to bother to roll against yours, save for the chance to roll a 1. Pass.
 
[heavy] Volcanic (Melee Tactics Toolbox): once per day you can give yourself 20% miss chance through concealment and do a little bit of fire damage to the area around you, for one round. It’s way too expensive for what it does.
 
[shield] Weeping (Agents of Evil): this doesn’t seem to have a limit on uses per day, and giving all enemies a slew of -1’s may not be bad. But it only works if all those enemies are good and not immune to mind-affecting effects.
+3 Abilities
[light] Brawling: a lottttttt of people are mad about this. Luckily for you, unarmed damage is something other classes look at so you can avoid this.
 
Moderate Fortification: at +3, this gives you a 50% chance to avoid a crit or sneak attack. It gives just the right amount of wiggle room to put something(s) else on your armor and still effectively reduce crits. If you have no idea what you want on your armor, default to this.
 
Ghost Touch: if you need it, you need it. Campaign dependent. I would hate to tell you to invest into it and have you never even get to use it, but it is that good.
 
[armor] Invulnerability: DR5/magic. Are you kidding me? I actually forgot to even include this the first time I wrote this out because it’s that forgettable. Useless.
 
[armor] Sensing: you can gain blindsense 60 if you otherwise have been blinded or are in an area of magical or even non-magical darkness. You also get Blindsight for the first 5ft, meaning your melee attacks are just fine. This trumps even Darkvision, but the concealment miss chance still applies for enemies more than 5 ft away from you. That’s why I told you to invest in Seeking.
 
Spell Resistance [15]: at what point can you afford this? Will enemy casters have an effective CL of at least 13? Cuz on a d20 roll of 2 they will succeed.
 
[armor] Titanic: you’re considered 1 size larger for combat maneuver like problems. If something is going to swallow you whole, I am still not sure this is the best answer. Once per day you can enlarge yourself like Enlarge Person. Seems really bad for the price.
+4 Abilities
[shield] Bastion (Armor Master’s Handbook): if you fail a Will save, you get to stay sane/functional for a number of rounds equal to your enhancement bonus. Get to the cleric, get yourself fixed before something dumb happens to you. Great insurance.
 
Spell Resistance [17]: again, by the time you can afford this, most enemies can beat your SR with a 2.
+5 Abilities
[full-plate or Hellknight plate] Dread Wing (Armor Master’s Handbook): reduce your armor’s bonus by 4 to gain wings, for as long as you want. This is good because of its indefinite duration, but it costs +5 which means unless you want your armor to do exclusively this you’re going to be SOL. Still, indefinite duration flight is one of the best abilities to invest into, and you have plenty of ways to get your AC back up. Figure out if its better than Radiant Flight for you (probably not). You can have a lower enhancement bonus, but that kinda defeats the purpose of having armor. +6 armor however still costs less than Wings of Flying.
 
Heavy Fortification: do you really hate crits? Do you really hate rogues? If moderate wasn’t enough for you, this gives you 75% chance to negate crits and sneak attacks. The odds are favorable, but chances of this being the best choice for your money is not that high. Still, it’s there if you have nothing else to invest into.
 
[shield] Reflecting: once per day, you get Spell Turning. Still worth it if you want nothing else on your shield.
 
Spell Resistance [19]: you’ve invested all this money and magic unto your armor just for it to not work at least 50% of the time? I want better SR buffs, this is garbage.
 
[heavy] Unbowed (Armor Master’s Handbook): if you happen to get hit by an attack that would kill you, you can instead completely give up your (minimum) +6 armor to negate the damage and do maybe a couple d6 of area damage. This is a fate worse than death.
Flat Cost Abilities
[armor; 7,000] Adhesive: gain a climb speed for 10 rounds/day. Decent utility for a flat cost, but it seems like you’d be better off saving that money to invest in flight of some kind since flight is a much more versatile movement type than climb.
 
[leather or fabric armor; 4,500] Amorphous: turn yourself into a blob to get through tight spaces. This has some fantastic utility, even if it’s only once per day.
 
[1,000 or 2,000] Buoyant (Magical Marketplace): ignore check penalties on Swim. Not bad for the price, especially in a game where that matters.
 
[4,000] Burdenless: If you’re on one of those builds that’s pinched for STR, this ability improves your carrying capacity by a lot. Take it if your DM cares, honestly even if you don’t think it’s necessary. No one likes to get hit by a Carrying Capacity audit.
 
[armor; 5,000] Comfort (Pathfinder Society Field Guide): never worry about taking the feat Endurance, or about casting Endure Elements ever again. It also keeps your armor squeaky clean.
 
[armor; 5,000] Corsair (Advanced Class Origins): +5 on acrobatics checks, not bad I guess. You don’t use grit or panache do you?
 
[armor; 5,000] Creeping: no ACP on stealth checks, and you can grant yourself your enhancement bonus on stealth for 1 minute. If stealth matters to you, this still probably isn't worth it given armor training.
 
[cloth or padded armor; 1,000] Cushioned (Armor Master’s Handbook): you’ll never use this armor.
 
[armor; 5,000] Deceiving (Agents of Evil): once you get past level 5, you will radiate an aura of your alignment. If ever you think that might be a problem, take this and change your perceived aura. May not be useful, but if you need it here it is.
 
[armor; 10,000] Delving: burrow speed of 10 ft. Figure out breathing on your own, this can really help in dungeon crawls to get around doors, walls, etc.
 
[30,000] Determination: of all the abilities that I’ve listed involving dealing with dropping to 0 or lower HP, this is the one worth looking at, as it automatically hits you with a Breath of Life spell once per day. That can revive you from death, friend, or buy you an extra turn.
 
[18,000; 42,000; 66,000] Energy Resistance, Improved, & Greater: investing all the way into ER 30 will make you feel really special when you get to use it. Sadly, this effect can be replicated by spells, and paying for a friendly caster’s pearls of power or runestones of power is more cost effective. Still, it’s here and doesn’t eat up your +’s on your armor or shield.
 
[armor; 49,000] Etherealness: once per day become ethereal for as long as you want. This is better than casting the spell, so rejoice at finally being better at something than the wizard. Hella expensive though.
 
[armor; 4,000] Expeditious: three times per day you can get +10 to your movespeed for a single round. There are better options that last longer.
 
[armor; 2,700] Glamered: need to disguise the fact that you’re wearing armor? Need to change into something formal? Does the armor you’re wearing not fit your character concept of running around in a mankini, but you still want the mechanical benefits? Add this to your armor for such occasions.
 
[7,500] Hosteling: if you took an animal companion or familiar through feats or an archetype, this can help you cart your buddy around when you need to go into town. You still gotta feed them though.
 
[armor; 3,750] Jousting: +5 on ride checks. If you’re never going to invest in the ride skill, but want a decent bonus for cheap, here it is.
 
[light armor; 6,500] Locksmith (Armor Master’s Handbook): if you somehow got shoehorned into being the trap guy, this not only gives you a set of masterwork thieves tools, but you no longer apply your ACP, and gain your armor’s enhancement bonus on the check. Seems better for rogues.
 
[armor; 5,000] Malevolent (Agents of Evil): +2 on Bluff and Intimidate checks during combat. So more goods for the intimidate monster. If the enemy is prone or flat-footed you get to add your armor’s enhancement bonus on attack rolls. That’s not too bad if you wanna invest in bluff/feinting or trip. Too bad you probably don’t, better on rogues.
 
[armor; 18,000] Martyring: if an enemy crits you, then you can ‘cast’ Mass Cure Light Wounds, which I’m pretty sure you can target yourself with. Do you really wanna drop 18,000 hoping your DM will crit you? Not useless, but the uses are unfavorable.
 
[light armor; 9,100] Phase Lurching (Armor Master’s Handbook): ewwwww slimy mucus. Still, once per day you can pass through a 5 ft wall as if going ethereal. It’s a utility option, but it’s pretty good if you’re one of those fighters who invests into knowledge checks/whatever skills your DM wants for you to know how thick walls are to skip past doors.
 
[2,250] Poison-Resistant: +3 resistance bonus doesn’t stack with a cloak.
 
[armor; 10,000] Putrid: DC 14 Fort effect (again, sheesh) that can affect your allies and doesn’t even do anything good.
 
[7,500] Radiant: light yourself up like a torch on command, and upgrade that light to a Daylight effect once per day. Not bad if you need it otherwise, since Daylight is usually more expensive to do regularly.
 
 
[heavy armor; 15,000] Radiant Flight (Armor Master’s Handbook): for up to 10 minutes (broken up minute by minute) per day, you grant yourself a fly speed of 30. This is better and cheaper than Winged Boots and they also give off light. You give up speed for longevity, but this is mostly a combat only flight mode. Still worth it.
 
[5,000] Rallying: fear effects, meh.
 
[armor; 4,500] Restful: gain the sleep benefits of the Ring of Sustenance. Honestly, the ring costs less and does more for you. Might be worth considering if both your ring slots are being used.
 
[armor; 27,000] Righteous/Unrighteous/Vigilant/Unbound: 10 rounds of righteous might may seem cool, but for the cost it’s pretty weak considering it never scales in CL. It is better than Enlarge Person, and it gives you DR5/alignment.
 
[armor; 3,750; 15,000; 33,750] Shadow, Improved, & Greater: if you don’t have the skills to invest into Stealth, these enhancements are the next best thing. Even if you did, to get some favored class bonus or something, this stacks with it. Take it if you care.
 
[armor; 3,750; 15,000; 33,750] Slick, Improved, & Greater: if you don’t have the skills to invest into Escape Artist, these enhancements are the next best thing. Even if you did, to get some favored class bonus or something, this stacks with it. Take it if you care.
 
[leather or hide; 7,500] Trackless: bonus doesn’t stack with Shadow for stealth, and the armors you have to use suck.
[49,000] Undead Controlling: if you’re in an undead heavy campaign, this armor can save you a lot of trouble with hordes of enemies, or you can attempt to force a save from an enemy lich. Or you could bring an army with you, whatever you want man.
 
[leather or dragonhide armors; 15,000] Venom-Eating (Armor Master’s Handbook): this I like, sort of. If you’re going against users of poison, then you can store a dose of that poison for each +1 on your armor. Then you can apply it to your weapon as a standard action, with no fear of poisoning yourself. It can hold only one type of poison at a time though. I like it, but it’s not good.
 
[shield; 5,000] Wyrmsbreath: Reflex DC 11 for up to 5d4 of energy damage? Wizards don’t even bother with Burning Hands, why should you spend 5,000 gp on being able to do it only after you’ve been hit by 25 energy damage?
Specific Armors
[33,160] Advocate’s Armor (Melee Tactics Toolbox): +3 leather that triggers its ability on a crit or sneak attack. You get a DC 19 Will save to compel the attacker by some contract you make up. Good ability, decent DC, crappy armor.
 
[30,250] Alchemist’s Suit (Melee Tactics Toolbox): +1 quilted cloth armor that has some slots for vials. You can use potions when you get hit by a crit, bludgeoning damage, or some other uncommon things. Too expensive for crap armor.
 
[5,570] All-Seeing Armor (Inner Sea Gods): +1 chain shirt, not great, but still one of the best light armors out there. At will Detect Magic and Read Magic. Yes.
 
[6,160] Arachnid Harness (Armor Master’s Handbook): +1 leather that turns you into Doctor Octopus. It has some neat utility for climbing, but is otherwise underwhelming.
 
[18,670] Arboreal Armor (Heroes of the Wild): +1 Wild wooden armor. It can give you +4 enhancement to natural armor for 10 rounds, while also slowing you down. Better for druids who want wild armor early.
 
[22,500] Armiger’s Armor (Melee Tactics Toolbox): if you’re going the adamantine full-plate route, consider this seriously. It can be donned by touching it and speaking a command word, so standard action? It’s also +1 and light fortification, which means it’s great for tanks.
 
[21,650] Armor of Fragmenting Stone (Monster Summoner’s Handbook): +2 full plate made of an ambiguous material that can summon a little elemental guy for you once per day after you take a certain type of damage. Not very useful for the price.
 
[16,175] Armor of Insults: +1 studded leather that more or less insults you for buying it by being a piece of crap. DC 16 will Once Per Day effect on crappy armor.
 
[18,300] Armor of the Pure Legion (Armor Master’s Handbook): +1 steel lemellar, with a couple cool abilities. SR 5+BAB against divine spells, so not that great, but still probably better than the actual SR enchantments. It also has a continuous Endure Elements vs. heat and once per day lets you reroll a save vs. a mind-affecting divine spell.
 
[14,160] Armor of the Sands (Melee Tactics Toolbox): +2 leather armor, it gives you endure elements and the ability to see through sandstorms and dust storms. Once per day, you can become ‘incorporeal’ by turning into sand against a single attack.
