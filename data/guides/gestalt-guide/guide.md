﻿What Is Gestalt?


Gestalt is a character building variant that is a little like multiclassing, except that instead of taking two classes at alternate levels you take both classes simultaneously. 


1. You select the greater value or progression for your HD, BAB, base saves, and skill points.
2. You gain the class features from both classes, including class skill list.
3. Class features that two classes share accrue at the rate of the faster class. For example, a slayer 3 gets Sneak Attack +d6, and a Rogue 3 gets Sneak Attack +2d6. A gestalt slayer//rogue gets Sneak Attack +2d6 like the rogue, not +3d6 like a multiclass Slayer 3 / Rogue 3.


This results in characters that are more powerful and versatile than usual. However, they do not get any more actions, non-bonus feats, or gear, which means it's easy to spread a gestalt character too thin.


Gestalts are often used to compensate for small parties by making sure that the party still has access to a diverse range of abilities. For example, one PC could be both a fighter and a cleric while the other is both a rogue and a wizard. Alternatively, gestalting can be enjoyed in a party of normal size as a high-power gaming experience in which players employ abilities from multiple classes for spectacular synergy. It can also be used to explore unusual concepts through combining classes; for example, a band of thieves consisting of a fighter//rogue, cleric//rogue, and wizard//rogue.


More detailed information about the gestalting concept can be found on the 3.X SRD.


How do I use this Guide?


This Guide is for pointing out synergies, weaknesses, and build ideas for various gestalt class combinations. While I give combinations ratings describing their level of synergy in terms of power and versatility, it’s important to note that gestalting isn’t always about making the most powerful character - you can have a lot of fun with mediocre gestalts that express interesting concepts. However, if that’s your goal then make sure that the group is on the same page. Don’t bring a Brawler//Magus to an Alchemist//Wizard game, and vice-versa.


The Guide covers:
* Optimization Principles: a basic framework for how to think about putting together a gestalt character, with a few examples
   * Multiclassing in Gestalt: a general discussion of how multiclassing can work in gestalt, 
   * Summons and Pets in Gestalt: comments on how gestalting affects the utility of your pet, and what to do about it.
* Class combinations: a discussion of build considerations for gestalting each specific class, including key abilities or archetypes, followed by a brief rating and description of every gestalt combination using that class. To avoid duplication, combinations are listed alphabetically by the first class: gestalting Wizard and Alchemist is under Alchemist, as Alchemist//Wizard, and not under wizard//alchemist. However, if you’re looking at an alchemist//wizard you should read the general discussion under the Wizard entry. [Note: combinations are a work in progress!]

Optimization Principles


Like creating any other character, gestalting is about balancing the need to do well in your primary role with a level of versatility. Actions, feats, stats, and gold are limited resources that have to be split between a character's possible roles (archer, swordsman, healer, scout, and so forth) and the more things a character tries to do, generally speaking, the less good they will be at any one thing. On the other hand, if your primary strategy is not effective in a given encounter you want other options.


Most classes are designed with particular synergies, focuses, and secondary options in mind; for example clerics are designed to be better in melee than wizards, and their spell list grants them more spells that increase their weapon damage. Gestalt combinations don't have these preselected synergies – you have to find them yourself. And while it's easy to see why a Barbarian//Wizard might not be a great idea, other problems or synergies aren't always obvious. The questions you should be asking are:


1) How well does each class support the character's primary role in combat? Ideally both classes should contribute in some way to whatever you want the character’s main focus to be.


2) What else can I do? Any character will eventually run into a situation where their usual strategy isn't so useful, and it's good to have a backup. You'll also want to be able to contribute out of combat. This is especially important in a small party where each gestalt is expected to cover multiple roles.


3) How are my defenses? Either you're in a small party and taking more hits per PC or you're fighting higher CR encounters, so it's important to be able to take (or avoid) more damage and make your saves. Remember that arcane spell failure can limit a gestalt's armour choices.


4) What actions am I using? You'll get more use out of your actions if you have a good mix of action types – standard, move, and swift – available, rather than having a lot of competing actions of the same type. Abilities which are free actions or no action are especially useful.


5) Can I get the stats and feats I need? Picking a primary role helps, but you still want to make sure gestalting doesn't add too many secondary stats, and that you're not trying to do something complicated like shield bashing without bonus feats. Conversely, if you do have bonus feats it's easier to fill out your secondary role or strategies.


Example: Fighter//Bard:
(1) Primary role is weapons combat, whether ranged or melee. The fighter provides full BAB and HD, lots of feats, and weapon and armour training, and the bard can self-buff with performance or spells. Archetypes such as the arcane duelist offer extra combat benefits like weapon enhancement and the ability to cast bard spells in heavier armour. (2) Secondary roles include support, some healing, and a variety of skills, thanks to the bard. (3) Lots of HP and all good saves. Arcane spell failure limits your AC a bit, but you can still use shields and at minimum light armour (or mithral breastplate). (4) Attacks and spells compete for your standard action, but many of your spells are swift actions and bardic performance can eventually be used as any action type, which is very handy. Most of the fighter's class features passively augment either attacks or defenses, and a few bard benefits (such as versatile performance) are passive. (5) Stats are a little spread out, but not more than a single-class bard. Plenty of feats. Verdict: Very good gestalt, potent but still versatile.


Example: Fighter//Wizard:
(2) The Fighter//Wizard gets a lot of abilities that support combat, and a versatile casting list, so it has a lot of options. (1) However relatively few abilities such as Weapon Focus (Ray) or the diviner's initiative bonus benefit the other side of the combination. (3) You get plenty of HP and two good saves, but can't cast spells in armour at all without spending feats and swift actions on Arcane Armour Training, which means your defenses are not a whole lot better than a standard wizard – certainly not great for melee. (4) The Fighter//Wizard also suffers from competition between spells and attacks since it has fewer swift action spells. Eventually Quicken becomes a factor, but then you're quickly eating up high level spell slots. (5) It's hard to get an Int high enough for good spell DCs and also have physical stats high enough for weapons. Bonus feats help you from getting too spread out between fighting and casting, but it's not enough. Verdict: Under-specialized. You either try to split your focus (and end up not great at either) or you gain little benefit from one class's features most of the time. 


Example: Sorcerer//Wizard
(1) The sorcerer-wizard gets tons of spell slots and both prepared and spontaneous casting. The wizard's bonus feats, some school abilities, and the sorcerer's bloodline arcana can be used with casting abilities from the other class. (2) However, it doesn't do much aside from cast from the sorcerer/wizard list – something either class could do before gestalting. While it's more flexible and effective in how it uses those spells, it has few or no abilities to support weapon combat, no increase in the range of spells it gets access to, and no improvement in mundane skills or in (3) defenses compared to either class individually. And since almost all your spells and special abilities use standard actions (4), usually you will spend your turn casting as one or the other class. At higher levels you can afford to burn through a few extra slots with Quicken, but it's still costly. (5) Finally, splitting focus between two casting stats means that your spell DCs from one or both classes will be lower than usual. You can fix this with the Sage bloodline, but then your bloodline arcana doesn't add anything extra to either class's spells. Verdict: Over-specialized in arcane casting.


The Fighter//Wizard and Sorcerer//Wizard are representative of problems with similar combinations.


Full BAB // full arcane caster seems like a great idea, because you get the best of both worlds. However you often actually end up with a split focus, trying to spend feats, stats, and gold on being competent at two deeply dissimilar things while not having the actions or synergistic class features to actually do both. Some combinations such as the Ranger//Sorcerer in addition can stretch your stat requirements by requiring a second above-average mental stat on top of physical requirements. These constraints aren’t impossible to work around, and some specific full-BAB mages can be more effective. Using a combat class that's comfortable in light or no armour helps. You can also make your actions stretch farther by delivering multi-touch spells like Chill Touch using unarmed strikes or natural attacks, or by using a reach/AoO build that lets you sneak in more attacks on turns when you cast. However taking advantage of these often takes very careful building, and these characters have a low optimization “floor” in that it’s easy to execute it very poorly. It’s usually safer to pair either the combat or caster class with a partial caster who can offer a little of the other features, while still supporting your primary focus. The Magus is a particularly good choice because it actually does offer the ability to cast and fight in the same turn via spell combat.


Pairing two full arcane casters is popular among players who want ALL THE MAGIC but since full arcane casters invariably have d6 HD and poor CMD, Fort and Ref saves you end up REALLY FRAGILE. You also don't get as much as you'd think out of the second spell list thanks to action limitations. I highly recommend instead pairing one full arcane caster with a partial or divine caster, which will improve your durability while typically also granting you more versatility in your spell list and special features. 


Finally, note that while the Sorcerer and Wizard are considered very strong classes, they aren't as good in combination as the Fighter//Bard. In gestalt it's common for the generally weaker classes to be very good choices when combined with classes that complement their strengths and weaknesses well.


Multiclassing in Gestalt


It is possible to multiclass a gestalt character by taking different class combinations at different levels, but it adds a layer of complexity and I will cover it only briefly.


There are two mechanical complications involved in multiclassing gestalt. First, BAB and saving throws can become a little difficult to keep track of if you're not using the same progressions at each level. For example, a Wizard//Swashbuckler gets full BAB and good Ref and Will saves, while a Wizard//Magus gets average BAB and good Fort and Will saves, so how do you handle a Wizard// Swashbuckler/Magus? Some believe that it's only whether the stat is increased that counts, and that a Fighter 1 / Sorcerer 19 // Wizard 20 would get full BAB by staggering the levels at which they get an increase from Sorc or Wiz, but I believe that's counter to the intent. The second issue is that some prestige classes can become unbalanced if you can qualify too easily or too early. For example, taking Sorcerer 4 // Oracle 4 and then switching to Mystic Theurge // Fighter gives you two full casting progressions, only one point shy of full BAB, and barely-delayed fighter features; that’s OP even by gestalt standards.


One option for dealing with the first issue is counting BAB and saves fractionally, such that a character gets 1/2, 3/4, or 1 point of BAB each level, and gets 1/3 of a save bonus for every “poor” save and 1/2 of a save bonus for every “good” save, using the higher value between each class taken that level. Round all fractions down to get your actual modifier, with a one-time + 2 to every “good” save.  In this case you may need to follow the 3.X SRD’s suggestions on restricting prestige classes: no prestige class levels before level 5 even if gestalting allows you to qualify early, no gestalting two prestige classes at once, and no prestige classes that advance two classes at once (such as Mystic Theurge, Arcane Trickster, or Rage Prophet).


Alternatively, you can count each “side” of the gestalt as a separate character before gestalting, taking the best total at each level. This not only handles the BAB and saves progression but also balances prestige classes: each “side” can take a prestige class if and only if that “side” can qualify for that prestige class on its own. For example, a Fighter // Sorcerer 4/Oracle 4/Mystic Theurge is legal because a Sorc 4/Oracle 4 can qualify for Mystic Theurge levels. However a Sorcerer 4/Fighter 4 // Oracle 4/Mystic Theurge 4 would not be allowed because an Oracle 4 does not qualify for Mystic Theurge. 


From an optimization point of view, the easiest way to make an effective multiclassed gestalt is to select one primary class that you will progress to level 20 on one “side” of the gestalt, and then use the other “side” to cherry-pick useful and complementary abilities from multiple classes. Examples:
1. Wizard 20 // Swashbuckler (Inspired Blade) 1 / Magus 6 / Fighter 13. The Wizard gets its full casting progression while the Swashbuckler contributes a quick early-game advantage in durability and rapier competence, the Magus brings spellstrike and spell combat (usable with wizard spells at character level 7 thanks to the Broad Study arcana) and the fighter rounds out the build's combat ability, resulting in a character with full casting, almost full BAB, lots of combat feats, and the ability to cast and fight in the same round.
2. Unchained Monk // Cleric / Empyreal Sorcerer / Mystic Theurge gets a mobile, unarmoured combat focus on the one side and a diverse set of casting abilities on the other side, including combat buffs, a lot of Bad Touch potential, and Wis stat synergy.
3. Bard 6+ // Swashbuckler 1 / Urban Bloodrager 2+ / Paladin 3+ gives you full bardic music and casting plus full BAB, finesse, parry and riposte, Dex rage, fast movement, uncanny dodge, smite, Cha to saves, and immunity to fear and disease. From there you can invest more heavily in either paladin or bloodrager, or even switch to fighter for some bonus feats.




Summons and Pets in Gestalt


The power of these options depends largely on why you are gestalting.


If you are compensating for a small party, extra bodies are great! They reduce the chances you'll be overwhelmed by numbers and help your character to effectively fill multiple roles at the same time instead of just filling multiple roles at different times.


If you are gestalting for a high-power experience, pets and summons have a harder time contributing meaningfully because they are balanced for a standard game; thus they are relatively weak compared to a gestalt party and the challenges such a party will face. There are four ways to handle this. From most investment to least:
1. Devote lots of resources to making the pet powerful enough to contribute. This is difficult, but possible with some combinations such as Barbarian//Hunter or Bard//Summoner. 
2. Use the pet for support (usually a mount or flanking partner) and instead of giving it offensive feats and items concentrate on making it mobile and tough enough to fill a support role without dying. 
3. Keep the pet out of combat and use it for utility, such as scouting. This is usually only worthwhile when the pet is not very strong to begin with and doesn't come with a high opportunity cost - familiars and ranger's companions work well in this role.
4. Don't take the pet! Even the most pet-based classes in the game do have options for playing without pets, so if you like the class as a whole but don't want the complications described above, consider trading the pet for something that will benefit you directly.


Class Combinations


I discuss both general gestalting guidelines for each class and each combination of two classes for gestalting. Each combination entry contains a summary of the basic abilities (BAB/HD, saves, skills, casting, and stats needed), and specific comments on synergy or how to build the gestalt.  Entries are listed in alphabetical order by the first appearing class in the combination, so for example Barbarian // Oracle is found under the “Barbarian” section.


Rating System:
Red: Very bad. Combination fails to synergize or works at cross-purposes, has significant weaknesses.
        Basics: Poor BAB/HD, one good save, 2 skill points, no casting, needs all stats
Orange: Fair. May have little synergy or significant weaknesses.
        Basics: Med BAB/HD, two good saves, 4 skill points, partial casting, needs 5 good stats
Green: Good. Some synergy and acceptable weaknesses, or very versatile.
        Basics: Good BAB/HD, all good saves, 6 skill points
        Full casting or two synergistic* part-casters
        3 key stats, or two key stats and two secondary
Blue: Great, combination of synergy and versatility, few weaknesses.
        Basics: d12, save or skill bonuses ('+'), 8 skill points, two full casters or synergistic full and part
        2 key stats, no more than one secondary
Purple: Best, either ridiculously potent in the chosen area or both strong and very versatile.
        Basics: 8 skill points with skill bonuses ('+'), two synergistic full casters, single key stat


*Synergistic casting generally means two of: varied lists, shared stat, both prepared and spontaneous.


With reference to stats, “physical” indicates a need for physical stats in variable arrangement depending on build, usually with Dex or Str key and the other two secondary.

Alchemist


An unusual pseudo-part-caster, the Alchemist doesn't suffer typical restrictions of casters like arcane spell failure, but also doesn't offer access to caster abilities like crafting, and has different good saves. It does best when paired with a strong martial class that benefits from the alchemist's self-buffing abilities and ability to wear armour, or with an int-based caster for increased durability. Explosive missile discovery works nicely with martial builds focusing on ranged weapons.


Beastmorph (for pounce) and Vivisectionist (for sneak attack) are good archetype choices for combat focused gestalts, and can be combined. The Mindchemist archetype or the Cognatogen discovery are good for gestalting with casters. The Metamorph archetype is an interesting way to reduce MAD, since it removes your Int-dependent class features (bombs and extracts) leaving the mutagen and giving you a powerful shapeshifting ability. Gun Chemist provides a very nice explosive ordinance ability to pair with other firearm-focused features - and you get to keep Mutagen.


The Herbalist alchemist uses Wis for all class abilities, and the Mnemostiller uses Cha for some class abilities including bomb DCs. Full analysis of the effect of these archetypes is pending, but it is safe to assume that the Herbalist will combine well with Wis-based casters as well as Monks. The Mnemostiller is more complex and may not work as well with martial classes such as Paladin but may be superior to base alchemist for Cha-based full casters such as Sorcerer or Oracle.


Alchemist // Arcanist **** (Mindchemist)
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 4 skills+, full & part casting, Int Cha
Extremely versatile casting with casting stat synergy and as much durability as you can expect from a gestalt arcane caster. The way the alchemist is written, you can copy any spells on your spellbook into your formula book as well, paying copying costs only instead of copying plus research. You won't be able to take advantage of the Alchemist's light armour proficiency. 


Alchemist // Barbarian ****/*
Basics: Full BAB, d12, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, Str Con Int
Drugged Rager. Between Mutagen and Rage your physical stats can easily get absurdly high. Defenses are great, aside from the poor Will save: medium armour, natural armour from mutagen, DR, miss chance from extracts, and discoveries granting healing or fortification. Plus other support/utility extracts. You can use prepared infusions while raging – they don't require concentration. Archetype ideas to consider:
* Drunken Brute lets you consume potions (and possibly prepared infusions) as a move action that does not provoke an AoO. Note: Drunken Brute is not to be confused with the Drunken Rager archetype, which is of no use to Alchemist gestalts. 
* Beastmorph gives you Pounce without spending three rage powers, and opens up non-beast totem options.
* Metamorph can provide massive strength and durability without Int requirements. At level 1 you’re looking at +10 to strength between rage, mutagen, and alter self, and that increases to +18 with greater rage, greater mutagen, and monstrous physique III (huge) at level 12. Note that you’re fatigued for 1 round after transforming, which can prevent you from raging if you wait to transform until the start of a battle. Loss of the versatility from extracts is also a bummer, but you still get access to a variety of senses and modes of movement.


Alchemist // Bard ** or ***(Chronicler of Worlds***)
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills+, two 6 level casting, physical Int Cha
Lots of skills and support, but offensive options are tricky due to a combination of MAD and medium BAB progression. The mutagen (or cognatagen) helps, but you'll likely still be well behind more focused gestalts. Best options are probably an archer-grenadier, dervish dancer (with Dex mutagen), metamorph (less MAD, neat infiltrator), or natural weapons demoralizer with Feral Mutagen (grants +2 to Intimidate), Power Attack, and Cornugon Smash. Defense about as good as a standard alchemist, plus the option of a shield.
Shade L0rd says: The Chronicler of Worlds archetype keys Bard features off of Intelligence, removing the MAD component at the cost of Inspire Heroics, Greatness and Versatile Performance.
Weirdo says: Thanks for catching that archetype! The planar-themed abilities that the archetype gets are a bit more circumstantial than the abilities you trade in, but reducing MAD is worth it. This would enable a very respectable casting build, solid green at least. 


Alchemist // Bloodrager
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 & 4 level casting, Physical Int Cha
Similar to Alchemist//Barbarian, but you're more MAD, and the bloodrager's ability to self-buff is less exciting when you can also do that with extracts. However you can make a really nasty natural weapons build using Feral Mutagen and multi-touch spells like Frostbite or Chill Touch. Depending on how generous your stats are and whether you have a flanking buddy, you probably want Viviscectionist (great damage when flanking, still needs Int) or Metamorph (less utility but no need for Int and physical stats through the roof).


Alchemist // Brawler
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, physical & Int
Hard to go wrong combining alchemist buffs with full BAB and combat features. Still, no particularly good synergy. Save a feat for Iron Will. 


Alchemist // Cavalier
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, physical & Int
An odd combination that has some non-obvious synergy. Rather than riding your mount, use it as a flanking partner with the Vivisectionist archetype, and use pounce from Beastmorph to get use out of your charge bonuses. Alternatively, you can take Daring Champion for a dex build – though this adds a Cha requirement and you'll also want a good Wis to make up for your poor save.


Alchemist // Cleric
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 4 skills, full and part casting, all stats
Very MAD, even ignoring Cha and giving up on channeling. You're also not getting much in the way of the basics, and no particular synergy.


Alchemist // Druid ** (Beast Viv//Nature Fang ***) (Herbalist ****)
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 4 skills, full and 6-level “casting,” physical Int Wis
The alchemist and druid bring a lot of the same stuff to the table in terms of basic numbers and available buffs. Also, Natural Spell doesn't allow you to use extracts or bombs while wild shaped, so you'll need to either use extracts for pre-buffing, pass out infusions, and ignore bombs; or else de-emphasize wild shape. One neat archetype combination is Beastmorph Vivisectionist//Nature Fang. Beastmorph+Feral Mutagen gives you back many of the advantages of wild shape but in a humanoid form, Studied Target improves your accuracy and to-hit (and becomes a swift action a level before you get Pounce from Beastmorph), and slayer talents modify your full progression sneak attack. The natural weapons combat style is not very strong – consider archery for versatility. Metamorph//Nature Fang similarly shifts your shapeshifting to alchemist, but is easier to pull off with no requirement for an Int bonus or flanking partner. Druid (Urushiol)'s poison is thematic and effective.


Tentatively rating Herbalist at blue since it’s hard to go wrong with distinct partial and full casting both keying of Wis, without any defensive weakpoints. Natural Magic is a little less exciting when you get druid casting, but there are potential advantages to the extract format (infusions, for example). Trading bombs for seedpods isn’t fantastic since you’re stuck with a weapon you can’t use in wildshape instead of taking an archetype that trades bombs for something more useful to you.


Alchemist // Fighter
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, physical & Int
Alchemist buffs work very nicely with the fighter's combat competence - you can easily get a massive AC and very strong physical stats with this combo. Action economy is good with a lot of passive abilities and long-duration buffs. The main question is whether to keep bombs. Vivisectionist gives great damage for styles with lots of attacks such as TWF, but there’s something to be said for the versatility of being able to lob a ranged energy attack. Ranged fighters should keep bombs and take Explosive Missile discovery. Note that you can use Fighter feats (ex: Weapon Specialization) to improve your bombs. Weapon training doesn't apply to bombs, but the Opportunist fighter does give you +1 to attack and damage with bombs, Int to splash weapons (in case you traded it away with an alchemist archetype such as Vaultbreaker) and adds a few other bonuses including 2 extra skill ranks (to be used on Bluff, Sense Motive, Stealth, and Sleight of Hand). The cost is that your normal weapon training is delayed until level 9, and you swap half your bonus feats for other alchemical tricks and rogue talents. Gun Chemist // Trench Fighter is a great combination between explosive ordinance, gunsmithing, and trench warfare.


Alchemist // Gunslinger *** (Gun Chemist****)
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, Dex Wis Int
Alchemists have excellent buffs for ranged specialists, and vestigial arms help with reloading pistols. You can take Explosive Missile to launch bombs out of your weapon, though only as a standard action. Gun Chemist allows you to launch explosive ordnance from your gun starting at first level, and multiple shots per round at level 8.


Alchemist // Hunter
Basics: Med BAB, d8, Fort and Ref,  6 skills, two 6 level casting, physical Int Wis
Animal companion is a handy flanking partner for a vivisectionist, but that's the extent of this synergy.


Alchemist // Inquisitor
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, two 6 level casting, physical Int Wis
No particular synergy here, too unfocused. Living Grimoire reduces MAD, which makes it marginally better for a bomber build that wants to be able to hit something over the head in a pinch – but not strong enough for a good combat build. 


Alchemist // Investigator
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills+, two 6 level casting, physical Int
You don't get much in the way of increased combat capability or magical versatility. Still, you've got all your saves and a lot of options for utility between discoveries and talents, so that's something. You'll want to look into archetypes that trade away overlapping abilities like swift alchemy and poison resistance/immunity – Empiricist being the easiest choice. Not a bad place for metamorph – you'll miss bombs, but you get extracts from Investigator anyway, and the shapeshifting is very useful.


Alchemist // Magus
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 4 skills,  two 6 level casting, Int & Physical
Various magical combat-boosting abilities all based on Int. Let the alchemist handle self-buffs and area debuffs via bombs while the magus focuses on touch spells and group buffs. For a combat-focused gestalt your BAB/HD are disappointing (and if you're not planning on doing weapons combat magus is a waste). Note also that your Str-boosting mutagen will decrease your Int; Dex or Con mutagens are a safer bet. Extracts don't work with spellstrike or spell combat. You can however deliver a bomb and ray at the same time using Grenadier and Myrmidarch or Eldritch Archer.


Alchemist // Kineticist
Basics: Med BAB, d8, Fort and Ref, 4 skills, 6 level casting, Con Dex Int
A fairly versatile blaster with some utility. The bomb and kinetic blast have similar effects and separate mechanics, so they don't really synergize, but at least you can diversify damage types. Alternatively, elemental annihilator is much more attractive when you can get utility from extracts and pairs well with vivisectionist for melee – which also decreases your need for Int to improve your bombs.


Alchemist // Medium ** (Metamorph ***)
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 4 skills, two 6-level casting, Int Cha
The archmage and hierophant seance bonuses don't work with your extracts or bombs, so no casting synergy. Metamorph + Champion or Guardian spirit works well enough for melee.


Alchemist // Mesmerist ***
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, two 6 level casting, Int Cha Dex 
Moderately versatile; primary strength in debuffing with potential for area control, support, healing, ranged damage, social utility, and knowledge skills. Not a ton of synergy between features, though the Mesmerist's stare will soften targets up or add extra damage to bombs -  the Lethality improvement is good for a poisoner alchemist. Another option is the Interrogator Alchemist, which lets you add another penalty to Will saves, but at the cost of bombs and mutagen (thus most of your versatility). Barbarian


The barbarian is a strong martial class, tough and capable of massive damage in melee or, with the right build, solid damage at range. Note: spell sunder works at range. It works best with hybrid classes that enhance the barbarian's combat options while also providing some utility options. Pure martials generally don't add a lot of versatility to the barbarian. Pure casters rarely synergize well.


The barbarian's trickiest element is the inability to cast while raging. There are a few ways to manage this. The easiest, if your spell list co-operates, is picking out of combat spells, prebuffs, or occasionally first round buff or control options. Moment of Clarity and Mad Magic give you a limited ability to ragecast (specifically, one round per rage), at the cost of 1-2 rage powers and possibly a feat depending on how badly you care about keeping rage bonuses while you cast. It’s a reasonable route if you want to be able to cast an emergency mid-combat spell - or if you can find an easy way to rage cycle. Alternatively, the Dreadnought suffers no penalties or limitations during a rage, but also gains only half the usual benefits. This archetype makes it possible to build a broader array of gestalt barbarians that actually integrate casting with combat, including reach casters.


Some GMs may interpret the restriction against “abilities that require patience or concentration” more broadly than spellcasting. With respect to psychic casters, it's also important to check whether rage counts as a “hostile emotion effect.” Descriptions note if a combination's effectiveness is subject to this table variation.

Bard


The bard is a versatile part-caster with two big draws for gestalt: utility and action types. Swift action spells and move or swift action performance can often be combined with attacks or spells from another gestalt class. As typical for hybrids bard pairs best with charisma-based full casters and with martial characters. When gestalting with a caster, take advantage of neat bard-only spells like Glibness or Saving Finale. For archetypes:
* Geisha is nice for unarmoured gestalts – you lose just weapon proficiency and bardic knowledge in exchange for a bonus on courtly skills, the ability to use performance as a long-duration buff (tea ceremony) in addition to its normal form, and Scribe Scroll. 
* When combining bard with a martial class, consider Arcane Duelist or Chelish Diva for the ability to cast bard spells in medium or even heavy armour. The dervish archetypes and the archaeologist (“selfish bards”) let you activate your main buff more quickly at low levels, and for the Dawnflower Dervish and Archaeologist your personal combat bonus is bigger than Inspire Courage's group buff. That makes them strong options if you're in a small party or someone else has Inspire Courage.
* Chronicler of Worlds turns the bard Int-based, swaps Bardic Knowledge, Inspire Greatness, and Inspire Heroics, for more circumstantial planar-themed abilities, and gives you a Linguistics-based version of Versatile Performance. The archetype pairs well with Int-based casters (particularly full casters) and is also worth considering for any character that expects to see a lot of extraplanar enemies, as long as your second class isn’t Charisma-based. (Thanks to Shade L0rd for flagging this archetype.)
* Dwarven Scholar is useful if your second class relies heavily on Wisdom. It’s particularly nice for reach builds, between bonus feats and getting proficiency with one dwarven weapon (dorn-dergar, longaxe, longhammer, and giant-sticker all have reach).
Ilde Senescence says: While it does lose inspire courage which can be painful, reducing down to a single (primary) casting stat does open up building towards masterpiece DCs (The Waning Bolero is a favorite) and the replacement song allowing for what amounts to party-wide martial flexibility isn't much to sneeze at.
Weirdo replies: The utility of war chant is limited by the combat feats that you know (which is fortunately increased a bit through trading versatile performance for bonus feats). Whether it’s a good trade compared to inspire courage depends on your fighting style and your party’s. I note that several teamwork feats, including Precise Strike and Outflank, are eligible for War Chant.

Bard // Occultist ***
Basics: Med BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills+, two 6-level casting, Int Cha physical
Lots of utility, only passable offence made more difficult due to MAD. When considering implement schools, you'll want to give extra weight to spells not on the bard list. 
* Abjuration has a lot of these: Shield, Resist Energy, Magic Circle Against [], Protection from Energy, Life Bubble, Dimensional Anchor, Stoneskin, Spell Immunity, Antimagic Field, and Globe of Invulnerability.
* Conjuration's got a few goodies across multiple levels: Mage Armor, Mount, Minor Creation, Major Creation, Teleport, Wall of Stone, Heal, and Create Demiplane.
* Divination also has a handful of new spells: Augury, Status, Arcane Sight, Commune, True Seeing, and Prediction of Failure.
* Enchantment mostly features bard spells without the early access the bard gets. There are a handful of additions - Inflict Pain, Mind Swap, Mindwipe, and Antipathy/Sympathy – but overall it's just inferior to bard options.

* Illusion, like enchantment, is almost entirely shared with the bard, with minor exceptions such as Mask Dweomer or Illusory Wall. Now, looking at build options:
* For a combat build you'll want Panoply Savant (Warrior), ideally with a combat bard archetype such as Arcane Duelist (strength-based melee with medium armour and a heavy shield) or Arrowsong Minstrel (longbow with buckler panoply implement – counts as full BAB for feats). You won't have a lot of extra focus after the panoply, so choose your other implements based on spells and maybe one or two utility focus powers. Conjuration and Illusion are good choices. Purge Corruption and Shadow Beast are worth considering. Divination's resonant power will probably not see much use due to the high cost, and its spell list is only meh, but Mind Eye is great. Enchantment, Necromancy, and Evocation generally rely too much on DCs.
* A caster has more options depending on how they prioritize stats. Transmutation and Abjuration are still excellent choices for the spell lists alone. Divination gets a lot more attractive when you have the focus to invest in it. Evocation and Necromancy also look a lot better if you can get your Int high enough for competitive DCs. Silksworn is a strong choice here, though unlike some gestalts losing armour hurts. I'd recommend it particularly for manipulator builds. Focus on Cha for mental focus, high DCs on superior bard enchantment spells, and social skills. Enjoy the sneaky bonuses from silksworn eloquence and silksworn deception. Use implements for buffing and utility. Alternatively, Chronicler of Worlds bard makes you entirely Int-based - compared to Silksworn you keep armour, get a bit more out of occultist spells, and have the option to choose an occultist archetype, but have less implement versatility. Note an interesting synergy between the Dirge Bard's ability to influence undead with mind-affecting effects, and the necromancy implement's resonant power applying a penalty to undead saving throws.

Like the barbarian, the bloodrager is a strong martial class, tough and capable of massive damage in melee. Brawler


Brawlers get a large number of attacks with unarmed strikes, close, or monk weapons and can have good AC with light armour and a shield, losing no attack potential for the latter thanks to the way their Flurry works. Brawlers are also among the most flexible combatants in the game, thanks to – what else? - Martial Flexibility. This ability lets you pick up circumstantially useful feats as needed. They benefit from accuracy bonuses, damage bonuses, a good will save, out of combat utility, and any abilities that help them full attack more often due to dependence on Flurry.


If you don't want to have to keep track of all those combat feats, Mutagenic Mauler and Snakebite Striker gives you worthwhile passive damage boosts instead, plus a few minor abilities. Snakebite is especially good when your other class gives you a pet to flank with. Shield Champion is a really neat build for switch-hitting; the Brawler is otherwise weak at ranged combat, though you can do OK with shuriken or wushu darts in a pinch, and the Winding Path Renegade (Wind) gives you an extra bit of range.


Compared to the similar Monk, the Brawler's main advantages for gestalt are (1) Martial Flexibility (2) not needing Wis (3) ability to use armour and shields. It's also very good at pulling out combat maneuvers very effectively when needed, compared to the maneuver archetypes for monk (maneuver master and tetori) which are severely hampered when their speciality maneuvers become impractical. The Monk, depending on the build, can get better maneuverability, better defenses against non-weapon attacks, better ranged damage, and of course mystical powers. Monks tend to be better than Brawlers for characters that are overall Wis-based or that want combat to be a secondary role to casting.

Cleric


This powerful but somewhat bland caster can be gestalted most effectively by playing to the main types of cleric build. It mostly suffers from lack of skill points. If your other class has only 2 skill points, I recommend either the Roaming Exorcist or Herald Caller (but not Cloistered Cleric) archetypes for 4 skill points, or Cardinal for 6. The Cardinal works best with martial classes that restore the BAB and armour proficiencies (in which case you're trading a domain and spontaneous cures for 4 skill points and some class skills).


Combat: combine cleric with a full-BAB class, ideally one that doesn't use Int or Cha. Use cleric buffs to improve your own combat ability; check out the Strength or Growth domains. You can also cover healing out of combat, and have options to buff your allies or summon things. Herald Caller is handy for the latter – your combat half should provide the armour proficiencies you need anyway. Avoid enemy-targeting spells since you probably won't have a high enough Wis to deal with saves and SR. Tends to be a well-balanced gestalt.


Casting: combine cleric with another casting class, ideally one that also uses Wisdom, for increased versatility and possibly increased durability. This build is usually the best positioned for debuffing and controlling undead in addition to having support and summoning options.


Reach: use your standard actions to cast and a reach weapon to make attacks of opportunity. While this build requires heavy investment in feats and stats (you need better Dex than most combat clerics) it pays off in excellent action economy. This build can work when paired with either martial or casting classes, but it's especially important that the other class not use Int and Cha, and bonus feats or reach-enhancing abilities are extra welcome. I also like this build when combining clerics with classes that, like clerics, have both melee-focused and casting abilities, like Inquisitor or Druid. Using full-BAB AoO instead of iterative attacks can make up for having only an average BAB progression.


It's also notable that while clerics are MAD enough that they often end up with a low or middling Cha, they do have some useful Cha-dependent features that you should look into if your second class uses Cha. Channeling can be used not only to restore HP but grant buffs (eg through variant channeling) or remove conditions (Merciful Healer archetype), and with the Quick Channel feat you can Channel as a move action, which is very nice action economy for a caster cleric. The Evangelist archetype gives you a limited bardic performance – it's a generally worthwhile archetype that gets better with a higher Cha for more rounds/day. Controlling Undead (Undead Lord archetype recommended but optional) is also much improved by a higher Cha, since the Command Undead feat grants a Cha-based save to your prospective minions.


Alternatively, if you want to really go Cha-focused, consider the Elder Mythos Cultist. This archetype switches your casting stat to Cha and lets you use Cha for Will saves instead of Wis, but comes with limitations: vulnerability to insanity, restricted deity and domain choices and CN/CE alignment. You also trade Channel Energy for a more universally damaging Channel the Void effect, and lose spontaneous casting. Overall the restrictions on domains and loss of spontaneous casting and Channel make this a sub-par healer and ineffective undead controller. However, it can still excel at debuffing (including “bad touch”), summoning, and melee or ranged combat.


The Sacred Attendant gives you a small AC bonus if your other class doesn't wear armour.

Druid


A very versatile class. Spells include a good selection of control spells, buffs (especially long-duration defensive buffs like Barkskin and Resist Energy, good for your action economy), and utility. Wild shape offers mobility, scouting, pointy teeth, and sometimes reach. Natural Spell lets you cast spells from any class while Wild Shaping. Gestalting usually consists of doubling down on a “combat druid” build by picking a complementary full-BAB class, or on a “caster druid” build by selecting a full caster. However some hybrids also complement the class well, and many combinations work well as reach casters. The Feyspeaker archetype notably swaps your casting stat to Charisma, which makes a very good match with Cha-based casters.


There are a few ways to use Wild Shape, each of which relates differently to gestalt. The “tiger” is the easiest combat build and involves taking a form with lots of natural attacks and ideally pounce. It works well with most melee classes, but particularly those with large per-attack damage bonuses. The “T-rex” involves taking a big form with one powerful attack. It's usually less effective than the tiger but can work well with Vital Strike or other specific combos. The “Songbird of Doom” involves taking the form of something tiny and dextrous, getting Dex to hit and damage (usually Weapon Finesse + Agile Amulet of Mighty Fists) and attacking with large increases to accuracy and to AC and good damage. Works nicely with classes that encourage dex-based combat or improve reach, but sometimes considered “cheesy.” The “turtle” involves turning into a defensive form (usually not actually a turtle; small fliers and air elementals work well) and casting. Can overlap with the “Songbird” if you want to put in the investment to have a viable non-casting strategy.


If you want to stay humanoid in combat, you should choose an archetype that limits or eliminates Wild Shape in exchange for other powers. The standout options are Goliath Druid, Nature Fang, Skinshaper, and Supernaturalist. Goliath Druid is best for strength-focused combat builds, especially two-handers. Nature Fang and Skinshaper are more versatile and have better utility options. Supernaturalist is a neat option if your other class grants natural weapons that can’t be used with wild shape, or improves summoning. Note that the Supernaturalist’s summoning improvement does enhance celestial/fiendish animals, but not true outsiders. The Sunrider is a niche archetype that grants modest bonuses to allied mounts – it could be interesting if the entire party chooses to play mounted gestalts of one flavour or another.


The Animal Shaman archetypes with their standard-action summoning can work well if your second class enhances summoning. Lion and Saurian Shamans are generally the strongest.

Fighter


The fighter has the potential to excel in a number of styles of combat. Unfortunately, it also has several major weaknesses: it lacks out-of-combat utility, resistance to magic, and the ability to improve performance in the toughest fights. The best fighter gestalts patch these weaknesses while complementing one or more fighter combat styles. The most straightforward choices are part-casters and divine full casters which provide improved saves, skills, and buffs. These casters also benefit from the solid combat base and generous bonus feats of the fighter. Combining fighter with other martial classes tends to leave you with an over-specialized character, but sometimes you can stack potent combat abilities while still mitigating the downsides.


Fighter typically gestalts less well with full arcane casters due to arcane spell failure issues and conflicting action economy. This can be mitigated in a few ways. A ranged build minimizes the danger associated with little or no armour, and fighters make effective archers. The Arcane Armour training feat (available at level 3) lets you cast in a Mithral Shirt with no spell failure at the cost of a swift action, while Arcane Armor Mastery (level 7) lets you cast in a Mithral Breastplate (or if you’re using it from 3.5, a Celestial Plate). The Child of Acavna and Amaznen gives improved armour resistance but losing weapon training is probably not worth it. A reach build improves action economy as you can use your standard action to cast spells while making attacks of opportunity. And of course you might as well make use of Arcane Strike.


Advanced Weapon Training abilities of interest:
* Armoured Confidence - a nice intimidate bonus for builds that give you fun things to do with intimidate.
* Armed Bravery and Fighter’s Reflexes - handy for covering weak saves
* Trained initiative - handy for caster fighters or sneak attacking fighters
* Warrior Spirit - a versatile weapon buff, particularly useful for fighter//martial combinations.


Note: guide has not been systematically updated to account for Advanced Armour Mastery.


For archetypes:
* If your second class encourages the use of light or no armour, look at Dawnflower Dervish, Dragonheir Scion, Mutation Warrior, Skirmisher and Lore Warden. Skirmisher also brings you up to 4 skills/level, while Lore Warden adds a bonus 2 skill ranks to be spent on any Int-based skills. That said, if you decide to keep armour mastery, consider the advanced armour mastery abilities Master Armourer and Armoured Master with the Sprightly Armour feat.
* Eldritch Guardian gives you a potentially combat-capable familiar at low cost, useful for flanking buddies or teamwork feats. 
* Drill sergeant gives you tactician at the cost of bravery and advanced weapon training options.
* Roughrider gives a number of bonuses for fighters planning on doing mounted combat with a pet from another class. Losing advanced armor and weapon training hurts, but note your attack and damage bonuses from Mounted Mettle also apply to your mount. 
* Trench fighter gives you dex to damage with firearms, so it’s nice to pair with other firearm-focused archetypes that don’t give that bonus. 
* For a theme game, the Blackjack fighter is an interesting archetype for everyone to have, as it gives you access to a neat tactics ability and you can still customize your combat style using weapon training and some of your bonus feats. 
* Other archetypes generally focus on improving a specific fighting style such as archery or spear & shield (phalanx fighter) - refer to fighter guides for comments on the effectiveness of these archetypes for the intended purposes.

The gunslinger is, unsurprisingly, very focused on its gun. It works best with classes that can improve its ranged combat through martial or magical abilities; good Will saves, skills, and a backup option such as switch-hitting or magic are a plus. It also works passably for a switch-hitter when paired with a melee focus, or as a ranged backup for a caster, but this is usually not ideal because the gunslinger's intense focus doesn't provide much to someone who wants ranged combat to be a secondary option. The only archetype with notable gestalt implications is Mysterious Stranger, which works best when paired with a Cha-based full caster who won't  be using their gun every round, but will have lots of panache and a nice damage bonus for Focused Aim.

Hunter


Hunter is a real pet class. It doesn't just have a pet, it's designed around the pet, with most of its features improving the pet in some way. That means that in a small party the pet can easily fill the melee role while you focus on casting or ranged damage, and even in a high-power game it's possible to build and buff the pet to contribute meaningfully if you use teamwork feats wisely.


The melee build is perhaps the most potent, but also the least versatile, as both the hunter and the companion fill the same role. This makes it less good for a small party, but very nice in a high power game. Flanking feats such as Outflank and Precise Strike add significant boosts to both the hunter and companion, especially to builds with a large number of attacks. Pack Flanking allows you to “flank” when adjacent to your companion, which allows for mounted builds. Since you'll generally want to pair Hunter with a martial class, it's likely you'll end up missing a good Will save; the Hunter's above-average Wisdom helps but doesn't eliminate this weakness.


Ranged builds are slightly more versatile than melee ones, but the focus of both pet and master is still on damage. I'd only suggest this build if you really want an archer, but your group needs a frontliner. In this situation, make sure you prioritize your pet's defences since it'll take a lot of hits. You'll be looking to build like a ranged hunter, but select a second class with a good set of archery features.


Caster builds are highly versatile. The Hunter provides a competent frontliner in the pet, a baseline of martial competence if you don't want to cast, and a starter set of spells. The second class should provide the primary casting – ideally Wis-based and with the ability to wear armour. Teamwork feats will be significantly less useful to both you and your companion; consider trading them for a domain and the celestial/fiendish template using the Divine Hunter archetype.


The pet-free option, Feral Hunter, is quite similar to a Druid but with better skills, partial casting, a Ref save instead of a Will save, and an animal focus that's slightly more versatile than a free stat belt. The value of this trade depends on the other half of the gestalt as well as the level. The Feral Hunter is best at low levels where its skills and early-access Ranger spells pull a lot of weight, while the Druid becomes more powerful at high levels with more potent casting (especially 7th-9th level spells) and advanced elemental forms.


Generally the Hunter is designed for wilderness settings. If you're playing in an urban game, try the Roof Runner or Courtly Hunter archetypes, or even Sacred Huntmaster Inquisitor.

Inquisitor


The Inquisitor's big strengths are self-buffing and utility (particularly social, investigative, and tracking). It works very well with most martial classes. Their Bane ability is particularly effective for archers and other combat styles involving making a large number of attacks with a single weapon.
Inquisitor can be gestalted effectively with wis-based full casters. Int-based full casters can also work with the Living Grimoire archetype, though this reduces your overall weapons combat ability and pushes you into using a light mace (albeit one with scaling damage dice). 


Most archetypes are minor thematic variations, but some offer significant benefits for certain types of builds. Sacred Huntmaster and Monster Tactician are good choices for casting builds in a small party. Preacher is good if you can't find useful teamwork feats. Teamwork feats tend to be melee-slanted, but ranged characters should look at Escape Route, Friendly Fire Maneuvers, Lookout, Swap Places, Target of Opportunity, and Volley Fire. Alternatively, if you’re building an archer and feel like being able to really ruin a spellcaster’s day, take Spellbreaker combined with the Spellkiller Inquisition, which adds a +6 bonus to concentration checks to cast within your threatened area and an extra +2 if you hit them with a ranged attack. Sanctified Slayer and Relic Hunter are good if you anticipate a lot of combats in an adventuring day. Sanctified Slayer adds a straightforward swift-action combat boost. 


Relic Hunter is a bit more complicated, offering a powerful but limited selection of utility and/or martial powers at the cost of reducing the scope of your spell list. It's a great archetype for a casting build, which get more benefit from implement powers than the typical inquisitor buffs, and can cover some of the casting basics with their other spell list. Martial builds have more to lose, but should also consider the Relic Hunter if the overall build needs more utility and already has a lot of swift actions. See Occultist for notes on implement focus powers, though keep in mind that with fewer focus powers you should be making choices based more on resonant powers and spell list, which affects choices as follows:
   * Similar to the occultist:
   * Abjuration - a smattering of core defensive spells. Resonant and base powers are pretty good.
   * Divination - an array of moderately useful utility spells. Resonant and base powers are great, focus powers are worthwhile – top choice for the Relic Hunter.
   * Better for the Relic Hunter:
   * Enchantment is more versatile, including buffs like Wrath, Heroism, and Prayer in addition to mind control – this makes it a good choice for a support or combat build. Powers  skippable.
   * Evocation gets smite spells, light spells, Blistering Invective, Divine Favor, and Divine Power. Since focus powers add energy rays (base power), blasts, and walls, the inquisitor again ends up with more versatility from this school. Only thing you're really missing is force effects.
   * Less good for the Relic Hunter:
   * Transmutation's spell list is lacklustre. The resonant and base powers are good enough that martials still need this school, but casters can wait until level 4 or 7.
   * Conjuration basically just has healing, and the Side Step power.
   * Illusion gets Invisibility, the “Disguise” line, and that's about it. 
   * Necromancy is pretty thin with only Inflict and a few not-fantastic debuffs.


*Update - Archetype: Sworn of the Eldest makes the Inquisitor Cha-based, slightly adjusts the skill set, and swaps the solo tactics and teamwork feats for domain spells. This archetype hasn’t been incorporated into the ratings consistently, but generally it will be a very good choice for gestalting with Cha-based casters or archers (since these are less likely to miss the teamwork feats). It’s probably also worth it for other characters that are fairly Cha-invested, and a fine option for other characters that won’t miss teamwork feats.

A canny and versatile class, the investigator has a lot of things that frontliners want – extra saves, lots of skills, magical utility, and two juicy combat self-buffs. Kineticist


Broadly, you have three options.


First, focus on blasts. Your second class should be a martial class that can improve your blasts. This means general bonuses to weapon damage like Studied Combat – not effects that improve manufactured weapons or spells. Gestalting with partial casters can work well for ranged energy blasters who don't care as much about accuracy or HP. Elemental Annihilator and Kinetic Knight are good archetypes for blast-happy builds, with the latter serving as a heavy armour melee-only option. 


Second, elementally enhance attacks with Kinetic Fist or Energize Weapon. This adds a little more than half a point of damage per kineticist level to your attacks – mediocre, and it costs burn until level 5, but after that it's an unlimited bonus. You can also debuff with substance infusions if you can handle the burn. Some of the defence talents are excellent, and ranged and area blasts and utility talents add some versatility. For this type of build the other gestalt half needs to handle the martial style. Note that Energize Weapon is more limited than Kinetic Fist – it works on only a single weapon (no TWF) and only with energy blasts (no terrakinetic weapons). Air is a good elemental choice for Energize Weapon builds, bringing electric blasts, the magnetic infusion (free with energize weapon at level 11) and wings of air. Note also that holding manufactured weapons prevents you from gathering power.


Third, combine with a full caster, with kineticist providing improved durability and extra combat options. Single element builds are safer as casting gestalts since the casting half provides more strategic versatility.

Medium


The medium is best as a “secondary” class for gestalt, which is to say that you should use your other class to define your usual tactics and then use spirits to either supplement your primary role or increase versatility.  This works best with full-BAB classes, since the Champion and to a lesser extent the Guardian offer good combat abilities for general use and most martials will get a lot out of the ability to select increased casting or skill abilities for specific missions; the good Will save is an added bonus. However, casters can also benefit from the seance bonuses of the archmage or hierophant and from the choice between expanded spell options, various skill or defence options, and (for some) the champion's ability to enter combat.


Consider Spirit Dancer for more flexibility in switching between spirits. Also note that while the medium's psychic spells aren't subject to armour spell failure, the ones they get from the archmage spirit are arcane and can be spoiled by your armour.

Monk


On their own, most monk variants don't have the offense necessary to stand up beside other classes. However, they do have a lot of defensive or mobility options and other neat tricks that can be very valuable when paired with a more active class, whether martial or caster – and the variants that do have strong offense still work well for characters that want a little extra punch. Unarmed strikes optional. If going without armour hurts, look at the Sohei archetype or archetypes that get rid of flurry and/or fast movement (such as Sensei or Master of Many Styles) which lose less when wearing armour. Remember to use Qinggong to trade out any abilities you won't use (slow fall, high jump) for handy spell-like abilities. There's something for everyone!


First off, characters that use Charisma will want either Scaled Fist (dragon themed charisma based monk), or the Nornkith (fey themed charisma based monk). Only the Scaled Fist is compatible with Unchained Monk, which gives it a significant advantage in gestalting with part-casters (which will badly want the full BAB).


Characters that want ranged combat as a primary strategy or a complement to Wis-based casting will love Zen Archer. It comes with great offense and good mobility. You lose out on evasion and poison/disease immunity, but keep unarmoured AC, saves, and some defensive bonus feats. Less good for Int or Cha casters, but still worth looking at.


Sohei is your pick for mounted combat, reach, or switch hitting (with thrown weapons or bow and temple sword) thanks to its ability to flurry with unusual weapons. Weapon Training is a fairly powerful passive boost, especially with gloves of dueling. It also gives you access to advanced weapon training and weapon mastery feats. For a thrown build, for example, you can take Focus Weapon and Trained Throw for damage increase and Ricochet Toss to retrieve your weapon. MAD combos that can't afford a good Wis will appreciate light armour, at least at low levels. 


Other melee builds generally want Unchained Monk for superior mobility and strong offense, especially if the second class doesn't already have full BAB. Full-BAB classes have more options. Flowing Monk, Maneuver Master, and Tetori give you better battlefield control through combat maneuvers. Master of Many Styles makes it really easy to use combat styles including Ascetic Style, and unlike most archetypes you keep all your special defenses – it's also relatively easy to pair with other archetypes. Hungry Ghost Monk gives you vampiric powers in exchange for monk resistances, and is especially good with crit-fishing builds.


Wis Casters should consider Sensei for a support/control build with good use of combat maneuvers, at the expense of mobility. It is compatible with Ki Mystic for extra support at the expense of special defenses. Master of Many Styles is good for defense and mobility with Crane, Snake, Monkey, and Panther Styles. Elven Battle Style is also a good choice for Int-based casters if you don't mind the two prerequisites. Note that having a monk weapon is not significant without flurry – the exception is if you want Ascetic Style.


Note: while monks are generally required to be lawful, you can get around this requirement with the Enlightened Warrior trait (be adopted by aasimar if necessary) or the Martial Artist of Menhir Guardian archetypes. This is useful for gestalting with barbarian or divine casters following Chaotic deities.

Occultist


The occultist is an extremely versatile class, but individual occultists are limited by implement school selection and amount of mental focus. Because Focus is such an important resource, an occultist normally wants more intelligence than spellcasting alone requires, so watch your MAD! Your other class should guide your role and thus implement selection:


If your other class is martial, you want Transmutation for offence, and usually at least one of Abjuration or Illusion for defence (mirror image). Divination's a tough choice – the powers are great, but to get the most out of them you're diverting focus from Transmutation. Definitely take it if you expect to alternate between combat and non-combat days. Otherwise it depends on how much Focus you have and whether you have racial Darkvision. Conjuration's got some good utility, and Side Step. Evocation is fine as a backup option, and the force spells are useful, but it's not high priority. Necromancy is probably a pass - but since the Necromantic Servant shares your BAB and HP it can make a handy extra body in a small party or if you need a disposable flank partner. Enchantment is skippable. Armour doesn't interfere with occultist spells, so feel free to suit up if you get heavy proficiency. Haunt Collector archetype with the Champion is a nice damage buff and is best applied to the conjuration or necromancy schools.


Casters are looking to fill in any holes in their spell lists and for synergistic casting benefits. The latter come from necromancy (undead creation), conjuration (minor duration boost), and evocation (damage boost plus shape mastery). Haunt Collector with the archmage or hierophant spirits can also be a nice boost if you cast a lot of damaging or healing spells. The Mage's Paraphernalia gives you metamagic abilities and expanded access to divination, evocation, and necromancy spells.


Partial casters work a bit better with the Occultist than most other part-hybrids because you can build the occultist with a bit more martial or caster focus than most hybrids. A martial occultist//part caster will want to take the Trappings of the Warrior panoply to get full BAB with one weapon. This can limit your fighting style options depending on how your GM interprets the use of a shield. It also cuts down on your options for utility schools compared to the martial//occultist (see above), but usually the non-occultist part caster has a good amount of utility. A casting occultist//part caster often wants Silksworn as this increases your amount of focus and number of implement schools.


In terms of archetypes, Silksworn is a great pair with unarmoured classes, since you get bonus implement schools and spells per day in exchange for losing the ability to cast in armour. It also gets to add both Intelligence and Charisma to its Mental Focus, which is helpful when gestalting with charisma-based classes (though note you still cast using Intelligence). Similarly, if you gestalt Occultist with something that needs a lot of Wis, you can take Reliquarian for Wis-based focus and keep your Int modest – though in this case consider taking Relic Hunter Inquisitor instead. The Relic Hunter gets at least as many implement schools as the Reliquarian and is entirely Wis-based, though it does sacrifice focus powers and by strict RAW can't take Panoplies (but ask your GM as this may be an oversight). Panoply Savant is also worth a look.

Oracle


As a spontaneous caster with a limited list of spells known, the oracle has a higher level of specialization than most divine casters. Mysteries reinforce this specialization – and thanks to mystery spells, also allow you to specialize in roles not typical of the cleric, such as blasting. As a result, it's fairly easy to find synergies between your oracle's specialty and your second class. However, it's especially important to make sure that your gestalt maintains some versatility. A Fighter//Oracle should make sure their spell selection includes group buffs or summons in addition to personal combat buffs, and a Red Dragon Sorcerer // Flame Oracle should have options other than fire damage. The Spirit Guide archetype can improve versatility, as it gives you a small selection of abilities which you can change from day to day. 


The oracle is also notable for its ability to use Cha instead of Dex for purposes of AC and either CMD or Ref saves, through the Lore (Sidestep Secret/Reflex), Nature (Nature's Whispers/CMD), and Lunar (Prophetic Armour/CMD) Mysteries. These abilities are most useful when your second class is also Charisma-focused (such as paladin) and/or prefers light or no armour (such as sorcerer or swashbuckler), since your Cha modifier is limited by your armour's max dexterity modifier.


Note also that the Oracle, unlike most divine casters, does not provide a good Fort save, so if your second class also has a poor Fort save you will want other ways to boost that defense.


Like the cleric, the Oracle can be built primarily for combat, casting, or as a reach build. 


Combat: You almost always want your other class to have full BAB and no requirement for Int or Wis. Battle Mystery is the obvious choice, though if you want access to a wider variety of revelations consider Ancestor, Dark Tapestry (Many Forms & Wings of Darkness), Metal, Stone, and Flame (Cinder Dance). Wood is a good choice for archers or reach weapon users, and Lunar provides the best combat revelations for a low-Dex build. Consider Warsighted if your chosen mystery has few revelations you are interested in, though note that this will delay your first revelation until level 3 – problematic if it's important to your concept (such as Prophetic Armour) - and you’ll need Extra Revelation for any additional revelations after that.


Reach oracles gestalt similarly to the combat oracle, except they don't necessarily need the second class to have full BAB – just be sure to buff and look out for accuracy-increasing revelations like Wood Bond, Dance of the Blades (Metal), or Spirit of the Warrior (Ancestor). They also have little use for the Dex-replacement revelations since you still need a good Dex for extra AoO. 


Casting: Most mysteries will give you useful stuff, but particularly note Bones and Juju for necromancers (plus other offensive options), Flame or Winter for blasting, Apocalypse for blasting and controlling, and Heavens for ridiculous debuffing. Also keep in mind that the Waves and Winter mysteries’ Freezing Spells revelation provides an excellent debuff that is extra useful when combined with a class that provides save-based cold spells. Lore’s Mental Acuity gives you up to a +5 inherent bonus to Int, which can be very useful for gestalting with Int-based casters like the wizard, though see below, and the mystery is otherwise lacklustre; if you’re really needing to minimize MAD you can also take Sidestep Secret, but as a caster you probably still want Dex for initiative. Casting oracles are particularly likely to run into the low Fort save issue. 


Extended on Lore Mystery: When planning a Lore Oracle / Int caster, keep in mind that the +5 is equal to the increase you’d normally get by putting all of your level-based increases into Int, which more or less gives you level-based advancements to two casting stats at the same rate as a SAD caster. However, you still have to worry about splitting your initial stat allotment and your stat headband budget between the two stats, which means you’re not on the same footing as a SAD caster even after devoting your mystery to this. If your starting stats are generous and versatility is at a premium (for example in a small party) this may still be worth it. Indeed, if you lean into Oracle as a support caster you could potentially use some or all of your level increases and Mental Acuity to increase your Int above normal limits, which is great for DC-focused mages - just make sure your initial stats and expected wealth support enough charisma increases to cast your higher level oracle spells! One last caution: Mental Acuity isn’t available until level 7, so make sure you’ve planned to have acceptable casting stats through level 6.


General: The Time and Wind mysteries are fairly useful all-around with mobility and defensive options, and the Ancestor and Lore mysteries (and the Spirit Guide archetype) are notable for providing all Knowledge skills as class skills. The Life mystery gives you good action economy for healing and is the easiest way for an oracle to handle condition removal.

Paladin


The paladin's big weakness is out of combat utility - two skill ranks per level, limited class skills, and limited magic mostly focused on combat or healing. It also doesn’t give you a lot to work with in combat style - no bonus feats of any kind pushes the Paladin towards a simple two-hand fighting style, though it can certainly benefit from more complicated styles if the other half supports. Its biggest strength is durability, with excellent saving throws and lay on hands. As a rule, paladin gestalts excellently with Charisma-based casters, whether full or partial.

Psychic


The psychic is unusual as an int-based spontaneous caster with access to many key arcane buffs and debuffs such as Enlarge Person or Sleep, but without suffering arcane spell failure. This makes them a great pair for full divine casters since you get diversified casting without losing armour. In this case, it's important to pick a psychic discipline that matches your divine class's casting stat. Similarly, Psychics are often better matches for armour-wearing martials than full arcane casters are, though the phrenic pool and discipline place greater than usual stat requirements. Such combinations work best when using spells for utility or pre-buffing – and don't overlook swift action spells like Burst of Speed or Thought Shield. Mutation Mind is potentially a useful archetype for a more combat-oriented Psychic gestalt, though as it grants an Enhancement bonus to Strength it doesn't stack with a magic belt and may be better suited to someone who wants melee to be a backup plan. Unsurprisingly Psychics also generally work well with partial casters, as long as stat requirements are complementary. Phrenic Amplifications will work with psychic spells from another class, which gives you a lot of extra flexibility in how you use them, though it does reinforce your spellcasting vulnerability to emotion effects.

Ranger


Rangers are well-balanced martials, with good combat abilities, lots of skills, and two good saves. They make synergistic and versatile characters when gestalted with full divine casters, and also work well with most partial casters and some psychic classes. Full arcane casters tend to find their focus split more between spells and weapons, but can still use some ranged combat style feats (such as point blank shot and precise shot) with rays, and spells requiring attack rolls do benefit from favoured enemy. Some martial characters also work well with rangers, depending on fighting style and whether the other class needs utility and a good reflex save.


The most useful Combat Styles for most characters are Two-Weapon Fighting, Weapon and Shield, or Archery. Most combat styles have something to offer, but some feat-light styles such as Two-Hand Fighting or Natural Weapons are less useful than taking Archery to switch-hit. Archers can also get extra tricks with Divine Marksman (reliable damage bonus on non-favoured enemies) or for gestalts with a decent Cha Hooded Champion (panache with bows).


If your enemy types are unreliable (and you don't want to rely on Instant Enemy), you can take the Wild Hunter archetype to trade favoured enemy for animal focus, or wild stalker for rage (this also replaces your combat feats and hunter's bond). Similarly, favoured terrain can be replaced with more reliable but minor abilities using Infiltrator or Shapeshifter (though the latter only works for natural weapons builds). Guide replaces your favoured enemy but also relies more heavily on favoured terrain. Alternatively, check out Slayer. If you're worried your animal companion won't keep up with a high-powered gestalt game, try Divine Tracker.


Rangers have a modest need for Wis to cast their spells, but this is usually not a problem. If your point buy is tight, consider a spell-less archetype such as Divine Marksman, Trapper, or Skirmisher. Note Skirmisher does use Wisdom for uses/day, but it may be preferable to not having enough Wis to cast spells.

Slayer


Slayers are well-balanced martials, with good combat abilities, lots of skills, and two good saves. They make synergistic and versatile characters when gestalted with various forms of partial or full caster, which add magical options and round out the saving throws. Spiritualist


The spiritualist is another pet class, with a phantom about halfway in power between an animal companion and an eidolon. As such it's usually more useful in gestalt games with a smaller party size. However the phantom does have useful tricks outside being a meatless meat shield, such as delivering touch spells at range, buff and debuff auras, and incorporeal scouting. The bonded manifestation can also be used to enhance the spiritualist's own abilities. Two archetypes to consider. First, the Fractured Soul switches the spiritualist from a wisdom-based to a charisma-based class, improving stat synergy for some matches. Second, the Ectoplasmaticist swaps out the phantom entirely for the ability to manifest ectoplasmic weapons, which are essentially a longsword or pair of shortswords that gain reach and magical enhancement as you level. You also gain a limited form of spellcombat/spellstrike, allowing you to cast a spiritualist spell during a full attack. These abilities allow nice battlefield control and action economy for many martial and “reach caster” builds. Interestingly, the spiritualist can wear armour but can also gain armour bonuses from bonded manifestation or the ectoplasmatist, so it works well with both armoured and unarmoured classes.

The summoner is most useful when gestalting to compensate for a small party, since the extra bodies are super useful. Usually in this case you should gestalt with another caster in order to fill a support and/or control role.


Summoner is less useful in high-powered gestalt, since your summons are comparatively weaker against your high-powered foes. However, the eidolon is still a fairly powerful pet and can contribute with judicious buffing, or as a mount or flanking partner. The Naturalist gives your eidolon a little extra boost at the expense of your minor personal bonuses, which is useful if you're planning on sending your eidolon to the front lines while you stand back and cast.  The synthesist can also be strong for some builds in a high-powered game since it adds powers to the PC. Note that you only use the eidolon's BAB for your summoner levels, so gestalting with a full-BAB class still gives a synthesist full BAB. Replacing your physical stats is less exciting if your gestalt comes with a high stat buy, but can make builds with MAD mental stats workable.


Summoner // Swashbuckler
Basics: Full BAB, d10, Ref and Will, 4 skills, 6-level casting, Dex Cha
Synergy is modest, but nothing clashes in this combo, either. Flanking with the eidolon is a good tactic. Since Precise Strike doesn't work with natural attacks, this doesn't work very well with Synthesist.


Summoner // Warpriest (Synthesist)
Basics: Med BAB, d8, Fort and Will, 4 skills, two 6-level casting, physical Wis Cha
Not enough personal combat ability in the summoner to justify this, unless you're going Synthesist (which also helps with the MAD). Even then, not fantastic. Try Summoner//Battle Oracle or Summoner//Paladin.


Summoner // Witch
Basics: Med BAB, d8, Will, 2+ skills, full and 6-level casting, Int Cha


Summoner // Wizard
Basics:  Med BAB, d8, Will, 2+ skills, full and 6-level casting, Int Cha
Squishy, even with an eidolon meat shield. Stick to buffs on the summoner side to reduce your need for Cha. See also Summoner//Sorc. 




Swashbuckler


Swashbuckler // Warpriest
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4 skills, 6-level casting, Dex Wis Cha
Heavy on swift/immediate actions, and the warpriest's heavy armour proficiency is a waste. However the warpriest's buffs are very nice and the sacred weapon damage is a meaningful boost for the lighter weapons the Swashbuckler usually uses. Between that and the bonus feats, a whip build is particularly rewarding. You can get Slashing Grace at level 1, Whip Mastery and Combat Reflexes at level 3, and Improved Whip Mastery at level 5, with another 10 bonus feats and 8 ordinary feats to spend on the stuff that makes wielding a whip fun (or diversifying yourself). And thanks to Sacred Weapon, Precise Strike, Swashbuckler Weapon Training, etc., that whip hurts. Note that Opportune Parry and Riposte doesn't actually require you to threaten the opponent, so it's useful before you get Improved Whip Mastery. Thrown Weapons are also a fine option with Flying Blade and Arsenal Chaplain (the latter to qualify for Ricochet Toss).

Vigilante


The vigilante's most notable feature is its dual identity – if you want a secret identity, this is the class. Fortunately it also provides a varied suite of abilities that, with the correct archetype, complement pretty much any other class. It provides a lot of intrigue-oriented utility – stealth, disguise, and social manipulations – largely through the social identity talents which you buy separately from combat abilities. Lethal Grace gets special mention as the best RAW way to make a Dex build with a weapon other than a scimitar (along with the fighter's similar Trained Grace advanced weapon training option). The vigilante's appearance features use Cha so it's nice if your second class does as well, but it's not important enough that you can't choose a second class with other priorities. Note that if your second class has alignment restrictions or a code, you must uphold those restrictions in both social and vigilante identity.


The Avenger's full BAB and combat talents make it a solid choice for giving a partial caster more martial ability, though watch out for the d8 HD and the poor Fort save. It's also not bad when paired with some martial classes for even more combat ability (as well as good Ref and Will saves and extra skill utility) but lacks a bit of versatility when used in that way. The avenger has more to offer melee builds than ranged ones, but can still work with ranged characters – using talents for combat feats is helpful for these builds.  Alternatively, Mounted Fury trades full BAB for a mount, but otherwise keeps access to the juicy Avenger combat talents, making it a good option for combining with classes that already have full BAB.


Stalker works for martials or full casters but lack of full BAB or other accuracy-increasing buffs makes it a poor complement to partial casters which will lag behind other gestalts in both martial and magical potency.


You can combine either Stalker or Avenger with most noncasting archetypes including the psychometrist, which offers occultist implements and works well with Int-based classes. 


Similarly, the partial caster vigilantes – Warlock, Cabalist, Zealot, and Magical Child – are good for rounding out a full-BAB class or full-caster with a complementary ability score. While they don't get access to the best combat talents like the Avenger, you can still use their limited talents for some combat basics like Lethal Grace or  Shield of Blades, or magical utility specific to the archetype. Warlock and Cabalist are your Int-based partial casters, with wizard and witch lists respectively. Cabalist works slightly better in melee thanks to bleed powers and shadow jump. Zealot is Wis-based, with inquisitor casting and talents, and the only partial casting vigilante capable of wearing medium or heavy armour. Magical Child is generally not a great archetype, but its Cha synergy is useful in some combinations. 


While the martial avengers are potentially interesting even without dual identity – their diverse talents and good will saves set them apart from other martial options – the casting vigilantes stack up less well against other partial casters. If you don't want a secret identity, strongly consider magus or occultist instead of warlock or cabalist, bard or summoner instead of magical child, and inquisitor instead of zealot.


Roleplay note: There’s nothing in the rules that says you can’t use your second class’s ability in both your identities, and ruling otherwise might put you at a distinct disadvantage compared to other gestalts. However, if the entire team is gestalting vigilante, if your character would normally outshine some party members, or if you just don’t mind party power imbalances, talk with your GM to see if it makes sense to have some or all of your other class features to be associated with one specific identity. Maybe your cleric//vigilante character is a kindly priest who by night fights the enemies of their deity under the guise of a warrior, and using your cleric powers in your vigilante identity could bring unwanted attention - or maybe that character appears to be a simple scholar in their social identity and using magic would give them away as the divine champion. Perhaps your fighter//vigilante is from a culture where swordsmanship is common enough that a minor courtier being an unusually fine fencer isn’t enough for people to suspect them of also being The Sapphire Shadow. Maybe your wizard//vigilante runs a magic supply store and is free to use first or second level spells without drawing attention, but not more potent spells. Or your alchemist//vigilante is free to drink all the “potions” they like in either identity but not their mutagen.


Alchemist  //  Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, (two) 6-level casting, physical Int
Avenger vigilante gives you an excellent combat build, while warlock or cabalist expands on controller options. Of the two casters I recommend Mindchemist/Warlock: bombs supply fire-based blasting and control effects, and mystic bolts round out your energy type options and let you melee (Weapon Finesse recommended). TWF works with both (assuming you get fast bombs). You also get versatile partial casting lists for buffing, utility, and some additional control spells. Perfect Recall makes the combo an excellent sage - the social identity is probably a scholar. 


Arcanist // Vigilante
Basics:  Med/Full BAB, d8, Ref and Will, 6 skills, full casting and optional 6-level, Int Cha


Barbarian // Vigilante
Basics: Full BAB, d12, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Cha
Barbarian // Mounted Fury Vigilante is an excellent mounted combat build – you get a mount that shares your teamwork feats and can rage with you, bonuses to charging, and pounce. If you want casting, I suggest the zealot (able to wear medium armour, good buff and utility spells), though cabalist works well with a lightly armoured urban barbarian.


Bard // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, Ref and Will, 6 skills+, (two) 6-level casting, physical Cha
A bit too much overlap here. The avenger is offensively solid but fragile, and the casting options are underwhelming, especially since the only one with stat synergy is Magical Child. Best build is probably a ranged avenger build, using talents for feats and Signature Weapon.


Bloodrager // Vigilante
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Cha
The mounted fury isn't as strong an option as for the barbarian thanks to more limited options for sharing rage powers. Best reason to go this route is if you want a Magical Child throwing really impressive tantrums.


Brawler // Vigilante
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Cha
Brawler//Stalker is probably the closest you can get to Batman. Living Shield is great for grapplers. Fist of the Avenger, while somewhat redundant with your ordinary unarmed strike, still adds a moderate damage bonus. Shield of Blades works well with Dragon Style. Lethal Grace is a solid option for Dex brawlers. 


Cavalier // Vigilante
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Cha


Cleric // Vigilante (Avenger)
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, full casting, physical Wis Cha
Cleric // Avenger is the way to go here. I recommend a reach build with buffing and summoning. Reach clerics are normally feat-starved, so avenger can help add combat feats (in addition to a few more interesting talents). Keeping foes at a distance means the d8 vs d10 HD doesn't matter as much as it would for some characters, and since most attacks will be AoO (therefore top BAB) it doesn't matter that you have fewer accuracy boosts than, say, Cleric//Ranger. For a casting-focused option Zealot gives you more magical versatility, though I'd rate it a step lower due to fewer skills and weak combat potential. 


Druid // Vigilante  (Avenger)
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, full casting, physical Wis Cha
Like Cleric//Vig, Avenger makes a very nice reach build. Casting-focused options are Zealot or Feyspeaker//Magical Child. Neither is particularly strong, but of the two I prefer the Fey Child due to better skills, more interesting thematics, and only using one mental stat.


Fighter // Vigilante
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Cha
Fighter gives you access to a lot of what the avenger has to offer (full BAB, combat feats, armour training), so this is a good combination for the stalker, casting vigilantes, or archetypes that give up a lot of talents. Feline wildsoul stalker is a good way to go if you specifically want Mad Rush.


Gunslinger // Vigilante (Zealot, Avenger)
Basics: Full BAB, d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, Dex Wis Cha
Thematically compelling, especially since the vigilante's gunslinging archetype is so underwhelming. I recommend using Zealot for buff and utility spells. Avenger is also good for extra combat feats (in which case consider Mysterious Stranger for Cha synergy). Mounted Fury gives you a mount and charge abilities you won't use in exchange for two talents and taking a longer time to switch identities – your call.  Lethal Grace is handy for switch hitting.


Hunter // Vigilante (Avenger)
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, 6-level casting, physical Wis Cha
Avenger makes a well-rounded melee fighter with support/utility casting and a flanking buddy. The ranged build is slightly less powerful but worth considering for a small party. Options without full BAB lack oomph, though Zealot is least problematic because of ability synergy. Mounted Fury is redundant and costs you BAB. On the Hunter side, Courtly Hunter is thematically interesting, though it's a pity you can't use subtle form to give you a different “social identity” pet from level 1. 


Inquisitor // Vigilante (Avenger)
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills+, 6-level casting, physical Wis Cha
This is one of the better options for a ranged vigilante since Judgment and Bane encourage making lots of attacks – you'll need to take Avenger and use a lot of talents for combat feats to make it work.


Investigator // Vigilante (Avenger)
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, Ref and Will, 6 skills+, 6-level casting, physical Int Cha
With the Avenger's full BAB and combat talents, you can keep up in melee. Ranged combat is not recommended between the feat tax on using Studied Combat at range and the lack of particular support for range on the Avenger side. Unfortunately this is also a little more MAD than unusual since you need extra Con to compensate for d8 HD and a poor Fort save. Consider psychometrist for some extra Int-based tricks – I recommend starting with Transmutation and Legacy Weapon, which compensates for losing combat talents, then picking up Abjuration, Divination, or Illusion.


Kineticist // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, pseudo-casting, Dex Con Cha
If you take Unkillable make sure you & your GM are on the same page about how it interacts with the nonlethal damage from Burn – strict interpretation of the rules says that Diehard only kicks in when you're knocked out by lethal damage but most people let you take a little extra nonlethal as well.


Magus // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, all saves, 6 skills, 6-level casting, physical Int Cha
Unsurprisingly, you want Avenger's full BAB. Psychometrist is a complementary archetype thanks to Int synergy. Legacy Weapon stacks with Arcane Pool? Armour skin and its heavy training extension lag only slightly behind the magus' armour training extensions, making them good 8th and 14th/16th level choices, while Lethal Grace is a boon for Dex magi who just don't want to use a scimitar.


Medium // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, Ref and Will, 6 skills, partial casting, physical Cha


Mesmerist // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8, Ref and Will, 6 skills, 6-level casting, physical Cha


Monk // Vigilante
Basics: Med/Full BAB, d8/d10, all saves, 4/6 skills, no / 6-level casting, physical Wis Cha
My favourite variation here is Unchained//Zealot, which gives you perfect BAB/HP/saves, partial casting for buffing/utility, and stat synergy. You can also use the Channel Energy talent to qualify for Crusader's Flurry. Avenger is less useful when you already have full BAB and flying kick, but it remains a solid if less versatile pair for non-unchained archetypes like Tetori or Maneuver Master. Finally, Unchained Scaled Fist//Magical Child with a pseudodragon familiar is a slightly less powerful but very flavourful combo. Useful talents include Living Shield (for grapplers), Shield of Blades (two-handed monk weapons), and Lethal Grace (Dex monks). If you did take Avenger, Fist of the Avenger, while somewhat redundant with your ordinary unarmed strike, still adds a decent damage bonus.

(Anti)Paladin // Vigilante

Roleplaying is potentially tricky for this one. Both your social and vigilante identity must be LG, and your paladin auras will be active in both, which can draw unwanted attention. You're also potentially easy to bait, as your social identity won't be permitted to allow harm to come to innocents if your vigilante identity can prevent it. Warpriest


I've heard people say that warpriest isn't worth it in gestalt when you can combine cleric with fighter. However, I think warpriest works as well as the cleric when gestalted with most full BAB classes, because the passive and swift action combat buffs do not compete for a combat build's attention in the same way as standard action spells do. A reach build is fairly effective at managing those concerns but if you want a different combat style and casting is secondary, the warpriest suits. Works much less well in builds that lack a combat focus (i.e., most classes lacking full BAB).


Warpriest // Witch (pre-Eratta Scarred Witch Doctor)
Basics:  Med BAB, d8, Fort and Will, 2 skills+, full and 6-level casting, Int Wis physical
Remember how I said warpriest is preferable to cleric when gestalting with a combat focused class? Witch isn't combat focused. If you can get the old Scarred Witch Doctor... it's still not great, but at least you have one less stat to worry about and decent HP.


Warpriest // Wizard
Basics:  Poor BAB, d8, Fort and Will, 2 skills+, full and partial casting, Int Wis physical
Warpriest + mage = waste. Still not completely nonfunctional, but you'll do better with Cleric//Wizard, Magus//Wizard, or Occultist//Wizard.


Witch


Witch works somewhat better than other arcane full casters with some martial classes. The witch has the advantage that its hexes can be used when spells can’t, such as while wearing armour. That means that if your other class prefers to wear armour, you can lean on your hexes and use your spells for long-duration and utility effects. In addition, Hex Strike lets you slap a specific hex effect on Unarmed Strikes (or monk or natural weapons, if you take Ascetic Style or Feral Combat Training).


White haired witch is a particularly martial witch. Its hair attacks are most effective with classes that grant full BAB and enhance grappling and tripping. Note that the hair is considered a natural weapon, and that you don’t gain the grappled condition while grappling with your hair.


Invoker gives up four hexes for a flexible buff. In addition to a short-duration enhancement bonus to physical stats, you can switch between lowering DR/SR or increasing hex and patron spell DCs, spell or natural attack damage, spell duration (in rounds), and weapon attack rolls. This is handy if you want to spread your focus between spells and weapons.


Note: while I was writing this guide, the Scarred Witch Doctor was errata'd to lose its Con-based casting. Prior to this change, SWD was an unusually effective combat witch when gestalted with some martial classes. Because of this unique status, and because I have seen some discontent with the change, I have left a few specific references to the old SWD in the guide, in case your GM allows you to use the old Con-based version.


Finally, Seducer makes the Witch Charisma-based, which can be useful in increasing stat synergy. Unfortunately it is not compatible with either White Haired Witch or Invoker.


Witch // Wizard
Basics:  Poor BAB, d6, Will, 2 skills+, two full casting, Int
I'm never going to give a gestalt this fragile a really good rating, but... this is pretty decent, as Arcane//Arcane goes. At least you get a diverse spell list and work off one casting stat.


Wizard


Ah, the wizard. Arguably the most powerful class in the standard game... and they still can be magnificent powerhouses in gestalt, if you build them right. But with their need for superb intelligence, their problems with armour, and their lack of passive abilities, the Wizard is like the Sherlock or Dr. House of gestalt: amazing when working with those capable of catering to its massive ego, and at best competent with those that have difficulty doing so. They work best with Int-based partial casters that provide additional casting versatility as well as durability. Being able to do something other than cast spells is nice, too.  
Frank Lazar says: They can also work wonders with  martial classes that forgo all but light armor if you’re willing to either give up your swift actions with armor training feats or suffer a bit of spell failure every now and then. The combo with Magus and Spell Blending arcana is also an option.
Weirdo’s response: There are definitely ways to build very strong Wizard//Martial gestalts; unfortunately these aren’t a style I have experience with and I haven’t been able to put the time into a good detailed analysis. At first glance I’d lean to a reach build, possibly archery or fencing grace. As an Int-based partial caster, the Magus plays very nicely with the Wizard.


Conjuration (Teleportation) and Divination have useful school powers for all kinds of characters, offering mobility and a significant initiative bonus. Admixture is useful for blasters since it allows you to make sure you have the right element on hand. Transmutation (Enhancement) is nice paired with more martial classes since it offers physical enhancement bonuses (does not stack with a belt) and natural armour (stacks with existing bonuses).
