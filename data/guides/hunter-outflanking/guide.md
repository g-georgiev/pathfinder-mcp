﻿Outflanking Hunter
This is a guide directed purely at the Hunter who will be outflanking opponents with his animal companion. This guide is meant to be a specialized guide compared to a more generalized guide like C. Barnes’s guide: https://www.dropbox.com/s/g6qxya9d9sbnbir/Hunter%20Guide.pdf?dl=0. 


There are two basic builds for the hunter: a melee combat build and a ranged combat build. The Outflanking Hunter is based on engaging in melee combat. If ranged combat is what you are looking for you can read the guide posted above or find one based on that playstyle. If you want to get into the thick of combat standing toe to toe with your animal companion, then this is the guide for you.


Disclaimer: Of course this is my opinion and weighted by suggestions of others as they come up. I will change anything based on feedback if it changes my opinion. As always, check with your DM to verify that anything I suggest is allowed or works the way I suggest. Every table is a little different than the next.


Here is the color-guide used for the guide.  
Purple - Highly recommended, some of the best options out there.
Blue - Awesome you can’t go wrong making sure you chose these options.
Green - Good.  These are the filler pieces that round out the character. 
Orange - OK options. These are personal preference, but I’d take green options first.  
Red - This is bad.  Don’t waste character points on this.
Black - Black is used when I feel that something can not be rated or will be rated separately later in the guide.


ROLES
Hunters can fill a variety of roles. Out main role in this guide is that of a front-line melee combatant. We want to take down our opponents as quickly as we can. Access to two characters, and thus two health pools, also allows us to play a tanky role and soak up some of the damage for the team. 


We also have a variety of support spells in our arsenal. Being a 6th level caster, we are somewhat limited in our casting compared to a full caster, especially the druid.

ATTRIBUTES
Dex vs Str: (16-18) (two weapon vs 2 hand) 
One of these stats should be purple, but which one is up to you. A high strength character will likely use a 2-handed weapon, while a dexterity character will use two weapon fighting. 


2-handed fighting is the style that does more damage per hit (and overall) and is less likely to miss. This also means that your attacks of opportunity will do more damage than the attacks of opportunity with two weapon fighting. 2-handed also has less necessary feats. 


The main advantage of two weapon fighting is that you have more chances to score critical hits. This will give your animal companion more attacks of opportunity with outflank. This is a very feat intensive fighting style and requires acquiring multiple weapons as opposed to focusing on one. 


Strength (10-14)- If using two weapon fighting, strength is not that important. It does increase your damage per hit, but only a little bit for each modifier. Your damage will come from criticals (allowing your animal companion to attack more often), passive damage bonuses (sneak attack and favored enemy) and enchants like flaming or holy.


Dexterity (12-16) - Even when not your main your main combat stack dexterity has a few advantages. First it gives you a bonus to your armor level, which is important in the thick of combat. Probably more importantly, it can give you more attacks of opportunity with combat reflexes. 


Constitution (10-14)- Constitution is health. As a front line fighter, you will need as much health as possible. Nothing else really relies on this stat, as far as the class is concerned. 


Intelligence (10 or 13)- If you want to pick up the feat pack flanking you will need 13 intelligence as a prereq. This means 13 Int is ideal as pack flanking is a strongly recommended feat. If you don’t want pack flanking 10 int is fine, we start with 6 skills per level.


Wisdom (12-16)- Wisdom is important to a hunter as it is our casting stat. This means that our bonus spells, our difficulty checks, and our possible spells cast are all determined by wisdom. We are not a primary caster so our DC’s being low is not much of a problem. Eventually we need 16 wisdom to cast our highest level of spells. Technically we could do this with a +6 wisdom headband and 10 base wisdom, but that’s kinda silly. I wouldn’t suggest less than 12 in this stat if you can help it.


Charisma (8-10)- The dump stat. The only use of charisma is to use social skills. Even then unless you are maxing them out intelligence is more important.


Suggested 20 Point buys (before racials):
16 str, 13 dex, 13 con, 13 int, 13 wis, 8 cha - This gives you a strong starting point for a 2-handed hunter. You will probably want to put a point into each con, dex, then wisdom as you level up. 


13 str, 16 dex, 13 con, 13 int, 13 wis, 8 cha - This is a strong starting point for a two weapon hunter. You can raise either strength or constitution before the other in this build and raise wisdom third. 
RACES
Note: What we are mostly looking at is stats and a few other perks. A race that can buff your chosen stat is probably a good place to start. There are some other interesting race perks too, but a lot of the additional perks are situational and based on your campaign and play group. 


Dwarf - A boost to con and wisdom are both pretty good, but not necessary. You don’t lose out on any good stats to get these though. I feel the favored class bonus is completely useless.


Elf - You get a Dex bonus, but suffer a Con penalty. The int bonus is ok, but we don’t need higher than 13. This is a viable option to anyone using two weapon fighting, but one of the floating bonus races would probably be a better option. The favored class bonus for elf is kinda hard to evaluate. 


Gnome - While they do less damage due to being small, they have a higher attack and better AC. That being said gnomes also have a penalty to strength and while a bonus to con might make up for it some, the bonus to charisma is pointless. However the damage reduction to our animal companion for a favored class bonus is nice. 


Half-Elf - The first floating attribute bonus race. The half-elf favored class bonus is an extra 5ft. movement to our animal companion every 5 levels. While this takes a long time to take effect, it can be pretty strong on a slower animal. 


Halfling- Blue because it’s the better option for small races (would be green overall). The bonus to dex is better than con. The strength loss still hurts, but they can make viable two weapon users. The 1/4th bonus to saves for a favored class bonus is alright.


Human- Another floating attribute bonus. While humans are typically the best race due to their extra feat, for this class I suggest trading it out. Eye for talent gives us the ability to give our animal companions +2 to any ability score. Getting our animal companions to 3 intelligence means we can give them any feat we want and they can put ranks into any skill (less important than the feat but still a perk). Or we can put the +2 into strength and give our animal companions more damage and attack. Whether this is worth a feat is up to the player, but I think it is. 


Half-Orc - Another race where you get to pick your attribute buff. The racial talent Shaman Enhancement for half-orc grants +2 str, dex, or con for your animal companion (also grants you +2 spellcraft). It’s not quite eye for talent, but it does make half-orcs a pretty good option on its own. Half-Orcs can also pick up toothy to gain a bite attack opening them up for the teamwork feat Snapping Flank.


Other Races:
There are too many races to go over them all, but if you see a race you want to play, play it. If it grants you some cool effect with your animal companion or grants your ability points in the stats you want, even better. If it’s a bad option, playing a race you enjoy is better than a boring race that’s good.

FEATURES
Animal Companion- This is why we are playing this class. Not really much else to say.


Spells- We have a large spell list that gives us almost anything we want. I love spontaneous spellcasting as well. We are not a 9th level casting class though, and it isn’t the focus of the class/build.


Animal Focus- Animal focus is an amazing ability that grants us boons to our main attributes. Even though they don’t stack with belts of whatever the fact that we can change to another useful stat when we get the belts allows it to remain a viable ability. Even when we have a belt giving us +6 to str/dex/con we can still find focuses like aspect of the stag giving us +20 movement. Add to this that all these buffs are available to our animal companion as well. This is one of the reasons that hunter is the best animal companion class.


Nature Training, Wild Empathy, Track, Speak with Master, and One with the wild-- These are all flavor in my opinion rather than class features. 


Precise Companion / Outflank- this is the core ability of this class build. This ability lets our animal companion attack every time we crit and vice versa. It also gives us an extra +2 to attack when we are flanking our opponent with our animal companion.


Hunter Tactics- This is also a great skill. Knowing that someone in our party will always have our teamwork feats allows them to actually be useful. It also lets our teammates take some teamwork feats to join in on the fun. 


Teamwork Feat- This is a great addition to hunter tactics, but it isn’t all that necessary. There are only a few must have teamwork feats for this class build and we could pick them up naturally if we need to, but free stuff is always a boon if you ask me.


Improved Empathic Link / Greater Empathic Link- Another flavorful ability that has no real implications.


Woodland Stride- I guess some people may use this if they play in a campaign where it’s relevant. I never have.


Second Animal Focus- The first animal focus is amazing and I love having more. That being said the secondary choice I’ll pick with this isn’t going to be as useful as the first, so I feel I must rate it slightly lower.  


Raise Animal Companion- Very dependant on your playgroup, but if your animal companion is likely to die then this will obviously be useful.


Master Hunter- Let’s face it we are only talking about the permanent aspect that we can now use on ourself. It’s fine, but probably not necessary at this point.

FEATS
There are many feats we can go over, but I’m going to focus on some of the best and some of the biggest traps for our Outflanking Hunter. I’m going to organize these into 5 categories: Teamwork feats that we can pick up for free every three levels, feats for our 2-handed hunters, feats for our two weapon hunters, and then feats that can be utilized by both. Lastly I’ll talk about feats for your animal companion.


Teamwork Feats
Broken wing Gambit: This is a risky move. We don’t have a lot of health to spare. Still, if we find ourselves in later levels needing more attacks of opportunity this is an option.


Coordinate Maneuvers: This is ok for those who focus on some sort of maneuver build, like grappling or tripping.


Escape Route: This is awesome for moving around the field of combat, it’s even better if you are playing a build that rides your animal companion.


Pack Flanking: This requires some hoops to jump through (combat expertise and 13 int), but always flanking opponents who you and your pet threaten is worth most of those hoops. It’s even better if you are riding your animal companion.


Gang up: This feat requires an additional ally in combat with your opponent. Overall this is subpar to pack flanking and still requires the same prereq’s. May be rated higher if pack flanking wasn’t available, but as it is this isn’t a feat worth taking.


Paired opportunist: This feat should be picked up as soon as possible. We are trying our best to create attacks of opportunity and creating twice as many when we succeed is very nice. On top of that it gives us +4! to our attacks of opportunity! Seriously, ASAP!


Precise Strike: An extra 1d6 damage per hit is nice. Kind of a filler feat once you have the other ones you really want.


Seize the Moment: This is a great feat! But, it’s a trap because we already have this feature in the strictly better Outflank. You cannot receive two attacks of opportunity for the same action, so it does not stack. 


Improved Spell Sharing: Many of our buffs last much longer than we could possibly need. Splitting the duration with our animal companion is a good idea. There is a cheesy exploit that lets us trade our most recent teamwork feat for this one, cast our spells, then trade back. This basically gives us this feat for free while not in combat. (check with your group if the cheese level is appropriate) 


Improved Outflank: This is a third party feat, but if it’s allowed take it. An additional +2 to attack and causing opponents to be flat footed when you score a crit is very strong. It’s worth asking about at least.
Improved Outflank (official version): On the other hand the official version is another way to make it easier to flank your opponent. Now instead of only flanking your opponent when you are on opposite sides of them from an ally you can flank them from 1 space away from that space. Honestly, I don’t find this to be all that useful. If you can get one space away, you can probably easily get to where you would flank them normally. It doesn’t help mounted hunters at all. The only advantage vs pack flanking is not needing combat expertise.


Snapping Flank: This feat is awesome, but you need to find a way to gain a bite attack first. If you have that option, pick this up as well.


Ferocious Loyalty: This is a +1 attack bonus most of the time. It’s on par with Weapon focus, but a little better due to giving your animal companion the feat as well and being able to use it on multiple weapon types.


2-handed weapon Feats
Power Attack: Even though we are only a ¾ Base attack bonus class this is one of our best ways to increase our damage. We get a +4 bonus most of the time due to flanking (we’re nearly always flanking) which can really help make up for the attack penalty.


Weapon Focus: Another +1 attack is useful, but not a feat I would take over most of the other options. (Take Ferocious Loyalty first)


Cleave: This is a nice feature if you do pick it up, but it’s not something we want to focus on. We want to gang up on our opponent, not get surrounded. Also being surrounded and taking a -2 to AC isn’t my favorite idea. 


Great Cleave: Again we don’t really want to be put into a situation where we can hit three or more targets without moving, because that means they can hit us back. If you can find a way to make this work, go for it. Also doesn’t come into play until level 10. 


Combat Reflexes: Special note this only comes online when you have 14 or higher dex. Buffed stats count, so it’s a good one to pick up around level 7. It’s also very dependant on how many times per round you are triggering attacks of opportunity. Even with a 18-20 crit range that’s only 15% of the time. Take this feat, but not too early. If you start to see missed opportunities it’s time to pick this up. Knowing me I will pick it up as soon as I have the dex to make it useable because missed opportunities will drive me nuts.


Two Weapon Fighting Feats
Weapon Finesse: Assuming you have higher dexterity than strength, This is your first mandatory feat tax for two weapon fighting. If your strength is close to your dex (say 14 vs 16) it’s not as necessary, but if you have 18 dex and 10 strength this becomes an absolute must.


Power Attack: Don’t take this as a two weapon fighter. You already have enough penalties that you have to find ways to make up for. Compared to a 2-weapon fighter who gets +3 damage for every -1 attack, a two weapon fighter only gets an average of 1.5 damage per -1 attack. This isn’t worth it.


Two Weapon Fighting: Without this feat, when fighting with two light weapons you take a -4 to attack with your primary weapon and -8 to attack with your off-hand. This reduces that to -2/-2. Without this feat, you cannot realistically fight with two weapons. 


Improved Two Weapon Fighting: This is the feat that grants you an iterative attack with your off-hand weapon. The point that this becomes viable is somewhere around level 11 where you have stacked enough bonuses your iterative attacks can hit. 


Combat Reflexes: Combat reflexes is less powerful for a two weapon fighter than a 2-handed fighter because you do less damage per hit. That being said you will want it anyway when you are gaining more than one opportunity to make an attack of opportunity each round.


Weapon Focus: Raising attack is a little more important on a two weapon fighter than a 2-handed fighter due to the negatives to attack. Not a high priority feat, but get it when you can.


Feats for any Outlanking Hunter
Toughness: An extra health per level is equivalent to increasing constitution by 2. Being that we playing where the damage is flowing this is not a bad idea. There are other, more flashy abilities that I would rather pick up though.


Improved Initiative: This feat has always bothered me, but some swear by it. I’m not one of those people. If you are playing in a game where you and your companion do not take your turn together you should almost always hold your turn to take them together anyway.


Improved critical: This is a really strong advantage for your character who wants to crit as much as possible. The feat has less value due to being able to do the same thing with spells and enchantments on your weapon. Taking this feat lets you use those resources elsewhere.


Dodge: +1 to your AC is kind of weak for a feat. It’s fine filler, but there are better options.


Boon Companion: This feat opens up many options, specifically multiclassing and archetypes. I want to make this purple, but I realize that many people won’t want to take this route. I’ll talk more about the advantages in the multiclass section below.


Lightning Reflex, Iron Will, and Great Fortitude: An extra 10% chance to make your saves is fine, but somewhat situational. This may be more dependant on your playgroup or the adventure you are playing in. Iron will is the best even with our higher will save, because failing will saves is worse that fort or reflex fails.


Butterfly Sting: This is a very powerful feat if you get your animal companion to hit harder that you. Don’t take it without also taking paired opportunist as that is the only way for your crit to still give your animal companion another attack. (this rating assumes you and your companion take your turns together)


Totem Beast: This allows one Animal Aspect to be granted to our Animal Companion at all times. It uses our Animal Companion’s HD instead of our level so it’s a little weaker than our class feature, but still pretty darn good.


Planar Focus: This feat opens up our Animal Focus class feature to 10 new options. The best of which is probably the fire aspect granting our animal companion so it deals an extra 1d6 damage per 4 levels.


Spirit Gift: This feat grants your choice of a few abilities for 1 min./ level to your animal companion. The most notable are DR 5 and fast healing 1. It’s a cool little boon to your animal companion.


Evolved Companion: 1 point of evolution for the low low price of needing 13 charisma. If for some reason you already have 13 charisma (rolled dice, rp reasons?) then it would maybe be a green feat. There are a lot of options, but likely not worth a full feat on your character.


Nature’s Soul / Animal Ally: A build that takes a little longer to mature, but can be really effective. Take this at level 5 in any class you want. Followed up with boon companion and dip into hunter as you like to follow this guide with most of the boons of another class. Feat intensive, but could lead to a fun build.


Feats for our animal companions
Combat Reflexes: The entire goal of this class option is to get as many attacks with our companion as possible. This is a very important feat to get at some point. 


Improved Natural Attack: One of the few ways of increasing your animal companions damage without sacrificing anything. It really depends on your animal companions damage from said natural attack. Going from a d6 to a d8 is an average one point of damage. It is a little better after multiple increases to your companions size are taken into consideration 4d6 to 6d6 is an increase of 7 damage. 


Improved Natural Armor / Dodge: 1 AC is fine, somewhat of a filler feat though.


Armor Proficiency (light): A character who wears armor without proficiency takes the armor's armor check penalty on attack rolls as well as on all Dex/Str based skill checks. Chain shirt has a -2 penalty, and leather has a 0 penalty. This means anyone can wear leather without having proficiency with no penalty. If you want that extra 2 armor from upgrading to chain shirt, you can make the shirt mithril and have 0 penalty. Even without this trick it’s at best a +2 to attack at best. 


Armor Proficiency (Medium): Assuming I convinced you to not take light armor prof. Don’t spend two feat for this one. It grants your Animal Companion about 2 armor at the cost of 10ft. of movement. Instead pick up dodge and Natural armor if you need extra armor. Heavy armor follows suit, but is even worse.


Power Attack: One of the strongest ways to increase damage. If you have an animal companion with one attack (such as bite) then they get a +3 damage for every one point loss in attack. Combining this with your character taking butterfly sting is exceptionally awesome.


Weapon Focus: Granting a +1 to attack is useful if you are having trouble hitting. Most of their attack is going to come from stacking strength as high as possible.


Dirty Fighting: This feat is great for any animal companion trying to make use of combat maneuvers. This also opens up all the improved (Trip, grapple, etc.) feats.  


Improved Trip/Grapple/Etc.: I really like the animal companions that can do extra on their attacks. Tripping is probably my favorite. These feats allow the maneuvers to stay relevant longer. I’d only focus on one though. This naturally leads to the greater maneuver feat for your chosen feat. These grant you something extra when doing the chosen maneuver. For example, Greater Trip grants you an attack of opportunity when you successfully trip your opponent.


Toughness: Extra health is nice, but not very flashy. I’d rather find a way to raise con by two. Note that this is based on HD, so at 7 you get one less bonus than you would on your character, increasing to 2 less at 11 and so on.

Archetypes and Dual Classing
Once you have all the teamwork feats you want, there isn’t a whole lot of useful features in the last few levels of the class. The downside to multiclassing in this class is that your animal companion will fall behind. This can be overcome by picking a class with an animal companion or with the boon companion feat (up to 4 levels). 


If you are playing all the way to 20, the only way to get your 3rd iterative attack is to take 4 levels of a full base attack bonus class. Most full B.A.B. Classes are good options.


Also of note is the level at which you want to get hunter. Level 8 gets you the middle buff of all the animal aspects, level 15 gets them to the highest level. Level 16 gets you your first level 6 spell, also 16 means that you can take 4 levels in any class and boon companion will cover the animal companion level loss. 


Under most conditions, I would suggest taking 3 levels in hunter before any other class Beyond that is personal preference.


Ranger- Ranger is the second class that comes to mind when talking about animal companions (after Druid which does not multiclass well with hunter). A 10 Hunter/10 Ranger will end up with a base attack bonus of 17 (enough for the third iterative attack) and with boon companion, you can still have a full strength companion. You are basically 5th and 6th level spells for some combat feats and a higher Base Attack Bonus. This is good for two weapon fighters as they are highly feat intensive and need the extra attack.


Fighter- With boon companion the most you can go into fighter is 4 levels and keep a top tier animal companion. The feat you use is made up for (and then some) by 4 levels of fighter. 4 Levels of fighter won’t lower spell levels available any (less uses though), but will grant you 3 bonus combat feats and 1 higher Base Attack Bonus. 


Barbarian- There is an archetype, Mounted Fury, that grants an animal companion and gives your animal companion a small rage boost. It still requires using the boon companion feat to keep at full power, but allows you to take more levels in Barbarian if you’d like. If you only want 10 levels in hunter and don’t need the extra feats from Ranger then this is a solid option. (also a pretty cool thematic class if you plan to mount your Animal Companion). There is a rage using teamwork feat (Amplified Rage) that is really good, but only available to orc/ half-orcs, so their race goes up in value if you plan to go this route. (especially consult your DM for this one though as by RAW you can only get a horse or camel as your mount)


Bloodrager- Again there is a mounted combat archetype: Bloodrider. Everything I just said about the barbarian and a few arcane spells to cast.


Brawler- Brawlers get some extra combat feats and can access one for a min/level as it’s needed. One of the largest advantages is the class negates the need for 13 int. You will still need combat expertise to pick up pack flanking. Wild Child (archetype) lets you go further into the class (or avoid taking boon companion feat).


Cavalier- This was suggested as a good option, but I’m not entirely familiar with the Cavalier. It does get a mount which stack in levels with your animal companion. Also there is an archetype called Mother’s Fang that grants Combat Expertise without the need for 13 int.


Unchained Rogue- If you are looking to be a dual wielding hunter, this is one of your best options. The additional damage per hit from sneak attack really help to make up for the lack of damage from low strength. Add to that dex to damage at level 3 letting you drop strength. Pick up two keen kukris and go to town. 


Mammoth Rider- This is an awesome, on theme prestige class. If it fits your campaign (giant animal companions may not work in dungeons) then this is a viable prestige class. 


Evangelist- As long as the flavor for evangelist fits your campaign this prestige class loses almost nothing for some nice added perks. 


Divine hunter (archetype)- Trading out teamwork stuff for a domain and the celestial template? I’ll pass. You still get outflank, but there are a few teamwork feats I’d rather have than a domain. 


Feral Hunter (Archetype)- Trade our animal companion? NO!


Packmaster (Archetype)- My absolute Favorite Archetype. I’ve seen others call it a trap and find that to be a little crazy. There are two ways to play this. One is to have 1 Animal Companion with boon companion applied to it and have 1-3 utility companions (a hawk to scout, 2 ferrets to steal things). The other way to play this is to take Boon Companion twice and have 2 combat Animal Companions. These Companions are at full power until 8th level. If you are running a campaign to 12th level (many adventure paths) then that is 2/3rds of the adventure. At 10th level they are level -1 and at 12th they are level -2. If nothing else at level at level 8 you can retire your second animal companion (replacing it with a low level utility companion) and keep leveling your primary companion as normal. What do you lose for doing this? Nothing! Every feature simply modifies the original feature to work with this archetype if you want to. You could build the core class as written with this archetype if you want.


Primal Companion Hunter (Archetype)- This archetype trades Animal Focus for the ability to grant your companion temporary evolution points. I don’t like that this feature is temporary, only lasting 1 min/ level, but it’s a decent option. It gets much better if you have good access to magical items as animal focus doesn’t stack with a belt of giant strength (and such), while this will stack with magic items. 


Verminous Hunter (Archetype)- I’m putting this here for completionist sake, but I don’t know how to rate it. On the surface it looks like a normal Hunter with some different flavor. There are some interesting Vermin Companions like the wasp or mantis if you want to go this route. The Archetype also has a lot more options for animal focus.

How to pick your Animal Companion 
As with race selection the most important choice when selecting an animal companion is selecting one that you like and fits your character/story. If you are open to multiple options then there are some things you can look for.


Multiple Attacks: Having multiple attacks (for example a cat with bite and two claws) is typically better on an animal companion, especially with pounce to grant full attacks on a charge. On an outflanking hunter it is of note that the attacks of opportunity will do less damage per swing, but having more attacks that don’t rely on attacks of opportunity outweighs this difference. Some animal companions can stack these attacks even higher by having more natural weapons or taking evolutions to gain more natural attacks.


One Natural Attack: The best way to maximize the damage of attacks of opportunity is to have one natural attack. This way the animal companions get 1.5 times the strength bonus added to their damage. There are still ways to get more than one attack during a full attack such as the multiattack trade out at level 9 granting your companion an iterative attack. Single natural attacks also gain the most advantage from the spell magic fang. 


Damage Comparison: Large Cat vs Wolf. The two claw attacks adding to his bite attack from the pouncing cat leads to the most damage. 7 higher damage at 10 strength. As strength gets higher the damage gap increases by 1.5 per 2 str (cat gets +3 damage wolf gets +1.5). For every extra bite (AoO, Haste, Snapping Flank, etc) the damage gap decreases by .5 per strength modifier (the base damage of the bites cancel each other so just the strength bonus matters). 


Thus, A single natural attack starts to break even with three extra attacks. Not taking damage reduction into account at this point the Cat is still doing 7 higher damage.


Damage Reduction always lowers the Cat’s lead by 2 per point of damage reduction. For this exercise let’s call that inconsequential. 


The wolf deals more damage when the animal companion will hit with 4 or more extra bite attacks. The Cat will do more damage when the animal companion will hit with 2 or less extra bites.


This does not take into consideration the iterative attack that the wolf picks up at 9. Assuming it hits with the iterative (25% less chance), this closes the gap to 2.5 damage with no extra attacks and str breaks even. Every extra bite attack will pull the Wolf into the lead further and further. The wolf will not get pounce though, so getting to enemies may cancel this attack’s bonus at times. 


More attacks, such as an Velociraptor or evolutions, further throw this calculation in favor of the multi- attacking animal companion. 


Natural Armor: some animal companions such as the boar, have high natural armor making them exceptionally hard to hit. This is especially good if you hope to hide behind your animal companion. 


Trip and Grab: Animal Companions who add combat moves onto their attacks give more options while playing to control the battlefield. Tripping an opponent means you (usually) take them out of combat for a round and get an extra attack of opportunity as they try to stand up. Grapple condition means they have to use their turn to escape the grapple and/or must focus on the grappling animal companion. You can also move at half speed while grappling an opponent so repositioning is a thing here. 


Rideable Companions: Using this class build as a mounted combat option is very viable. Pack Flanking and Outflank all work from your companion’s back. An Animal Companion needs to be one size category larger than you to be an effective mount. 


Fly, Swim, and Climb Speeds: These are somewhat dependant on your campaign, but it’s always nice to have some extra forms of movement. Being able to fly, especially on a mounted animal companion is a huge boon. I can’t imagine playing in a pirate campaign with an animal companion who can’t fly or swim.
