# Class Features

Class Features


        (1) Spells and Knacks: The mesmerist is a ⅔ spontaneous psychic spellcaster that focuses on illusion and enchantment magic to trick and beguile their foes. The rules text here is absolutely, 100% boilerplate for spontaneous casters, so I’m not going to do a deep dive on these particular class features. Your spell save DC will be equal to 10 + Spell Level + Charisma modifier, and you get to swap out some of your spells starting at 5th level. Everything else is normal spontaneous caster stuff: save your spells known for the absolute best-of-the-best spells at any given level, and dedicate wands or scrolls to the rest where you need versatility. This is especially important for the mesmerist, who (unlike many other ⅔ casters) does not have any favored class bonuses that provide bonus spells known.


        (1) Consummate Liar: Consummate liar works on a couple different levels. First, and most obviously, this class feature allows you to lie like a boss. If that baker is concerned about who stole the pie from the windowsill, it certainly wasn’t you—you would never do something like that, honest!—but you think you saw a figure in a cloak grabbing it a few minutes ago. You get the gist: mesmerists are even better liars, bluffers, blavers, cheats, malingerers, cardsharps, crooks, con men, and swindlers than rogues are, so hop to it. The second interaction that consummate liar sets up is one avenue for how the class can regularly hit enemies in combat: feinting. Unlike many other ¾-BAB classes, the mesmerist doesn’t actually get many buffs to attack rolls, whether from spells or class features. Investigators have studied combat, inquisitors have judgment and bane, magi have their arcane pool, etc., but none of your bold stares and none of your mesmerist tricks make enemies any easier to hit! One solution is to feint enemies in order to make them lose their Dexterity bonus to AC. This might be a small bonus to hit in some cases, but against rogues, swashbucklers, and monsters with levels in other classes that are similarly light on their feet, you’ll definitely be helping the team out—and yourself in the process—a great deal. Many times, however, the Blinding Stare feat will do a better job of making enemies unable to competently defend against your attacks.


        (1) Hypnotic Stare (Su): The signature ability of the mesmerist, and the one that you’ll use most consistently in and out of combat for your entire career, hypnotic stare allows you to impose a penalty on an enemy’s Will save while you focus your psychic power on them. At later levels, hypnotic stare also accepts upgrades called bold stares that allow you to stack additional rider effects on top of that initial -2 to Will saves. Here’s the rundown:


A mesmerist can focus his stare on one creature within 30 feet as a swift action. That creature takes a –2 penalty on Will saving throws. This penalty changes to –3 at 8th level. A mesmerist can maintain his stare against only one opponent at a time; it remains in effect until the mesmerist stares at a new target, the opponent dies, the opponent moves farther than 30 feet away, or the mesmerist falls unconscious or dies. The mesmerist can remove the memory of his stare from the target’s mind; the creature doesn’t remember that it was affected (nor does it realize that it is currently being affected) unless the mesmerist allows it. The hypnotic stare is a psychic effect, and relies more on the mesmerist’s focus than the target’s perception of his stare. It can’t be avoided in the same ways a gaze attack can. The mesmerist can use this ability even while blinded, but must succeed at a DC 20 concentration check to do so. Staring at a creature requires the mesmerist’s focus, so if he uses a gaze attack or similar ability, he must target the subject of his hypnotic stare or voluntarily end the stare. The penalties from multiple mesmerists’ stares don’t stack, nor do they stack with penalties from witches’ evil eye hexes. This is a mind-affecting effect.


        Some salient points here: first, hypnotic stare is mind-affecting, which means that undead, vermin, mindless constructs, and other beings that are immune to mind-affecting effects simply will not take any penalties at all from this class feature. The exception to this rule is if you take the Psychic Inception bold stare—which, by the way, is 1000% required for mesmerists, as the class can’t pull nearly as much weight as other classes if its main debuffing route is blocked off. Second, you can only stare at creatures that are within 30 feet, and if they run or get far enough away from you, the stare will break automatically. The mesmerist’s weapon proficiencies are poorly suited to ranged combat, and this point further complicates the process of building a ranged mesmerist. Third, you can only stare at one creature at a time, so save it for the biggest, baddest enemy on the field. Fourth, creatures don’t know they’re under the effects of a hypnotic stare unless you want them to. This is very important to remember! There’s no save and no spell resistance check needed to achieve this effect—if you don’t want them to know, then they don’t know. Combined with the fact that psychic spellcasting has no verbal or somatic components, and mesmerists more than any other class are capable of tanking someone’s Will save, casting some kind of enchantment spell, and getting an enemy under their direct control without the person ever becoming aware of the fact that they’re being manipulated. I love it when mechanics and flavor align.


        (1) Mesmerist Tricks (Su): As far as I can tell, mesmerist tricks are a bit unpopular among Pathfinder players. While that’s regrettable, it is true that utilizing mesmerist tricks effectively (much like utilizing teamwork feats effectively) requires a fairly high degree of both systems mastery on the part of the mesmerist player and cooperation/communication among party members. Much like the hypnotic commands given by real hypnotists (or “real” hypnotists, depending on who you ask) each mesmerist trick has a trigger condition and a consequent effect. My recommendation to any aspiring mesmerist would be to write down your available tricks on notecards that explicitly state the trigger condition and the consequent effect. From there, you can give a copy of the notecard to the person you’ve implanted the trick in while keeping a copy for yourself, hopefully ensuring that one of you remembers to use the trick in the relevant situation. There’s very little in the world that feels worse than implanting a trick, getting pasted by a monster, and later realizing that a trick that you forgot was active could have saved you.


A mesmerist can create hypnotic bonds with his allies, implanting magical suggestions in their minds that he can later activate. Each day, he can implant a number of these tricks equal to 1/2 his mesmerist level (minimum 1) plus his Charisma bonus (if any). He can have only one trick implanted at a given time, and implanting a new trick ends the previous one (the mesmerist still loses the use of this ability he spent on the previous trick).


        Pretty simple. Your total number of tricks per day will range from about 4 at 1st level to the high teens or low 20s at 20th level, but you can keep all these tricks to yourself, distribute them all to allies, or do some combination of both. Until you reach 5th level, though, you can only have one trick implanted in any given person.


To implant a trick, the mesmerist must take a standard action and either touch a willing creature or implant the trick in himself. A creature can be the subject of only one mesmerist trick at a time. The mesmerist can activate the trick as a free action when a triggering condition is met (as defined in the trick’s description), even if it isn’t his turn. The subject must be within medium range (100 feet + 10 feet per level) for the mesmerist to trigger the trick. The mesmerist monitors for the trick’s triggering condition through a subtle telepathic connection, so he doesn’t need line of sight to trigger it—but anything that blocks telepathic contact prevents him from triggering tricks. An implanted trick lasts until the next time the mesmerist regains his spells.


        Implanting a trick is a standard action—better done outside of combat, in other words—but tricks can be activated as free actions no matter whose turn it is. The range on trick activation is much better than for your hypnotic stare, but still not long enough to help someone who’s at long range. The clause about telepathic connections shouldn’t be relevant unless you’re regularly being targeted by effects like telepathic censure or telepathic silence, but it is very useful to know that you don’t have to have line of sight to an ally in order to activate a trick.


Once triggered, a trick is no longer implanted, and can’t be triggered again until the mesmerist implants the trick again. The duration of the effect caused by triggering a trick is either instantaneous or appears in the trick’s entry. The DC for any mesmerist trick or masterful trick that requires a saving throw or skill check is 10 + 1/2 the mesmerist’s level + the mesmerist’s Charisma modifier.


        This paragraph doesn’t bring any revelations with it. I think you probably knew that tricks went away after they got triggered, and the text of the tricks themselves makes it pretty clear that most of them are instantaneous effects.


The mesmerist knows one trick at 1st level, and learns another trick at 2nd level and every 2 levels thereafter (to a maximum of 11 tricks at 20th level). Each daily use of mesmerist tricks can be used to implant any trick the mesmerist knows. Unless specifically noted in the mesmerist trick’s description, a mesmerist can’t choose a particular trick more than once.


Your arsenal of “tricks known” is a little limited to begin with, and there’s no feat that will teach you a new trick. Choose carefully!


(1) Painful Stare (Su): Painful stare is the source of most of this class’ damage, and functions in much the same way for mesmerists as sneak attack functions for rogues. Since you don’t get access to any high-damage martial weapons, and since precision damage from painful stare doesn’t multiply on a critical hit, there are generally few reasons to try to build a crit-fishing or Strength-based single-class mesmerist—you’re just playing against your own strengths. If you’re willing to dip into or out of mesmerist, that calculus will likely change a bit, but we’ll go over that in more depth in the dipping section.


When an attack that deals damage hits the target of a mesmerist’s hypnotic stare, the mesmerist can cause the target to take an amount of additional damage equal to 1/2 the mesmerist’s class level (minimum 1). The mesmerist can use this ability as a free action, and can use it even if it isn’t his turn. If the mesmerist uses this ability to increase his own damage, the additional damage increases by 1d6 points for every 3 class levels the mesmerist possesses. This damage is precision damage and is not multiplied on a critical hit. A mesmerist can trigger this ability only once per round, but a single creature can take damage from multiple mesmerists’ painful stares in a round.


Okay, well, pretty much all of this is important. Sorry. The key here is that you can activate painful stare whether or not you’re the one hitting an enemy. You get a decisive damage boost if it’s you that lands the blow, but that’s not strictly necessary—you can activate it as a free action when one of your colleagues hits an enemy, too. Unfortunately, you can only trigger painful stare once per round (although the the Manifold Stare feat changes this!), so my advice would be to attempt to trigger it on your own attacks, and if you miss every attack, trigger it on the next successful attack you can. Your damage output will definitely lag unless you’re consistently proc’ing this class feature, so make it a priority to lay painful stare down on whichever enemy the team seems to be focusing on most consistently. If the damage scaling is difficult to parse based on the wording, just know that painful stare triggers for allies will range from 1 damage (1st level) to 10 damage (20th level), or 1 damage (1st level) to 6d6+10 damage (20th level) if you’re the one triggering the stare.


(2) Towering Ego (Su): Oof, towering ego is so good it hurts. Psychic classes live in constant fear of emotion and mind-affecting effects punching through their Will saves and negating their emotion components, so the fact that you can add your Charisma modifier to (that’s in addition to, not instead of) your Wisdom modifier makes all the difference in the world. If you’re ever scared, though, you’re really scared, and losing your towering ego bonuses will make it that much harder to defend against other mental incursions.


(3) Bold Stare (Su): For my money, bold stare is the single most important class feature of the mesmerist. Sure, tricks are fun, and sure, a -2 or -3 penalty to Will saves is alright, but layering more debuffs on top of hypnotic stare is what really turns you into a supercharged single-target debuffing machine. Note that all of your bold stares stack, so hop down to the bold stares section and go shopping.


(3) Touch Treatment (Su): Personally, I love touch treatment. It’s an easy class feature for archetypes to give up, but I would try to keep it if at all possible—many of the status conditions on the moderate and greater condition lists wreck encounters, and you never know when the ability to break enchantment at will will come in handy. There’s not a lot of interesting rules text here: just remember that you get 3 + your Charisma modifier uses per day, and that you can touch yourself as a swift action (I’ll just bet you can, nod nod nudge nudge wink wink) and you’ll be fine.


(5) Manifold Tricks (Su): Increasing the number of mesmerist tracks that you can have implanted in yourself or your allies at the same time can only do good things. There’s nothing specifically forbidding you from implanting multiple instances of the same trick, so communicate with your allies about whether they’d like you to be able to enact the same trick multiple times in the same combat, or whether they’d like some variety.


(5) Mental Potency (Ex): This class feature falls a bit flat, unfortunately. While color spray and similar spells based on HD can go over like gangbusters at low levels, they rarely scale hard enough to be useful beyond, say, about 6th level. Mental potency doesn’t fix that scaling significantly—even if you can affect creatures with 7 or 8 HD with color spray at 20th level, you’re never going to be fighting creatures with so few HD at that level. HD-capped spells are rarely a wise use of resources, so I’m hesitant to give this class feature a good rating.


(11) Glib Lie (Su): Glib lie is a lot of fun in social encounters, and the caster level DC of 15 + your mesmerist level is actually really difficult to beat. In a head-to-head battle between an inquisitor and a mesmerist to see who could tell or detect a lie, I’m honestly not sure who would win, but it’d be a hell of a match-up. Certainly no other class could keep up with your deception abilities.


(12) Masterful Tricks (Su): Masterful tricks are more powerful than normal tricks, but correspondingly more expensive. We’ll get into them in much greater detail later.


(20) Rule Minds (Su): Permanent, disposable thralls: that’s the mesmerist dream, right? As fun as this ability is, I don’t think it’s any better than some of the alternative capstones available to the mesmerist. Let’s get into those, shall we?


Alternate Capstones: But wait, there’s more! Chronicle of Legends introduced alternative capstones that you can take instead of your normal capstone. Each class has one class-exclusive capstone, and then several others that are more general for all characters, all spellcasters, etc. Do note that you can’t select an alternative capstone if your archetype replaces the rule minds class feature. Here are my ratings:


* Perfect Body, Flawless Mind (Ex): PBFM is the correct capstone answer for just about any mesmerist. You can boost Dexterity, for a permanent +4 to AC, Reflex saves, initiative, and potentially attack rolls; Charisma, for a permanent +4 to Will saves, social skill checks, touch treatment, tricks per day, spell save DCs, etc., or some combination of the above. There are few forces more powerful than huge attribute bonuses, so be smart and pick this capstone when you get the chance. Buying inherent bonuses of this magnitude would cost somewhere around 220,000 gp, which beats With This Sword quite handily.
* Piercing Gaze (Su): I’m not convinced that Piercing Gaze is better than PBFM, but it’s quite good—certainly the best non-PBFM option available. What a monster debuffer you’ve become. Welcome to the endgame, my friend. Welcome to ZomboCom.
* With This Sword (Ex): A good runner-up to PBFM, but still inferior to it.
* Deep Magics (Su): Not a worthless alternate capstone, but by no means one of the best. Mesmerists would only get six spells known, anyway.
* The Boss (Ex) and Won’t Stay Dead (Ex): More story points than anything else—fitting sunsets for your characters. They won’t have overwhelming effects in combat.
