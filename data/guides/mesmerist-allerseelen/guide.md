# MES 100: Musings on Mesmerists

MES 100: Musings on Mesmerists


One of the design choices that I made for my original guide to the Pathfinder inquisitor class and subsequently fell away from was to write a section comparing the inquisitor to other, similar classes (paladin, cleric, and warpriest) in order to figure out more about how the inquisitor was mechanically similar to and different from those classes. After spending so much time reviewing classes and Pathfinder 1e content, I’ve also begun to develop a sense of...the spirit of a class that I’m reviewing, I guess you might say: a sense of what the class is strong at, what the class is weak at, how the design decisions that Paizo made on everything from armor and weapon proficiencies all the way down to the specific spells on the class’ spell list inform how the class is played. The Mesmerist Roles section goes into some detail on this, of course, but that section is designed more as a primer on the mechanics of how to put together different builds, not as a philosophical treatise on what a class is. So starting with this guide (and perhaps going back to revise earlier guides at a later date) I’m going to reinstate this section as we ask the question: what are mesmerists?

---

# The Overview

The Overview


A quick-and-dirty understanding of the mesmerist class would perhaps frame them as a combination of the rogue, bard, and witch classes in Pathfinder 1e. This is reductionistic, of course—none of the classes in Pathfinder 1e are all that similar to one another when you really drill and drill and drill down to the bedrock, not even the hybrid classes and their parent classes. But at a glance, there’s a lot to be compared here. Like the rogue, mesmerists have a good number of skill ranks and can expect to deal most of their damage via pools of precision damage d6s from painful stare; like the bard, mesmerists are experts at social interaction, team buffing, and enchantment or illusion magic; and like the witch, mesmerists have a slew of hard-hitting debuffs that can be used all day long in addition to their native spell slots. Each of these classes has a fundamentally different way of playing, and mesmerists somehow have to combine it all into one package.

---

# Dealing Damage

Dealing Damage


Let’s start with a core question for any d8 HD, ¾-BAB, ⅔ spellcasting class: how are you going to deal damage? Variability among mesmerist builds exists, and it is possible to build a Strength-based mesmerist if you make some smart multiclassing choices, but for the most part, mesmerists should plan to deal most of their damage in melee range with light weapons and Weapon Finesse. With only light armor proficiency, single-class mesmerists often have to consider Dexterity builds as their first choices in order to maintain acceptable AC values, but the 30-foot range limitation on hypnotic stare, one of their key debuffs, prevents them from falling too far back out of a fight (as, say, a ranged combatant) unless they’re willing to give up a significant portion of their debuffing power or invest heavily in feats that extend hypnotic stare’s range. For any mesmerist, however, whether Strength-based melee, Dexterity-based melee, or ranged, the class can expect to deal the lion’s share of its damage not through static bonuses to damage such as weapon training (fighter), studied target (slayer), smite evil (paladin), or favored enemy (ranger), but through painful stare, an ability that lets them tag on a pool of precision damage d6s to any hit the mesmerist lands on an enemy. The closest analog to painful stare is sneak attack, but where sneak attack scales up to 10d6 damage by 20th level and can keep on chugging along no matter how many times a rogue hits an enemy in a round, painful stare can—at base—deal only 6d6+10 damage at 20th level...and only once per round. Lacking meaningful ways to buff their damage through spellcasting, mesmerists therefore need to focus on four components if they want their damage output to remain relevant: 1) increasing painful stare damage, so that each hit hurts more; 2) increasing the number of times per round that painful stare can be triggered; 3) increasing the number of attacks the mesmerist can make, as painful stares triggered by allies’ attacks deal far less damage than painful stares triggered by the mesmerist’s own attacks; and 4) circumventing precision damage immunities so that enemies cannot escape your hungry eyes.
Barring favored class bonuses, Problem #1 (increasing painful stare damage) can be solved in precisely one way: the Intense Pain feat, which comes online for single-class mesmerists at 7th level. Intense Pain will increase the painful stare damage your mesmerist PC deals by about 33% over the base ability, and should essentially be your go-to Stare feat once you acquire it. This feat brings your raw damage on single hits to levels comparable with the rogue—although I think we can do a little better than that, don’t you? To deal with our damage output on full-attack actions, we’re going to need to trigger painful stare more than once per round, and that leads us to Problem #2 (triggering painful stare multiple times per round). You’ll solve this problem through the Manifold Stare feat, which you’ll take three times in order to trigger painful stare on every iterative attack (+15/+10/+5) plus the additional attack you receive through common spellcasting buffs. Manifold Stare acts as a force multiplier on Intense Pain, ensuring that we can get in and get ours on each and every full-attack. Now, can you reasonably expect to hit every round for four attacks? No, of course not. But that’s no reason not to plan for it! Some rounds you will hit four times, and you want all four of those attacks to deal as much damage as possible. And if you miss, well, you’ll never regret being able to trigger a Stare feat on an ally’s attack, although Blinding Stare or Confusing Stare become much more attractive choices than Intense Pain when you’re not dealing full sneak attack-level damage. Problem #3 (increasing attacks per round) can be solved easily with common team buffs like haste, channel vigor, or blessing of fervor. Overall, you don’t need to worry much about acquiring extra attacks—between your iteratives acquired through BAB progression and buffs floating around, you’ll generally have to plan more for Problem #2 than Problem #3. There’s no particular reason to drive hard toward other common bonus attacks like Rapid Shot, either, because you’re constrained by the 3rd/9th/15th level Manifold Stare feat progression. Unless you have more painful stare triggers left for the round, any addition attacks will deal piddly damage—you’re not like an inquisitor, where every additional attack stacks greater bane damage ad infinitum. Problem #4 (dealing with precision damage immunity) is...probably difficult. The Psychic Inception bold stare is the biggest single purchase you can make to ensure that painful stare works, as enemies immune to mind-affecting effects are immune to hypnotic stare, and therefore immune to painful stare, as well. “Psychic Inception is a mandatory bold stare improvement for mesmerists” isn’t exactly a hot take, but it’s non-controversial for a reason. The Anatomical Savant feat lets you ignore some percentage of sneak attack immunity, although that feat’s text doesn’t mention precision damage generally, only sneak attack—some GMs might rule that painful stare damage doesn’t qualify. Shadow Strike lets you deal precision damage to creatures with concealment, which you’d be well advised to take if you’re focusing on dealing damage as a mesmerist. Keeping a cheap +1 ghost touch dagger on your person would also be a good idea for dealing with incorporeals. Unlike the rogue, however, you don’t have to flank, blind, demoralize, or attack from Stealth or invisibility in order to deal your precision damage, and in practical terms that will mean that you deal it a lot more consistently than a rogue. A victory for Upfish!

# Tanking Hits

Tanking Hits


        Of course, no amount of offense in the world will help you out unless you survive long enough to bring that offense to bear, right? So we’ve got to think about defense, too. The mesmerist is an odd bird when it comes to defense in that it’s more lightly armored than some ⅔ spellcasting classes (occultist, inquisitor, warpriest, etc.) who get access to at least medium armor, if not heavy. As I mentioned above, single-class mesmerists are almost always going to be locked into Dexterity builds, which tend to have slightly lower AC numbers (at least at early levels) than heavy armor/shield builds. But AC, at least as far as the mesmerist class goes, is actually a bit of a misdirect on Paizo’s part, because the class’ defensive staying power doesn’t primarily come from buffing itself—it comes from debuffing enemies. Now, don’t mistake me: you should never underestimate the power of self-implanted mesmerist tricks and spells like displacement and mirror image to get you out of tight spots. Mesmeric Mirror, Astounding Avoidance, and many other mesmerist tricks are almost purely defensive in nature, and can help you weasel out of soaking whatever stray blows make it to you. By and large, though, the mesmerist has to survive in melee range by reducing enemies into quivering piles of jelly before they get the chance to do any serious harm.
        At the core of this debuffing dynamo is hypnotic stare and the various bold stare improvements you get. Decimating an opponent’s Will save is the first step in landing a spell, offensive mesmerist trick, or painful stare rider effect that will further dismantle a foe, so activating hypnotic stare against the enemy who most threatens you is often the correct defensive tactic. Of course, you’ll need the Psychic Inception bold stare improvement so that you can affect all enemies equally with hypnotic stare, and the Disorientation bold stare improvement makes enemies less likely to hit you even if they’re trying really hard. After that come the fireworks, though: Stare feats such as Blinding Stare and Confusing Stare can be triggered as free actions when you trigger painful stare, and in later levels can even be combined with Compounded Pain; masterful tricks such as Cursed Sanction and Vision of Blood can hand out brutal debuffs; and your spell list is practically bursting at the seams with vicious penalties to foes’ ability to mount successful attacks against you. If you want an easy mnemonic to remember the the basic principles of how mesmerists should stay alive on the battlefield, just think: debuff to defend. Debuff to defend. Debuff to defend.
Against saving throws, mesmerists are actually remarkably hardy! Towering ego is a fantastic class feature that will keep you safe from any number of Will save-based spells and supernatural abilities, and the fact that it protects you from involuntarily losing the emotion components of your psychic spellcasting is just icing on the cake. The Sapped Magic bold stare also fits in with the mesmerist’s organizing principle of debuff to defend by dropping enemy spellcasters’ save DCs. On the trick side, Astounding Avoidance, Gift of Will, and Mental Fallback are all intended as anti-saving throw defenses, and even if you do fail a save, your touch treatment class feature can often shed any status conditions you might have acquired as a swift action, leaving you none the worse for wear. Mesmerists may not have many tools for dealing with Fortitude saves, but against Reflex or Will saves, they’re surprisingly one of the best-defended classes in the game.
So where does all this leave you on defense? Well, it’s fairly clear that mesmerists excel in fights against single targets. Remember, debuff to defend, and the most reliable tools that mesmerists have for that task are hypnotic stare and bold stares (single-target), painful stare rider effects (single-target), offensive mesmerist tricks (single-target), and spells (probably an even mix of single-target and AoE). Against single targets that are valid candidates for mind-affecting effects, there’s very little that mesmerists can’t survive, provided that they’ve been built competently; add even one extra enemy on the field, though, and all of a sudden the prognosis gets much bleaker. Mesmerists are therefore best served by responding decisively and unilaterally to remove minions from a fight so that they can focus all of their single-target debuffing on the biggest, baddest target. Color spray, synaptic pulse, confusion, fear, crushing despair, glitterdust, mass mydriatic spontaneity, mass synesthesia, greater song of discord...the mesmerist spell list is set up for AoE debuffing if you do it right, but you need to act immediately in an encounter so that you can concentrate on what’s important in the room: the boss.

# Casting Spells

Casting Spells


        The mesmerist spell list is built to do four things, and four things only: 1) debuff, 2) debuff, 3) debuff, and 4) social interaction, I guess? Oh, sure, there’s the odd battlefield control spell (mind fog, impossible angles) or self-buff (mirror image, displacement, hermean potential), but on the whole, there is no problem so intractable that the mesmerist spell list thinks it cannot debuff itself out of it. Whether your enemies are rich or poor, young or old, humanoid or non-humanoid, spellcaster or martial, the mesmerist spell list has some nasty tricks up its sleeves tailored just for them. Now, I won’t deny that many of the mesmerist spells simply don’t work against some enemy types—247 out of your 554 total spells are mind-affecting (about 45%), so unless you take the Psychic Inception bold stare improvement, the spirit walker archetype, or some third avenue that I’m not aware of, constructs, vermin, undead, etc. are all going to be pretty unimpressed by your pocket-watch-pendulum act. This reliance on mind-affecting effects is a defining weakness of the mesmerist class, and one that you should plan for both by abilities that allow your mind-affecting effects to function more reliably (e.g., the Psychic Inception bold stare improvement) or by purchasing a variety of spells that don’t feature the mind-affecting tag.
        Speaking of variety, you don’t have much of it. You have 34 abjuration spells, 27 conjuration spells, 66 divination spells, 178 enchantment spells, 14 evocation spells, 137 illusion spells, 49 necromancy spells, and 48 transmutation spells. If you added up all the abjuration, conjuration, divination, evocation, and transmutation spells, you’d still have only a few more spells than your total number of enchantment spells! I go into more detail on which schools of magic I think are most effective for mesmerists down in the Mesmerist Roles section (spoiler: it’s enchantment, illusion, and necromancy), but for now suffice it to say that there are advantages and disadvantages to having such a focused spell list. On the one hand, feats like Spell Focus that apply to only one school of magic are that much more powerful when so many of your spells come from that school, but on the other hand, you’re that much more screwed if you run into enemies who are either immune or highly resistant to your favored school. No matter how specialized you get in enchantment, illusion, necromancy, or, I don’t know, divination, if you’re a masochist, it’s worth remembering the classic investment principle of diversification. If you’re not diversified, there’s gonna come a time when some enemy (let’s be real, it’s probably going to be an ooze) straight-up eats your lunch; if you’re diversified, you stand a chance of adapting and overcoming. Nothing’s a guarantee in Pathfinder, but that advice generally won’t steer you wrong.
        Regarding spell save DCs, mesmerists are actually as strong as full casters (some would say stronger) when they’re able to get someone under the effects of their hypnotic stare and hit them with a Will save-based effect. If you think about it, even though mesmerists get only 6 levels of spells instead of 9, their hypnotic stare class feature shifts their effective spell save DCs up by 2, or by 3 once they hit 8th level. What this means on a practical level is that mesmerists cast spells ranging in power from 4th level to 9th level—even their weakest spells are still pretty dang strong. Toss in the Sluggishness bold stare, and you can add Reflex saves to your list of viable effects. (It should be mentioned, though, that you have far fewer Reflex save-based spells than Will save-based spells.) By the time you’ve finished adding Spell Focus, Greater Spell Focus, racial increases to spell save DCs, etc., mesmerists can make truly fearsome offensive spellcasters. That spellcasting supremacy is predicated on the assumption that you’ve got someone affected by your hypnotic stare, however, which means you’re relegated to single targets only, you must be able to affect them with mind-affecting effects (Psychic Inception, Psychic Inception, Psychic Inception…), and you must stay relatively close to the target in order to maintain your hypnotic stare. Satisfy all those constraints, and mesmerists are every bit as powerful as full casters.

# How’s the Action Economy?

How’s the Action Economy?


Pretty good, thanks very much! Classes generally need to be able to trigger their class features or spells as swift actions in order to be really competitive in the Pathfinder action economy—if everything you do takes a standard action, you’re going to be spending forever getting buffed, and the combat will be over by the time you start slaying in earnest. This is why classes like the occultist (which has basically zero self-buffing swift actions) have trouble compared with classes like the warpriest (swift action fervor), magus (swift action arcane pool), and paladin (swift action smite and lay on hands). But I’m happy to report that mesmerists are quite sleek in this regard. Most of your swift actions will probably be spent tagging enemies with hypnotic stare, since the ability has a tendency to break with enemy positioning, enemy death, or just new targeting priorities on your part; on rare occasions you may also wish to use touch treatment on yourself to eliminate a troublesome status condition. Your other abilities, however, including painful stare and many of your mesmerist tricks, are free actions that can be triggered even when it’s not your turn. How’s that for flexibility? Basically the only thing that mesmerists need their standard action for is spellcasting, and you should either be casting spells as an alpha strike to cripple minions or as a “kick ‘em while they’re down” tactic against a tough single target that you’ve already softened up a bit with hypnotic stare, bold stare, painful stare, and perhaps a trick or two. In either case, you should be 1) casting before others get a chance to act, or 2) casting once you’ve earned yourself a bit of a reprieve through other debuffing mechanisms. So enjoy that slim, sexy action economy, you stud.

# Any Changes You’d Make, Allerseelen?

Any Changes You’d Make, Allerseelen?


Funny you should ask that! Yes, there are some select changes I’d make to the mesmerist. Careful third-party changes can improve many of the acknowledged design flaws in Pathfinder, and the mesmerist class definitely has features that I would call design flaws. Let’s go over what my proposed changes are:


1. Allow one additional painful stare trigger per round at 3rd, 9th, and 15th level; the Manifold Stare feat now allows mesmerists to use Stare feats more than once per round. Mesmerists are incredibly dependent on the Manifold Stare feat to deal damage, but as written, painful stare can only be triggered once per round for about the same damage as a rogue’s sneak attack; if you want to buy into a better damage progression, you need to take Manifold Stare three times, sometimes trading away bold stare improvements in order to do so. This is an unnecessary and onerous tax on the class. Giving mesmerists the ability to deal painful stare damage two, three, and four times per round (at 3rd, 9th, and 15th levels) still leaves them behind the rogue in pure damage, so the balance is there. If players really want to push for extra damage (Intense Pain), extra debuffing (Blinding Stare, Confusing Stare), or some combination of the two (Compounded Pain), then my suggestion is to alter the Manifold Stare feat to allow Stare feats to be used two, three, and four times per round, depending on how many times you’ve taken the feat. Without Manifold Stare, mesmerists can only use Intense Pain or any of the other Stare feats once per round. The net effect of this change is that the power floor comes up to a reasonable level, but the ceiling doesn’t get any higher. This change is sorely needed.
2. Extend the effective range of the hypnotic stare by 10 feet every 4 class levels—40 feet at 4th level, 50 feet at 8th level, etc. Here’s another problem: mesmerists basically can’t do ranged builds. There are three basic obstacles: first, not enough feats. Changes #1 and #3 would at least allow ranged mesmerists to skip Manifold Stare and Point-Blank Shot, making a ranged build more reasonable. Second, no proficiency with anything but crossbows, a playstyle that requires—guess what?—more feat investment for Rapid Reload and potentially Crossbow Mastery. Change #4 gives mesmerists shortbow proficiency, which won’t break the damage curve and allows them to get by without crossbows, so that’s another problem solved. Third, and perhaps most intractably, hypnotic stare has a pitiful range of 30 feet, and that range never changes unless you take multiple instances of the Extended Stare feat, each of which increases the class feature’s range by 10 feet. This puts ranged mesmerists into the impossible position of wanting to get as far from enemies as possible (d8 HD, poor Fortitude save, light armor, etc.) but needing to remain within 30 feet in order to use their best class feature. How would you feel if a fighter’s weapon training bonuses only functioned against foes within 30 feet? Extending the range of hypnotic stare as a mesmerist gains psychic power not only makes thematic sense, it also enables ranged builds to function effectively without a single ill-timed five-foot step breaking hypnotic stare and ruining your next turn.
3. Use some of the Elephant in the Room feat tax rules, particularly relating to Weapon Finesse and Point-Blank Shot. Because mesmerists are not given the tools to create single-class Strength builds (since they receive no martial weapon proficiency, medium armor proficiency, or heavy armor proficiency) they’re all but required to use Dexterity builds, which means feat taxes in the form of Weapon Finesse and Point-Blank Shot. Call me a romantic, but I believe feats should add something to the character, not simply make you a functional combatant. And it’s not like there’s a Strength-based option for mesmerists that I’m ignoring—if you’re not Dexterity-based as a single-class mesmerist, you’re going to get splattered. So follow the Elephant in the Room rule that gives every player Weapon Finesse and eliminates Point-Blank Shot from the ranged feat progression.
4. Give mesmerists shortbow and rapier proficiency. For a d8 HD class that’s ostensibly meant to participate in combat, single-class mesmerists get shockingly few weapons to do so. Whip, sure, but that requires a bunch of feats to function. Sap, sure, but that’s nonlethal and doesn’t damage all enemies. Sword cane, sure, but that’s just an inferior copy of the rapier. Crossbows, sure, but they’re just bows with more feat requirements. The crit range is the main difference between the rapier and the sword cane, but giving mesmerists rapier proficiency won’t break the bank because most of a mesmerist’s damage comes from painful stare, and precision damage doesn’t multiply on critical hits. Shortbows have a shorter range and smaller damage die than crossbows—neither of which mesmerists really care about, since hypnotic stare’s range is small and, again, they deal most of their damage through painful stare. What mesmerists really care about is streamlining their feat progression, which is an advantage that shortbow and longbow builds have over crossbows. So just give ‘em shortbow proficiency, GMs. Treat ‘em like investigators.

# MES 101: Class Chassis

MES 101: Class Chassis


Any deep understanding of a Pathfinder class has to be built on a rock-solid foundation of knowledge about what a class’ features do and don’t do. To that end, it’s become customary in all of my guides to go through the class features of each class with a fine-toothed comb, pointing out rule interactions, errata, and nuances of wording that might have large impacts on the class without being immediately obvious to novice players. Before that, though, we have to delve into what exactly the rubric is for the abilities, items, archetypes, etc. that will be reviewed in this guide.

---

# The Rating System

The Rating System


Throughout this guide, you’ll see feats, spells, class features, etc. rated using a color coding system that was first introduced by Treantmonk and since copied in many other guides. Everyone tends to use their own colors, though, so here’s what we’ll be working with:


Purple ratings are singular, the one ability at a given level or for a given playstyle that is the cream of the crop. The apex, the zenith, the pinnacle: purple options are the royalty, the nobility. You will pick purple abilities, and damn the torpedoes.


Blue abilities are supercharged goodness: options that define the way a class is played, or that otherwise provide some exponential leap in effectiveness. Try your utmost to pick up these options.


Green abilities are almost universally useful, a good pick for nearly any build. If they’re not gamebreakingly powerful, they’re definitely holding up the line at a solid B+.


Yellow abilities are our C standard, neither good nor bad. A yellow rating can also denote situational abilities that might be useful to some playstyles but not others.


Red abilities are the traps. Stay away from these.

# The Chassis

The Chassis


d8 Hit Die: Like every other ⅔ casting class, mesmerists have a d8 HD and ¾ BAB progression. A d8 is fine, it just means that you won’t be quite as comfortable under heavy, sustained attack as other full martial, d10 HD classes like the ranger, paladin, or fighter. Even if you end up building a melee mesmerist build, it’s best to think of the class as a debuffer, socialite, and striker, rather than a tank.


¾ BAB Progression: ¾ BAB progression and d8 HDs go together like peanut butter and jelly, macaroni and cheese, salt and vinegar. Having slightly lower bonuses to attack rolls just means that the class will have to come up with debuffs to enemies’ AC, buffing spells, or other means of getting on track for combat. Since mesmerists get no bonus feats, they’ll also have to settle for pretty Plain Jane combat builds—no fancy BAB-gated feat lines or anything like that.


⅔ Psychic Spellcasting: Psychic spellcasting is a bit of an odd bird; you can find the complete rules here. The long and short of it is that psychic spellcasters use emotion and thought components to cast their spells, rather than somatic or verbal components. While this has advantages in certain situations (e.g., when you want to cast a spell stealthily or while being grappled) it also comes with fairly large disadvantages, too, primarily from emotion components: you can’t cast any psychic spell while under the influence of a non-harmless mind-affecting or emotion effect. Since a not-inconsiderable number of abilities found in the bestiaries are mind-affecting or emotion effects, psychic spellcasters have to take extra precautions around shoring up their Will save, ensuring that they can reroll failed saves, or else setting contingencies in place for stripping those effects away soon after they begin.


Skills: Mesmerists get 6 + Intelligence skill ranks per level, which is excellent—on par with investigators and bards, the other resident skill experts of Pathfinder 1e. If your group hasn’t investigated the Background Skills ruleset, I highly recommend you do so, because the class thrives when you’re able to build in more and more of the flavor that less-optimal skills afford. The skills available to you are detailed below—if it’s italicized, that means that it’s a class skill, conferring a permanent +3 bonus the first time you put a rank in it.


* Perception: Without a doubt, Perception is the most important skill in the game, determining everything from your participation in surprise rounds to your ability to spot loot, traps, and secret doors. Mesmerists may not have habitually high Wisdom scores, but that doesn’t excuse them from maximal investment in Perception. It really is necessary.
* Bluff, Diplomacy, Intimidate, Sense Motive: The entire mesmerist class is geared around being a sneaky, conniving socialite that can lie, trick, and bluster its way around scrutiny. Although you’ll get support from your bold stares and spells, mesmerists first and foremost need to invest in their skills. Intimidate is probably the least important of these skills; Bluff and Diplomacy, potentially the most important.
* Knowledge (Arcana, Dungeoneering, Local, Nature, Planes, Religion): With four out of the six monster identification skills represented on the mesmerist class skill list, you can at least be halfway decent at Knowledge jockeying. I wouldn’t necessarily recommend maximal investment here, but if I found spare ranks lying around, this is probably the first place I’d put them.
* Stealth: There are very few disadvantages to being able to move stealthily in Pathfinder, and with the benefit of some cheap armor enchantments, you could get very good at it.
* Use Magic Device: Use Magic Device is widely regarded as one of the strongest, most versatile skills in the game because of it allows you to use wands, scrolls, etc. from different classes or even pretend to be a different race, class, alignment, etc. for the purposes of wondrous items. This is one area in which just about every mesmerist should dedicate significant skill investment, especially with your Charisma casting stat.
* Acrobatics, Escape Artist: Mesmerists wear light armor as a general rule, and wearing light armor means (1) your armor check penalty will allow you to succeed at Dexterity-based skill checks relatively easily, and (2) you won’t be able to tank attacks of opportunity or grapples like heavily-armored full-BAB classes. Acrobatics and Escape Artist collectively fix your attack of opportunity and grappling problems. Even though you can still cast psychic spells while grappled, I still wouldn’t recommend staying grappled for long—it usually leads to getting paralyzed, constricted, raked, swallowed whole, etc., and you don’t want that.
* Spellcraft: Spellcraft’s primary uses are identifying magical items and identifying spells as they’re cast, both of which are incredibly helpful. Should you choose to advance into magical crafting, Spellcraft will also be the skill check you make—another perk.
* Climb, Fly, Swim: Some early ranks here may be merited in order to prevent you from plummeting to your death or drowning, but neither is relevant in the long term.
* Disable Device: Not a high priority for mesmerists, although builds with high Dexterity scores could potentially pick up a class skill through Fey Thoughts and become pretty good in this area.
* Heal: As a Charisma-based class, mesmerists are designed more for spellcasting and social encounters than healing, although you can still do some team buffing through your mesmerist tricks and touch treatment. I’d generally leave Heal checks to other classes, though.
* Linguistics: The king of the background skills! Linguistics grants more languages as you pump more ranks into it, so it’s one of the few background skills that has immediate, demonstrable benefits to a character. Definitely use one of your two background skill ranks on Linguistics if you’re playing with that ruleset.
* Profession: Profession skills are probably among the better background skills you could have, since Paizo APs often go to some lengths to include Profession checks as alternatives to Knowledge or social skill checks. Not a priority if you’re not playing with background skills, though.
* Appraise: Of no real mechanical use to mesmerists except as a one-rank background skill.
* Craft: Craft is mostly used for mundane crafting, which mesmerists definitely won’t be doing. Pass.
* Disguise: It’s not the worst skill in the world, just easily superseded by low-level magic.
* Handle Animal and Ride: Not particularly necessary for any mesmerist build.
* Knowledge (Engineering, Geography, History, Nobility): Very, very niche skill checks; you’re probably better off leaving these to someone else in the party. Focus yourself on monster identification so that you can keep your party alive.
* Perform: Perhaps useful for a bard gestalt or variant multiclass, but not generally needed.
* Sleight of Hand: Not really necessary unless you plan to be planting evidence on some of your hapless, hypnotized victims.
* Survival: Survival is more useful than a lot of people give it credit for; it’s not really in your wheelhouse, however.


Armor Proficiencies: You get better armor than full casters, but only barely. Having access solely to light armor will constrain your build options, forcing single-class mesmerists to focus on Dexterity-based builds in order to maintain a decent AC. This could be a great deal better, if I’m honest.


Weapon Proficiencies: Oof, another subpar portion of the class chassis. With only simple weapons and hand crossbows, saps, sword canes, and whips available, single-class mesmerists will likely have to rely on longspears for Strength-based builds, sword canes or whips for Dexterity-based builds, and crossbows for ranged builds. It’s not the worst, but it’s far from the best.


Good Reflex and Will saves, Bad Fortitude save: I consider the strong Reflex/Will save progression to be the second best in the game after strong Fortitude/Will. Wherever you stand on the issue, everyone can agree that strong Will saves are by far the most important to have—and Will saves are doubly important for psychic spellcasters, whose emotion components can be stymied pretty much exclusively by failed Will saves. Fortitude can be compensated for fairly easily, both by boosting your Constitution score at point buy or else by taking the Great Fortitude feat later in the game.


Attribute Allocation: As a ¾-BAB striker class, mesmerists are going to be expected to fill a few roles, and their dual focus on both combat and spellcasting will necessitate some amount of resource allocation split between mental and physical attribute scores. Strength-based melee mesmerists will probably want to emphasize Charisma a little less, instead acquiring medium or heavy armor proficiency and going for Strength > Constitution, Charisma > Dexterity, Wisdom > Intelligence. Dexterity-based builds (whether Weapon Finesse builds or ranged builds) have a bit more attribute wiggle room, and can instead go for Dexterity, Charisma > Constitution > Wisdom > Strength, Intelligence. Although I love Intelligence as an attribute because of its role in skill monkey builds, it’s unquestionably the least important attribute for mesmerists, so dump it down to 8 if you need to.

# Class Features

Class Features


        (1) Spells and Knacks: The mesmerist is a ⅔ spontaneous psychic spellcaster that focuses on illusion and enchantment magic to trick and beguile their foes. The rules text here is absolutely, 100% boilerplate for spontaneous casters, so I’m not going to do a deep dive on these particular class features. Your spell save DC will be equal to 10 + Spell Level + Charisma modifier, and you get to swap out some of your spells starting at 5th level. Everything else is normal spontaneous caster stuff: save your spells known for the absolute best-of-the-best spells at any given level, and dedicate wands or scrolls to the rest where you need versatility. This is especially important for the mesmerist, who (unlike many other ⅔ casters) does not have any favored class bonuses that provide bonus spells known.


        (1) Consummate Liar: Consummate liar works on a couple different levels. First, and most obviously, this class feature allows you to lie like a boss. If that baker is concerned about who stole the pie from the windowsill, it certainly wasn’t you—you would never do something like that, honest!—but you think you saw a figure in a cloak grabbing it a few minutes ago. You get the gist: mesmerists are even better liars, bluffers, blavers, cheats, malingerers, cardsharps, crooks, con men, and swindlers than rogues are, so hop to it. The second interaction that consummate liar sets up is one avenue for how the class can regularly hit enemies in combat: feinting. Unlike many other ¾-BAB classes, the mesmerist doesn’t actually get many buffs to attack rolls, whether from spells or class features. Investigators have studied combat, inquisitors have judgment and bane, magi have their arcane pool, etc., but none of your bold stares and none of your mesmerist tricks make enemies any easier to hit! One solution is to feint enemies in order to make them lose their Dexterity bonus to AC. This might be a small bonus to hit in some cases, but against rogues, swashbucklers, and monsters with levels in other classes that are similarly light on their feet, you’ll definitely be helping the team out—and yourself in the process—a great deal. Many times, however, the Blinding Stare feat will do a better job of making enemies unable to competently defend against your attacks.


        (1) Hypnotic Stare (Su): The signature ability of the mesmerist, and the one that you’ll use most consistently in and out of combat for your entire career, hypnotic stare allows you to impose a penalty on an enemy’s Will save while you focus your psychic power on them. At later levels, hypnotic stare also accepts upgrades called bold stares that allow you to stack additional rider effects on top of that initial -2 to Will saves. Here’s the rundown:


A mesmerist can focus his stare on one creature within 30 feet as a swift action. That creature takes a –2 penalty on Will saving throws. This penalty changes to –3 at 8th level. A mesmerist can maintain his stare against only one opponent at a time; it remains in effect until the mesmerist stares at a new target, the opponent dies, the opponent moves farther than 30 feet away, or the mesmerist falls unconscious or dies. The mesmerist can remove the memory of his stare from the target’s mind; the creature doesn’t remember that it was affected (nor does it realize that it is currently being affected) unless the mesmerist allows it. The hypnotic stare is a psychic effect, and relies more on the mesmerist’s focus than the target’s perception of his stare. It can’t be avoided in the same ways a gaze attack can. The mesmerist can use this ability even while blinded, but must succeed at a DC 20 concentration check to do so. Staring at a creature requires the mesmerist’s focus, so if he uses a gaze attack or similar ability, he must target the subject of his hypnotic stare or voluntarily end the stare. The penalties from multiple mesmerists’ stares don’t stack, nor do they stack with penalties from witches’ evil eye hexes. This is a mind-affecting effect.


        Some salient points here: first, hypnotic stare is mind-affecting, which means that undead, vermin, mindless constructs, and other beings that are immune to mind-affecting effects simply will not take any penalties at all from this class feature. The exception to this rule is if you take the Psychic Inception bold stare—which, by the way, is 1000% required for mesmerists, as the class can’t pull nearly as much weight as other classes if its main debuffing route is blocked off. Second, you can only stare at creatures that are within 30 feet, and if they run or get far enough away from you, the stare will break automatically. The mesmerist’s weapon proficiencies are poorly suited to ranged combat, and this point further complicates the process of building a ranged mesmerist. Third, you can only stare at one creature at a time, so save it for the biggest, baddest enemy on the field. Fourth, creatures don’t know they’re under the effects of a hypnotic stare unless you want them to. This is very important to remember! There’s no save and no spell resistance check needed to achieve this effect—if you don’t want them to know, then they don’t know. Combined with the fact that psychic spellcasting has no verbal or somatic components, and mesmerists more than any other class are capable of tanking someone’s Will save, casting some kind of enchantment spell, and getting an enemy under their direct control without the person ever becoming aware of the fact that they’re being manipulated. I love it when mechanics and flavor align.


        (1) Mesmerist Tricks (Su): As far as I can tell, mesmerist tricks are a bit unpopular among Pathfinder players. While that’s regrettable, it is true that utilizing mesmerist tricks effectively (much like utilizing teamwork feats effectively) requires a fairly high degree of both systems mastery on the part of the mesmerist player and cooperation/communication among party members. Much like the hypnotic commands given by real hypnotists (or “real” hypnotists, depending on who you ask) each mesmerist trick has a trigger condition and a consequent effect. My recommendation to any aspiring mesmerist would be to write down your available tricks on notecards that explicitly state the trigger condition and the consequent effect. From there, you can give a copy of the notecard to the person you’ve implanted the trick in while keeping a copy for yourself, hopefully ensuring that one of you remembers to use the trick in the relevant situation. There’s very little in the world that feels worse than implanting a trick, getting pasted by a monster, and later realizing that a trick that you forgot was active could have saved you.


A mesmerist can create hypnotic bonds with his allies, implanting magical suggestions in their minds that he can later activate. Each day, he can implant a number of these tricks equal to 1/2 his mesmerist level (minimum 1) plus his Charisma bonus (if any). He can have only one trick implanted at a given time, and implanting a new trick ends the previous one (the mesmerist still loses the use of this ability he spent on the previous trick).


        Pretty simple. Your total number of tricks per day will range from about 4 at 1st level to the high teens or low 20s at 20th level, but you can keep all these tricks to yourself, distribute them all to allies, or do some combination of both. Until you reach 5th level, though, you can only have one trick implanted in any given person.


To implant a trick, the mesmerist must take a standard action and either touch a willing creature or implant the trick in himself. A creature can be the subject of only one mesmerist trick at a time. The mesmerist can activate the trick as a free action when a triggering condition is met (as defined in the trick’s description), even if it isn’t his turn. The subject must be within medium range (100 feet + 10 feet per level) for the mesmerist to trigger the trick. The mesmerist monitors for the trick’s triggering condition through a subtle telepathic connection, so he doesn’t need line of sight to trigger it—but anything that blocks telepathic contact prevents him from triggering tricks. An implanted trick lasts until the next time the mesmerist regains his spells.


        Implanting a trick is a standard action—better done outside of combat, in other words—but tricks can be activated as free actions no matter whose turn it is. The range on trick activation is much better than for your hypnotic stare, but still not long enough to help someone who’s at long range. The clause about telepathic connections shouldn’t be relevant unless you’re regularly being targeted by effects like telepathic censure or telepathic silence, but it is very useful to know that you don’t have to have line of sight to an ally in order to activate a trick.


Once triggered, a trick is no longer implanted, and can’t be triggered again until the mesmerist implants the trick again. The duration of the effect caused by triggering a trick is either instantaneous or appears in the trick’s entry. The DC for any mesmerist trick or masterful trick that requires a saving throw or skill check is 10 + 1/2 the mesmerist’s level + the mesmerist’s Charisma modifier.


        This paragraph doesn’t bring any revelations with it. I think you probably knew that tricks went away after they got triggered, and the text of the tricks themselves makes it pretty clear that most of them are instantaneous effects.


The mesmerist knows one trick at 1st level, and learns another trick at 2nd level and every 2 levels thereafter (to a maximum of 11 tricks at 20th level). Each daily use of mesmerist tricks can be used to implant any trick the mesmerist knows. Unless specifically noted in the mesmerist trick’s description, a mesmerist can’t choose a particular trick more than once.


Your arsenal of “tricks known” is a little limited to begin with, and there’s no feat that will teach you a new trick. Choose carefully!


(1) Painful Stare (Su): Painful stare is the source of most of this class’ damage, and functions in much the same way for mesmerists as sneak attack functions for rogues. Since you don’t get access to any high-damage martial weapons, and since precision damage from painful stare doesn’t multiply on a critical hit, there are generally few reasons to try to build a crit-fishing or Strength-based single-class mesmerist—you’re just playing against your own strengths. If you’re willing to dip into or out of mesmerist, that calculus will likely change a bit, but we’ll go over that in more depth in the dipping section.


When an attack that deals damage hits the target of a mesmerist’s hypnotic stare, the mesmerist can cause the target to take an amount of additional damage equal to 1/2 the mesmerist’s class level (minimum 1). The mesmerist can use this ability as a free action, and can use it even if it isn’t his turn. If the mesmerist uses this ability to increase his own damage, the additional damage increases by 1d6 points for every 3 class levels the mesmerist possesses. This damage is precision damage and is not multiplied on a critical hit. A mesmerist can trigger this ability only once per round, but a single creature can take damage from multiple mesmerists’ painful stares in a round.


Okay, well, pretty much all of this is important. Sorry. The key here is that you can activate painful stare whether or not you’re the one hitting an enemy. You get a decisive damage boost if it’s you that lands the blow, but that’s not strictly necessary—you can activate it as a free action when one of your colleagues hits an enemy, too. Unfortunately, you can only trigger painful stare once per round (although the the Manifold Stare feat changes this!), so my advice would be to attempt to trigger it on your own attacks, and if you miss every attack, trigger it on the next successful attack you can. Your damage output will definitely lag unless you’re consistently proc’ing this class feature, so make it a priority to lay painful stare down on whichever enemy the team seems to be focusing on most consistently. If the damage scaling is difficult to parse based on the wording, just know that painful stare triggers for allies will range from 1 damage (1st level) to 10 damage (20th level), or 1 damage (1st level) to 6d6+10 damage (20th level) if you’re the one triggering the stare.


(2) Towering Ego (Su): Oof, towering ego is so good it hurts. Psychic classes live in constant fear of emotion and mind-affecting effects punching through their Will saves and negating their emotion components, so the fact that you can add your Charisma modifier to (that’s in addition to, not instead of) your Wisdom modifier makes all the difference in the world. If you’re ever scared, though, you’re really scared, and losing your towering ego bonuses will make it that much harder to defend against other mental incursions.


(3) Bold Stare (Su): For my money, bold stare is the single most important class feature of the mesmerist. Sure, tricks are fun, and sure, a -2 or -3 penalty to Will saves is alright, but layering more debuffs on top of hypnotic stare is what really turns you into a supercharged single-target debuffing machine. Note that all of your bold stares stack, so hop down to the bold stares section and go shopping.


(3) Touch Treatment (Su): Personally, I love touch treatment. It’s an easy class feature for archetypes to give up, but I would try to keep it if at all possible—many of the status conditions on the moderate and greater condition lists wreck encounters, and you never know when the ability to break enchantment at will will come in handy. There’s not a lot of interesting rules text here: just remember that you get 3 + your Charisma modifier uses per day, and that you can touch yourself as a swift action (I’ll just bet you can, nod nod nudge nudge wink wink) and you’ll be fine.


(5) Manifold Tricks (Su): Increasing the number of mesmerist tracks that you can have implanted in yourself or your allies at the same time can only do good things. There’s nothing specifically forbidding you from implanting multiple instances of the same trick, so communicate with your allies about whether they’d like you to be able to enact the same trick multiple times in the same combat, or whether they’d like some variety.


(5) Mental Potency (Ex): This class feature falls a bit flat, unfortunately. While color spray and similar spells based on HD can go over like gangbusters at low levels, they rarely scale hard enough to be useful beyond, say, about 6th level. Mental potency doesn’t fix that scaling significantly—even if you can affect creatures with 7 or 8 HD with color spray at 20th level, you’re never going to be fighting creatures with so few HD at that level. HD-capped spells are rarely a wise use of resources, so I’m hesitant to give this class feature a good rating.


(11) Glib Lie (Su): Glib lie is a lot of fun in social encounters, and the caster level DC of 15 + your mesmerist level is actually really difficult to beat. In a head-to-head battle between an inquisitor and a mesmerist to see who could tell or detect a lie, I’m honestly not sure who would win, but it’d be a hell of a match-up. Certainly no other class could keep up with your deception abilities.


(12) Masterful Tricks (Su): Masterful tricks are more powerful than normal tricks, but correspondingly more expensive. We’ll get into them in much greater detail later.


(20) Rule Minds (Su): Permanent, disposable thralls: that’s the mesmerist dream, right? As fun as this ability is, I don’t think it’s any better than some of the alternative capstones available to the mesmerist. Let’s get into those, shall we?


Alternate Capstones: But wait, there’s more! Chronicle of Legends introduced alternative capstones that you can take instead of your normal capstone. Each class has one class-exclusive capstone, and then several others that are more general for all characters, all spellcasters, etc. Do note that you can’t select an alternative capstone if your archetype replaces the rule minds class feature. Here are my ratings:


* Perfect Body, Flawless Mind (Ex): PBFM is the correct capstone answer for just about any mesmerist. You can boost Dexterity, for a permanent +4 to AC, Reflex saves, initiative, and potentially attack rolls; Charisma, for a permanent +4 to Will saves, social skill checks, touch treatment, tricks per day, spell save DCs, etc., or some combination of the above. There are few forces more powerful than huge attribute bonuses, so be smart and pick this capstone when you get the chance. Buying inherent bonuses of this magnitude would cost somewhere around 220,000 gp, which beats With This Sword quite handily.
* Piercing Gaze (Su): I’m not convinced that Piercing Gaze is better than PBFM, but it’s quite good—certainly the best non-PBFM option available. What a monster debuffer you’ve become. Welcome to the endgame, my friend. Welcome to ZomboCom.
* With This Sword (Ex): A good runner-up to PBFM, but still inferior to it.
* Deep Magics (Su): Not a worthless alternate capstone, but by no means one of the best. Mesmerists would only get six spells known, anyway.
* The Boss (Ex) and Won’t Stay Dead (Ex): More story points than anything else—fitting sunsets for your characters. They won’t have overwhelming effects in combat.

# MES 151: Mesmerist Roles

MES 151: Mesmerist Roles


        It’s become customary in my guides for me to lay out the roles—both in-combat and out-of-combat—that Pathfinder classes can become great at. The mesmerist is a bit of an unusual class in that its combat role is very narrow, absent multiclassing, but it makes up for its general lack of combat punch with lots of spellcasting power and the ability to either pump up allies or debuff enemies through the floor. Mechanically, I like to think of the mesmerist as some kind of bizarre cross-pollination of the bard, witch, and rogue classes: you’ve got the bard’s high Charisma modifier, buffing abilities, and face skills, the witch’s unlimited debuffing abilities (i.e., hypnotic stare, bold stare, and any of the stare feats that modify painful stare), and the rogue’s general weapon/armor proficiency set-up and ability to deal precision damage. Provided that you leverage your strengths appropriately (or multiclass prudently when you need to grab other classes’ strengths) the mesmerist can be a monstrously powerful class, but it also has lots of opportunities for mediocre builds. Additionally, because such a vast proportion of the mesmerist’s abilities involve either mind-affecting effects (45% of your spells are mind-affecting, and hypnotic stare is mind-affecting) or precision damage (which certain types of enemies are immune to) mesmerists are at the mercy of AP design more than other classes: if you’re in a campaign full of undead and haven’t taken the spirit walker archetype (heck, even if you have taken the spirit walker archetype) you’re going to have a bad time as a mesmerist, period. Mesmerists do best in intrigue games featuring a lot of humanoids, where their high Bluff scores, feinting prowess, and mind-affecting effects can be put to their best use. In general, the more exotic the enemies you expect to face, the less a mesmerist will be the right tool for the job. My two cents!

# Combat Roles

Combat Roles


        “Combat roles” refers to something that every class needs to figure out: how am I going to deal damage in combat? Full casters can often get away without any reliable means of dealing damage in combat, provided that they can offer enough buffing, debuffing, summoning, battlefield control, etc. to support the team anyway, but ¾-BAB ⅔ casters must often juggle twin mandates: deal effective damage, and offer spellcasting or skill functionality outside of combat. Much of a class’ suitedness to certain combat roles is decided by their weapon and armor proficiencies, as well as—in the case of ¾-BAB classes—their ability to self-buff before the combat is nearly over. We’re going to spend this next section discussing where mesmerists naturally fit, and brainstorm some avenues for covering your weaknesses.




Melee (Strength)


        Strength-based melee builds are often considered the bog-standard melee combat builds, and I’ll be honest: mesmerists are pretty much hot garbage in this arena. The base mesmerist class is proficient with light armor only, which means that single-class mesmerists will need to pump their Dexterity at point buy in order to achieve an acceptable AC for the early levels; if they then want a halfway decent Charisma stat and a Constitution score that won’t cause them to die in the face of a light breeze, they can’t afford to also prioritize Strength. Compounding this issue is the mesmerist’s generally poor weapon selection—simple weapons plus sap, hand crossbow, sword cane, and whip doesn’t exactly scream “melee blender.” So what’s the solution? Quite simply, you must multiclass. Paladin 2, fighter 1, and slayer 2 are all decent options for a melee mesmerist, but I’m a huge fan of the paladin dip, for a number of reasons: first and foremost, divine grace is incredible, and while it doesn’t double-stack with towering ego, it can supersede towering ego even when you’ve had your emotion components blocked. Take this dip, and you’ll almost never fail a save. That’s valuable. Paladin 2 also gives you a 1/day smite evil, medium/heavy armor proficiency, proficiency with martial weapons, better Fortitude saves, better HP, and more. If you take the Magical Knack trait, you can nullify the hit to your caster level, ensuring that the only penalty you take is slightly delayed spells and class features. Irrespective of which avenue you take to get to your Strength-based melee mesmerist, you’re likely to be a terror on the battlefield: heavily armored, wielding a greatsword or greataxe, able to eliminate status conditions on yourself (via touch treatment) as a swift action, pulling out implanted tricks, self-buffing with spells, etc. It’s a fun role, but not one that the mesmerist class can just stumble into: it takes some planning.




Melee (Dexterity)


        Even a casual glance at the mesmerist class will tell you that this was clearly the role that Paizo designed mesmerists around. Dexterity-based melee builds always involve high Dexterity scores, light armor, and a weapon used with the Weapon Finesse feat or the agile enchantment; certain weapons also have [Weapon] Grace feats that allow you to add your Dexterity modifier to damage—Fencing Grace for rapiers, Slashing Grace for light or one-handed slashing weapons, Starry Grace for starknives, Dervish Dance for scimitars, Bladed Brush + Slashing Grace for glaives, etc. Unfortunately, as a ¾-BAB class with no bonus feats, mesmerists are chronically crunched in their builds, making the three to four feats you’d need to spend on Weapon Finesse + Weapon Focus + [Weapon] Grace a bit hard to stomach. After all, you also have spellcasting priorities in Combat Casting, Spell Focus + Greater, Spell Penetration + Greater, as well as feat priorities for your stares in Compounded Pain, Intense Pain, Manifold Stare, etc., all of which must be attended to in order for you to reach your full potential. So what’s the play? Well, you have two potential options. First, you can multiclass again. Swashbuckler 1 and unchained rogue 3 are your best bets; each will kickstart a Dexterity-based combat build, although each also costs you some progression in your primary mesmerist class. Your second option is to simply ignore Dexterity to damage, at least through feat investment. That’s right! You can ignore Dexterity to damage if you want to—most of a mesmerist’s damage will come from painful stare + Intense Pain + Manifold Stare anyway, so if you want to ignore Weapon Focus and the [Weapon] Grace feats until you can save up enough scratch for an agile weapon, that’s a perfectly acceptable route to take. The downside to this route is that single-class, Dexterity-based mesmerists will be left with pretty poor weapon options. Sword canes, daggers, and sickles will probably be about all you can muster.
        One special playstyle that deserves some mention in the Melee (Dexterity) category is the whip playstyle. Although whips are no easier on feat investment than any other Weapon Finesse build—Weapon Finesse + Weapon Focus (Whip) + Whip Mastery will be the absolute minimum you’d want to consider, and Improved Whip Mastery is great to have if you can spare the feat—they threaten a wider area and can be quite useful for builds that focus on attacks of opportunity with Combat Reflexes. Although longspears and other reach weapons are possible in Strength-based melee builds, whips are about as close as Dexterity-based melee mesmerists will ever come to “reach.”




Ranged (Dexterity)


        Ranged mesmerist builds are also quite hard to build, again because of feats, dirty feats. Since the base mesmerist class’ only viable ranged weapons are crossbows, you have to take all the usual ranged combat feats (Point-Blank Shot + Precise Shot + Rapid Shot, plus Clustered Shots and Improved Precise Shot later) and feats for reloading crossbows faster (Rapid Reload, plus potentially Crossbow Mastery if you’re wielding a heavy crossbow or want to stop provoking attacks of opportunity for your reloads) unless you’re able to snag bonus weapon proficiencies from your racial selection. For a class with no bonus feats, the four to seven feats you need to piece together a convincing ranged build are...intimidating. And in all honesty, I’m not sure it’s worth it! Unlike rogues or inquisitors, who can multiply their sneak attack and bane damage the more times they hit an enemy (one of the most attractive features of Rapid Shot), all mesmerists are limited in the number of times they can deal their painful stare damage based on how many times they’ve taken Manifold Stare, a level-gated feat that will never allow them to deal painful stare damage more than four times per round. Not to mention that hypnotic stare and all of your bold stare improvements cease to function if you ever move more than 30 feet from an enemy! Doesn’t exactly inspire you with the confidence to build a ranged mesmerist, right? It can be done, of course, but it’s also going to require some multiclassing, my suggestions for which are down in the dipping section.

# Casting Roles

Casting Roles


        Under normal circumstances, I would break spellcasting roles down into several categories, including buffing, debuffing, battlefield control, and summoning. Mesmerists are odd birds, however, in that they’re only really capable of two roles: buffing and debuffing. Anybody with the right spell list can buff, provided that they have the requisite Charisma score to cast spells of that level—buffing spells don’t require saves or spell resistance checks, so there’s no real specialization needed. Debuffing, however, very much is a specialized spellcasting role, and in order to maximize your spell save DCs, you must pick a certain school of magic associated with Spell Focus and Greater Spell Focus. This, therefore, is the mesmerist’s dilemma: which school of magic do I pick? Mesmerists have relatively few conjuration, abjuration, transmutation, and evocation spells, making them poor choices for Spell Focus. Divination is getting there, but these spells are focused more on reconnaissance and knowledge-gathering instead of combat. The three schools of magic left—not coincidentally, the schools with the most spells available on your spell list—are enchantment, necromancy, and illusion. If you’re going to focus on debuffing or enemy control, chances are very good that you should be focusing your feat investment in one of these schools. Let’s go over the pros and cons of each.




Enchantment


Enchantment is the hands-down best choice for a mesmerist to specialize in. Enchantment spells are the most common on your spell list (178 of your 554 available spells are enchantment spells), they contain some of the hardest-hitting debuffs that benefit from Spell Focus + Greater, and they’re a hell of a lot of fun to use—dominate person, mortal terror, confusion, feeblemind—the list goes on. Of note, all but 9 of the enchantment spells on your spell list have the mind-affecting tag, so this is a terrible school to specialize in if your campaign features high numbers of undead, vermin, plants, constructs, etc. The Psychic Inception bold stare improvement can alleviate this issue somewhat, and pairing Psychic Inception with the spirit walker archetype can essentially make your mind-affecting spells fully functional against undead, but for all other enemy types, you’re a no-go. Probably the worst campaign I can imagine for a mesmerist is Iron Gods. That many constructs...shudder.




Necromancy


Necromancy contains 49 of your 554 available spells, and in spite of this more limited selection, my opinion is that it’s the second-strongest spell school for mesmerists. You have fewer buffs in this school, but the debuff list is impressive: bestow curse, calamitous flailing, enervation, possession, howling agony, blindness/deafness, all the old chestnuts. The tags are more varied here, too, with a smattering of mind-affecting, emotion, fear, pain, death, and curse effects, making the school more effective against exotic enemy types that enchantment won’t be quite as equipped to handle. That said, many of the spells in the necromancy school tend to be either fantastic or real stinkers, so you’d want to focus your spell investment on only the best of the best while drawing no-save buffs, etc. from other spell schools.




Illusion


As you might expect, illusion is the most difficult school of magic to define in cut-and-dried terms. In the hands of a creative player with an enthusiastic GM, illusion can be exceptionally powerful, as its spells allow players to mimic other spells (the shadow [school] spells such as shadow conjuration, shadow evocation, etc.) or shape enemies’ perception of reality so completely (via figment spells) that nearly any effect can be achieved. Occult Adventures also added a number of illusion spells that are exclusive to (or favored by) the psychic casting classes, including oneiric horror, synesthesia, and mantle of doubt, all of which give you some offensive debuffs that aren’t figment spells. (Figment spells are, of course, the beating heart of the illusion school, so you’d still be well-advised to take spells like silent image, major image, and permanent image.) The issue is that illusions tend to be very all-or-nothing: either enemies can’t figure out what’s going on, and you wipe the floor with them, or they pass their saves to disbelieve and wipe the floor with you. What’s more, enemies in the later stages of Pathfinder play will almost always have blindsight, blindsense, tremorsense, lifesense, constant true seeing, or other tools that will allow them to simply disbelieve your illusions automatically. There’s nothing less fun than investing in illusion magic for 15 levels only to find out that none of your tricks work on the enemies in Book 6 of a Paizo AP.

# Ancillary Roles

Ancillary Roles


Ancillary roles are the roles that Pathfinder characters might expect to play outside of their primary combat or casting roles, including scouting/reconnaissance, anticaster functions, skill monkeying or Knowledge jockeying, non-magical healing, etc. Only in rare instances would you want to make these roles the main focus of a build, but some amount of support for them helps to create a well-rounded character that can contribute off the battlefield as well as on. 




Face


        In terms of their ability to interact in social situations with other characters, mesmerists are rivaled only by bards/skalds and possibly inquisitors. This is a great thing! The mesmerist spell list poaches many of the bard/skald’s best social spells, including chestnuts like glibness and gullibility, and with their consummate liar and glib lie class features, a Charisma casting stat, and enough skill ranks to ensure good investment in all their face skills at every level, mesmerists can be extremely smooth operators, sweet-talking or outright lying their way out of many situations that would otherwise result in combat. Psychic spellcasting is still detectable even though you cast spells without somatic or verbal components, but Conceal Spell and Improved Conceal Spell would pave over some of those difficulties if you were willing to walk that road. Add to this your hypnotic stare class feature and bold stare improvements (which, remember, enemies don’t know they’re affected by unless you choose for them to know!) and you can often make enemies far more susceptible to your Will save-based spells (gullibility, passing fancy, utter contempt, deceitful veneer, etc.) than they otherwise would have been.

# Anticaster

Anticaster


        I’ve refined my thoughts on the anticaster role considerably over the years, and my most convincing essay on the subject thus far can be found here, in my guide to the Pathfinder occultist. In that link, I lay out a number of areas that Pathfinder classes need to be proficient in to become high-quality magehunters, including strong touch AC, energy resistances, and Fortitude/Will saves, counters to defensive caster tricks like mirror image or displacement, counters to battlefield control and summoning, mobility options, ways to debuff concentration checks, etc. Mesmerists simply do not have many of these tools available to them. What they do have are plenty of ways to debuff an enemy caster through the floor while another more specialized ally takes the caster on head-to-head. Sapped Magic and Distracted are great bold stare improvements for this playstyle, helping make casters both less likely to pass their concentration checks to cast defensively (Distracted) and less threatening even when they do manage to cast a spell (Sapped Magic). Most of the purple- or blue-rated mesmerist tricks, likewise, will be extraordinarily effective against spellcasters if implanted in such an ally: Astounding Avoidance, Gift of Will, etc. are almost tailor-made for martial allies facing spellcasters. Once you have the requisite bold stare improvements and mesmerist tricks covered, being an effective anticaster facilitator is just a small matter of picking an appropriate spell (preferably one keyed off of a Fortitude save) to cripple the enemy while your ally closes in. Stare feats such as Confusing Stare or Blinding Stare can also help you break down a caster without you even needing to be close.

# MES 201: Races

MES 201: Races




With our discussion of the mesmerist’s roles and class chassis out of the way, you’ve hopefully made a determination about whether the class is likely to suit the needs you have in your campaign or not. If not, thanks for reading, bon voyage, and have fun out there! If so, it’s time to actually build a mesmerist PC. The first place that many people start when building a PC is their race/ancestry—an important choice, as race/ancestry determines your attribute bonuses, your racial traits, and any favored class bonuses that the class might have. There are a punishing number of racial options available in Pathfinder, so my custom is to review the core races that make up much of Golarion society, then highlight a few less common races that have unexpected perks for the class. It’s not actually that difficult to figure out whether a race is good for the class you’re investigating, though, so I’ll show you what I’m typically looking for:


   * Bonuses to Charisma and Strength/Dexterity. Charisma is one of the mesmerist’s most important attributes, determining everything from your spell save DCs, to the number of tricks you can use per day, to your towering ego bonus to Will saves, to your facility with face skills like Diplomacy and Bluff. Strength and Dexterity, meanwhile, are Pathfinder’s main attack stats, depending on whether you’re crafting a Strength-based melee build or a Dexterity-based Weapon Finesse or ranged build. Dexterity / Charisma pairings are shockingly common among the Pathfinder races, so mesmerists are nicely set up for a wide selection here.
   * Bonus feats. Mesmerists are chronically feat-starved, and even though only a few races offer bonus feats (humans and half-elves are the big ones among the core races) you’ll still want to seriously consider your feat priorities before selecting a race. Humans start to look mighty enticing when you realize how long it takes to progress two more levels to that next feat.
   * Favored class bonuses. Favored class bonuses are bonuses that Pathfinder races can take in lieu of bonus skill ranks or HP at every level. As later additions to the Pathfinder canon, the occult classes never got as many favored class bonuses as other classes, but they still do reasonably well for themselves. There are some common themes among the favored class bonuses: first, bonus tricks per day. Gnomes, halflings, and kitsune all get this favored class bonus; it’s best suited for defensive, support-based mesmerist builds that want to bulk up their allies, and pairs very nicely with the vexing trickster archetype. Second, bonuses to Acrobatics and Bluff checks. This is the elf favored class bonus, and it’s excellent for feinting builds and the vexing daredevil archetype. If you’re looking to make one of those, chances are good that half-elf with an elven curve blade is going to be your best bet because of this favored class bonus. Third, painful stare damage bonuses. Although both dwarves and half-orcs technically get this favored class bonus, the half-orc is the better mesmerist race by a country mile. You can’t really maximize mesmerist damage without this favored class bonus, so get used to seeing some half-orc builds. Fourth, towering ego bonuses. Humans get this favored class bonus, but it’s limited to a +2 total. You should still take it, but it stinks that you can’t scale it for a full 20 levels. Finally, bonuses to CL for enchantment spells for the purposes of spell duration. This one is huge for half-elves, and helps cement their place as one of the best mesmerist classes in the game—certainly the best for enchantment builds. An effective +50% to the duration of all your enchantment spells makes your rounds/level spells much more effective in the low levels, and can boost your days/level spells (charm monster, e.g.) to monstrous durations at later levels.
   * Vision. Mesmerists have both light and dancing lights on their spell list, but having better vision never hurt anyone.
   * Skill bonuses. These bonuses have to be evaluated in light of how useful the skill is. Bonuses to Perception, like the elf and half-elf’s Keen Senses? Pure gold. Bonuses to Appraise or Handle Animal? Poop on a stick.

# Dwarf

Dwarf


        With a favored class bonus that’s easily superseded by the half-orc and a ruthlessly mediocre stat array, dwarves simply don’t make good Charisma-based classes. Whatever character concept you had for a dwarven mesmerist, try to make it with another class—you’re facing an uphill slog here.


Pros
	Cons
	Hardy. A perennial favorite of dwarf PCs, the hardy racial trait gives you a +2 racial bonus to saves vs. poisons, spells, and spell-like abilities, which covers a huge array of effects.
	Exceptionally poor attribute bonuses. Remember what our criteria were? Bonuses to either Charisma, Dexterity, or Strength, and hopefully no penalties to Charisma or your chosen physical stat. Not only do dwarves not grant any relevant bonuses to physical or mental attributes, they also take a -2 Charisma penalty. That’s really, really hard to stomach.
	Darkvision.
	

	Decent favored class bonus. I’m not sure why dwarves got slower scaling than half-orcs, but at any rate, more painful stare damage never hurts.

# Elf

Elf

Mesmerists are weak against Fortitude saves, and at least some mesmerist builds live in melee, where HP counts. # Gnome

Gnome


        Gnomes are without question one of the strongest mesmerist races, and certainly the strongest spellcasters (with the possible exception of half-elf enchanters) thanks to their racial traits that grant a +1 to the save DCs of spells in the mesmerist’s three main schools of magic: illusion, necromancy, and enchantment. With great stat distributions and a good favored class bonus, there’s nothing to disqualify these lil’ fey bois from being fine mesmerists.


Pros
	Cons
	Ideal stat distributions. Gnomes can make admirable ranged, Weapon Finesse, or casting-based mesmerists, thanks to Charisma and Constitution bonuses.
	None. Gnomes are very, very solid mesmerists.
	Highly skilled at spellcasting. Thanks to some of their racial traits (faerie dragon magic for illusionists, fell magic for necromancers, and genial magic for enchanters) gnomes essentially get free Spell Focus, which makes them the strictly optimal choice for many casting mesmerist builds.
	

	Good favored class bonus. More mesmerist tricks per day won’t ever steer you wrong. Well, unless you traded away your mesmerist tricks with an archetype. Then just take HP or skill ranks.
	

	Small size. Since mesmerists don’t typically rely on large weapon die sizes to deal damage, Small races can compete much more readily. Bonuses to AC, attack rolls, and Stealth are all just fine by me.
	

	Low-light vision.

# Half-Elf

Half-Elf


        The competition between half-elves and half-orcs as the premier Medium-sized mesmerist race is fierce. Half-elves have much more flexible favored class bonuses than half-orcs, and can use their adaptability racial trait (or any of the racial traits that replace adaptability) to become pretty much anything you want them to be. Probably the best all-around race for mesmerists: not quite as specialized in spellcasting as gnomes or as specialized in damage-dealing as half-orcs, but truly chameleonic as far as what you can turn them into.


Pros
	Cons
	Floating stat bonus. Floating stat bonuses are the name of the game for half-elves, half-orcs, and humans, and they’re flexible enough to let you build any kind of mesmerist you want.
	None. Like gnomes, halflings, and half-orcs, half-elves are exceptional mesmerists.
	Great selection of favored class bonuses. Since half-elves count as both elves and humans, they can get 1) bonuses to feint builds from the elf favored class bonus, 2) bonuses to CL for the duration of enchantment spells, and 3) bonuses to their towering ego bonus...or some of all of the above! The ability to pick and choose from any of these three favored class bonuses is a huge perk for half-elves. 
	

	Adaptability. Adaptability (and the racial traits that replace it) is a fantastic racial feature. Skill Focus can help your Bluff score for feint and face builds, your Acrobatics score for vexing daredevils, or anything else you can think of. Moreover, ancestral arms, weapon familiarity, and dual-minded are all excellent replacements for adaptability, if you’re interested in replacing it.
	

	Multitalented. A lot of mesmerists will end up multiclassing, so getting favored class bonuses for both classes is a small but noticeable perk. Take blended view or fey thoughts if you’re not planning to multiclass.
	

	Low-light vision, but no darkvision.

# Halfling

Halfling


        Between gnomes, halflings, half-orcs, half-elves, and humans, the core races have no shortage of options that suit Dexterity and Charisma-based classes. And while that’s not a great fact of life for, say, Intelligence-based classes, the mesmerist is most definitely not an Intelligence-based class. It’s all upside, baby! Halflings make for excellent melee Dexterity-based fighters, with bonuses to the class’ two most important attributes and a penalty to Strength, which means nothing to a finesse combatant. Thanks to the fleet of foot racial trait, halflings can get all the bonuses of being a Small-sized race (better AC, attack rolls, Stealth checks, etc.) without sacrificing move speed. They’re not quite as good at spellcasting as gnomes, but frankly, who is?


Pros
	Cons
	Great stat bonuses. Bonuses to Dexterity and Charisma are exactly what most Weapon Finesse mesmerist builds need, and a penalty to Strength is largely irrelevant. Muleback cords, baby!
	No racial low-light vision or darkvision.
	Good racial traits. Fleet of Foot and Adaptable Luck are the only two alternatives that I would consistently recommend.
	

	Good favored class bonus. Extra mesmerist tricks is a pretty common favored class bonus, and it’s solid if not quite amazing.
	

	Small size. Since mesmerists don’t typically rely on large weapon die sizes to deal damage, Small races can compete much more readily. Bonuses to AC, attack rolls, and Stealth are all just fine by me.

# Half-Orc

Half-Orc


        Oof, but it’s difficult to pick between half-orcs and half-elves! Sacred tattoo + Fate’s Favored is an enormous boost to the mesmerist’s survivability that easily competes with the half-elf’s adaptability trait. The half-orc’s big advantage is that it’s the best damage dealer out of the core races, thanks to its phenomenal favored class bonus. Painful stare is the mesmerist’s primary source of damage, remember!


Pros
	Cons
	Floating stat bonus. Floating stat bonuses are the name of the game for half-elves, half-orcs, and humans, and they’re flexible enough to let you build any kind of mesmerist you want.
	None. I’m getting tired of saying it, but many of the core races are great as mesmerists. Except you, dwarves. You know what you did.
	Incredible favored class bonus. Adding half a point of damage to painful stare may seem like a small thing, but I can’t emphasize enough that it’s not! It adds on about 30% more damage to each painful stare you trigger with your own attacks, and 100% more damage if an ally triggers painful stare. If you want to deal optimal damage as a mesmerist, you need to be a half-orc, plain and simple.
	

	Great racial traits. Sacred tattoo combined with the Fate’s Favored trait is a classic combo to give you +2 to every save at 1st level, and while it’s a little cliche by now, it’s ruthlessly effective. You can trade away weapon familiarity, too—single-class mesmerists won’t have the Strength scores to take advantage of the racial weapons, and multiclass mesmerists will have picked up other weapon proficiencies from their dip. You get to pick what it’s replaced with.
	

	Darkvision.

# Human

Human


        Although humans are still one of the best all-arounder races in the pantheon of core races, they lack the specialization to match other races in one-on-one matchups. Half-orcs deal more damage; gnomes cast spells better; halflings make better swashbuckler wannabes; half-elves get superior favored class bonuses. If you really, really need that bonus feat, by all means, go with good old homo sapiens, but other races probably have you covered better if you can tighten your belt and introduce a little more austerity into the build.


Pros
	Cons
	Floating stat bonus. Floating stat bonuses are the name of the game for half-elves, half-orcs, and humans, and they’re flexible enough to let you build any kind of mesmerist you want.
	No racial low-light vision or darkvision.
	Bonus feat. You know it, you love it, and the haters love to hate it! Humans get a bonus feat at 1st level, and for the perpetually feat-starved mesmerist, that’s a big deal. I wish I could defer the free feat until about 7th level or so, but that’s another story…
	

	Free skill rank each level. For most mesmerist builds, Intelligence gets the short straw and ends up as your dump stat. That’s fine—to quote the inimitable Sondheim (RIP), you were raised to be charming, not sincere—but the lack of skill ranks does rankle sometimes.
	

	Decent favored class bonus. Towering ego is one of your best class features, so it makes sense that you’d want more of it. The only pity is that it caps out at 6th level, after which you’ll need to focus on skill ranks or HP. Bother.

# Nagaji

Nagaji


        Nagaji are descendents of the various races of naga, and like their serpentine ancestors, nagaji maintain some of the hypnotic qualities of snakes about them. For nagaji mesmerists, this fact is reflected in their favored class bonus, which allows them to use spells like hypnotic pattern on much higher-level enemies than they’d normally be able to. 	None. Nagaji could make interesting and effective enchanters or illusionists—a nice contrast with gnomes and kitsune.
	Good racial save bonuses. Bonuses against poisons help you out with your weak Fortitude save, and bonuses against mind-affecting effects help you maintain the emotion components of your spells.

Adding more HD on top of your mental potency class feature gives you the potential to be able to use effects like cause fear or color spray well past their normal expiration dates. Enemies will still outstrip the HD limits faster than you can raise your max HD, but give Paizo credit for trying.

# MES 240: Stares

MES 240: Stares


Before we get started on our bold stares, it’s worth recapping what the strengths and weaknesses of the class feature are. As far as strengths go, well, these are some incredibly strong debuffs, rivaled perhaps only by witch hexes, and they don’t require enemies to fail saves or you to pass spell resistance penetration checks—if you stare at someone, they’re debuffed. As far as weaknesses go, first and foremost bold stares are mind-affecting, so they cannot affect mindless creatures (vermin, e.g.) or other creatures that are immune to mind-affecting effects (constructs and undead, e.g.). Second, bold stares have a very short range, 30 feet, and automatically end of the target of your stare moves out of that radius. Third, bold stares always affect only one creature—no AoE debuffing for you unless you do it with spells. Alright! Let’s get to the ratings.

---

# Bold Stares

Bold Stares


Psychic Inception: Look, the mesmerist is a class that focuses on mind-affecting effects. Fully 45% of your spells are mind-affecting (247 spells out of 554 total spells), your hypnotic stare is mind-affecting, and your painful stare doesn’t work if your hypnotic stare doesn’t work. If you run up against creatures that are flat-out immune to mind-affecting effects, you will be of very little use to anyone. Why would you do that to yourself when Psychic Inception is right here, practically begging you to look into its eyes and say, “I do”? C’mon. You know you’re going to fight undead and constructs eventually. This is a tabletop RPG. Just do it.


Disorientation: Disorientation would easily earn the purple rating in this section if not for Psychic Inception, which gives you an idea of how powerful it is. Debuffing attack rolls is a time-honored tradition for witches with the Evil Eye hex, and it works just as well for the mesmerist. In conjunction with other debuffing (fear effects, for example) you can really start to stack up some nasty penalties.
Nightmare: Mesmerists have plenty of hard-hitting fear effects on their spell list, including castigate (cowering condition), mass castigate (AoE cowering condition), fear (AoE panicked condition), mantle of doubt (lots of debuffs), mortal terror (shaken condition scaling all the way up to helpless), and phantasmal killer (save-or-die). Since Nightmare is essentially a free Persistent Spell for every spell with the fear descriptor, this becomes terrible for enemies in a real hurry.
Sapped Magic: Mamma mia, that’s a spice meatball! Wow. Another very strong contender for the purple crown. Sapped magic pretty much tanks the offenses of any spellcaster of any variety, and if you think about it in terms of granting your allies a +2 or +3 bonus to all their saves and all their caster level checks to penetrate spell resistance, wouldn’t you take that ability without hesitation?
Sluggishness: Monster Reflex saves tend to be pretty poor as the CR tranches march on, and Sluggishness will only compound your enemies’ weakness. The movement speed penalty is decidedly meh; the Reflex save penalty, however, is decidedly not meh.


Distracted: The penalty won’t be stiff enough to give most full casters problems, but if you happen to have a fighter or other magehunter who likes to use Disruptive on your team, you can set up a nice little anticaster wombo combo.
Restriction: Dim light is a little more feasible for adventuring parties to work in than total darkness (right, Disquiet?), and lucky for you, Restriction actually has quite a good debuff attached. Forcing enemies to treat every square as difficult terrain means they can’t charge, can’t run, can’t 5-foot step...heck, some enemies have movement speeds of 5 feet, and they simply won’t be able to move at all. Makes kiting big bosses considerably easier.
Timidity: Penalties to damage rolls will ultimately mean a lot less than penalties to attack rolls. If you think about it, a -3 to attack means that enemies have a 15% lower chance to hit and deal any damage; a -3 to damage, however, might represent only a 5% damage penalty if an enemy is dealing 60 damage in a single hit. Timidity is by no means useless, but I’d prioritize other bold stares first.


Allure: This can be an effective bold stare if you’re interacting with a target and know that you’re about to backstab them or suddenly attack—debuffing Perception might make it easier for you to pass secret messages coordinating your allies, conceal weapons from a search, etc., and debuffing initiative obviously makes the target slower to react in combat, increasing the likelihood that you can catch them flat-footed and deal significant damage quickly. The issue with Allure is that it will do very little if you’re already in combat with creatures, which is the situation that most Pathfinder builds focus on.
Oscillation: The range is a little restrictive, but miss chances and concealment are usually solid enough to merit at least a yellow rating.
Susceptibility: Susceptibility isn’t awful, but with so few bold stares, are you really going to prioritize something that the mesmerist should be consistently excellent at doing without any help?
Unaided: A nice perk, but not critical unless you’re constantly fighting inquisitors, hunters, cavaliers, etc. who are giving themselves Outflank.


Disquiet: Adventuring parties tend not to fight in total darkness, which pretty much hamstrings Disquiet. If you’re a GM trying to create a terrifying antagonist, sure, create some devil with mesmerist levels, the see in darkness universal monster ability, and deeper darkness as an at-will spell-like ability. That’ll get scary in a hurry.
Infiltration: If I wanted to debuff Perception checks, I’d almost certainly choose Allure over Infiltration. Initiative penalties beat CMD penalties every time.
Lethality: Lethality, my foot. This is really meant to be a monster bold stare; poisons and diseases aren’t generally tools that PCs use to win fights.
Nightblindness: Wow, is that underwhelming. No, you do not want to reduce darkvision range by 10 feet.
Sabotage: Fun for roleplay purposes, but no. Maybe for GMs looking to take the wind out of a PC bard’s sails.
Sensed: Hmmm, but how are you going to even know that a creature is there if you can’t beat its Stealth check to spot it?

# Painful Stares

Painful Stares


Manifold Stare: Manifold Stare allows you to trigger your painful stare class feature more than once per round, and I’m going to go on record as saying that I think it’s the single most important feat for mesmerists to take. Yes, more than Intense Pain, and yes, more than Compounded Pain. My reasoning for this opinion is that mesmerists are simply not built to deal damage with their abilities: their weapon proficiencies are extremely limited; they basically have to wear light or medium armor, which locks most mesmerists into Dexterity builds that deal less damage than Strength builds; they don’t get any intrinsic bonuses to attack or damage rolls; their spell list isn’t well-suited to self-buffing; and they don’t have the BAB to use Power Attack, Piranha Strike, or Deadly Aim very effectively. So what’s a mesmerist to do? The simple answer is to rely on your painful stare class feature, which deals scaling precision damage on each hit against a foe, whether you’re doing the hitting or not. At 20th level, a mesmerist using Intense Pain can deal 9d6+10 precision damage on one hit; at that point, however, you’ll have two other iterative attacks—not to mention haste—and by default mesmerists can only deal their painful stare damage once per round. With each instance of Manifold Stare, however, you get the chance at an extra 9d6+10 precision damage, for a maximum of 36d6+40 with three feats invested. Or what about Blinding Stare and Confusing Stare? Oh, sure, enemies might pass their saves the first time around, but what about the second, third, and fourth attempts? Will they be so lucky then? All this to say: Manifold Stare is excellent whether you’re debuffing or dealing damage, and every mesmerist should take the full three feats at some point. My recommendation would be to grab the first around 5th level, then grab the next around 11th, and finally swap your 15th-level bold stare for the final instance. It’s a lot of investment, but it’s incredibly worth it.


Blinding Stare: Blinded is a phenomenally powerful status condition, and unlike many of the other stare feats, Blinding Stare allows you to affect the same enemy as many times as you want in 24 hours. Tied with Confusing Stare as the best debuffing stare feat.
Compounded Pain: Compounded Pain lets you add two Stare feat rider effects to your painful stare instead of one. It’s a late-game addition to the painful stare canon, but nevertheless a phenomenal ability, allowing you to deal more damage (Intense Pain + Penetrating Stare), debuff enemies even further (Blinding Stare + Confusing Stare), or some combination of the two. Essential for high-level mesmerists.
Confusing Stare: Oof, another incredibly hard-hitting debuffing stare. The fact that you get to pick which one of two d100 rolls you’d like enemies to take on the table further increases your chances of denying enemies the opportunity to act normally.
Intense Pain: Mesmerists are not known for being the most damaging class out there, but Intense Pain starts to turn the tables on that narrative, squeezing roughly 30% more damage out of painful stare over the course of your career. By 20th level, in fact, you’ll deal 9d6+10 precision damage with Intense Pain, which compares favorably with the rogue’s sneak attack progression, and with Compounded Pain + Manifold Stare, you can even start to deal rogue-like sneak attack damage on every hit if you want! This is how mesmerists were intended to deal damage, so plan to take Intense Pain, Compounded Pain, and Manifold Stare at some point in your development.


Disconcerting Stare: You can basically forget the Disable Device and Perception check penalties, although Perception penalties can be nice if you’re trying to sneak past someone. What you’re really here for is the 1-minute Reflex save penalty, which can be applied early in a battle and left to operate by itself. Paired with the Sluggishness bold stare, you can really tank monsters’ Reflex saves—and those already tend to be quite weak as CR tranches go by.
Excoriating Stare: As with Demoralizing Stare, how is this an 11th-level ability while Blinding and Confusing are only 7th? Anyway, sickened is a better status condition than shaken for a debuffer, so this isn’t totally wasted.


Bleeding Stare: The damage is pretty lackluster, which is unfortunate. Bleed effects are always nice sources of passive damage.
Demoralizing Stare: Real talk, though: how is Demoralizing Stare a higher-level feat than Blinding Stare or Confusing Stare? It makes no sense in the generally accepted meta of which status conditions are strongest. Anyway, you should have a number of ways to make people shaken through garden-variety Intimidate checks. No need to duplicate that here.
Fatiguing Stare: Fatigued is a mild status condition, and since it can’t ever progress to exhausted through this ability, I don’t think I need to give Fatiguing Stare a stellar rating.
Penetrating Stare: Naaah. I mean, okay, but naaah. Intense Pain is basically guaranteed to outperform Penetrating Stare in the long run, and against a wider variety of targets. About the only use for Penetrating Stare that I can think of is if you’re using the weaker, ally-triggered version of painful stare often—Intense Pain will not outperform Penetrating Stare then, at least not against targets with DR. I know which I prefer.
Perturbing Stare: You know what else denies enemies their Dexterity modifier to AC? The blinded condition. You know what causes the blinded condition? Blinding Stare. I suppose if you wanted to make an enemy with blindsight flat-footed? I’m not sure that niche case is worth a feat, though.
Venomous Stare: Between widespread poison immunities and sky-high Fortitude saves at 11th level and beyond, I doubt that Venomous Stare will do much for you, especially not when you can use it against a given enemy only once per day; I won’t deny that the ability damage is alluring, however.


Entwining Stare: Intended pretty much exclusively for monsters. PCs won’t often have the grab ability.
Overpowering Stare: This ability is basically a carbon copy of Confusing Stare, but way worse. Later entry (11th level, rather than 7th) and targets can’t be affected more than once per day. Nope! Just get Confusing Stare. Not like anyone is concerned about PFS-legal options anymore.

Devilbane Mundanity: Better at penetrating spell resistance, but come on, given the option between Sapped Magic and Mundanity, which is better? Sapped Magic, every time.

# MES 251: Mesmerist Tricks

MES 251: Mesmerist Tricks


Beyond touch treatment and native spell slots, a mesmerist’s tricks are what really help them contribute to an adventuring team. Selecting tricks is a delicate process because, barring one favored class bonus from the freaking merfolk race, mesmerists will never gain more tricks than their base progression gives them. It therefore behooves you to consult carefully with your party about what their needs are: do they often find themselves failing saves, and if so, which ones? Do they tend to get targeted by some attacks, but not others? Are certain status conditions more crippling to them than others? Figuring out the answers to these questions will often involve a deeper dive into another class’ mechanics than you perhaps bargained for, but believe me, the effort pays off! When your party’s wizard gets grappled without freedom of movement active, that could very, very easily turn into a PC death or even a TPK, depending on the strength of the wizard and the strength of the enemy. With Slip Bonds or Free In Body implanted in that ally, you can just whisk them out of harm’s way with a free action when it’s not even your turn. Or what about if your party’s barbarian is terminally susceptible to dominate person? Turning tail and running while Othmog the Unhinged gets that bloody glint in her eye is no one’s idea of a good time—but a quick stop at the Mental Fallback store ensures that that bad time stops before it even starts. Once you get to know your party and the tools they need on a regular basis, mesmerist tricks are some of the funnest “I veto your reality and substitute my own” powers. Heck, your GM’s facial expression when you act as bait for an enemy, then Spatial Switch yourself with your most lethal melee ally is worth the price of admission alone.

---

# Normal Tricks

Normal Tricks


Gift of Will: Thanks to towering ego and a strong base Will save progression, mesmerists will consistently have one of the highest Will saves in the game, at least when their emotion components are satisfied. Why not loan that Will save out to your martial colleagues? Allied fighters, barbarians, etc. will all be able to jump into combat with a lot more confidence if they know they’re not likely to fail a save against dominate person in the first round.


Astounding Avoidance: It’s evasion and stalwart, but as a trick! Extremely versatile and incredibly powerful, especially at 12th level when subjects take only half damage even on a failed save. You want this.™
Compel Alacrity: A great trick for ranged and spellcasting allies, who hate to get within threatened range. Compel Alacrity doesn’t allow any attacks of opportunity for movement, so you can just scoot them on out of there.
Mesmeric Mirror: Mmm, mirror image that’s triggered exactly when you need it most. That’s tasty. Definitely a top-shelf defensive trick.
Slip Bonds: Another fantastic defensive trick that will save you or allies from getting grappled, pinned, swallowed whole, tied up, etc. And at 12th level, you could even use it to slip out of a moving carriage, jail cell, etc., which makes it all the better.
Spectral Smoke: Spectral Smoke works really, really well if an ally has previously buffed the team with ashen path. Picture it: the boss thinks it’s safe to move in on an isolated target, but just as they get there, a fog cloud pops up, they can’t see, and the party closes in for the kill. And if they waste actions trying to disperse the fog, so much the better—it can’t be dispersed! It is an illusion, though, so don’t try this against foes with true seeing or somesuch active.


False Flanker: The trick that makes rogues your obedient lapdogs! Getting free flanking on even one full-attack action can be incredible for classes with sneak attack, so consider this trick very strongly if your team includes backstabbers and cutthroats.
Fleet in Shadows: It’s not like darkness or dim light are uncommon in Pathfinder, which makes Fleet in Shadows a solid tool for letting allies put on a burst of speed when they need it most.
Linked Reaction: It’s nice to implant this trick in both the most and least perceptive members of your party. This helps you act in surprise rounds more consistently (by stealing your more perceptive colleague’s Perception check) but also helps your allies act in surprise rounds more consistently (by stealing your Perception check).
Mask Misery: A fun trick for running out the clock on nasty status conditions that your allies might get tagged with, and there’s a Greater version in the masterful tricks that can even let you get up to the major status conditions handled by touch treatment.
Meek Facade: An interesting tanking trick that can force an enemy to focus on your strongest ally for 1 round, granting them considerable AC bonuses to avoid getting splattered.
Misdirection: An alternative to False Flanker for rogues and slayers, although Misdirection applies to only one attack instead of all attacks made that round.
Psychosomatic Surge: The healing doesn’t scale particularly well, but it’s HP when you (potentially) need it most.
Reflect Fear: Hmmm. Kind of like Mask Misery had a baby with an Intimidate check to demoralize. It’s a decent option for fights against creatures that you know will have unnatural presence or something similar.
Reflection of Weakness: Another trick that combines a little bit of defense with a little bit of debuffing. Will saves negate much of the ability, which keeps me from rating it blue, but it is solid.
Shadow Splinter: Redirecting damage from an ally to an enemy is cool, but what’s even cooler is if you can get an enemy to genuinely believe that their ally attacked them. Lots of potential there.


Chain of Eyes: Making you a “co-scout” is an interesting idea, but unless you plan to perform intrigue operations almost every day, Chain of Eyes won’t find enough uses to be worth purchasing.
Enchanting Words: The moments when mesmerists can’t make these checks themselves are probably few and far between, but I can think of at least a few times in War for the Crown that this trick would have been excellent.
Life Revier: Similar to Enchanting Words, but for Knowledge checks instead of Diplomacy and Intimidate checks. It’s fine.
Mesmeric Pantomime: The bonuses are likely to be quite large, but the relatively limited use of most Strength- and Dexterity-based skill checks hinders a stronger rating.
See in Darkness: Calling this “see in darkness” feels a little misleading, since this trick doesn’t actually allow anyone to see in supernatural darkness. But sure, darkvision is fine if anyone on the team needs it.
Telepathic Link: Maybe good for communicating in the moments before a fight breaks out, but shouting is usually fine when you’re in the throes of combat.
Vanish Arrow: Kind of like Snatch Arrows, but limited by your Sleight of Hand score. It’s neither ideal nor terrible.


Break Stupor: Fascination and sleep aren’t huge threats. I wouldn’t waste a trick known on this one.
Fearsome Guise: So much trouble, and at the end of it all you’re only performing an Intimidate check to demoralize. Blech.
Levitation Buffer: Combat maneuvers are not a strong use of mesmerist tricks.
Psychic Impression: Eh? I mean, you could just prep a spell like object reading or something to do this.
Umbral Shield: Definitely not intended for most PC mesmerists.
Unwitting Messenger: I can think of a few intrigue uses, but nothing that I would want to rely on regularly.
Voice of Reason: Visual illusions are way too rare to be worth investing in a trick.

# Masterful Tricks

Masterful Tricks


Cursed Sanction: Basically a reflexive bestow curse with a save DC that scales with your mesmerist level. This can be incredible for crippling bosses. Hit them with hypnotic stare to debuff their Will save, use Intimidate and a cruel weapon to make them shaken and sickened, add ill omen, and finally trigger Cursed Sanction to drop their attacks, saves, and skill checks right through the floor. I’d gladly pay the extra cost to implant this masterful trick.


Free in Body: A worthy upgrade to Slip Bonds, Free in Body is a prime trick to implant in ranged or spellcasting allies who simply must be outside of melee range in order to function.
Mental Fallback: Although Gift of Will is a better preventative option for allies with terminally low Will saves, Mental Fallback is a fantastic contingency that ensures no harm will come to you if your strongest warrior gets mind-controlled.
Spatial Switch: You can do a lot of fun stuff with Spatial Switch—a very sneaky, cunning, mesmerist-esque ability.
Vision of Blood: An absolutely vicious trick to pull with a crit-fishing ally (or you yourself, if you have a keen rapier, kukri, or something similar), since crits don’t allow your target a save against becoming stunned for 1 round. Once a target is stunned, you can steal their unattended weapon, hit them with boatloads of sneak attack damage, run around them without provoking attacks of opportunity, and more. The rogue will love this one.


Greater Mask Misery: As good as Mask Misery, which is to say pretty good. Getting upgraded to stronger status conditions is a nice touch.
Spell Anticipation: It’s basically a retaliatory spell that you get to cast if your ally gets targeted with a spell. Any strong debuff with a close or medium range will be appropriate—conditional curse is a classic for mesmerists.


Faked Death: Similar to mislead. Personally, I think you have better, cheaper tricks for protecting casters and other squishy allies that need to be protected, but the decision is yours.
Shadow Blend: The benefits seem a little anemic for this level, but they’re by no means useless.


Allay Pain: How common is nonlethal damage among the monsters you encounter? Be honest.
Avian Escape: Turning allies into Tiny-sized birds, while hilarious, is not a commonly used combat tactic for a reason.
Concealing Veil: With such a short duration, about the only thing that Concealing Veil could do is scoot you past a magical checkpoint where NPCs are inspecting the PCs for evil intent with detect evil.
Umbral Transformation: Short duration, and staggered afterward. I’ll pass.
Willful Ignorance: I guess there’s a point, but why wouldn’t the mesmerist be doing the lying?

# MES 282: Spells

MES 282: Spells


The mesmerist spell list is not the most diversified portfolio in the game, as you’ll see from the spell school counts below. Enchantment, illusion, and necromancy spells make up the bulk of your offensive casting, with a modest number of abjuration, transmutation, and divination spells mostly providing self- or team buffs. Fully 45% of the spells on the mesmerist list are mind-affecting, which makes it all the more essential that you take the Psychic Inception bold stare at 3rd level. It’s worth it just to be able to debuff mindless enemies with hypnotic stare, bold stare, and painful stare, but it’s also worth it because it allows you some chance (not a huge chance, but some) of affecting mindless creatures with mind-affecting spells. You want every bit of help you can get! Anyway, the breakdown of the number of spells you receive per magic school is as follows:


Abjuration: 34 spells
Conjuration: 27 spells
Divination: 66 spells
Enchantment: 178 spells
Evocation: 14 spells
Illusion: 137 spells
Necromancy: 49 spells
Transmutation: 48 spells
Universal: 1 spell

# Knacks

Knacks


Detect Magic: Absolutely every spellcaster needs access to detect magic, which is why it’s my pick for your 0-level purple spell. Go and find some traps and treasure.


Dancing Lights: Movable light is good, even if you do need to be concentrating in order to use it. Light is the other illumination cantrip that’s worth your time, so consider getting both in the long term.
Ghost Sound: Since enemies get a Will save to disbelieve your ghostly noisemaking, this cantrip will be of limited use as you increase in level, but it’s incredibly potent in the early game for distracting enemies, creating diversions, etc.
Light: Yup, every adventuring party needs light. Get either dancing lights or light at 1st level.
Mage Hand: A good spell for interacting with things that you’d rather not be standing right next to. The weight/force limit isn’t ideal, of course; if you want to pull mage hand-esque shenanigans, but with a higher weight cap, play an aether kineticist.
Message: Always good for covert communication between teammates.
Prestidigitation: Change the color of poisoned food, clean your filthy body, make portions of a wall or floor dirty or clean to mark danger or give yourself directions in a maze, amuse children...prestidigitation can do a lot if you’re creative, so have fun.


Open/Close: Good for opening doors and chests that you suspect may be trapped.


Detect Poison: Probably the best argument is for detecting poisonous creatures, but that’s pretty niche.
Detect Psychic Significance: Not impossible that this could come into play, just a little unlikely.
Read Magic: Someone else on the team will probably be responsible for reading magic, but it’s not an inherently bad spell.
Touch of Fatigue: Okay at low levels, but doesn’t scale at all due to the low save DC.
Unwitting Ally: Can be useful at low levels for preventing flanks, but the Will save obviates it in the long term.


Bleed: If an enemy is unconscious, they’re not a threat, and someone can always coup de grace them at their leisure.
Daze: Even with mental potency, daze is a bad spell. That Will save will be totally ineffective after a few levels.
Flare: The dazzled condition is weak, and enemies will almost never fail their Fortitude save.
Haunted Fey Aspect: So...it’s going to prevent 1 point of damage...once? That’s incredibly unworthy of a cantrip slot.
Lullaby: Very on-brand for mesmerists, especially since you do get some sleep abilities, but few enemies will fail their Will saves in the long run. If you want to debuff someone’s saves, use hypnotic/bold stare.

# 1st Level

1st Level


Hermean Potential / Ill Omen: Hermean potential and ill omen are two sides of the same coin, allowing allies to reroll a certain number of d20s, taking the higher result (hermean potential) or forcing enemies to reroll a certain number of d20s, taking the lower result (ill omen). In either case, these spells are easily your best-in-class for 1st level, and good candidates for a ring of wizardry (if your GM will allow a psychic caster to have such an item) or a quicken metamagic rod. Quickened ill omen + hypnotic stare + a save-or-suck spell can be a brutal combo if you have a round or two to set it up.


Anticipate Peril: A great, cheap spell for improving your initiative bonus. I’m always a fan of anticipate peril.
Charm Person: A classic for mesmerists, doubly so because of hypnotic stare’s Will save penalty. Feel free to swap out for charm monster at higher levels—the save DC will remain too low as you grow.
Color Spray: A real workhorse at lower levels, color spray drops off a bit in power as you level, a fact that mental potency can’t really do much about. Enjoy it at low levels, though, because the combination of blinded and unconscious is vicious, especially for those focusing on illusion spell save DCs early. A real encounter-ender.
Expeditious Retreat: Always a good self-buff for any level.
Faerie Fire: A classic (and relatively class-exclusive!) spell for eliminating concealment and invisibility, you will never find yourself sorry that you have faerie fire ready at a moment’s notice. Plus, no save!
Grease: An all-purpose battlefield control spell that scales pretty well as enemies get bigger and bigger (and their Acrobatics scores get worse and worse). 
Heightened Awareness: Heightened awareness and anticipate peril can stack to give you some enormous boosts to initiative when you really need them.
Obscuring Mist: Every low-level team needs someone with obscuring mist: it’s a great getaway and battlefield control spell, and if you later get access to ashen path, your entire team can abuse fog and mist tactics.
Ray of Enfeeblement: Pretty hefty penalties, even on a save for half. There are two tactics here: attack the strongest enemies (full martials) who will likely succeed on the save, or attack the weakest enemies (full casters, rogues) who will likely fail on the save. Both have their uses for blunting attack and damage rolls (martials) or for making encumbrance or paralysis a real, immediate problem (casters).
Silent Image: A classic in any illusionist’s repertoire, silent image is notable because enemies only receive a save to disbelieve the illusion if they physically interact with it. Be honest: if your character sees a wall in front of them, do they automatically suspect that it might be an illusion and attack it or toss a rock at it? Probably not. Where silent image is concerned, in many cases illusion might as well be reality.
Touch of Gracelessness: Between ray of enfeeblement and touch of gracelessness, mesmerists have the tools to penalize enemies’ physical ability scores quite effectively at 1st level. Fortitude saves for half (rather than Fortitude negates) will ensure that these spells stay at least somewhat relevant throughout the game.
Vanish: Limited-duration invisibility is exactly what the doctor is always ordering. You’d think she’d order something else once in a while, but nope, always limited-duration invisibility.


Auditory Hallucination: A better save DC than ghost sound, and you can confine the “sound” to the minds of specific enemies, rather than allowing anyone in range to hear (and potentially disbelieve) the sound. Good for intrigue purposes.
Bane: Bane has a lot of weaknesses, notably its Will save to negate and its mind-affecting/fear descriptors, but it’s a great AoE debuff at low levels.
Burst of Adrenaline: This spell would encompass Fortitude and Reflex saves, attack rolls, initiative rolls, etc., and the fatigued condition is quite mild. A decent choice for high-level play.
Comprehend Languages: A classic buff that helps smooth over tense negotiations with other creatures.
Deja Vu: Can be crippling to enemies at low levels if you catch them doing an inopportune action.
Delusional Pride: Not half-bad as a low-level combat debuff, actually, and it stacks with all your other debuffing modes. At higher levels, of course, you have much better options.
Disguise Self: Sure, it’s good enough for what it needs to do. Mesmerists are likely to be sneaky snakes who want to fool people like this.
False Face: Good for retaining a stable of identities that you can swap out to enact your various machinations. I know you’ve got at least a few. Don’t lie.
Hideous Laughter: An early-game save-or-suck spell that benefits from hypnotic stare.
Illusion of Calm: A nice spell for denying enemies attacks of opportunity for common spellcasting activities.
Innocence: A nice bard/skald exclusive that the mesmerist gets access to! Lies told only to convince others of your innocence are a little narrow, but this is a big enough skill bonus that I’m willing to overlook that.
Mental Block: The Will save at the end of each round prevents mental block from attaining blue status, but it’s still a brutal debuff for just about anyone. Heightened mental block can still be a big threat against martials at later levels, too.
Murderous Command: Burns enemies’ actions, fouls up enemies’ positioning, potentially deals damage. I can think of worse 1st-level spells.
Paranoia: Can be a pretty fun spell to tag a bulked-up martial enemy with, watching as they use reach weapons or natural reach to attack their own allies. The debuff also works well with flanking.
Psychic Reading: A good information-gathering spell.
Remove Fear: Extra useful for psychic casters like the mesmerist. Even outside that, remove fear helps your team immensely in some fights.
Shadow Trap: Shadow trap either entangles enemies and keeps them in place or it forces them to burn multiple rounds of actions trying to escape. I’m fine with either outcome.
Telempathic Projection: Charm person is better for direct charm offensives, but telempathic projection can boost skill checks where charm person can’t. It has its niche, to be sure.
Unnatural Lust: The short duration, save bonuses, and Will save to negate ultimately limit what unnatural lust can do, but it’s a great way to get enemies to burn their actions if they fail their saves.
Unprepared Combatant: Sluggishness is one of the better bold stare improvements, and combined with hypnotic stare and unprepared combatant, you can hit enemies with a -3 to Will saves, a -7 to Reflex saves, and a -4 to initiative without much trouble. Best used before a fight starts.
Unseen Servant: A timeless spell, unseen servant, and actually pretty good for triggering traps, serving you tea, etc.
Ventriloquism: Unlike silent image, which must be interacted with in order to be disbelieved, ventriloquism grants an immediate save to anyone who hears you throwing your voice. Enemies likely won’t fall for your puppet stand-up routine, but you never know.


Adoration: A +2 morale bonus to Diplomacy checks isn’t even really worth a 1st-level spell, but it’s functional, at least.
Animate Rope: I like that there’s no save, but you have much better ways to debuff people than animate rope.
Aphasia: Aphasia’s best use is as an anticaster tool, but even with your hypnotic stare’s Will save penalty, casters won’t have much trouble passing their saves when they get the opportunity for a new save each round.
Assumed Likeness: Okay for social encounters in the early game, but the bonuses and Will save don’t scale well.
Beguiling Gift: Interesting for intrigue applications. Offering enemies objects imbued with nodes of blasting is an oldie, but a goodie.
Blurred Movement: Decent for avoiding some attacks of opportunity, but otherwise not very useful.
Bungle: Kind of like an inverse true strike. Unfortunately, the Will save to negate and HD cap make ill omen a better choice in nearly every situation.
Call Animal: Good for nipping low-level animal encounters in the bud, but not much more than that.
Compulsive Liar: Again, very on-brand for mesmerists, but the intrigue applications are probably limited.
Coward’s Cowl: Eh. I think there are easier, more reliable means of getting a +1 to some checks.
Crime of Opportunity: A fun, fun spell for mesmerists to frame a rival with. Could make a good distraction, too.
Demand Offering: Not terrible, but also not great.
Detect Undead: You’ll know whether you’re in a game that would benefit from detect undead.
Fastidiousness: Even thought fastidiousness has basically zero combat uses, it’s one of those quality of life spells that you’d genuinely want if you were a real-life adventurer.
Fool’s Gold (AA): Fool’s gold + node of blasting is a classic spell combo: offer someone a bribe, then watch as they get debuffed through the floor and hit with a bunch of psychic damage. It’s pretty limited otherwise.
Fumblestep: Tengu-only. It might work a few times at low levels, but won’t scale well into the endgame.
Fumbletongue: Between the mind-affecting tag, the short duration, and the Will save to negate, fumbletongue isn’t the world’s most effective anticaster spell. For 1st level, though, it’s not terrible.
Implant Urge: Implant urge is fine for intrigue applications.
Interrogation: I guess it’s a good way to get your hands on some answers when you need them most. It’s evil, though.
Jitterbugs: The debuff is strictly okay.
Linked Legacy: I feel like this is something that GMs mostly allow players to do for free, but if not…?
Polypurpose Panacea: Fun, but not mechanically powerful.
Ray of Sickening: There are plenty of more powerful debuffs available.
Recharge Innate Magic: If you have some spell-like abilities, sure.
Shadowfade: Essentially 1st-level invisibility with some strings attached. Not terrible, but you may as well wait for invisibility.
Sleep: Sleep can end encounters in the first few levels, but its scaling is abysmal, even with mental potency. Lose it at your first opportunity if you do take it.
Sow Thought: Permanent duration on a 1st-level spell is interesting, and while I don’t think you’d be able to use this spell effectively against monsters (the save DC won’t scale well enough) it can remain useful against commoners and other low-level pawns that you want to manipulate and incept at will.


Detect Charm: Sure, why not? People under the influence of compulsion effects can be trouble. Stick with a scroll, though.
Detect Secret Doors: A single scroll or wand with a few charges will let you just wander through a floor of a dungeon, pinging for secret doors. That’s enough.
Diagnose Disease: Ideal as a scroll or wand with a few charges.
Healing Thief: Mesmerists get this spell at a considerable discount, relative to other classes, but even so it’s best as a scroll spell for stealing fast healing or regeneration.
Invigorate: A scroll is all you’d need to keep an exhausted ally functional for one combat.
Magic Aura: A scroll or wand, if it’s anything.
Mindlink: Easily done as a scroll.
Obscure Object: Again, you’d only need a scroll or wand, even if these effects were ever of interest to you.
Remove Sickness: The target cap never scales like remove fear, which makes it a little more appropriate for scrolls and wands.
Share Language: The very definition of a wand/scroll spell.
Speechreader’s Sight: A good scroll if you’re in an intrigue-heavy game.
Thought Echo: As speechreader’s sight—a good scroll for an intrigue game.
Undetectable Alignment: With basically no effects that scale with CL, undetectable alignment is about as good a wand spell as you could hope for. Perfectly functional at CL 1.
Vocal Alteration: Fine as a scroll. You definitely don’t need it as a spell known.


Blend: Elves aren’t terribly good mesmerists, and blend isn’t a terribly good spell.
Burst of Insight: Although burst of insight mimics burst of adrenaline, the dazed condition here is head and shoulders more crippling to you than the fatigued condition in burst of adrenaline. Can’t recommend.
Cause Fear: Cause fear is not eligible for mental potency, which makes it all the worse. Mind-affecting, fear, spell resistance, Will negates, HD cap. This is about as bad as a debuff can get.
Charge Object: I’m having real trouble seeing the point.
Command: Language-dependent, mind-affecting, Will negates, spell resistance, limited action options. Nah.
Compel Hostility: Mesmerists weren’t built to tank hits.
Confusion, Lesser: One target, for one round? Nope.
Decrepit Disguise: What’s the point of disguising an item as a crappy version of itself?
Discern Next of Kin: Why would you…?
Disguise Weapon: Disguising a weapon as a different kind of weapon—now there’s the world’s best idea.
Doom: You have way, way better debuffing options.
Echo: Again, I fail to see the point.
Egorian Diplomacy: If you’re going to allow an enemy a Will save to negate, use charm person instead.
Fool’s Gold (VC): Uhhh...for cheating shopkeeps out of their wares, I guess?
Forbid Action: The ability lasts only one round, which isn’t nearly enough to be worthwhile.
Forced Quiet: Very niche bonuses with a short duration.
Hide Bruises: Truly a poor spell. Can’t think why you’d want this.
Hobble: Saves every round, and you can’t even block 5-foot steps. Blergh.
Hypnotism: I know, I know, you’ve got mental potency. Hypnotism is still an exceptionally bad spell. Use sleep if you want a low-level save-or-suck.
Instant Portrait: In case you need to paint...a picture of someone?
Intensify Psyche: Small bonuses to some skill checks, but some pretty nasty penalties to saves.
Lock Gaze: For PCs without gaze attacks, this seems unneeded.
Magic Mouth: Pretty useless.
Memorize Page: When would you ever need this?
Memory Lapse: An exceptionally short range of wiped memories doesn’t bode well for this spell.
Mirror Mantis: Seems like a lot of hassle to make someone shaken.
Obscure Poison: Don’t use poisons.
Opportunistic Loyalty: Very circumstantial, and a Will save negates, to boot.
Quintessence: What’s the use here?
Reveal Secrets: Is there something here that you couldn’t learn through discern lies or similar questioning methods?
Songbird: Meant to be a bard spell, not a mesmerist spell.
Theft Ward: Not an adventuring spell for fairly obvious reasons.
Wizened Appearance: Highly unnecessary.

# 2nd Level

2nd Level


Mirror Image: Mirror image is one of the best defensive spells in the game, and should easily clock in at the top of your list of shopping items for 2nd level. Don’t think you can get out of mirror image if you have the Mesmeric Mirror trick, either—you ideally want both.


Alter Self: Alter self is ideal for snagging new movement and vision modes, natural attacks, and mild attribute bonuses. You could do much worse for a 2nd-level spell.
Animus Mine: Mind-affecting effects are hideously common in Pathfinder; animus mine therefore represents either one of the best damage-dealing debuffs you have in your arsenal (if enemies haven’t been bitten yet) or one of the best deterrents against them intruding on your will again (if they’re twice shy). Coupled with towering ego, any enemy that tries to hit you with a mind-affecting effect is likely to regret their life choices instantly.
Anticipate Thoughts: Especially coupled with hypnotic stare, anticipate thoughts is a brutal, brutal thing to do to a martial enemy with a low Will save. You can easily scale up to a +5 bonus to AC, attack rolls, and damage rolls on the regular if you’re careful about your target selection.
Aversion: One enemy becomes sickened or nauseated for days/level in a particular location. If a boss fails their save against aversion in their throne room, it’s game over.
Blindness/Deafness: Your earliest magehunting tool, and one that will serve you faithfully throughout your career. It’s just a pity that there aren’t any bold stares to let you debuff Fortitude saves.
Focused Scrutiny: The spell has an excellent duration and excellent, untyped skill bonuses. Great for mesmerists acting as the party face, especially since the largest bonuses are reserved for Wisdom-based skills that you wouldn’t innately be great at.
Glitterdust: Fun fact: mesmerists are the only class that receives both faerie fire and glitterdust. They’re both great spells, and they’re actually not mutually exclusive! Faerie fire has long range; glitterdust has medium range. Faerie fire hits a 5-foot radius burst; glitterdust hits a 10-foot radius spread. Faerie fire’s penalty to Stealth checks is -20; glitterdust’s is -40. Faerie fire negates concealment; glitterdust blinds enemies. Faerie fire has no save, but allows spell resistance; glitterdust has a save, but doesn’t allow spell resistance. You could honestly get both in the long run, and I wouldn’t even be mad.
Hold Person: Normally I’d give hold person a green rating, since it allows a Will save every round to negate, but you’re a debuffing machine: enemies should regularly fail Will saves if you want them to fail Will saves. You’ve got Persistent Spell, hypnotic stare, ill omen, mad hallucination, and tons more goodies to ensure that enemies can be held when you want them to be. Cast on a big, dumb martial and then watch as your big, dumb barbarian friend delivers a coup de grace while they’re paralyzed.
Honeyed Tongue: An excellent social interaction spell, although you need to ensure that your bonuses are high enough that you can’t fail so badly that you alienate someone.
Howling Agony: Hard to believe this spell isn’t evil, but there you have it. Targets either need to take some fairly stiff penalties or else become effectively staggered for rounds/level, and you can affect a bunch of creatures. Those material components, though...good lord.
Inflict Pain: A -4 to all attack rolls is nothing to sneeze at, especially in conjunction with your Disorientation bold stare. What’s more, you get one round of full effect even if they save!
Investigative Mind: An amazing spell for crushing Intelligence-based skill checks. If you have an Intelligence-based class on your team, though, you might still want to leave this role to them. You have more important things to focus on.
Invisibility: What 2nd-level blue spell list would be complete without invisibility? You know it. You love it. You’ll dang well take it.
Minor Image: Mesmerists are great at illusion magic, so getting minor image should be a shoo-in for the class at some point.
Mortal Terror: A much easier way of stacking fear conditions on an enemy than the Intimidate skill. Pair with emotive block for extra fun with spellcasters.
Perceive Cues: Between honeyed tongue, perceive cues, investigative mind, glibness, focused scrutiny, and more, there’s a great argument to be made that mesmerists may be even better party faces than bards and skalds—and that’s hard to do.
Phantasmal Affliction: AKA bestow curse for illusionists. The double saves to negate any of the conditions are...unfortunate, but its lower spell level and the fact that you can do so much with phantasmal affliction (everything from immediate penalties on attack rolls and saves to ticking ability damage to days/level ability damage) helps compensate for being less powerful than poison, bestow curse, or any of the other effects it replicates.
Placebo Effect: Placebo effect is essentially your touch treatment class feature as a spell. In the long run, you’ll be able remove all of these status conditions with no spell support, of course, but in the early levels, being able to suppress conditions like stunned, blinded, paralyzed, or panicked is hugely important. A good candidate for retraining once you get higher levels of touch treatment.
Pyrotechnics: A classic AoE battlefield control and debuffing spell. The save effects will likely start to lose effectiveness as levels go by, but the smoke cloud never will. Remember to have someone on your team grab ashen path so that you’re not affected.
Touch of Idiocy: No save for a 1d6 penalty to Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma. Crazy good. If you’ve ever fought a crysmal at low level, you know how destructive touch of idiocy can be.
Twilight Haze: At least as good as fog cloud, which is already quite good. Battlefield control that can’t be pushed away with gust of wind is always nice.
Unadulterated Loathing: It’s aversion, but for people instead of places. Both nice options to create the nauseated condition in your foes.


Blistering Invective: Blistering invective has been one of my flavor-favorites in Pathfinder since I wrote my first guide to the inquisitor class, and it’s still a chestnut. Watch out for that language-dependent tag, though—it’s not a great spell unless you’re fighting other humanoid creatures regularly.
Blur: Miss chances from concealment will serve you better in the endgame than AC bonuses, so blur remains a relevant defensive spell more or less permanently. True seeing will beat it, of course, but that doesn’t become common until you’re fighting at 15th level or higher.
Calm Emotions: Useful for quieting unruly mobs, countering bards or barbarians, and generally being a nuisance to creatures who rely on morale bonuses.
Castigate: A fun inquisitor-exclusive spell, now yours for a song! Castigate has an almost ludicrously small list of viable targets, between the “Target: one living creature” text and the emotion, fear, language-dependent, and mind-affecting tags, but if an enemy fails their save, they’re screwed for at least one round, possibly more.
Cat’s Grace: Mesmerists are a primarily Dexterity-based class. On the one hand, that means mesmerists love Dexterity bonuses; on the other hand, that means that they’ll likely be purchasing a belt of incredible dexterity at their earliest opportunity, the enhancement bonus of which conflicts with cat’s grace. It will remain a relevant buff for practically anyone on the team, though, so it’s a good spell to have throughout the game.
Delay Pain: There are only 25 spells in Pathfinder 1e with the pain descriptor—that’s 25 out of 3039 total spells—but those that are on the list are absolutely brutal debuffs, usually necromancy effects, that can easily cripple a PC. I won’t say no to hours/level protection.
Delay Poison: Like delay pain, delay poison can help you ignore not only contact or injury poisons that deal ability damage, but also any spell, spell-like ability, extraordinary or supernatural ability, etc. with the poison descriptor, which, spoiler alert, is a lot of them.
Detect Magic, Greater: Probably the best use of greater detect magic is the ability to match spellcasters with their spells even when you don’t see the casting.
Diminish Resistance: Not every fight will need debuffing of energy resistances, but it’s nice trick to have at need.
Disfiguring Touch: Fairly mild debuffs, especially when you compare to something like bestow curse, but that’s why it’s not getting a blue rating.
Eagle’s Splendor: As cat’s grace. You don’t get bull’s strength, fox’s cunning, or any of the other stat-boosting 2nd-level spells, which probably tells you all you need to know about how Paizo intended this class’ attributes to be distributed.
Emotive Block: Way, way better than cognitive block, for the simple reason that emotion components are much more easily exploited than thought components. No wizard is going to get scared when you demoralize them with Enforcer, but then when you hit them with hypnotic stare and emotive block and it becomes apparent that they’re not going to be able to cast any spells for the duration of the shaken condition, they’ll start pooping their pantaloons.
False Life: A proactive cure moderate wounds. You could do worse.
Haunting Mists: Haunting mists has a markedly shorter range than fog cloud, its closest cousin, but the chance for debuffing helps offset that weakness. I’ll never pass up more debuffing in this class.
Heckle: Heckle is a fairly solid debuff that creates shaken-esque penalties but also stakes with shaken, sickened, etc. Mind-affecting, single-target, and Will negates, but otherwise solid.
Mad Hallucination: The penalty to Will saves is what we’re after here. Hypnotic stare + ill omen + mad hallucination can start a fight with a -5 penalty, and that’s tough stuff for enemies to recover from.
Oneiric Horror: Oneiric horror has a bit of an edge over hold person where target selection is concerned, but if you think about it, the two spells are quite similar: both involve targets being able to take no offensive actions for rounds/level, both are mind-affecting, and both offer a Will save to break the effect at the end of each round. Personally, I like hold person better at this level, since paralyzed is a much stronger condition while it lasts than fatigued, but greater oneiric horror at 3rd level will start to turn that narrative around. If you’re not fighting primarily humanoid opponents, though oneiric horror is better than hold person.
Oppressive Boredom: Oppressive boredom is most similar to oneiric horror and hold person: targets can’t take any actions until they succeed at a Will save, but they’re not paralyzed or anything like that. Better target selection than hold person in exchange for a weaker debuff.
Pilfering Hand: My favorite use for pilfering hand is to disarm casters of their component pouches, spellbooks, holy symbols, wands, scrolls, etc. You can even ready it to trigger whenever an enemy draws an item.
Rage: The bonuses you receive from rage won’t be universally desirable to other classes, and the fact that it requires concentration bumps it down a notch. Still a decent party buff.
Reckless Infatuation: A fantastic spell for inflicting a perma-staggered condition on someone if your team can coordinate battlefield control or other spells to keep them separated. Unadulterated loathing is better, though.
Shadow Anchor: Shadow anchor is actually quite a good enemy control spell, but the fact that it’s only for wayangs and requires a successful touch attack limits how good it can get.
Shamefully Overdressed: A good spell for getting humanoids to voluntarily give up their wondrous items. Of little use against other types of creatures, though.
Silence: Great for shutting down verbal components and sneaking about.
Suggestion: An oldie, but a goodie. Enchantment spells are what the mesmerist is all about.


Babble: Aversion is generally more powerful for 2nd-level debuffing, but babble also isn’t terrible.
Bestow Insight: Kind of like a supercharged aid another action for skill checks. I don’t know that this would be worth a 2nd-level spell slot to me all that often, but it’s also not a bad spell.
Build Trust: A less-obvious version of charm person, and one that will work surprisingly well on low-level commoners and other NPCs, especially with the benefit of hypnotic stare. Still a bit niche, though.
Cognitive Block: Thought components are far less devastating to spellcasters than emotion components, since they can still do a 5-foot step, center as a move action, cast a spell as a standard action, cast a quickened spell as a swift action. If you staggered them, maybe? But that’s very unlikely.
Confess: Fun from a flavor perspective, but mechanically weak enough that it doesn’t earn a stronger rating.
Deflect Blame: The ultimate “wasn’t me!” trick. But not too useful outside of intrigue uses.
Delay Disease: An investigator/alchemist favorite for use with alchemical allocation, the delay spells vary in usefulness depending on how crippling their effects are. Diseases are ultimately a minor nuisance in Pathfinder 1e, not anything life-threatening, so delay disease is pretty limited.
Detect Thoughts: More relevant than detect anxieties on a day-to-day basis.
Euphoric Cloud: Poison immunity is a concern, as well as the fact that fascinated is a weak status condition. Not utterly useless, though.
Hidden Presence: The advantage that hidden presence has over invisibility is that hidden presence can shut down blindsight, tremorsense, etc., whereas invisibility will be detected right away. The disadvantage of hidden presence is that it’s a low-level spell with a lowish spell save DC, and most of the enemies who would detect you with blindsight or tremorsense will be high-level creatures capable of passing those saves. If this is really, truly what you want to do, I’d recommend the Dampen Presence feat instead. Maybe you’re an enigma mesmerist who wants to be really stealthy?
Instigate Psychic Duel: This is more of the psychic class’ jam than yours, but you can still do it fairly well. The binary mindscape rules are complex, though.
Jealous Rage: Seems a bit convoluted for a debuff, but I don’t know your tastes.
Levitate: Jump into the sky, Guy / Just rise off your feet, Pete / Stay in the air, Claire / Gotta levitate, Kate / There must be fifty ways to learn to hover. Thanks, xkcd. Anyway, levitate isn’t a bad spell, it just doesn’t have many combat uses. Fly will become common soon enough anyway.
Mark of Spite: Mesmerists at least have more HP for these tactics than the other full casters on the list, but it still seems like a waste of resources (both spells and HP) in most circumstances. Regarding the Fortitude save, I would assume that the save negates only the initial mark, not your ability to deal damage each round thereafter.
Open Book: Open book is permanent, which can be a fantastic means of performing a magical background check on someone, monitoring whether they’re planning to betray you, etc. It’s good enough that I won’t even rate it black!
Painful Revelation: A good way for dedicated illusionists to deal some damage even on an enemy’s successful save to disbelieve a figment.
Psychic Leech: If the attribute bonuses were any other type than enhancement, I might consider at least a green rating. As it is, the juice isn’t particularly worth the squeeze.
Shifted Steps: Has some intrigue or combat applications for dedicated illusionists.
Stricken Heart: A guaranteed round of the staggered condition pulls it up from the basement. This would probably be a choice spell for a phantom blade spiritualist with spellstrike and spell combat, but it’s not great for the mesmerist.
Suppress Charms and Compulsions: Charm and compulsion effects are generally bad. Why not protect against them?
Violent Accident: Hear me out: violent accident is actually a lot of fun. No, it’s not mechanically optimal for straight combat, but 5d8 points of damage will outright kill nearly any 1st-level NPC, and because there’s no save to resist its initial effects and it has long range, you can essentially get away with murdering people commoners scot-free. I mean, I’m not encouraging murder, I’m just saying that if you wanted to murder NPCs, this is how you could do it without ever getting caught.


Alpha Instinct: With a long duration, niche purpose, and no other CL-dependent effects, alpha instinct is an ideal scroll spell for wilderness campaigns. It’s no spell known.
Animal Messenger: Doesn’t need to be a spell known; I’d say a scroll will suffice.
Apport Object: A scroll or wand is what’s needed here.
Codespeak: It’s like when Rusty and Danny Ocean pull a Lost In Translation on Linus in Ocean’s 12. Fun, but unnecessary except in intrigue scenarios.
Commune with Birds: Okay, sure, a scroll could be fine.
Conditional Favor: I actually quite like conditional favor, but it’s not nearly common enough to be a spell known. Get a scroll if you want.
Detect Anxieties: For intrigue uses, I suppose.
Detect Mindscape: Seems like something a psychic spellcaster should be able to do, but you won’t do it nearly often enough to need more than a scroll.
Enchantment Sight: Sometimes people are dominated or charmed. I wouldn’t mind having this permanency-ed so that you can tell when other people are tampering with your meat puppets.
Enshroud Thoughts: Animus mine will be a much more effective deterrent in many cases, but enshroud thoughts isn’t bad.
Hidden Knowledge: Uhhh...if you need to pass a polygraph test?
Hidden Speech: More intrigue shenanigans.
Knock: A great wand to have if there’s no Disable Device expert on the team.
Locate Object: It’s a pretty niche spell.
Mantle of Calm: Maybe as a scroll? You don’t face rage effects often enough to make sense as a spell known.
Mind Over Matter: I certainly wouldn’t use mind over matter offensively—too slow, too many failure points. Instead, use it to help allies recover from ability damage faster (by distributing 8 points in one attribute to 4 points in two attributes, e.g.) or make really inconvenient penalties into relatively more convenient penalties (Strength to Intelligence for a fighter, e.g.).
Mindlocked Messenger: Great for intrigue games, so you’ll have to decide whether that’s right for you.
Object Reading: I’d leave this to occultists, generally, but you can get a scroll if you’re interested.
Passing Fancy: Again, okay for intrigue scenarios.
Restoration, Lesser: The fact that you have lesser restoration on your spell list just means you should plan to get a wand for it. Let other divine casters handle this as a spell known.
Share Language, Communal: As share language.
Share Memory: You wouldn’t want it as a spell known, but as a scroll it’s fine.
Silent Table: Even if you needed this, it’d be a scroll.
Sympathetic Aura: As magic aura.
Tongues: As much as I love tongues—and someone on your team definitely needs access to it—you don’t necessarily have to learn it as a spell known, especially with so many other spells competing for your slots.
Voluminous Vocabulary: Probably superseded by tongues in many cases, but you never know when you may need to read or write, too.
Whispering Wind: With sending coming up at 3rd level, I can’t recommend whispering wind too highly.


Absurdity: The juice simply isn’t worth the squeeze, I’m afraid. Don’t debuff your team just to ignore some types of fear effects.
Animal Trance: Somehow even worse than hypnotism, and with that spell you practically have to dig a hole just to set the bar so low.
Anonymous Interaction: The criminally small amount of time you can erase from targets’ minds is the torpedo that sinks this spell.
Blood in the Water: Meant for sahuagin, not PCs.
Burning Gaze: Burning gaze has got to be in contention for one of the worst 2nd-level spells in Pathfinder. And it’s too bad, because it’s such a cool concept! No scaling damage, Fortitude negates, spell resistance, and a short duration all drive nails into its coffin.
Catatonia: Why would you…?
Compassionate Ally: You have much better ways of destroying enemies than compassionate ally.

# 3rd Level

3rd Level


Bestow Curse / Conditional Curse: Okay, okay, wait! Hear me out. Look: bestow curse is one of the best debuffs in the game, and certainly the best debuff in the game for its level. Let’s make that clear. But there’s actually a very subtle trap here, in that mesmerists are the only class in the game to receive both bestow curse and conditional curse at the same spell level. Conditional curse is a notable improvement over bestow curse for two reasons: first, it’s got better range—close range instead of touch makes a huge difference. Second, the DC to remove the curse with magic is markedly harder for enemies to hit with conditional curse. The fact that you must specify some condition to be met within a year and a day is pretty trivial for PCs; enemies will either die in the first few seconds of your encounter with them, or else they’ll run away and likely break the curse anyway. For this class only, then, you want conditional curse, not bestow curse. You’ll likely want to choose the -4 to attack rolls, saves, skill checks, and ability checks option so that you can continue to layer on debuff after debuff until the enemy is a pile of quivering jelly.


Audiovisual Hallucination: Kind of like an enemy-targeted minor image. Great for mesmerist illusionists.
Calamitous Flailing: Toss this spell on the creature with the most potent natural or weapon attacks, and watch as they run around braining their own allies and themselves.
Charm Monster: Days/level and no type or size restrictions make charm monster an easy contender for the purple title at 3rd level.
Confusion: AoE confusion never, ever gets old. Toss it on a crowd full of weak-willed minions, close the door, and come back in 30 seconds to mop up the remains.
Crushing Despair: I wish mesmerists got good hope, too, but I get why that would be OP. Anyway, crushing despair is an excellent AoE debuff. I love how many tools this class gets for completely hamstringing crowds of enemies.
Dispel Magic: Dispel magic is a ridiculously flexible magical tool that can do everything from removing debuffs from your team to removing buffs from an enemy to removing summoned creatures from the field. Multiple casters on every team should have access to this spell.
Displacement: Blur’s older, better-looking cousin, displacement is a top-shelf defensive spell that will keep you going strong in the endgame, especially in conjunction with mirror image and the Mesmeric Mirror trick.
Enchantment Foil: Here’s a neat fact: there are 116 spells with the emotion or fear descriptor in Pathfinder, 72 of which are enchantment effects; necromancy and illusion come in second and third place with 24 and 12 spells, respectively. It stands to reason, then, that if you want to protect your emotion components, which regulate not only your spellcasting but also when you receive your towering ego bonuses, your best bet is to guard against enchantment spells. Enchantment foil gives you a +4 untyped bonus against enchantment spells for hours/level (I’d recommend a lesser metamagic rod of Extend Spell so that you can bump that up to two hours/level) and even lets you trick enemy casters who think you’ve been successfully taken out of the picture. Pair with animus mine to turn yourself into a grim, forbidding mental bulwark.
Fear: Fear finally hits the sweet spot that scare and cause fear couldn’t touch. Multiple targets, no HD caps, better status conditions, and a partial effect even on a successful save. One of necromancy’s better 3rd-level debuffs.
Glibness: Having played a bard to a decently high level before, I freaking love glibness. It’s actually kind of wasted on bards and skalds, though, who are interested in replacing as many skills as possible—including Bluff—with their Perform skills via versatile performance. Not here! Your Bluff score is already likely to be one of the best in the game, and once you add an untyped +20 to tell lies, reality is whatever you say it is. Get someone to look at the word “gullible” written on the ceiling...twice.
Invisibility Sphere: An easy way for the entire party to stealth ahead, provided you all stick together.
Major Image: An illusion mesmerist’s best friend! Major image is undeniably the top figment at this level, so enjoy being able to make enemies believe whatever you want them to believe.
Mydriatic Spontaneity: Mydriatic spontaneity is a bit of an odd duck on the mesmerist spell list. Evocation magic with no mind-affecting tag, and yet a Will save to negate? Huh. Anyway, a failed save will make an enemy nauseated for rounds/level, which is pretty much game over, and careful cultivation of light levels might also tag on the blinded condition. Pair with motes of dusk and dawn for best effect.
Node of Blasting: Node of blasting is a super versatile trap spell, and even though only occultists get the cheapest spell level, it’s also not a bad idea for mesmerists. Throw it on coins that you use for bribes, then attack while bribed enemies are staggered; cast it on wires or filaments that you wrap around door handles, weapons, etc. Lots you can do if you prepare, especially since the nodes are permanent until discharged.
Overwhelming Grief: Overwhelming grief beats out greater oneiric horror by a fairly wide margin. First, it doesn’t have greater oneiric horror’s restriction to living creatures, so you can use the Psychic Inception bold stare to land it on constructs and undead, albeit with the usual save bonus and 50% chance of ignoring the effect for the round. Second, causing an enemy to lose their Dexterity bonus and take a penalty to AC is generally better than the small amount of Strength damage an enemy would take from greater oneiric horror. One of your top single-target enchantment debuffs at 3rd level.
Scrying: Scrying is your first foray into long-distance arcane espionage, and it doesn’t disappoint. This shouldn’t necessarily be a mesmerist’s role in a party, but it’s nice to know the option is here.
See Invisibility: The rare moment when bards, skalds, and mesmerists get shafted on spell level—3rd, rather than 2nd. It’s still a 100% necessary spell for any Pathfinder PC to have access to. Get scrolls if you’re trying to save on spells known.
Spite: The rare witch-exclusive spell, now available to you! Touch of gracelessness, shadow anchor, touch of idiocy, bestow curse, vampiric touch, and synapse overload are probably the best offensive, touch-range debuffs that you can store with spite. Watch out for that material component, though.
Synaptic Pulse: So...don’t use this when there are allies around, because you’ll probably stun them, too. But if you’re alone and surrounded, synaptic pulse can absolutely level a crowd of enemies. 
Synesthesia: Lots of debuffs here: half speed movement, 20% miss chance, -4 to AC, skill checks, and Reflex saves, forced concentration checks, resistance to Blind-Fight or true seeing...lots to like here. One of the best single-target illusion debuffs at 3rd level.
Vampiric Touch: With no save and fairly potent healing, vampiric touch is a good spell to keep you strong in the face of melee enemies.


Assume Appearance: Great for long-term infiltration as an imposter.
Aura of the Unremarkable: Fun for making normal people totally okay with all of the illegal things you like to do.
Aura Sight: Better than detect evil in a lot of ways.
Clairvoyance/Clairaudience: One of your very first magical spying abilities. Enjoy!
Demanding Message: A silent communication with a suggestion attached is nice, but I’m not sure that suggestion isn’t better.
Gullibility: Kind of like an enemy-targeted glibness. I’m knocking it down a rating because enemy-targeted anything features a save to negate and some kind of spell resistance check.
Hostile Levitation: A movement and attack debuff, rolled into one. Okay, I’ll bite.
Jester’s Jaunt: Jester’s jaunt has always felt too expensive for the effect—2nd level feels about right—but it’s an undeniably useful spell. Break someone out of jail, through a locked door, onto the other side of an enemy with huge reach...the possibilities are big.
Magic Aura, Greater: This spell has a lot of utility, actually. Don’t sleep on it.
Motes of Dusk and Dawn: Lots of versatility in this spell, and I love how movable it is. A great tool for counteracting either light or darkness in combat.
Oneiric Horror, Greater: I’m not entirely convinced that greater oneiric horror is much of an upgrade over oneiric horror, but it’s still a great spell to throw onto a martial enemy with poor Will saves. They’ll waste actions attacking shadows, and in the meantime they might deal themselves some Strength damage and take the fatigued condition so that they’re less threatening once they snap out of the horror. Mesmerists and psychics are the only classes to get this spell at such an early level, so enjoy.
Out of Sight: Particularly fun for infiltration schemes. If you get caught, you can just run around a corner and the person chasing you may forget that you exist entirely.
Penumbral Disguise: I like penumbral disguise quite a lot, and penumbral disguise + out of sight is an amazing spell combo for infiltrating enemy strongholds. Nice, long duration, Stealth and Disguise bonuses that scale very well with caster level, and you can even cast it on allies if they’re better at stealth than you are.
Ray of Exhaustion: Exhausted is a much better condition than fatigued, and you can get the latter through a pretty easy oneiric horror + ray of exhaustion wombo combo: oneiric horror makes an enemy fatigued with no save after the spell ends, at which point you hit them with ray of exhaustion. Whether they save or not, they’re already fatigued, so they automatically become exhausted for minutes/level.
Shadow Enchantment: Illusion-based mesmerists will appreciate shadow enchantment for its ability to mimic other enchantment spells, but the save to disbelieve for only 20% effect is harsh.
Shadowmind: Potentially useful for blinding enemies with darkvision and see in darkness.
Stage Fright: The debuff to concentration checks is pretty good on its own, but you have to remember also that initiative checks are a type of Dexterity check. How’d you like to start every enemy in a combat off with a -4 to their initiative check? Sounds pretty nice, no?
Synaptic Scramble: I prefer confusion in most instances, both for its larger target cap and its generally more useful effects, but synaptic scramble isn’t terrible. I think I’d sooner use overwhelming grief to cripple single targets, though.
Terrible Remorse: Overwhelming grief or the oneiric horror series are going to be better in many circumstances, but watching enemies deal damage to themselves is undeniably fun.
Triggered Suggestion: Fun for getting people to do things that they wouldn’t normally do.
Unflappable Mien: Some nice protective benefits here. Spell level seems a bit high—I’d give it to ⅔ casters at 2nd level, personally—but it’s not bad.
Witness: Good for long-distance spying.


Adjustable Disguise: Not really worth the improved spell slot over disguise self.
Agonizing Rebuke: Can be devastating against creatures with a bunch of natural attacks, but otherwise fairly ho-hum.
Bereave: Not enough meat on this spell to earn a better rating, but it does have some nominal uses.
Control Summoned Monster: Could come in handy in certain fights.
Curse of Magic Negation: Targeting spellcasters with Will-based debuffs usually doesn’t work out so well, but it’s clutch if you can land the failed save.
Detect Desires: Like detect thoughts, potentially useful enough to be worth a spell known.
Draconic Malice: The issue with draconic malice is that it only eliminates fear or mind-affecting immunities for living creatures—constructs and undead are excluded. Psychic Inception is going to be a lot more effective in most cases.
False Life, Greater: 3rd-level spells are a very valuable resource for ⅔ casters—more so than 2nd-level spells—but the option for more temporary HP are here if you want them. Kind of like a preemptive cure serious or cure critical wounds. I’d still prefer vampiric touch in most cases.
Fearsome Duplicate: Fun for starting negotiations off on the right foot.
Geas, Lesser: For the mesmerist class only, you can actually replace lesser geas with conditional curse, replacing the geas with the objective that will break their curse. Two in one, see?
Inveigle Person: Unlike charm person, inveigle person doesn’t grant any save bonus if you or your allies are threatening the creature, and it treats all people, not just you, as though they were trusted friends. I’d sooner heighten charm person, though, rather than spending an entirely new spell known on inveigle person.
Invigorate, Mass: Sure. This is nominally useful.
Isolate: Potentially for assassinating guards, etc., but pretty limited in scope.
Mindscape Door: Mesmerists aren’t necessarily the best class for mindscapes, but they can at least function there.
Nondetection: Mesmerists in particular have many better ways of beating divination magic, but nondetection is a classic nevertheless.
Repugnant Taste: I think you’ve got better ways of making an enemy nauseated or sickened (mydriatic spontaneity, for one) but repugnant taste isn’t without its uses.
Resilient Reservoir: Resilient reservoir is a half-elf exclusive, so keep that in mind. You’d need to get hit with a lot of low-damage attacks in order to rack up any appreciable benefit, which is the spell’s main weakness.
Seek Thoughts: Has some potential uses for information gathering.
Sensory Amplifier: An okay team buff, but nothing exceptional. The short duration is probably what limits it the most, not to mention its best effect (the bonus to Perception checks) can be reliably beaten by spells like tears to wine.
Smug Narcissism: Amusing, but of limited mechanical use.
Trade Items: Could potentially be used to steal enemies’ weapons, spellbooks, etc., but it’s not all that likely to work.


Analyze Aura: Analyze aura is better on the occultist spell list, which gets the spell at 2nd level. It’s still good here, just kind of expensive.
Aura Alteration: At days/level, a scroll will work just fine.
Elemental Speech: Let tongues take care of this stuff for you.

Illusory Script: Could be a decent scroll.

Marionette Possession: Marionette possession might combo quite nicely with charm monster, but otherwise you’re better off leaving it alone.

Remove Blindness/Deafness: Scroll, definitely.
Remove Curse: Scroll, indubitably.
Seeds of Influence: Scroll, inevitably.
Sending: Sending is one of those spells that every party needs access to. Interplanetary and interplanar communication is simply way too useful to pass up, and helps keep parties together when you get separated. 
Shroud of Innocuity: Team infiltration can be a lot easier with shroud of innocuity.
They Know: Another very situational intrigue spell.
Tongues, Communal: Usually just one person uses tongues while the rest communicate through the translator.


Appearance of Life: Mesmerists don’t do necromancy.
Apport Animal: Mesmerists don’t do animal husbandry.
Bleed Glory: Mythic stuff is pretty rare.

Deep Slumber: HD limits are still a hard pass.

Vision of Hell: Mechanically weak, hurts your allies as much as your enemies, and eeeevil.

# 4th Level

4th Level


Freedom of Movement: Much like dimension door, every class that has freedom of movement on its spell list needs to take it. From underwater adventuring, to escaping grapples and pins with no checks involved, to shrugging off paralysis effects, freedom of movement does so much that it’s almost hard to believe Paizo was comfortable with it being one spell instead of several. And all at 10 minutes/level, too!


Amnesia: A brutal, brutal enchantment debuff that turns any enemy (especially enemies with class levels) into a mewling infant. Don’t get me wrong, many enemies will still be dangerous after amnesia, but not nearly as dangerous as they would be normally. Instantaneous duration is balanced out by a long casting time, the mind-affecting tag, and the restriction to living creatures.
Animus Mine, Greater: Greater animus mine triggers a much stronger retaliatory debuff against any enemy that tries to hit you with a mind-affecting effect. Although that’s great even on its own, that’s only half of the upgrade: the other half is that if the enemy fails their Will save and becomes stunned, you automatically succeed against the effect that triggered it. Doesn’t matter whether it’s your basic cause fear or Charon’s DC 40 memory loss ability—you succeed.
Arcane Eye: An invaluable spell for scouting the path ahead, arcane eye should always be ready with someone on your team, even if that someone isn’t you.
Break Enchantment: Break enchantment is a hard counter to most of the worst effects you or a teammate can be placed under: greater bestow curse, baleful polymorph, flesh to stone, etc. It’s a necessary spell.
Complex Hallucination: One of illusion’s most powerful spells at 4th level. Enjoy.
Dimension Door: Dimension door is exceptionally useful for any kind of combat or out-of-combat mobility. Everyone who can know this spell needs to know this spell.
Dominate Person: Dominate person actually has more weaknesses than I think most people realize: the one-round casting time, the mind-affecting tag, the restriction to humanoids, spell resistance, and—most crucially—a new save with a +2 bonus to throw off the effect if you order the subject to do something they wouldn’t normally do. It’s still an incredible spell, especially for mesmerists, with their ability to debuff Will saves so hard, but it’s not flawless by any means.
Enervation: With no save for half or to negate, enervation is a fabulous necromancy spell for softening up targets before hitting them with other debuffs. Mind you don’t forget that undead are healed by this spell.
Forbid Action, Greater: The language-dependent tag is still the biggest weakness of these spells, but otherwise you’re getting quite the upgrade from your basic 1st-level forbid action: the target cap is massively increased, the duration is massively increased, and targets get no repeat saves in future rounds to throw off the effect early. Can you imagine forbidding every martial enemy on the battlefield from attacking? For rounds/level?
Hold Monster: The resource is more expensive than hold person, but your target selection is considerably broader, too. Enchantment specialists will still love this spell.
Invisibility, Greater: Another absolute must-have for any class that can get it. Your precision damage from painful stare isn’t reliant on invisibility in the same way that, say, a rogue or slayer’s sneak attack damage would be, but that’s no excuse not to get this spell.
Riding Possession: Riding possession is a peerless spying spell, and the fact that you can cast mind-affecting spells on the subject while you’re possessing them makes it all the better. Spiritualists and occultists are generally better at the possession suite than mesmerists, but boy howdy, do mesmerists have a better complement of mind-affecting spells to use.
Shadow Conjuration: Dedicated illusionists can do phenomenal things with the shadow [school] spell suite—doubly so for shadow conjuration, since many conjuration spells don’t have saves apart from the initial Will save to disbelieve. Sure, it’s weaker than using the real spell, but mesmerists get almost no conjuration spells on their own, and shadow conjuration is insanely versatile.
Shadowform: A weirdly good illusion tanking spell. Each time an enemy attacks you or an ally, they have to save or treat you as only 20% real. On rounds that it works, you’re more or less immune to damage and on-hit save rider effects. Lovely, even if the duration is a little short.
Solid Fog: Debuffing and battlefield control rolled into one, and all with no save. Yum.
Synaptic Pulse, Greater: Even though I’m still not a fan of the fact that you can tag allies with greater synaptic pulse, it’s undeniable that stunning creatures for 1d4 rounds is hugely effective.
Thoughtsense: The duration is a bit shorter than similar spells like echolocation, but the larger range at least compensates somewhat. Blindsight is always a win.


Assume Appearance, Greater: Hard to imagine a long-term infiltration spell better than greater assume appearance. Infiltration is still pretty niche, but the spell is undeniably solid.
Battlemind Link: At 4th level, battlemind link is a rather an expensive team buff with a fairly short duration, but it does its work well. Obviously pick an ally whose fighting style (melee, ranged, spellcasting, etc.) matches yours.
Charm Person, Mass: The HD limit is high enough that I’m not going to ding the spell too much. Nice to be able to make a pack of humanoid NPCs your friends, although keep in mind that hypnotic stare only works on one target unless you’re an aromaphile or something.
Command, Greater: Similar to mass hold person. The language-dependent tag is probably the spell’s greatest weakness.
Detect Scrying: Detect scrying is pretty useful right out of the gate with its 24-hour duration, and can help beat diviners at their own game. I wouldn’t necessarily say this is something the mesmerist class is best suited to, though, with your extremely limited suite of spells known.
Envious Urge: Although there’s no technical restriction to humanoid targets, they’ll be the ones who most commonly wear weapons and armor, use wondrous items, etc. Think about whether your campaign is likely to feature valid targets for this spell before selecting it.
Hostile Juxtaposition: A pretty expensive resource to spend, but the effect is pretty cool. Reminds me of the Spatial Switch masterful trick that mesmerists can get.
Impossible Angles: One of my favorite battlefield control spells for occultists, although it works better on their spell list because they get etheric shards. Anyway, impossible angles can be good for messing with enemies’ movement, especially 5-foot steps, but the repeated Will saves to negate eat away at the spell’s utility.
Mindwipe: It’s fine, but enervation and amnesia will generally serve you better at this level.
Shroud of Darkness: Mmmm, the rare evocation spell! Absent darkness or deeper darkness, shroud of darkness is about the best tool you have for playing with light levels. The Will save to negate offsets the fact that the spell affects only enemies and doesn’t hinder your allies at all.
Synapse Overload: A divination spell that deals gnarly electricity damage and staggers enemies? Weeeird. I’m rating this green only because mesmerists probably won’t have taken Spell Focus (Divination), and hypnotic stare can’t help you with the Fortitude save for partial effect. It’s still one of your only spells that will deal direct damage.
Truespeak: A generally good spell for communicating with other beings. Aasimar even make good mesmerists.
Untold Wonder: It’s unclear to me whether untold wonder prevents non-harmless emotion effects from stripping away your emotion components; I wouldn’t think it does, since you’re still under the effect of a non-harmless spell—it’s just that the penalties have been turned into bonuses. It’s still quite good, especially if you have a bard or skald on your team, and especially if buffs like heroism are floating around liberally.
Zone of Silence: A good spell for sneaking an entire adventuring party around, conversing about how to kill enemies while standing 20 feet away, etc.


Akashic Communion: Akashic communion doesn’t exactly play to your strengths as a mesmerist, which makes it a better choice for other Knowledge specialists—wizards, psychics, bards, occultists, etc. It’s not terrible here, though, and the bonuses you get will help smooth over any lack of Intelligence you may have.
Apathy: You’ve got many stronger combat debuffs available, but this one isn’t bad for intrigue purposes.
Contagious Suggestion: HD limits again, and doing nothing that couldn’t be done by other iterations of suggestion.
Create Mindscape: Mesmerists and psychics are the only two occult classes who get access to both create mindscape and greater create mindscape, but this probably shouldn’t be a priority unless you’re a true illusion specialist.
Curse of the Outcast: You’re basically permanently ruining someone’s life, so there’s that, but it’s fun if you want to punish an NPC who wronged the party. Sorry, did I say fun? I meant lamentable.
Deceitful Veneer: A fun spell that’s only limited by its niche intrigue uses.
Feast on Fear: Actually not a terrible spell for peeling away minions from a fight, but the HD cap doesn’t scale with mental potency, and won’t be much use as the levels progress.
Harvest Knowledge: Not useless, but not super useful, either. Mesmerists aren’t great knowledge jockeys.
Illusory Wall: A fun trick for illusion-based mesmerists, but you probably could have made do with silent image unless the concentration on that spell is a big deal.
Modify Memory: A very mesmerist-esque spell, although I dislike the long casting time and the restriction to only 5 minutes of memories.
Pessimism: The duration is nice and long, but otherwise you’re essentially making a target sickened, and that could be accomplished very easily by cheaper spell resources.
Phantasmal Killer: I’ve been through so many phases with phantasmal killer. I thought it was cool at first, until I realized how many failed saves you need for it to work, and how limited the target selection is. With hypnotic stare, the Psychic Inception bold stare, and some other add-ons, phantasmal killer is actually pretty serviceable for a dedicated illusion specialist, and one of an illusionist’s only means of outright killing enemies through magic.
Primal Scream: I want to give a shout-out to primal scream. It’s not a bad spell, and certainly a nice emergency button when you need it, but every mesmerist should have enchantment foil and freedom of movement running more or less constantly. Don’t let yourself become subjected to these effects at all.
Telepathic Bond: Good for communicating silently with allies, but an expensive resource.
Telepathy: Unnecessarily short duration, but I suppose it works.
Tough Crowd: A decent full-team buff, although the spell level is way too high for what it does. This could easily have been a 2nd-level spell.
Utter Contempt: Uhhh...interesting. It’ll turn indifferent targets hostile, which is fun, but not too mechanically useful.


Denounce: A scroll of denounce would suffice, even if you wanted to use it.
Erase Impressions: A scroll, if it’s anything.
Hallucinatory Terrain: Maybe okay as a scroll. The long casting time keeps it from being combat-effective.
Locate Creature: Quite a useful scroll, although a wizard or other prepared caster might take care of this instead.
Mind Probe: Okay as an interrogation tactic.
Nondetection, Communal: Fine as a scroll, but you should probably let someone else deal with anti-divination stuff.
Passing Fancy, Mass: Intrigue scenarios only.
Restoration: An extremely useful scroll, but you shouldn’t need it enough (hopefully!) to warrant a spell known.
Seek Shelter: I suppose it could be useful for calling the local fuzz off your trail, but that’s a scroll if ever I’ve heard of one.
Sleepwalking Suggestion: I like it, actually. It’s too niche to warrant a spell known, but I like it.


Charon’s Dispensation: The very definition of niche. Sorry, Charon.
Curse, Major: You know how much I love bestow curse. C’mon. You know. But conditional curse does literally everything that major curse does, except at a lower spell level. And, to be honest, unless you set a condition that can be completed within the next 10 seconds, every enemy who runs into either of these spells will be dead before they can break the curse. So why not just go with conditional curse?
Daze, Mass: With no increase on the HD cap, you’re basically guaranteed never to run into enemies on whom mass daze would work. You’re, what, 10th level by now? Minimum? And the effect caps out at 4 HD, not counting mental potency? Yeah, no.
Dream: Only dreamstalker mesmerists will want this spell, and they can get it for free.
Forgetful Slumber: Sleep and more HD caps. Snoozer.
Hypnotism, Greater: Still bad. Pass.
Illusion of Treachery, Greater: Still way too niche, even for intrigue applications.
Mad Sultan’s Melody: Deals Wisdom damage to you, and affects only a rare subsection of enemies.
Majestic Image: Really meant to be an NPC spell.
Minor Phantom Object: The whole point of the creation spells is that they create real stuff out of nothing. If you’re just conjuring an illusion, what’s the point?
Old Salt’s Curse: Human-only, and you have many, many better spell choices available unless you’re playing in a Skull & Shackles campaign.
Phantasmal Asphyxiation: Even if you wanted to do the whole “two saves and you suck” spiel, phantasmal killer is clearly the superior option, and also available at this level.
Poison: Touch range, subject to poison immunities, and easily negated by even one successful Fortitude save. Leave it alone.
Reveal Emotions: I mean, eh? It’s definitely on-brand for an occult class, but “on-brand” is not the same thing as “useful.”
Serenity: Enemies can completely negate the damage by fighting defensively, which seems pretty crappy for a 4th-level spell.
Shocking Image: Don’t waste a 4th-level spell on this garbage spell. Just use mirror image instead.
Sleepwalk: Why would you…?
Steal Power: No way should you be wasting spells known on a spell that only works on mythic creatures.
Telepathic Silence: Too niche unless you’re fighting psychic spellcasters every day.
Vicarious View: Riding possession beats vicarious view 10 times out of 10.
Vigilant Rest: Really feels like this could have been a 1st-level spell. It’s certainly not worth the resource at 4th level.

# 5th Level

5th Level


Mind Fog: A -10 penalty to Will saves is almost impossibly high. Any enemy who fails their Will save on this effect will become silly putty in your hands.


Befuddled Combatant: Save-or-suck effects keyed off of fear auras, on-hit save riders, etc. are some of the biggest threats in high-level Pathfinder. Befuddled combatant can not only shave off a modest amount of damage from an enemy, it can also cripple their save DCs. Add in the Sapped Magic bold stare improvement, and you could easily hit someone with a -7 penalty to all their spell or spell-like ability save DCs in one round. How’s that for protecting the team, eh?
Feeblemind: The spell of choice at 5th level for destroying arcane casters, namely wizards, sorcerers, arcanists, bards, magi, skalds, summoners, and witches. If you suspect that a target you’re facing has class levels in any of those classes, hypnotic stare + feeblemind will put them in the grave.
Mydriatic Spontaneity, Mass: As good as mydriatic spontaneity, which is to say very good. Nauseated for rounds/level is good enough, even not counting the potential blindness.
Persistent Image: All of the good stuff from major image, but with no concentration needed. Good stuff.
Possession: Combat-effective possession, at last. I’m sad that mesmerists don’t get access to greater possession, as occultists do, but them’s the breaks.
Primal Regression: Huge debuffs, especially against spellcasters, and you can follow it up with dominate person/monster to take control of the biggest, dumbest martial enemy on the field, turning them against their allies.
Prying Eyes: An excellent intelligence-gathering spell. You’ll never regret having prying eyes.
Scripted Hallucination: As persistent image.
Shadow Evocation: Still a classic spell for illusionists. Getting access to any evocation spell you want is certainly not something that most mesmerists can do.
Synesthesia, Mass: Another great AoE illusion debuff. Enemies are certainly less effective while this is active.
Unwilling Shield: The expensive material component is worth noting, but otherwise unwilling shield is a great way to punish creatures for daring to attack you. The luck bonuses to AC and saves can also be easily supplemented with the Fate’s Favored trait or Chaldira’s Deific Obedience boons for a +2 to everything.


Castigate, Mass: How many tags can we fit into one spell? Let’s find out with mass castigate. It won’t work all the time, but even one or two enemies cowering for even one or two rounds can make a huge difference. Add in a slayer teammate with Dastardly Finish and Merciless Butchery, and you can just coup de grace enemies before they get a chance to recover.
Cloak of Dreams: The one-round casting time is unfortunate, but it’s a risk-free way to put enemies to sleep as they close with you. And since they’re helpless while asleep, the same combo goes for cloak of dreams as for mass castigate: coup de grace them while they’re helpless.
Dahak’s Release: I’m kind of surprised that dahak’s release doesn’t have the evil tag, considering who Dahak is in the Golarion pantheon. Anyway, this is a fabulous power if you happen to roll 26-75 on the percentile die, but 1-25 and 76-100 will most likely be you or your teammates, especially because you have to be close enough to touch an enemy in order to cast this spell.
Grand Destiny: A nice, long duration helps grand destiny along, although its common bonus type is unfortunate. Hermean potential at 1st level has less control over when the bonuses occur, but is a much cheaper alternative when you need it.
Inflict Pain, Mass: Inflict pain is an awesome 2nd-level debuff spell, but it starts to lose steam here as more enemies become immune to mind-affecting or pain effects. You’ll be looking increasingly for debuffs to saves, too, and mass inflict pain doesn’t debuff those.
Mage’s Private Sanctum: This is probably better left to a full caster, but you’ve got the means if you want them.
Mislead: It’s interesting, to be sure, but see invisibility or true seeing will put you out in the cold awfully quickly. Watch out for those at this level.
Nex’s Secret Workshop: Better than communal nondetection, because it will actually protect everyone for up to 20 hours/day.
Passwall: Great for progressing laterally through dungeons and other structures.
Phantasmal Web: Better than phantasmal putrefaction, but still less effective than mass mydriatic spontaneity.
Psychic Asylum: Such a cool spell. It’s your mind palace! Mesmerists aren’t prepared spellcasters, so that portion of the spell won’t apply, but everything else is top-shelf stuff.
Shadow Walk: It’s fast travel, but for mesmerists. As good as you would expect, although combat-focused spells may take priority. You can buy scrolls if it’s an issue.
Suggestion, Mass: Plenty of uses here, especially against hordes of low-level commoners.


Cloak of Shadows: Evil-aligned, and doesn’t do anything that you couldn’t get with displacement and the improved shadow armor enchantment. I’m inclined to think it’s not worth it.
Crime Wave: Hilarious and fun, but mechanically fairly irrelevant.
Deceptive Redundancy: Eh. Psyching out casters is generally a fool’s errand.
Dream Reality: Kind of like a proactive modify memory. It’s still niche, but potentially useful.
Foe to Friend: A hugely expensive resource, given the effect. I’d say you’re better off with calamitous flailing.
Foster Hatred: More niche attitude manipulation.
Mirage Arcana: Better than hallucinatory terrain, but still pretty limited.
Phantasmal Putrefaction: Mass mydriatic spontaneity is going to be much more effective in most circumstances than phantasmal putrefaction. Two saves each round means that enemies will very rarely be affected by it for long unless you can land it on people as they sit inside a mind fog or something.
Shadow Endurance: If you’re afraid your team won’t be able to help you up from unconsciousness regularly, then take shadow endurance. Otherwise, you’re simply denying your team the opportunity to help you.
Subjective Reality: I think I prefer shadowform to subjective reality, but it can help you to tank one creature while you focus on others.
Vengeful Outrage: At minutes/level, targets won’t have much time to find their objects of vengeance, much less engage them in combat. Dunno. I can’t see it working all that often.
Waves of Fatigue: The lack of a save is the only thing saving waves of fatigue from a red rating. The debuff is otherwise mild, and you can’t stack the conditions for exhaustion.


Dream Council: Will work just fine as a scroll.
Dream Scan: As above.
Joyful Rapture: Giving an ally a scroll of joyful rapture for when you’re placed under a non-harmless emotion effect is a good idea. Even though the target section of the spell calls out enemies, it doesn’t appear from the spell description that they’re actually affected.
Mage’s Decree: A scroll would easily suffice here.
Mind Swap: Maybe a scroll? Don’t think this would be good as a spell known.
Object Possession: Still leaves your body a bit too vulnerable to be a spell known.
Psychic Surgery: A very, very good scroll to have on hand. You never know when this could save an ally’s life.
Secret Chest: Fine as a scroll. Or, you know, use a bag of holding.
Seeds of Influence, Greater: Still best as a scroll.
Seeming: For group infiltrations of enemy organizations.


Appearance of Life, Greater: There’s no reason for mesmerists to use this spell, since they have no means of acquiring undead servitors.
Charnel House: A wand of grease would have been ten times cheaper, and just as effective.
Compelling Rant: You can stop reading the spell text as soon as you see “1d4 Wisdom drain.” The rest of the spell isn’t any better, but dealing Wisdom drain to yourself is a non-starter.
False Vision: Expensive material component, non-24-hour duration, and requires concentration to feed an enemy information that they would actually believe. Meh.
Inveigle Monster: Charm monster is better for your purposes.
Major Phantom Object: Again, the point of the creation spells is that they actually create real items. Without that, what’s left?
Nightmare: Only for dreamstalkers.
Phobia: The save DC doesn’t scale for some reason, which makes it more or less worthless in the long run.
Pox of Rumors: What an ineffectual way to smack-talk someone.
Repress Memory: I fail to see how this spell is better than common anti-divination tools.
Symbol of Pain: Ugh. Don’t use the symbol spells. They’re meant for NPCs.
Symbol of Sleep: As above.
Village Veil: So...protecting a little hamlet from roving marauders? At 13th level, you should be able to hunt them down, no sweat.

# 6th Level

6th Level


Song of Discord, Greater: Everyone who fails their save turns on their allies and begins ruthlessly attacking them for rounds/level, employing their deadliest spells, attacks, and abilities. Plus you give them a Strength bonus, plus even a successful save gets them to attack for one round!


Euphoric Tranquility: Assuming you can touch someone, pass your spell resistance check, and negate mind-affecting or emotion immunities (e.g., with Psychic Inception), euphoric tranquility is a no-save equivalent of charm person. Great for peeling minions away from hard fights or getting a boss to waste actions while you summon creatures, create battlefield control, or perform other combat actions that don’t directly threaten the target.
Geas: Geas (pronounced “gesh” in Irish dialect or “case” in Scottish dialect, by the way) is a great spell that can compel most living creatures with whom you share a language to obey your orders for weeks, months, or even years at a time. It’s not combat-functional, but if you ever want to stop a creature from betraying you, force it to sabotage its master’s plans, send it on a potentially suicidal mission, etc., geas will do the trick. It’s bloody difficult to remove via magic, too, so enemies can’t even rely on that.
Getaway: A great spell for teleporting the party out of danger, especially because mesmerists don’t normally get access to teleport or greater teleport.
Heretic’s Tongue: An absolutely brutal debuff that will destroy pretty much any spellcaster (but especially divine spellcasters) pending a failed Will save. It’s technically meant only for worshippers of Geryon, though, so...grain of salt.
Irresistible Dance: Fun flavor, and there’s nothing an enemy can do to prevent themselves from dancing for at least one round. Want that great wyrm red dragon to do the Charleston? He’s gonna do the Charleston.
Mantle of Doubt: A pretty great way to disincentivize enemies to attack you, or to punish them if they do. Shaken, save penalties, no flanking or attacks of opportunity, staggered condition, Wisdom and Charisma damage...lots to like here.
Overwhelming Presence: Great debuffs on multiple enemies. Heck, if you can make enough enemies helpless, your teammates can simply walk around coup de gracing people.
Scrying, Greater: Long-term magical espionage is now possible, although most BBEGs will probably take precautions against scrying.
True Seeing: Material component be damned—everyone needs access to true seeing. It simply cuts through too many common high-level spellcaster tricks to ignore.
Uncanny Reminder: It’s a summon spell and a debuff, rolled into one. What’s interesting here is that the creature you summon (a clockwork angel) is CR 15, while summon monster IX caps out around CR 13. The casting time is slow, and it can only be used on living creatures, but it has no mind-affecting tag, which is somewhat unusual for the mesmerist spell list.
Waves of Ecstasy: Stunned, staggered, multiple targets, good effect even on a successful save...I’m a fan of waves of ecstasy. In my mind, greater song of discord is even more devastating, but waves of ecstasy isn’t bad, either.
Waves of Exhaustion: Weaker status effects than waves of ecstasy, but you get a bigger AoE, no save, and no mind-affecting tag in exchange. We’ll call that even stevens.


Charm Monster, Mass: The HD limits are unfortunate, so you’re probably better off using a normal charm monster (heightened to 6th level, if need be) against high-HD targets. Against hordes of weaker enemies, though, this can be a great way to pick up a few disposable meat shields.
Hold Person, Mass: Facing crowds of humanoid enemies would be somewhat unusual at 16th level, but who knows what kind of campaign you’re in? The resource is expensive, given what the spell does, but then again, that’s why it’s not rated blue.
Impenetrable Veil: A fantastic spell that’s held back from a blue rating only by its expensive material component. I don’t think a full caster would want this more than mind blank, but a mesmerist just might.
Insanity: Insanity is fine, but in PC terms, there’s very little difference between an enemy being confused forever and an enemy being confused for rounds/level. I’d prefer a heightened confusion spell in nearly all ways.
Permanent Image: Lower-level spell slots will probably be just fine, even for illusion-specialized mesmerists, but permanent image isn’t an inherently bad spell.
Power Word Blind: The main benefit of the power word spell suite is that they don’t require a failed save to work; the main downside of the power word spell suite is that they only affect enemies who are below a certain HP threshold. Blinded is a strong status condition, and the HP thresholds here are higher than for power word stun or power word kill, but it’s still not what I would call a top-shelf mesmerist spell for this level.
Programmed Image: Great for luring enemies into traps and other effects that you want to proceed without concentration input or advanced timing planning from you.
Triggered Hallucination: Basically the enemy-targeted version of programmed image.


Create Mindscape, Greater: Still somewhat niche. It certainly doesn’t do you any good in combat unless you’re going to focus 100% on instigating psychic duels.
Demand: Will you ever need to use suggestion from this kind of extreme range? Seems like a pretty qualified “maybe.”
Demanding Message, Mass: For large-scale intrigue applications.
Hostile Juxtaposition, Greater: You’re probably going to have to use this spell with the “single target larger than you” option, rather than multiple creatures. It’s fine, but one teleport in exchange for a 6th-level spell seems costly.
Invisibility, Mass: A very expensive spell for something that should be easily duplicated by other spells or items at this level.
Project Image: I can’t think of any fantastic uses for project image, but I’m sure it’s got some. Doesn’t seem broadly powerful enough to merit a spell known in most cases.


Dream Travel: This is as close to class-exclusive as the mesmerist gets, but it’s incredibly niche, especially if you haven’t been focusing on doing anything with dreamscapes once you arrive.
False Vision, Greater: Far better than false vision, but still way too niche to merit a strong recommendation.
Knock, Mass: A scroll would certainly suffice.
Life of Crime: Life of crime is a lot of fun, but it’s not at all functional in combat. 
Phantasmal Revenge: Even if you wanted to cast this spell (which is unlikely) you’d do just fine with a scroll.
Veil: High-powered disguise self, basically. There’s no reason you’d need this as anything but a scroll.


Eyebite: You’re at least 16th level by the time you get 6th-level spells, which means that eyebite will only ever give you the sickened condition as a standard action, with a Fortitude save to negate. That’s not a good deal, especially as a 6th-level spell. The emotion and pain descriptors further limit your target selection.
Invisibility Bubble, Mass: For when the whole team needs to be invisible...underwater?
Night Terrors: Way too slow for PCs to use on enemies. It’s not a bad NPC spell, though.
Symbol of Distraction: Don’t use the symbol spells. Expensive and mostly ineffective.
Symbol of Persuasion: As symbol of distraction.
What Grows Within: An NPC spell that no PC will ever find themselves using.

# MES 305: Feats

MES 305: Feats


        Mesmerists tend to be even more feat-starved than other ¾-BAB classes, largely because they have a dual mandate to both debuff enemies (often via spellcasting, demoralization, feinting, bold stares, etc.) and to contribute some amount of damage to encounters (via normal combat feats and Stare feats). Especially from 7th to 15th level, when Blinding Stare, Confusing Stare, Intense Pain, Manifold Stare, etc. all come online, mesmerists are in an extreme feat crunch 24/7. Choose the absolute bare essentials to get out of the way at low levels, because the tidal wave hits right around 7th. If your GM has been following some of my recommendations about rebalancing the mesmerist class slightly (see here) then you’ll probably be a little less crunched, but not by much. My boy Meligaster is a hungry, hungry feat hippo.

---

# Core Combat Feats

Core Combat Feats


Universal


Deific Obedience: Celestial, Fiendish, Deific, Fey, and Monitor Obediences can be extraordinarily powerful if you get the right combo, and the evangelist prestige class isn’t a bad investment at all for the mesmerist class. And hey, wouldn’t ya know it, I already wrote a 400+ page guide to the Obedience feats! The hyperlink there will take you to the best deities to consider when playing a bard, which is a pretty good proxy for the mesmerist class—high Charisma modifiers, ⅔ spellcasters with ¾ BAB progression, good face skills, enchantment and illusion specialists, etc.
Divine Fighting Technique: Desna’s Shooting Star is probably the strongest DFT for mesmerists, although Sarenrae’s Mercy deserves an honorable mention because of how perfectly suited it is for demoralization builds that use Enforcer.
Flickering Step: Many of the conduit feats are pretty great, and Flickering Step is no exception. What, you can’t use a free dimension door a couple times a day?
Improved Initiative: More than many classes, mesmerists benefit from being able to go first, because it allows them to lay down a brutal AoE debuff before enemies get the chance to react. You’d be well-advised to fit this in somewhere.
Nature Soul / Animal Ally / Boon Companion: The classic three-feat trade for a fully scaling animal companion. You’ve got a good Charisma score for Handle Animal, so perhaps a decent investment.
Step Up / Following Step / Step Up and Strike: I’m not saying you’ll have room to take these feats, only that they’re excellent feats for any non-reach melee combatant who wants to hunt ranged and spellcasting enemies more effectively.
Wanderer’s Fortune: Wanderer’s Fortune effectively eliminates any fear you might have had about being grappled or swallowed whole. Mesmerists have freedom of movement on their spell list, but a bit more never hurts—this is one of the best spells in the game we’re talking about here.


Additional Traits: There are always more traits worth taking out there.
Armor Proficiency, Medium: Mesmerists are in desperate need of better armor options, so this feat is always a choice you can make. In general, though, brief multiclassing is a more highly optimized path to Strength builds and heavier armor proficiencies.
Big Game Hunter: A classic—if boring—passive bonus to hit and damage against larger creatures that isn’t really capable of steering you wrong after about 10th level.
Ghost Slayer: If you don’t want to spring for a ghost touch weapon to deal painful stare damage to incorporeals, Ghost Slayer will do the same thing, although the constant swift action input is unfortunate. I’d probably still stick with ghost touch most of the time, but it’s good to know the option’s here.
Great Fortitude: Static bonuses to saving throws aren’t the sexiest feats to take, but they are always solid choices if you’re casting around for something to do with a feat. As a Fortitude-weak class, mesmerists need all the help they can get.
Improved Critical: Mesmerists will deal most of their damage through painful stare, and precision damage doesn’t multiply on a crit. Some builds will still be interested in Improved Critical, but it’s not nearly as strong in this class as it is in others. The exception might be a Butterfly’s Sting build, which would definitely want Improved Critical or a keen weapon.
Lunge: Especially for reach builds, Lunge can be an excellent feat, allowing you to take oodles of attacks of opportunity against enemies that move toward you.
Noble Scion (Scion of War): Using your Charisma modifier instead of your Dexterity modifier for initiative checks is cool beans, especially if you’re building a spellcasting mesmerist.
Toughness: I mean, yeah, more HP never hurt.
Weapon Versatility: Probably the simplest, cheapest way to get around DR/damage type. That’s not usually penetrable with enhancement bonuses, so the feat is well worth considering.


Blind-Fight: Mesmerists can’t deal their painful stare damage to creatures with concealment (no precision damage to concealed creatures, bro) but even once they get Shadow Strike, they might still need Blind-Fight to help them out. Blind-Fight is easily replaced by select weapon enchantments, though—veering, truthful, heartseeker, etc.
Combat Expertise: I frankly would have preferred that your consummate liar class feature just granted you Combat Expertise as a bonus feat instead of trying to massage it around feinting. In fact, I would prefer this feat didn’t exist at all. Just use Elephant in the Room on this one. Butterfly’s Sting builds will be so much happier.
Critical Focus: Probably useful for mesmerists only as an adjunct to Butterfly’s Sting builds.
Weapon Focus: So many feats depend on Weapon Focus, it’s not even funny. Take it if you need prerequisites out of the way, but otherwise leave it alone. You have enough buffs.




Melee (Strength)


Furious Focus: Power Attack becomes a lot more palatable when you take no penalties on your first attack.
Power Attack: A little less necessary for ¾-BAB classes than for full-BAB classes, Power Attack is still a great feat to have for Strength-based mesmerists. In lieu of self-buffs to offset the attack roll penalties, mesmerists are mostly going to have to use spells to debuff enemies’ AC.




Melee (Dexterity)


Butterfly’s Sting: In out-and-out combat, mesmerists most resemble rogues, and in many cases Dexterity builds will have taken a swashbuckler dip to grab rapier or cutlass proficiency, Weapon Finesse, opportune parry and riposte, etc. Trouble is, crits do next to nothing for classes that deal mostly precision damage! But what if you could give those crits to someone else? Butterfly’s Sting is that feat. Sure, it’s only for Desna worshipers, and sure, the Combat Expertise tax totally bites, but man, is the payoff worth it.
Weapon Finesse: The original melee Dexterity feat par excellence, Weapon Finesse is absolutely required if you plan on being a lithe, lightly armored fighter. A number of simple weapons will work with Weapon Finesse, as will the sword cane or the whip if you’re more of a fancypants.


Combat Reflexes: There’s nothing specifically barring mesmerists from triggering painful stare on attacks of opportunity, although whether you’ll have the reach to take attacks of opportunity consistently is doubtful. Still, potentially worth a buy.
Piranha Strike: Power Attack for light weapon builds. Note that not all the weapons on your list (even those that seem like they intuitively should work with this feat) work with this feat: sword canes, for example, are one-handed weapons that can’t be used with Piranha Strike.
Weapon Focus / [Weapon] Grace: Slashing Grace, Fencing Grace, Starry Grace, etc. are all there for one purpose: to add your Dexterity modifier to damage on your weapon attack rolls. Usually I’d give these feats a blue rating for melee Dexterity builds, easy, but frankly speaking, the damage that a Dexterity-based mesmerist deals should be coming from painful stare + Intense Pain, not from their weapon damage die or a high Dexterity modifier. Mesmerists tend to have a lot of build priorities, and I’d rather you have Compounded Pain, Intense Pain, Blinding Stare, Confusing Stare, and all three instances of Manifold Stare before considering Dexterity-to-damage feats. If you’re fighting to take Weapon Focus and the Grace feats at low levels, you’re not taking your 3rd-level Manifold Stare, Spell Focus, Combat Casting, or any of the other bajillion high-priority feats you need. There’s an opportunity cost to all of this.
Weapon Focus / Whip Mastery / Improved Whip Mastery: Pretty much de rigeur for mesmerist whip builds. Mesmerists won’t need the Greater version for the most part, since combat maneuvers are often a trap for full-BAB classes, much less ¾-BAB classes with no bonus feats.


Accomplished Sneak Attacker: Only enigma mesmerists will have sneak attack dice, but they’ll want this feat, easy.
Agile Maneuvers: I wouldn’t recommend combat maneuvers to many people, least of all mesmerists, but if you’re going to play that way on a Dexterity build, you need Agile Maneuvers.


Amateur Swashbuckler: A 1-level swashbuckler dip is a much better idea.

# Ranged

Ranged


Clustered Shots: A classic for avoiding DR on ranged builds.
Manyshot: Mesmerists don’t benefit hugely from extra attacks with Rapid Shot, since their ability to trigger painful stare is based off of the number of Manifold Stare feats they have, but Manyshot is just extra damage on the top. Always good.
Point-Blank Shot / Precise Shot / Improved Precise Shot: The three most essential ranged feats for mesmerists. If you’re playing with Elephant in the Room feat tax rules, just skip Point-Blank Shot—what you really want is Precise Shot.


Deadly Aim: As a ¾-BAB class without many meaningful attack bonuses, mesmerists aren’t ideally situated to use Deadly Aim, but it’s not a horrible option, either.
Rapid Shot: Since most of a mesmerist’s damage will come from painful stare, which is limited in its number of uses per round by Manifold Stare, getting extra attacks doesn’t help as much as it would in other classes. Nice to be able to saturate your painful stare triggers, though.


Rapid Reload / Crossbow Mastery: Mesmerists emphatically do not have the feats for crossbow builds. You’re better off multiclassing or using racial traits for longbow access.

# Ancillary Feats

Ancillary Feats


        Ancillary feats encompass any feats that aren’t absolutely necessary to build a core combat build. These feats might include things that improve your ability to use skills, support your allies, branch out into combat maneuvers like feinting, etc., and can be used in a modular fashion to tack on secondary abilities to your mesmerist. Unless you’re working with a very peculiar build, none of the feats in this section should be the main attraction—they should give you a few more tricks up your sleeve.

---

# Demoralize

Demoralize


Cornugon Smash: The #1 single-target demoralization route if you’re a Strength-based mesmerist who needs to deal lethal damage. Make sure you pick up something like the Signature Skill (Intimidate) feat, because the shaken status condition doesn’t last long when you’re not using Enforcer.
Dazzling Display: If Enforcer and Cornugon Smash are the preferred single-target demoralization form factors for mesmerists, Dazzling Display is unquestionably the preferred AoE demoralization form factor. Pair with Signature Skill (Intimidate), and you can have entire crowds of monsters pooping themselves in fright.
Disheartening Display: Once you have the shaken or frightened condition up and running on a group of enemies, Disheartening Display is what allows you to push that fear to the extremes, potentially even reaching cowering with Signature Skill (Intimidate). It requires plenty of investment and a bit of a long-term outlook, but you can be patient, can’t you?
Enforcer: Enforcer is perhaps the premiere method of demoralizing single targets, although nonlethal damage immunities do tend to limit your target selection at times. Mesmerists would probably want to keep a +1 sap around for when they need to cosh someone really scarily.


Intimidating Glance: It’s really hard to scale the shaken condition’s duration well using only the base Intimidate skill, which is why so many demoralize builds eventually come to rely on something like Enforcer or Signature Skill (Intimidate). At the very least, though, Intimidating Glance solves the problem of Intimidate requiring a high action cost, which still makes this feat well worth it for dedicated debuffing builds. Hypnotic Stare (Disorientation) + shaken by Intimidating Glance + sickened by a cruel weapon + the Cursed Sanction trick = a lot of penalties.
Hurtful: A Power Attack / Furious Focus / Cornugon Smash / Hurtful build can do a lot of damage in a short period of time, but requires some feat planning to pull off successfully.
Motivating Display: The morale bonuses are easily superseded if you have someone with Flagbearer, heroism, etc., but if not, Motivating Display is a free buff to attack rolls and skill checks for something you’d be doing anyway on a Dazzling Display build.
Shatter Defenses: Mesmerists don’t have any particular reason to make enemies flat-footed, since painful stare deals precision damage regardless of an enemy’s condition. Shatter Defenses is another way (apart from feint) to lower AC, though, and that’s always worth something. Also has some great synergy with the malevolent armor enchantment.


Demoralizing Lash: Hobgoblin only, which is okay because hobgoblins make decent mesmerists and mesmerists are proficient with whips. Basically a quick and dirty means of increasing the duration of your shaken conditions if you’re not using Enforcer.
Demoralizing Stare: Real talk, though: how is Demoralizing Stare a higher-level feat than Blinding Stare or Confusing Stare? It makes no sense in the generally accepted meta of which status conditions are strongest. Anyway, you should have a number of ways to make people shaken through garden-variety Intimidate checks. No need to duplicate that here.
Violent Display: I suppose enigma mesmerists get sneak attack damage, but everyone else will have to crit-fish in order to trigger Violent Display. It’s a great feat, just not so great for mesmerists.

# Face Skills

Face Skills


Betrayer: A fun way to enter into a combat with a “new friend,” although the prereqs are unfortunate.


Axiomatic Discourse: A fun little feat for helping you not alienate people when you fail your Diplomacy checks. Not a priority, but nice to have.


Deceitful: Deceitful is unfortunately a fairly common prerequisite feat, but unless you’re building for those feats, you can skip it. Skill Focus (Bluff) would be more universally useful, since Disguise doesn’t see a lot of play.




Feints


Improved Feint / Greater Feint: Two indispensable upgrades to your feinting abilities, if you’re building a feinting build. Don’t forget that your consummate liar class feature allows you to ignore the Combat Expertise prereq, and that if you’re interested in building a top-shelf feinting build, the vexing daredevil archetype is probably what you’ll want to go with.


Disengaging Feint / Disengaging Flourish: They’re okay feats, but vexing daredevils will generally be able to use the Outmaneuver dazzling feint to achieve a similar effect without surrendering their standard action for the round.


Blistering Feint: Ifrit make decent mesmerists, and the ability to eke out a little more damage on a feint build is welcome. You have so, so many other priorities, though.
Mesmerizing Feint / Greater: I suppose I can understand thematically why feints don’t work well against mindless creatures—I guess they’re just too dumb to react to sucker punches like humanoids?—but even so, two feats just to be able to feint the full range of targets in the Pathfinder bestiaries seems like an unnecessary burden to place on a combat style that’s already not, ya know, ludicrously strong. Not like we’re building a TWF fighter here. Fortunately, only vexing daredevils will really get into hardcore feinting, and they get these feats for free.

# Non-Magical Healing

Non-Magical Healing


Healer’s Hands / Incredible Healer: The only two feats you need to get going with a non-magical healing build. Kinda thematic for mesmerists with touch treatment, no?


Acupuncture Specialist: Non-magical remove curse. That could be handy.
Improvisational Healer: Almost nicer for the potion benefits than for the Heal benefits! Nice not to have to tote around healer’s kits for treat deadly wounds, too.
Pathologist: Non-magical neutralize poison and remove disease. Again, could be quite handy.


Signature Skill (Heal): A late-game addition that pulls ahead of Healer’s Hands after about 15th level. Pick it up if you feel you need it by then, but otherwise you can ignore.




Support


Combat Advice: Spellcasting mesmerists will likely not have much to do with their move actions, so giving allies bonuses to attack is nice. I wish the bonus scaled or you got more attacks or something, but what can you do?


Battle Cry: Some mild buffs, but especially useful in preventing fear effects. You might find it useful—who knows?

# Universal

Universal


Combat Casting: Mesmerists can expect to do plenty of melee-range spellcasting, which means that Combat Casting is often a must.
Feign Curse: Feign Curse is a fun debuffing feat that can best be thought of as ill omen, but with no spell resistance, and keyed off a skill check, rather than a spell save DC. Mesmerists are going to be some of the best characters in the game to use this feat, so hop to it.
Persistent Spell: There aren’t a huge number of metamagic feats that mesmerists would be interested in, but Persistent Spell is one of them—nearly all debuffers want to consider it, actually. Persistent Spell forces enemies to roll twice on their saves against your spells, taking the lower result, which can be exceptionally useful when you want to get an enemy rolling on the debuff train but might need to stoke the engines a bit. Mesmerists have a number of AoE debuffing spells, too, which Persistent Spell plays nicely with.
Spell Penetration / Greater Spell Penetration: The mesmerist is a debuff-heavy class, which means that they’ll eventually need some means of dealing with spell resistance. Spell Penetration is the most common method of doing so. You can plan on entering these feats around 9th-11th level.
Varisian Tattoo: +1 caster level to a school of magic that you have Spell Focus for. Yes.


Heighten Spell: Another option for getting enemies to fail their saves against your spells is simply to heighten the spell to a higher spell level. This isn’t usually the mechanically optimal solution, since Persistent Spell gives you about a +4 to your save DC on average, but Heighten Spell still has its uses.
Lunging Spell Touch: Mesmerists get some fun touch spells, including bestow curse, dahak’s release, disfiguring touch, euphoric tranquility, irresistible dance, shadow anchor, synapse overload, touch of gracelessness, touch of idiocy, and vampiric touch. Using those spells at longer ranges definitely ain’t bad.
Scarring Spell: Mesmerists (especially those focused on the enchantment school, but also necromancy specialists) have a number of emotion or fear effects available to them; one failed save against a scarring spell starts to cause their save penalties to snowball out of control.


Acrobatic Spellcaster: An interesting option for vexing daredevils and those taking the elf favored class bonus.
Bouncing Spell: Bouncing Spell is another metamagic feat that’s commonly recommended for debuffing classes. It falls just a bit short on the mesmerist, because whichever enemy you bounce it to will not have your hypnotic stare penalties applied to it by default. Bouncing Spell also assumes a single-target spell, which may not be what you’re actually casting.


Extend Spell: A lesser rod of extend spell will do the trick.
Gifted Mesmerist: Ironically a really bad feat for any mesmerist. I just had to put it in, because it’s too funny.
Logical Spell: Another necessary metamagic feat for the mesmerist, but also served better by a rod.




Enchantment


Spell Focus / Greater Spell Focus (Enchantment): There’s a great argument to be made that enchantment is the mesmerist’s most important school of magic, as it contains nearly all of your best debuffs and is predominantly keyed off of Will saves, which—guess what?—you’re an expert at debuffing through hypnotic stare. There are plenty of other ways to build a spellcasting mesmerist, of course, but I don’t think there’s a more optimized way than this.

# Illusion

Illusion


Effortless Trickery: Gnomes get many of the best illusion feats, and Effortless Trickery is a great example. Being able to maintain concentration on a figment as a swift action opens up your action economy in a really nice way, although you do still need to worry about competition from hypnotic stare.


Spell Focus / Greater Spell Focus (Illusion): Illusion is the last school of magic that I would consider getting Spell Focus for, and even though the number of illusion spells that mesmerists have is rivaled only by the number of enchantment spells they have, I still consider illusion a bit of a weaker option. For starters, illusions are rarely capable of turning the tide of battle on their own—they create distractions, waste time or resources, or sow confusion among enemy ranks. Illusion does have some enemy-targeted debuffs, but those are rarer in this magic school than in necromancy or enchantment. Finally, many of your best figment spells offer no saves unless interacted with, making their spell save DCs somewhat less important than in other magic schools.
Threatening Illusion: Another gnome-exclusive feat that lets you flank using your illusions. It does cost you a spell level, though, so I’ve rated it less highly than Effortless Trickery.


Believable Veils: Talk about a bait-and-switch on the short description, eh? Believable Veils is a 1/day +2 to an figment or glamer spell save DC, which is strictly inferior to Mahathallah’s Deific Obedience boon. Sorry, Sivanah. Others just do illusions better than you do.




Necromancy


Spell Focus / Greater Spell Focus (Necromancy): Necromancy is easily the mesmerist’s second-best debuff school, containing spells such as waves of exhaustion, eyebite, possession, enervation, bestow curse, deafness/blindness, and howling agony. I still think I’d prefer enchantment in most cases, but necromancy is also not a poor choice.

# Bold Stare

Bold Stare


Extended Stare: The range of hypnotic stare is only 30 feet, which can be quite restrictive if enemies are fleeing from you. Honestly, I would give Extended Stare a bit of an upgrade, and allow it to have some amount of scaling with mesmerist level, say, +10 feet at 1st, 5th, 10th, 15th, and 20th levels, but then again, I’m a giver. I can’t help but give. Even if your GM won’t allow any scaling, the upgrade is badly needed, so it’ll still rank green.
Intimidating Glance: It’s really hard to scale the shaken condition’s duration well using only the base Intimidate skill, which is why so many demoralize builds eventually come to rely on something like Enforcer or Signature Skill (Intimidate). At the very least, though, Intimidating Glance solves the problem of Intimidate requiring a high action cost, which still makes this feat well worth it for dedicated debuffing builds. Hypnotic Stare (Disorientation) + shaken by Intimidating Glance + sickened by a cruel weapon + the Cursed Sanction trick = a lot of penalties.


Mental Stare: If anything, the fact that mesmerists can’t use their hypnotic stare without line of sight to a creature is an argument for creating a mesmerist with darkvision, rather than an argument for this feat. It’s nice to have, but planning for being blinded seems like a poor allocation of resources.
Obliviating Stare: I’m not saying it’s not useful for intrigue applications, only that intrigue applications are rarely optimal to focus your build on.


Gaze Reflection: Gaze effects from monsters are usually bad, it’s true, but they’re nowhere near common enough to justify spending a feat on.

# Painful Stare

Painful Stare


Manifold Stare: Manifold Stare allows you to trigger your painful stare class feature more than once per round, and I’m going to go on record as saying that I think it’s the single most important feat for mesmerists to take. Yes, more than Intense Pain, and yes, more than Compounded Pain. My reasoning for this opinion is that mesmerists are simply not built to deal damage with their abilities: their weapon proficiencies are extremely limited; they basically have to wear light or medium armor, which locks most mesmerists into Dexterity builds that deal less damage than Strength builds; they don’t get any intrinsic bonuses to attack or damage rolls; their spell list isn’t well-suited to self-buffing; and they don’t have the BAB to use Power Attack, Piranha Strike, or Deadly Aim very effectively. So what’s a mesmerist to do? The simple answer is to rely on your painful stare class feature, which deals scaling precision damage on each hit against a foe, whether you’re doing the hitting or not. At 20th level, a mesmerist using Intense Pain can deal 9d6+10 precision damage on one hit; at that point, however, you’ll have two other iterative attacks—not to mention haste—and by default mesmerists can only deal their painful stare damage once per round. With each instance of Manifold Stare, however, you get the chance at an extra 9d6+10 precision damage, for a maximum of 36d6+40 with three feats invested. Or what about Blinding Stare and Confusing Stare? Oh, sure, enemies might pass their saves the first time around, but what about the second, third, and fourth attempts? Will they be so lucky then? All this to say: Manifold Stare is excellent whether you’re debuffing or dealing damage, and every mesmerist should take the full three feats at some point. My recommendation would be to grab the first around 5th level, then grab the next around 11th, and finally swap your 15th-level bold stare for the final instance. It’s a lot of investment, but it’s incredibly worth it.


Ability Focus (Painful Stare): While not technically a stare feat, Ability Focus does apply to painful stare—and what’s more, it appears to apply to the stare feats, which modify painful stare rather than acting as their own abilities. This being the case, a +2 to the save DC for a Compounded Pain Blinding Stare + Confusing Stare is incredibly potent.
Blinding Stare: Blinded is a phenomenally powerful status condition, and unlike many of the other stare feats, Blinding Stare allows you to affect the same enemy as many times as you want in 24 hours. Tied with Confusing Stare as the best debuffing stare feat.
Compounded Pain: Compounded Pain lets you add two Stare feat rider effects to your painful stare instead of one. It’s a late-game addition to the painful stare canon, but nevertheless a phenomenal ability, allowing you to deal more damage (Intense Pain + Penetrating Stare), debuff enemies even further (Blinding Stare + Confusing Stare), or some combination of the two. Essential for high-level mesmerists.
Confusing Stare: Oof, another incredibly hard-hitting debuffing stare. The fact that you get to pick which one of two d100 rolls you’d like enemies to take on the table further increases your chances of denying enemies the opportunity to act normally.
Intense Pain: Mesmerists are not known for being the most damaging class out there, but Intense Pain starts to turn the tables on that narrative, squeezing roughly 30% more damage out of painful stare over the course of your career. By 20th level, in fact, you’ll deal 9d6+10 precision damage with Intense Pain, which compares favorably with the rogue’s sneak attack progression, and with Compounded Pain + Manifold Stare, you can even start to deal rogue-like sneak attack damage on every hit if you want! This is how mesmerists were intended to deal damage, so plan to take Intense Pain, Compounded Pain, and Manifold Stare at some point in your development.


Disconcerting Stare: You can basically forget the Disable Device and Perception check penalties, although Perception penalties can be nice if you’re trying to sneak past someone. What you’re really here for is the 1-minute Reflex save penalty, which can be applied early in a battle and left to operate by itself. Paired with the Sluggishness bold stare, you can really tank monsters’ Reflex saves—and those already tend to be quite weak as CR tranches go by.
Excoriating Stare: As with Demoralizing Stare, how is this an 11th-level ability while Blinding and Confusing are only 7th? Anyway, sickened is a better status condition than shaken for a debuffer, so this isn’t totally wasted.


Bleeding Stare: The damage is pretty lackluster, which is unfortunate. Bleed effects are always nice sources of passive damage.
Demoralizing Stare: Real talk, though: how is Demoralizing Stare a higher-level feat than Blinding Stare or Confusing Stare? It makes no sense in the generally accepted meta of which status conditions are strongest. Anyway, you should have a number of ways to make people shaken through garden-variety Intimidate checks. No need to duplicate that here.
Fatiguing Stare: Fatigued is a mild status condition, and since it can’t ever progress to exhausted through this ability, I don’t think I need to give Fatiguing Stare a stellar rating.
Penetrating Stare: Naaah. I mean, okay, but naaah. Intense Pain is basically guaranteed to outperform Penetrating Stare in the long run, and against a wider variety of targets. About the only use for Penetrating Stare that I can think of is if you’re using the weaker, ally-triggered version of painful stare often—Intense Pain will not outperform Penetrating Stare then, at least not against targets with DR. I know which I prefer.
Perturbing Stare: You know what else denies enemies their Dexterity modifier to AC? The blinded condition. You know what causes the blinded condition? Blinding Stare. I suppose if you wanted to make an enemy with blindsight flat-footed? I’m not sure that niche case is worth a feat, though.
Venomous Stare: Between widespread poison immunities and sky-high Fortitude saves at 11th level and beyond, I doubt that Venomous Stare will do much for you, especially not when you can use it against a given enemy only once per day; I won’t deny that the ability damage is alluring, however.


Entwining Stare: Intended pretty much exclusively for monsters. PCs won’t often have the grab ability.
Overpowering Stare: This ability is basically a carbon copy of Confusing Stare, but way worse. Later entry (11th level, rather than 7th) and targets can’t be affected more than once per day. Nope! Just get Confusing Stare. Not like anyone is concerned about PFS-legal options anymore.

# Mesmerist Tricks

Mesmerist Tricks


Contingent Trick: The prerequisites are a bit steep for all but dedicated trick builds (lookin’ at you, vexing trickster) but otherwise it’s a fantastic feat, giving you lots more variety as far as what tricks get triggered when. Definitely top-shelf.
Reflexive Trick: Assuming you can spare the immediate action (refresh your memory on how swift and immediate actions interact, if you haven’t done so recently), Reflexive Trick allows you to respond extraordinarily flexibly to any triggering condition. Easily one of the best trick feats.
Spell Trick: The sheer, ridiculous amount of versatility that Spell Trick offers makes it more than worth a purchase, especially since it only opens up at 10th level at the earliest. You should be able to spare a later feat for this tasty boi.


Extra Mesmerist Tricks: Sure, more tricks never hurt. I think I’d focus my feat investment elsewhere, with so many build priorities, but this isn’t a bad one if you have some extra feats kicking around.
Ready for Battle: Essentially a +2 morale bonus to initiative for all your allies, including yourself. I’ll take that as a feat, easy.
Split Trick: Helps you stretch out your tricks/day by only triggering tricks for those who actually need them. Lovely, if less essential than other feats.
Swap Trick: Editing implanted tricks is a great idea, especially since there’s no cost to doing so.


Bouncing Trick: Bouncing Trick doesn’t allow you to “recycle” trick uses (you need to spend an additional number of tricks to implant a bouncing trick), so its primary use is as an action economy lubricant—maybe you really want an ally to have Mesmeric Mirror, but you don’t want to spend the actions to run over to them and implant the trick in the middle of combat. I’m inclined to think this use is quite niche.


Ready for Pain: Nonlethal damage is staggeringly rare, and not seriously threatening even when it does appear.




Touch Treatment


Extra Touch Treatment: I would probably take Extra Mesmerist Tricks over Extra Touch Treatment, but this feat also isn’t bad, especially if you’re a melee mesmerist who might need to shrug off Fortitude save-based status conditions on a regular basis.

# Chart Caster

Chart Caster


The Basics


        A mesmerist that consults star charts in order to influence the destiny of their allies.


        Versatility: -1
        Power: +1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Class Skills: Alters class skills. Adding the remaining two monster identification Knowledge skills to the class is a nice little perk.


        (1) Subject of the Stars (Su): Alters mesmerist tricks and manifold tricks. Whether you like subject of the stars or the base mesmerist trick mechanics better is more or less down to personal preference. Base mesmerist tricks are faster to implant and more varied, but limited in number; this archetype’s tricks are slower to implant and less varied, but unlimited in number. If your caster is always going to want Slip Bonds and your fighter is always going to want Gift of Will, then yeah, this archetype is where to come for that, since you’ll be able to give them both as many instances of those tricks as they want starting at 5th level.


        (3) Feign Destiny (Su): Replaces touch treatment. I’ll be honest, I like feign destiny more than touch treatment. Many times, touch treatment is reactive: you or your ally failed a save, and now you’ve got a status condition on your hands (no pun intended) that has to be dealt with. Feign destiny is more proactive, helping all those near misses on saving throws or attack rolls to just tip over into successes. This, of course, is supercharged at 10th level, when your bonus becomes +5. Watch out for overlap with other competence bonuses, though—they’re very common, especially for skill checks and some attack rolls. Anyone playing with a bard (a class that habitually grants competence bonuses to attack rolls through inspire courage) may want to bag it.


Is It Worth It?


        I like it, yeah. Feign destiny is good enough that I’m interested, and subject of the stars is even more powerful than regular mesmerist tricks for certain parties that consistently need just one kind of trick.

# Dreamstalker

Dreamstalker


The Basics


        A mesmerist who infiltrates the dreams of other creatures—or forces them to fall asleep when they least want to—and then torments them while they lie at rest.


        Versatility: +1
        Power: -1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Dreams of Pain: Alters painful stare. Changing painful stare so that it deals nonlethal instead of lethal damage is...okay. Some enemies are immune to nonlethal damage, so that’s not ideal, but otherwise it’s a valuable skill to have for taking enemies alive and questioning them. There’s unfortunately no synergy here with the Enforcer feat, as Enforcer specifies that you need to deal damage with a melee weapon.


        (1) Somnomancer: Alters mesmerist tricks and replaces 1st-level mesmerist trick. What I love about somnomancer is that it doesn’t lock you into anything—it just guides you gently by the hand over to a huge pile of options and says, “Feel free to take or leave any of that.” The one compulsory part of the ability—the witch’s Slumber hex—is characteristically excellent, and you can then move on to take as many or as few of the other hexes and spells as you’d like. Especially since you later surrender manifold tricks, the easiest and best way to play a dreamstalker is to simply replace most or all of your mesmerist tricks with hexes or bonus spells.


        (2) Sleepless (Ex): Replaces towering ego. Oof. Now that’s a straight downgrade. Towering ego is one of your most valuable class features, and sleepless doesn’t even begin to match up. Fatigue and exhaustion? Immunity to sleep? Nah.


        (3) Touch of Night (Su): Alters touch treatment. In my experience, nausea and fear conditions are more common than exhaustion or sleep conditions, although picking up the ability to remove the staggered condition is quite nice. I’d still call this a net loss.


        (5) Dreamwalker (Su): Replaces manifold tricks. Huh. Interesting. Dreamwalker basically incentivizes you to attack enemies in binary mindscapes, dreamscapes, etc., rather than facing them head-on. Anyone who’s played Book 3 of Ironfang Invasion knows that there’s a particularly nasty pair of assassins that can seriously mess you up with dreamscapes and nightmare, and dreamwalker basically ensures that you rule the roost in the realm of the Sandman. 


        (11) Traumatic Spell: Replaces glib lie. Dreamstalkers don’t do quite as much interaction as other mesmerists, so this is fine by me. Really just another way to keep your enemies terrified, sleepless, and exhausted while you hunt them down in your binary mindscapes.


Is It Worth It?


        The loss of towering ego is definitely the biggest blow to the archetype—if not for that, I likely would have rated it green instead of yellow-green. As it is, it’s not terrible; I can certainly think of occultism-heavy campaigns (Strange Aeons, e.g.) in which dreamstalker would be an amazing archetype.

# Eyebiter

Eyebiter


The Basics


        A mesmerist that can rip out their own eyeball and send it on spying missions or channel gaze and stare effects through it.


        Versatility: +1
        Power: +0


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Eyeball Familiar (Ex): Replaces consummate liar and 1st-level mesmerist trick. The class features that you give up here aren’t essential to the class, so I feel comfortable rating eyeball familiar green, even with all its limitations. Good for scouting, etc., but under no circumstances should you ever allow it to be destroyed—if you do, you’re down an eye, and regeneration takes a long, long time to come online.


        (3) Ocular Occlusion (Su): Replaces touch treatment. I tend to like touch treatment, and in general support abilities can be counted on to always work, whereas debuffs rely on failed saves 95% of the time. Ocular occlusion’s debuffs are strong, at least, even if I’d personally stick with touch treatment for most builds. I think this is an even trade.


        (5) Staring Eye (Su): Replaces mental potency. Mental potency is far and away the least useful mesmerist class ability, so I’m fine getting rid of it. A little flexibility on debuff positioning really helps when you can only use hypnotic stare from 30 feet away.


        (11) Omnivisual (Ex): Replaces glib lie. Sure, another small combat buff. All-around vision will help you avoid getting overwhelmed by monsters, hit with sneak attack dice, etc.


Is It Worth It?


        I don’t think you gain anything incredibly valuable from the eyebiter archetype, but you also surrender only the very worst of your mesmerist class features, so it’s not like you lose out on anything, either.

# Fey Trickster

Fey Trickster


The Basics


        A mesmerist that gains the hunter spell list and some fey-themed class features.


        Versatility: +1
        Power: -1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Class Skills: Alters class skills. I’d say you come slightly ahead here. Appraise and Profession are useless, generally speaking, and Knowledge (Dungeoneering) is probably the least-used of the monster identification Knowledge skills. Knowledge (Nature) and Survival are pretty good, even if Handle Animal isn’t.


        (1) Nature’s Lure: Alters spellcasting. Hunters actually have one of the best spell lists in the game, although you wouldn’t necessarily think of that class when someone asks you who the best ⅔ casters are. Why? Well, they get every druid spell up to 6th level—and the druid spell list is incredibly strong—as well as every spell on the ranger spell list at an earlier level than the ranger gets! The druid spell list is full of powerful debuffs and battlefield control spells, and the ranger spell list has mostly self-buffs like gravity bow, lead blades, etc. that can instantly turn you into a better attacker. This isn’t necessarily better or worse than the base mesmerist spellcasting, but it is quite different.

        Vexing daredevil surrenders much of the mesmerist’s buffing capacity (via touch treatment) and debuffing capacity (via bold stare), but gives you the ability to feint like a boss. Enigma


The Basics


        Someone call Edward Elgar, ‘cause we’ve got some enigma variations! Much like the cipher investigator archetype, the enigma mesmerist tricks enemies into completely forgetting that they exist. 


        Versatility: -1
        Power: -1

The only issue with transfer affliction is that, until 14th level, you need to be actively suffering from a status condition before it’ll do...well, anything. This class feature might sneak up to green at 14th level, when you can replicate the effects of bestow curse (bestow curse be powerful) but until then I’d call it a straightforward downgrade.

Full sneak attack progression (or maybe slayer sneak attack progression) in exchange for painful stare might have been a swap I was willing to call even-stevens, but 4d6 sneak attack damage over 20 levels in exchange for manifold tricks is definitely not. Now, thanks to solipsism, you should be able to proc sneak attack a lot more regularly than a rogue, but you’d need to take a bunch of instances of Accomplished Sneak Attacker in order to boost your damage to normal rogue or even slayer levels. Why go to all this trouble when painful stare could give you equivalent damage and all the debuffing you could want?


        (11) Detection Void (Su): Replaces glib lie. Sure, fine. I’ll call this even.


        (20) Absentia (Su): Replaces rule minds. Without a save, this is actually quite powerful. You can just hang out in the bedroom of the BBEG, watching them eat cheerios in their boxers, and they’ll never even know you’re there.


Is It Worth It?


        I think the best argument for an enigma mesmerist is as an unchained rogue 3 or 4/enigma mesmerist X, where you hop into finesse training, sneak attack, and Accomplished Sneak Attacker early, then essentially treat yourself as a rogue with some spellcasting and the ability to go invisible more consistently than a normal rogue. As a single-class archetype that you take from 1-20, though, I can’t say I’d recommend enigma very highly—you give up way too much of your core mesmerist functionality for (mostly) inferior options.

# Toxitician

Toxitician


The Basics


        A mesmerist that’s trying to be an alchemist. I’ve never seen an execution fall so horribly on its face.


        Versatility: -2
        Power: -2


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Deft Fingers (Ex): Replaces consummate liar. Sleight of Hand is generally an inferior skill to Bluff, and with the exception of surreptitiously using your injections class feature on other creatures, you don’t need Sleight of Hand for anything. I’m inclined to think this is a bit of a downgrade.


        (1) Injections (Su): Replaces hypnotic stare. I was having trouble wrapping my head around injections for a bit, so I wrote out a pros and cons list to see whether I liked the ability. Turns out that I don’t! It’s a seriously inferior replacement for hypnotic stare that probably tanks the entire archetype. Judge for yourself, though.


   * Pro
   * Once triggered, injections have infinite range. As the heading says, one of the primary benefits of injections is that once you trigger them they continue working for as long as the duration lasts. Hypnotic stare, meanwhile, breaks if you ever move more than 30 feet from the target.
   * Not blocked by blindness. This one is very, very niche, but hypnotic stare requires a concentration check to use when you’re blinded, while injections do not. You’d still have to get into melee range with an enemy and pass your concealment d100 to even hit them with an injection, but we’ll ignore that.
   * Con
   * Still mind-affecting. Injections are still mind-affecting effects, so you don’t suddenly get to affect undead, vermin, etc. with your injections. Seems like this should have been a gimme, but there you have it.
   * It takes an hour to make injections. Base mesmerists roll out of bed each day ready to stare at people; toxiticians have to spend an hour crafting their injections. Minor, but a downgrade.
   * Enemies can figure out you injected them. Hypnotic stare, by RAW, does not allow any creature to figure out that they’re under the effects of hypnotic stare unless you want them to. Easy, breezy, beautiful. No matter how good you get at Sleight of Hand, though, some enemies will still beat your check with their Perception check, and that could spell major trouble.
   * Action economy and positioning are worse. Hypnotic stare is a very low-maintenance ability: you spend a swift action, and if an enemy is within 30 feet and not immune to mind-affecting effects, they take the Will save penalty. Injections, however, must be activated as a swift action, at which point you must get into melee range with a creature (yikes!) and then hit with a melee touch attack (yikes!) as a standard action (yikes!) to apply the injection. Action cost? Bad. Melee range? Bad. Need a successful attack? Bad.
   * Per-day uses are capped. This one hurts. Hypnotic stare is amazing because it can be applied as many times per day to enemies as you’d like, and remains in effect for as long as you’d like as long as no one dies or leaves the area. Injections, meanwhile, are capped at Mesmerist Level + Charisma Modifier uses per day.


        (1) Painful Injection (Su): Replaces painful stare. I’m not convinced that this ability is written very well. Does the damage keep proc’ing for as long as the injection lasts? So just 1d6+1 to 6d6+10 damage, every round, for 10 to 100 rounds? Because that would just be weird. You’d be an amazing assassin, but...weird. Anyway, for straight combat efficacy, painful injection is inferior to painful stare: no Intense Pain and no Manifold Stare means your damage output will suffer greatly, and your presumed inability to purchase stare feats will mean that much of your debuffing goes out the window, too.


        (3) Injection Improvement (Su): Replaces bold stare. Another bad replacement for a mesmerist class feature that didn’t need replacing. Toxiticians only get to use one (later two) improvements at a time, while base mesmerists just get to apply each and every bold stare they’ve purchased to each and every hypnotic stare they initiate. What’s more, a successful Fortitude save negates, where base mesmerists’ bold stares feature no save. Unacceptably bad.


        (3) Treatment Vials (Su): Alters touch treatment. Okay, here’s one I’ll get behind. Turning touch treatment into a potion-like consumable allows your teammates to heal their own damn status conditions, instead of relying on you to do it all the time. The only wrinkle is that your action cost goes from a swift action up to a standard. Noticing a pattern of this archetype shanking the mesmerist’s action economy to death?


        (11) Improved Injections (Su): Replaces glib lie. You lose glib lie in exchange for something the base mesmerist could do anyway. Why, Paizo?


Is It Worth It?


        Ha! No. Toxitician takes pretty much everything that’s good about hypnotic stare, bold stare, and painful stare and makes the good things bad. Smooth move, Ferguson.

# Dips

Dips


        For new players, “dipping” describes the process of taking some small number of class levels in a different class than the class the player intends to end up in eventually. One-level dips are the easiest, and most players agree that after about three levels you’re not really “dipping” anymore, but going for a truer multiclass. Dipping usually accomplishes a few things—the more things, the better the case for the dip.


   * Gaining class features that aren’t level-dependent. The paladin’s divine grace class feature, for example, simply adds your Charisma modifier to all your saving throws. You don’t need any further levels in paladin to enable the combo, which makes this particular dip attractive for Charisma-based classes.
   * Smoothing away feat prerequisites. Mesmerists are notoriously low on feats, which makes more involved combat builds difficult to achieve. Some dips throw a number of bonus feats at you in the first few levels, which helps you become combat-effective faster.
   * Weapon or armor proficiencies. This is a big one for mesmerists, who by default have access only to light armor and an underwhelming array of weaponry beyond simple weapons. If you want to build a ranged mesmerist, a Strength-based melee mesmerist, or heck, even a good crit-fishing Dexterity-based melee mesmerist, chances are good that you’ll need to dip at least one level.
   * BAB or saves. Mesmerists have ¾ BAB progression and weak Fortitude saves, plus what amounts to a mandate never to fail a Will save against an emotion or fear effect, lest they lose both their spellcasting and their towering ego save bonus. Classes that can shore up one or both of those weaknesses will be highly favored.

# Melee (Strength)

Melee (Strength)


   * Paladin 2 or Paladin 3 / Antipaladin 2: Paladin has an awful, awful lot to offer the mesmerist. For starters, we’ve got a d10 HD, full BAB, strong Fortitude and Will save progression, and access to shields, medium and heavy armor, and all martial weapons. Right off the bat, that’s a perfect setup for a Strength-based melee mesmerist. A 1/day smite evil isn’t awful, especially since the attack bonus scales with Charisma, rather than paladin level; lay on hands and at-will detect evil are also not bad, although lay on hands will stay perpetually weak with this dip. The real money in Paladin 2, though, is divine grace, which grants you a bonus equal to your Charisma modifier on all saves. If you build a paladin 2/mesmerist 2, you can easily have save bonuses of +9 (Fortitude: Base +3, Constitution +2, Charisma +4), +8 (Reflex: Base +3, Dexterity +1, Charisma +4), and +11 (Will: Base +6, Wisdom +1, Charisma +4 [Divine Grace]) by 4th level. If you’re willing to go even further into Paladin 3, you’ll get immunity to diseases and fear effects, the latter of which should be intensely interesting to any psychic spellcaster. The Riled mercy is also good for shutting down emotion effects. At any rate, this dip reigns supreme among mesmerist dips, especially for Strength-based mesmerists.
   * Fighter 1: A one-level fighter dip is great because it can be anything you want it to be: it can get you started on pretty much any combat style, it gives you some bonus BAB and Fortitude saves, and it shores up your armor and weapon proficiencies.
   * Iroran Paladin 1 / Scaled Fist UnMonk 1: Unarmed mesmerist builds are a bit wacky, but both of these dips play well with your Charisma modifier, and both give you some interesting tools.
   * Slayer 2: Another option for Strength-based melee mesmerists, slayer 2 gives you studied target, medium armor, bonuses to Fortitude and Reflex, and a slayer talent that should almost certainly be spent on a ranger combat style feat.

# Dipping To Mesmerist

Dipping To Mesmerist


        The mesmerist has a lot to offer as a dip, although where it really shines is as a dip for Charisma-based martial classes. Vigilantes, bloodragers, ninjas, rogues, paladins, antipaladins, cavaliers, samurai, swashbucklers—these classes are often in desperate need of ways to contribute to the team outside of damage, and mesmerist 2 will more than do the trick. Consummate liar is nice for feint-based classes like the swashbuckler; hypnotic stare helps soften up strong targets for allied spellcasters without noticeably cramping your action economy; mesmerist tricks can be used as powerful defensive options for you or allies; painful stare is a decent little damage boost; towering ego is fantastic for basically any martial class, almost all of which have serious holes in their Will saves; and having access to a few 1st-level spells like hermean potential or ill omen can’t help but improve the class. Mesmerist 3 gives you touch treatment, which you’ll probably be most interested in using on yourself as a swift action in order to strip away inconvenient status conditions, as well as a bold stare improvement that you’d likely use to purchase Disorientation or Sapped Magic.
Although it’s a bit less opportune, mesmerist 2 can also be used for Charisma-based casting classes. The opportunity cost of slower access to higher-level spells needs to be weighed carefully for classes like oracle or sorcerer, but if you’re willing to take the plunge, mesmerist 2 grants better BAB and Reflex saves, a few mesmerist tricks, a little bit of painful stare damage, towering ego—a huge bonus for any Charisma-based casting class—and hypnotic stare, which can greatly improve your ability to land single-target debuffs. For classes that want to focus exclusively on mind-affecting effects in a campaign rife with immune enemies, you can also push into mesmerist 3 for the Psychic Inception bold stare improvement. It’s not foolproof, but it will at least allow your hypnotic stare to always work on enemies that are immune to mind-affecting effects, and it’ll give your mind-affecting spells a 50% chance of working. Better than nothing, hey?

# Variant Multiclassing

Variant Multiclassing


        Variant multiclassing was introduced in Pathfinder Unchained, and allows characters to buy class features from other classes in exchange for about half their feats. Although variant multiclassing is generally more powerful on full-martial classes, which can skate by with fewer feats because of their bonus feats, there are still a few good variant multiclassing options for mesmerists. Bard is the most obvious choice for mesmerists, shoring up their relative weakness in Knowledge skills and also giving them some non-spell team buffing options via bardic performances. Rogue isn’t terrible, especially with some free sneak attack dice to add to your painful stare, but you never receive any rogue talents or finesse abilities, which makes tight Weapon Finesse builds even tighter. Among the base classes, oracle never fails to impress, given how excellent most revelations are, and inquisitor also isn’t bad for Intimidate/demoralization builds and all-around combat. Of note, variant multiclassing becomes stronger and stronger the more bonus feats PCs are given, so consider these options a bit more seriously if you’re going to be using Elephant in the Room feat tax rules or somesuch.

# Gestalt Builds

Gestalt Builds


Gestalt builds are builds that mash up two classes completely, taking the highest of any overlapping class features; they’re typically used when GMs either want a very high-power game or when there are fewer than four players and the GM needs to balance PC power with enemy power. It’s not usually that difficult to build a good gestalt character, since you’re getting so much power for free, but we can go over some of the things we’d look for in a gestalt pairing:


   * Full BAB or full casting. Gestalt builds are often most powerful when they’re combining two very dissimilar classes: fighter and wizard, sorcerer and vigilante, etc. Dissimilar class pairings enable each class to play to its strengths: the fighter contributes full BAB and a strong Fortitude save, while the wizard contributes full casting and a strong Will save. You can pair mesmerists with other ¾-BAB classes, of course, but you’ll be getting neither higher-caliber spells nor better BAB.
   * Strong Fortitude save progression. Fortitude save effects can be pretty dangerous, so it stands to reason that you’d want a strong Fortitude save.
   * Synergy with Charisma. This just makes good sense: you want to spend attribute points in as few places as possible, hence grabbing a gestalt class that also benefits from Charisma is ideal.

# Full Martials

Full Martials


        We’re just going to move alphabetically down the list, starting with barbarian. As much as I love the idea of something like a gestalt barbarian/hatemonger mesmerist, one of the main stipulations of the rage class feature is that you can’t use “any ability that requires patience or concentration,” which most people interpret as including spellcasting with concentration components, concentration checks of any kind, etc. Barbarian would be a match made in heaven otherwise—mesmerist shores up the Will save and grants a lot of versatility, and barbarian shores up the Fortitude save, BAB, and defensive staying power. Probably the better choice is bloodrager, which does many of the same things as barbarian while also allowing you to continue casting spells. Cavalier/samurai is okay, but if you’re going to go with a heavily armored mesmerist who rides a mount, why not paladin instead? Smite evil can easily become more powerful than challenge, you get divine health, fear immunity, and lay on hands + mercies, and there aren’t any differences in their saves, BAB, HD, or weapon and armor proficiencies. 
Fighter is another obvious choice for gestalt games. You’ve got armor and weapon training options, strong Fortitude save progression and full BAB, plus all the bonus combat feats you could shake a stick at. Fighter is particularly good if you want to build a demoralization machine, as they can qualify for all the goodies like Dazzling Display, Violent Display, Disheartening Display, Gory Finish, Dreadful Carnage, etc. without too much trouble while focusing their odd-level feats on all the usual mesmerist suspects. Bold stares, painful stare feats, and debuffing spells make fighter/mesmerist gestalts incredibly tough combatants to take down. Scaled fist unchained monks and brawlers each bring their own strengths to the table for unarmed strike builds, but I’m partial to the brawler for martial flexibility, a class feature that you simply can’t beat if you have a good level of systems mastery. Ranger doesn’t have anything to offer us, with its Wisdom focus, but slayer definitely brings the boom with studied target, combat style feats, and scaling sneak attack damage that stacks with painful stare. Swashbuckler brings a little less to the table than I’d like, since it’s still a lightly armored striker class with a strong Reflex save; building a fighter or slayer with a swashbuckler feeling in mind would probably yield better results in the long run. Finally, there’s the avenger vigilante to consider. Vigilantes can be almost anything you want them to be, including full-BAB wrecking balls, and there’s so much synergy between the two classes that it’s impossible to ignore: concealing your identity with carefully crafted lies, using Intimidate to scare enemies witless, playing the game of psychological combat. Strong contender for purple.

# Full Casters

Full Casters


        Starting once again at the top of the list, I really like arcanist/mesmerist pairings. You’d have to focus point buy on Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, and Charisma, leaving you somewhat strapped, but overall arcanists combine lots of things that are great about both the wizard and sorcerer classes while still allowing you to benefit from ¾ BAB, a d8 HD, better Reflex saves, and tons of debuffing options from your mesmerist side. Nice to have both psychic and arcane casting available, too—when you’re grappled, you cast mesmerist spells, and when you’re shaken, you cast arcanist spells. Cleric, druid, seducer witch, wizard, and psychic all have their strengths and weaknesses, but they’re mostly going to be too MAD to get much done—the exception being seducer witches, of course, which can become the premiere debuffers in the game through that unholy witch/mesmerist wombo combo. Sorcerer is a lot of fun, and doesn’t force you to prioritize Intelligence at all, unlike arcanist. You’d have to check a sorcerer guide for more information about bloodlines, however, because sorcerers and bloodragers are definitely not my strongest suit. The same could not be said for oracles, which I wrote a several-hundred-page guide on. Mysteries and revelations are extraordinarily powerful, and certain combos (shadow, whimsy, intrigue, etc.) would seem to fit the mesmerist class thematically as well as mechanically. Ready for my weirdest recommendation, though? Silksworn occultist. Although they’re not technically full casters, silksworn receive almost as many spells known and spells per day as sorcerers, and you get the enormous versatility of focus powers and silksworn eloquence to improve your face skills. A mesmerist/silksworn occultist would be the ultimate intrigue operator, and would never lack for non-spell combat options, between focus powers, mesmerist tricks, touch treatment, etc.

# ¾-BABers and ⅔-Casters

¾-BABers and ⅔-Casters


        Finally, we’ve got the ¾-BAB and ⅔ casting classes. As a rule, these classes won’t grant increases in your highest-level spells, your BAB, or your HD. What they can do, however, is graft other equally versatile classes onto the mesmerist. Starting at the top, we’ve got the bard/skald. There’s a lot of overlap between bards/skalds and mesmerists on everything from saves to BAB to spell lists, but where the mesmerist is the master of debuffing, the bard/skald is the master of buffing. A single gestalt mesmerist/bard can grease the wheels of combat to such an incredible degree that most teams will simply steamroll combat once inspire courage and bold stare are both activated. Hunter, investigator, alchemist, magus, and warpriest don’t have a lot to offer, mostly because the non-Charisma mental attributes required will start to make the class too MAD to accomplish anything. I have to give an honorable mention to the sworn of the eldest inquisitor, which turns the inquisitor’s casting stat into Charisma—it’s gathlain-only, but that’s no trouble, since gathlain are a pretty solid mesmerist race already. Finally, this list wouldn’t be complete without mentioning the unchained rogue, which feels like an unofficial parent class of the mesmerist already. Sneak attack + painful stare is an absolute delight, debilitating injury stacks wonderfully with Disorientation and other bold stare improvements, finesse training gives the mesmerist everything it needs to start a Dexterity build, free of charge, and any spellcasting added to the rogue chassis slaps hard. Tough to imagine anything more iconic. Ninja is eminently possible, but without a Paizo-official unchained version, probably less ideal than the unchained rogue. Besides, much of what people love about the ninja class is that it adds some spellcasting options to the rogue (vanishing trick, etc.), and the mesmerist needs no help there. A teisatsu vigilante is probably closer to being optimal if you still want the ninja flavor, and gives you the added deliciousness of social talents and vigilante talents.

# Prestige Classes

Prestige Classes


        Because the psychic classes were introduced so late in Pathfinder 1e’s publication cycle, there are very, very few prestige classes that truly suit mesmerists. Those that do suit the class, however, actually suit it quite well. We’ll start with an obvious recommendation that almost never goes awry: evangelist. The evangelist prestige class is associated with the Obedience feats (which, again, I wrote a massive guide about), which involve you performing some kind of task or obeisance thematic to your deity and receiving bonuses or special abilities in return. Evangelist is popular because you ultimately lose out on only one level of class features in exchange for an entire 10 levels of prestige class goodness; the big-ticket items in evangelist are always your deity’s three divine boons, so check On Bended Knee carefully to see which deities might suit your needs. Proctors and feysworn also deserve some mention as other prestige classes that come from the Obedience feats and grant solid bonuses. Esoteric knight is another option for mesmerists, although it leans considerably away from spellcasting and more toward straightforward combat applications. A larger HD, better Fortitude save, better armor and weapon proficiencies, bonus combat feats, and some magus-like psychic esoterica are all on the table here.

# MES 660: Magic Items

MES 660: Magic Items


If I’m being honest, there aren’t a great many good items for mesmerists—psychic classes in general, really. Since they were fairly late additions to the Pathfinder canon, the repertoire of cool, class-specific items simply never built up for them in the same way that it did for core classes. If I were building a mesmerist, I’d devote myself first to the Big Six and a mesmerizing tattoo (obviously), snag a metamagic rod or two, grab a few items for social interaction, and then spend the rest on consumables—wands, scrolls, and potions never go out of style for spontaneous spellcasters, who need versatility more than anything else. Maybe mesmerists will come earlier in the production cycle if they ever make it to Pathfinder 2e, and we can hope for some better items. # Wondrous Items

Wondrous Items


The Big Six: Before you go buying anything else, make sure that you’ve purchased a cloak of resistance, ring of protection, amulet of natural armor, magic weapon, magic armor, and mental attribute headband or physical attribute belt. All Pathfinder encounters are balanced around the basic assumption that PCs will have these items, so if you choose not to invest in them (or don’t know that investing in them is important) then your characters will suffer, guaranteed.


Mask of the Mesmerist (CL 5, 25,000 gp): +10 feet of range on your hypnotic stare is great, and the ability to implant one trick per day without expending any resources is also fine. The headlining item here is the -2 penalty that enemies take to attack the wearer of the mask, however: between the Disorientation bold stare and the mask of the mesmerist, enemies will take a -5 penalty to attack you while they’re affected by hypnotic stare—and that’s before any other debuffs. Quite strong, although you likely won’t have the pocket change until Book 5 or 6 of most Paizo APs.
Mesmerizing Tattoo (CL 5, 10,800 gp): The mesmerizing tattoo amps up the Will save penalties from your hypnotic stare class feature and enchantment spells, making it an unthinkably good purchase. Do so with all speed.
Rod of Metamagic, Logical (CL 17, 3,000 gp): All psychic casters need some means of escaping from mind-affecting effects. You’d hate to have to use this rod, but when you need it, you really need it. Remember that using touch treatment is an option in some cases!
Rod of Metamagic, Persistent (CL 17, 9,000 gp): You could ostensibly have a few Persistent metamagic rods hanging around, and I wouldn’t mind one bit—it’s an incredible spell for dedicated debuffers, as any veteran witch player can tell you. A lesser rod is going to be fine, since 1st- through 3rd-level spells are your bread and butter anyway. With a mesmerizing tattoo and your 8th-level upgrade to hypnotic stare, those 1st- through 3rd-level spells will hit as hard as 5th- through 8th-level spells, anyway, and Persistent Spell adds an effective +4 (or thereabouts) to your save DCs.
Rod of Metamagic, Quicken (CL 17, 35,000 gp): Quicken metamagic is incredibly costly, whether you’re paying with spell slots or items, but mesmerists are in a great position to be able to use this rod effectively. On what, you ask? Why, ill omen and hermean potential, of course! There’s no combo better than using quickened ill omen to get an enemy to roll every save they make twice, taking the lesser result, then hitting them with a giant pile of debuffs.


Eyes of the Liar (CL 5, 16,000 gp): Pricey, but outright immunity to thought detection and a bonus to your Bluff checks is very on-brand for mesmerists.
Ring of Eloquence (CL 3, 3,500 gp): A cheap and serviceable boost to your social interaction skills.
Ring of Psychic Mastery (CL 14, 50,000 gp): Very expensive, but potentially worthwhile to increase your spells per day.
Western Star Ioun Stone (CL 12, 4,000 gp): A noteworthy upgrade to a hat of disguise that doesn’t cost much more gold. CL 12 compared with CL 1 is a huge boost both in terms of duration and resilience to targeted dispels.


Cape of Bravado (CL 2, 7,000 gp): A great item for vexing daredevils and other feint-based mesmerists. Bluff and AC bonuses are just what the doctor ordered.


Eye-Stealing Locket (CL 3, 24,000 gp): Even accounting for the hypnotic stare penalty, the Fortitude save DC will be way, way too low to accomplish much by the time you can afford this item.




Unique Weapons


Sadist’s Lash (CL 11, 22,301 gp): One of the very few unique weapons that I would recommend for mesmerists, the sadist’s lash helps the class both deliver enchantment touch spells and grants massive penalties to their Will saves against enchantment effects if they fail a Will save. Granted, the save DC is quite low, but you’re bound to get some low rolls in among the successful saves.

Merciful (CL 5): Enforcer + a merciful weapon is a classic combo for debuffing with Intimidate. Assuming your GM is okay with the cognitive dissonance of having a cruel merciful weapon, you can do even more debuffing with that setup.

Strategy


        The Daredevil build is about as archetypal as you can get: an Errol Flynn-meets-Hannibal Lecter fighter who uses its innate Dexterity, skill with a blade, and uncanny ability to get inside an enemy’s head to dismantle foes piece by piece. There are a few things happening here, so let’s dive in. First, the build has a 1-level swashbuckler dip. It’s an easy way to pick up opportune parry and riposte, panache, Weapon Finesse, and bonuses to your Fortitude save, Reflex save, and BAB, all with minimal penalties down the line. The vexing daredevil mesmerist archetype is going to synergize quite naturally with this chassis, granting us a bunch of bonus feats focused on feinting (Improved Feint, Greater Feint, Greater Mesmerizing Feint). Combined with the half-elf’s Skill Focus: Bluff (or Acrobatics, if you prefer) and the elf favored class bonus granting us bonuses to our Acrobatics and Bluff checks, there should basically be no enemy you can’t outmaneuver and no enemy that you can’t feint, including those typically immune to mind-affecting effects.
        Feinting doesn’t typically allow PCs to take more than one attack (Improved Feint allows you to feint as a move action, but you’re still left with only a standard to attack), so the cornerstone of this build is the Surprise Strike dazzling feint that you’ll receive at 8th level. Sloppy Defense is going to be your go-to for targets with exceptionally high AC, but for most garden-variety targets, you should start with a feint (move action), then follow it up with a Surprise Strike (two attacks at BAB/BAB-5). At later levels you’ll pick up Improved Critical, Critical Focus, the Critical Strike dazzling feint, and the Vision of Blood masterful so that you can regularly stun-lock enemies with no save. I really, really wanted to work Butterfly’s Sting into this build, but it’s that stupid Combat Expertise prerequisite that spoils everything. If your GM is okay with waiving Combat Expertise (or is playing with feat tax system like Elephant in the Room) then you should absolutely pick up Butterfly’s Sting instead of Combat Reflexes at 7th level—the ability to hand out crits to your teammates is way too good to pass up, especially since none of your bonus damage from painful stare multiplies on a crit.
        Now, without any bold stare improvements (notably Psychic Inception, but also Sapped Magic, Sluggishness, etc.) or significant investment in casting feats, this build is going to be pretty terrible at targeting enemies with spells, which is why I didn’t bother to pump Charisma all that high. Instead, you should focus on solid, no-save team buffs or battlefield control spells: displacement, see invisibility, obscuring mist, pyrotechnics, freedom of movement, etc. I’d go so far as to argue that this build really shouldn’t be thought of as a mesmerist, but rather as a swashbuckler with some unusual tricks and spellcasting up its sleeves. I’m not super fond of the feint playstyle myself, but with so many feinting tools given to the class, I felt that I had to devote at least one build to it, and vexing daredevil is unquestionably the right archetype for the job.

Strategy


        This is an old, cheesy build that I’ve had in mind for a long time. In my mind, I call it the Jackie Chan ‘I Don’t Want Trouble’ build, because it’s designed to make you extremely deadly and hard to kill without you appearing to be a threatening combatant. It’s designed to do one thing really well: give you a crap-ton of AC that you can more or less loan out to adjacent allies. Although the feat progression might work better on a full unchained monk or full brawler chassis (and you can in fact substitute a brawler dee-yip for the unchained monk dee-yip, if you’ve got more systems mastery and prefer to have martial flexibility) the mesmerist class offers the incredible versatility of spellcasting and mesmerist tricks—and because the majority of a mesmerist’s damage comes from painful stare, rather than other sources, you don’t even have to worry that much about your small unarmed strike damage die, your pitiful Strength bonus, etc. So let’s jump into it!
        At the core of this build is the decision to fight defensively. To review briefly, the decision to fight defensively imparts a -4 penalty to all of your attack rolls in exchange for a +2 to AC. While this isn’t ludicrously bad, it’s certainly not a favorable exchange for PCs, who often need higher attack bonuses to hit monsters than monsters need to hit them in return. So let’s stack the deck in our favor, huh? We’ll start with 3 ranks in Acrobatics, which adds a +1 to your dodge bonus when you fight defensively (per the Acrobatics rules text), as well as Crane Style, which reduces your penalty to -2 and adds +1 to your dodge bonus to AC. By 3rd level, then, we’ve changed the math on fighting defensively to -2/+4, which is much better. Cautious Fighter at 5th level further improves this combo to -2/+6, and Crane Wing at 7th level ups it all the way to -2/+6 against ranged attacks or -2/+10 against melee attacks. Osyluth Guile at 11th level will give you about +6 to your AC against one opponent you can see when you fight defensively. At 13th and 15th level, the gloves come off: Blundering Defense and Uncanny Defense, two more halfling-exclusive feats, will grant your adjacent allies bonuses to their AC when you fight defensively, as well as bonuses to your Reflex saves and CMD. How exactly these bonuses shake out numerically is a little difficult to interpret, because while the rules text specifies that allies receive a bonus equal to “half the dodge bonus you gain,” you run into strange territory when you’re gaining different bonuses to AC against melee and ranged attacks (Crane Wing) or against one enemy (Osyluth Guile). At a bare minimum, allies will get a +3 to AC and CMD, which might scale as high as +8 if your GM rules that both Crane Wing and Osyluth Guile are eligible based on the text of Blundering Defense and Uncanny Defense. Slap on a few layers of displacement and mirror image, and your character should be nigh-impossible to hit, sporting a +16 bonus to AC vs. melee attacks, a 50% miss chance, mirror images, touch treatment, better-than-average saves from your unchained monk dip, and evasion to take advantage of that great Reflex save.
        Of course, tanking in Pathfinder only works if you’re enough of a threat that enemies can’t simply ignore you, hence the early investment in Manifold Stare. Your damage rolls will be pretty abysmal as a Small-sized halfling with barely scaled unarmed strike dice, so you’ll be relying almost entirely on painful stare and Intense Pain to deal damage. This strategy leaves you susceptible to mind-affecting immunity (which shuts down hypnotic stare—and therefore painful stare—until you can acquire Psychic Inception at 9th level) as well as precision damage immunity, but hey—I’m not a miracle worker, and I can’t change the way Paizo designed the class, which was as a mind-affecting, precision damage-heavy hybrid of the rogue, bard, and witch classes. If you were particularly worried about undead in your campaign, the spirit walker archetype works just fine with this build, and allows you to avoid having to spend a bold stare on Psychic Inception if you’d rather focus on Disorientation to make yourself even harder to hit. I wish I had about 10 more bonus feats in this build, but what are you gonna do?

If they step away, you take an attack of opportunity; if they draw and try to use a magic item, you use your attack of opportunity to sunder that item; if they try to cast a spell, you release your readied action, likely dealing massive damage and forcing them to abandon casting. Rinse and repeat until death.

# Strategy

Strategy

The goal here is to make a hybrid combatant/spellcaster who can be accompanied into battle by legions of undead servitors, and who can affect those undead servitors (or undead enemies, since I created this build with campaigns like Tyrant’s Grasp or Carrion Crown in mind) with mind-affecting effects.
        Each of the three classes used in this build brings something different: antipaladin greatly increases the offensive and defensive capabilities of the build, shoring up save bonuses and allowing us to dump Dexterity in favor of heavy armor; the spirit walker archetype makes undead entirely viable targets for mind-affecting effects, in addition to granting us Command Undead and the mesmerist’s usual host of buffing and debuffing tools; and finally, the evangelist prestige class—which has a very low opportunity cost, thanks to the aligned class feature—gives us animate dead 1/day as a material component-free spell-like ability, not to mention incredible immunities and save bonuses against death effects, positive energy, negative energy, fatigue, exhaustion, nonlethal damage, disease, poison, paralysis, mind-affecting effects, stunning, ability damage, ability drain, and negative levels. Animating the dead using Orcus’ first divine boon is only one avenue of necromancy available to us, however; you can also use command undead from the spirit walker spell list and the spirit walker’s Command Undead bonus feat to transfer animated dead out of your animate dead HD pool and into other pools as needed—see Brewer’s Guide to Undeath for details on the various HD pools, and make sure to stock up on scrolls of desecrate. When you’re not commanding undead servitors to die for their master, you can wade into melee with a huge martial weapon, Power Attack, Intense Pain, and Manifold Stare, or else hang back and use bold stares and necromancy spells to further break down the competition. Ray of enfeeblement, howling agony, blindness/deafness, conditional curse, calamitous flailing, vampiric touch, fear, ray of exhaustion, major curse, enervation, unwilling shield, possession, and waves of exhaustion should be your workhorse spells for living targets, because somewhat ironically, Fortitude-based necromancy spells won’t work on undead targets. For undead targets, simply use the best enchantment spells! Why not? It’s not like they’re immune to your mind-affecting effects anymore…
