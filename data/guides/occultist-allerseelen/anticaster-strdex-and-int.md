# Anticaster (STR/DEX and INT)

Anticaster (STR/DEX and INT)


        The notion of an “anticaster”—a character that specializes in killing spellcasters—is prevalent for one specific reason in Pathfinder: spellcasters are, bar none, the deadliest enemies you will face in the game. More than full-attacks, spells are the deadliest weapons you will square off against in 1e, and at later levels nearly every enemy is some form of spellcaster, whether that casting comes from spells or spell-like abilities. The elements that go into creating such an anticaster are somewhat nebulous, but I generally define the role as one that meets as many of the following prerequisites as possible:


      1. Energy resistance, strong touch AC, and strong save values, particularly Will and Fortitude saves. The strongest casters are also the squishiest. They know this, so they will try to remove threats with extreme prejudice, usually by targeting a foe’s weakest save: phantasmal killer, finger of death, dominate person, disintegrate, bestow curse, transmute flesh to stone, baleful polymorph, and more are all some of the save-or-suck effects that casters will try against prospective threats. The good news from a build standpoint is that Occultists are generally very strong in their defenses against these effects! You have a good Will and Fortitude save progression already, and the ability to add on more defenses through the Legacy Weapon (transmutation) or Aegis (abjuration) focus powers can help you respond flexibly to threats as they arise. Energy resistance is an honorable mention, as most of a caster’s evocation spells will tend to deal energy damage; anticipating which kind they’ll use can be tricky, but without a strong Reflex save, it’s the best defense Occultists have. Fire’s probably the most common, followed by electricity and then cold.
      2. Counters to common defensive caster tricks. Mirror image, blur, displacement, vanish, mislead—you know the ones I’m talking about. Legacy Weapon (transmutation) can again help you deal with miss chances, concealment, and cover, but you’ll also want to have a strong portfolio of spells like see invisibility, true seeing, echolocation, etc. to pair with focus powers and resonant powers like Radiance (evocation) and Third Eye (divination).
      3. Counters to traditional battlefield control spells. Walls, pits, fogs, mists, entangling or grappling effects, difficult terrain, etc. are all effects that casters love to use while they pound your team into a fine powder. At the very least, anyone stepping into the anticaster role has to have a means (preferably more than one) of countering these tactics for themselves, usually by ignoring the penalties: walls and pits can be flown over with fly or Mind Over Gravity (transmutation), fogs and mists can be penetrated with the ashen path spell, goz masks or fog-cutting lenses, and various enchantments from Legacy Weapon (transmutation), entanglements and grapples can be broken with freedom of movement…you get the gist. If possible, an anticaster should also stock up on ways to help the entire team ignore these effects: dispel magic and break enchantment are the workhorse spells here.
      4. Counters to summoning. Summoning disposable meat shields is another favorite caster trick, so load up on effects like a summon bane weapon with Legacy Weapon (transmutation), spells like dismissal or banishment, or simply summon your own creatures using Shadow Beast (illusion), Servitor (conjuration), or Necromantic Servant (necromancy) to lock horns with the mooks while you take care of the real threat.
      5. Ways to quickly close the distance between you and a caster, and ways to prevent a caster from escaping. If things go pear-shaped, casters love to use teleport, dimension door, plane shift, getaway, contingency, invisibility, mislead, gaseous form, ethereal jaunt, etc. to flee the field. I can’t think of any anticaster that won’t need both the Side Step focus power (conjuration implement school) and the dimensional anchor spell to get up in a caster’s business, then stop them from running. Etherealness is harder to deal with than teleportation, but ghost whip, force sword, purge spirit, entrap spirit, etheric shards, and a Legacy Weapon (transmutation) with the ghost touch weapon enchantment can make pretty short work of even incorporeal creatures.
      6. Ways to penalize defensive casting. Once you’re up in a caster’s face, you need two more elements: the ability to stop them from 5-foot stepping away, and the ability to penalize their concentration checks to cast defensively. If you can get these two elements on their feet, casters will have no choice but to cast defensively all the time—which increasingly risks losing their spells as they struggle against their lower concentration checks. Following 5-foot steps is most easily accomplished with the Step Up, Following Step, and Step Up and Strike feats (Step Up is the only key component of a magehunting rotation, but the other two feats really help—and not only against casters, but also against ranged and reach weapon users) and weapon enchantments like distracting and greater distracting (purchased with Legacy Weapon, naturally) are the most accessible ways for an Occultist to penalize concentration checks to cast defensively. 
      7. Ways to debuff a caster once you’re up in their business. Casters typically have low Fortitude and Reflex saves, so you’re perfectly situated as a caster yourself to lean on that weakness and debuff them through the floor. Necromancy in particular has a lot of nasty tricks to pull on casters (blindness/deafness is a low-level favorite) all of which should halt a caster’s ability to pose much of a threat to you or your team while you focus on killing them. If nothing else works, Occultists are capable of summoning an antimagic field, and the Trappings of the Warrior panoply still lets them behave as though they had full BAB even in an antimagic field. Who do you think wins between a full-BAB martial and a full caster in an antimagic field? If your money was on the martial, congrats, you’re not the sucker at the poker table.


If you’ve followed each of these steps dutifully, you’ve achieved the goal that every anticaster sets out for: to make all of a caster’s options bad options. They could try to blast you, but then they remember that you’ve got great saves and touch AC. They could try to hide, but then they remember that you can see invisible creatures and aren’t fooled by concealment. They could try to drop you into a pit, but then they remember you’re flying. They could try to run, but then they remember they’ve been locked in place by dimensional anchor. They could try to 5-foot step away, but then they remember that you’ll follow and take an attack of opportunity. Oh, and the cherry on top: they’re blind, deaf, staggered, entangled, prone, bleeding, on fire, cursed, etc. What casters primarily use to defeat martial characters are options: options to control, slow, penalize, obscure, summon, deter—and in their hour of greatest need, to escape or hide. Take away those options, and a caster is just a poorly armored nerd waving their arms around. There’s no feeling quite as satisfying as killing a caster that you’ve finally beaten at their own chess game; all it takes is some imagination, preparation, and systems mastery to do that consistently. Anticasters aren’t generally going to go toe-to-toe with casters on spell save DCs, but even so, an anticaster build will demand higher investment in Intelligence than most combat builds would need. Let’s call it STR, INT > CON > DEX, WIS > CHA for Strength-based anticasters, and DEX, INT > CON > WIS > STR, CHA for Dexterity-based anticasters.


Pros
	Cons
	You could single-handedly stop a TPK. No, that’s not an exaggeration. Casters are the deadliest enemies in Pathfinder, and killing them quickly could avert disaster for your team.
	Takes a bit more feat support. Step Up is a must, and you can layer on Following Step, Step Up and Strike, and more defensive feats as needed to be competitive against full casters.
	You have most of the tools you need already. Becoming an anticaster is less about radically redefining your build than it is about stocking up on a few choice scrolls, choosing your focus powers and implement spells with a bit more of a focused eye, and learning more about how casters tend to operate in combat. Certainly you’re more suited to this role than other non-magical martial classes.
	Takes a bit more systems mastery. Just as I wouldn’t recommend that new players attempt to play full casters, I wouldn’t recommend that new players attempt to beat full casters. It takes a while to get inside casters’ heads: how they think, how they move, how they react, what they’ll try next. Once you know that, it takes some in-depth knowledge of the system to pick the right tools to counter, ignore, or forestall their tactics.
	It’s satisfying. Checkmating a caster is a gigantic tactical and systems mastery puzzle to figure out, and there’s no feeling quite like flexing into the perfect combination of weapon enchantments, armor enchantments, feats, focus powers, actions, etc. to bring your team a big win.
	

	



Suggested Spells


        The good news about an anticaster loadout is that it doesn’t vary significantly from what you’d have been taking as a normal melee/ranged combat build. There are a few more targeted debuffs (rigor mortis, confusion, mindwipe, cold iron fetters, etc.) and straight-up anticaster spells (dispel magic, antimagic field, dimensional anchor, summoner conduit, etc.) but otherwise you should view an anticaster build as a refinement of a combat build, not an overhaul of one. Honestly, as long as you can work in two abjuration implements, one transmutation implement, and one or two implements from the conjuration, divination, and evocation schools, you should be golden—scrolls and wands will have to make up the rest. I wish Occultists got twice the implement schools they do, but they don’t, so we’re all going to have to live with the crunch and make the best of it.

Suggested Implements


        Abjuration and transmutation, I wish I knew how to quit you. Yes, the abjuration and transmutation schools are again at the top of the list. They have the best anticaster spells, and you simply must have Mind Over Gravity, Quickness, Aegis, and Legacy Weapon if you want to respond quickly to caster threats. Anticaster builds aren’t quite as beholden to Trappings of the Warrior as mainstream combat builds due to casters’ low AC, but full BAB still helps a ton, and Trappings grants you more abjuration and transmutation spells, all of which you’ll need desperately. By the time you get to 6th level, I would make an evocation or conjuration implement your priority, with a slight emphasis on evocation. Evocation’s focus powers beyond Radiance aren’t any great shakes for what you’re trying to accomplish, but its spells for regulating darkness levels, purging invisibility, and slapping around incorporeals are exactly what you’re starting to need. Conjuration should come in at 10th in time for Side Step and dimension door. Divination might be a good 14th-level pick for true seeing and constant see invisibility. The ratings stand at: Abjuration > Transmutation, Trappings, Evocation > Conjuration, Divination, Necromancy > Illusion > Enchantment.

Suggested Archetypes


Haunt Collector and Naturalist are probably your two most powerful anticaster archetypes from 1st level, the former for its Champion, Guardian, Trickster, and Hierophant spirit bonuses, and the latter for natural focus’ ability to shrug off really bad save-or-die effects. Panoply Savant isn’t quite as good here due to a reduced need for high BAB from Trappings when fighting casters, but still does great work if you want to go that direction. Finally, Psychodermist bursts late, but there’s no beating residual hatred and manifest abilities for versatility and power against endgame casters. Haunt Collector will have a lower ceiling, but it’ll also be a heck of a lot easier to play than Psychodermist, which requires very careful management of trophies and active monster abilities.
