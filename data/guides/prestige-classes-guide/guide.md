﻿Pathfinder Prestige Class Guide
  
As of December 2020, Paizo has released 122 Prestige Classes. Many are terrible, either because of poor design, or because they originate in D&D 3.5e and the mechanics were never updated. However, there are a lot of new and great ones that have gone unnoticed. I wanted to quickly go through most of them and give them a rating, if you disagree or feel I missed something please message or comment why. 
Rating System
The Prestige Classes are graded on 4 main categories: Martial power, Magic Progression, Utility, and Novelty. 
* Requirements means how difficult it is to qualify for the Prestige Class.
* Martial Power includes BAB progression, weapon and armor proficiency, and high hit dice. 
* Magic Power includes spells per day and abilities that increase your spellcasting power.
* Utility includes high skill ranks, and non-combat powers.
* Novelty, a.k.a. Flavor is the ability to experience something new, something you can’t get from regular classes. Prestige Classes make you jump through hoops and take sub-optimal choices, so they had better be fun to play.
Acronyms:
PrC=Prestige class
RAW=Rules as written

When to Prestige Class
All classes offer a variety of abilities that are drip-fed each level. If you rely on one of these class features, like Sneak Attack or Sorcerer Bloodlines, then taking a PrC will stop that feature from getting stronger unless the PrC says it continues to progress or the features stack. 
Wizard is an excellent class to add a prestige class to, as they rely primarily on spellcasting, and don’t get more features later on (although by RAW they don’t get new spells known). Summoners and Sorcerers, by contrast, rely heavily on their spell-like-abilities which only progress with class level, and so generally don’t mix well with prestige classes.
PrCs often require a certain number of ranks in a skill. Since the number of ranks you can put into a skill is capped by your character level, this is basically a level requirement. For example, a Genie Binder requires: “Skills: Diplomacy 4 ranks, Knowledge (nobility) 4 ranks, Knowledge (planes) 11 ranks.” Therefore, you must be level 11 before taking this PrC. I will try to put the highest rank requirement first so you know what level you must be.
Grading system
Sky Blue: Superior option, often more powerful than a base class.
Blue: Fantastic option, great reason to take the class.
Green: Solid option, you won’t be worse for taking it.
Orange: Okay option, or useful only in rare circumstances.
Black: Unrated, either because usefulness varies or because it’s not applicable.
Red: Bad option, useless or extremely situational.

Standard Action Buffs are Bad
I have made many characters I thought were “invincible.” I calculated all their buffs stacked together and thought, “no enemies can harm me.” Then, when it came to actual combat, we rolled initiative, enemies were right in front of me, and I had 3 buffs to stack as standard action each. This meant that if I wanted to be strong, I had to spend the first three rounds of combat sitting on my ass while my party-members attacked.
The lesson: if you have to know the fight is coming and stack buffs to be powerful, you’re not powerful. Several PrCs are rated lower due to their main features being limited resources that use a Standard Action to activate at the start of combat, effectively wasting your first turn or  surprise round.


Progression Helpers
Most prestige classes include a power drop in exchange for all the new stuff you get. There are some things you can do to mitigate this power drop, such as taking one of these feats:
Magical Knack - a.k.a. the multiclass trait. +2 caster Level progression capped at your Hit Dice. Also, it's a trait, so effectively free for multiclassing spellcasters.
Accomplished Sneak Attacker - +1d6 to your sneak attack, capped at ½ your character level rounded up. 
Shaping Focus - Wild Shape progression, an extra 4 levels of progression lets a druid take a dip or prestige class without penalty to your best ability. Because you need the Wild Shape feature, you have to already be a 4th level druid to take this.
Boon Companion - An extra 4 levels to your familiar/animal companion progression, capped at character level, means you can take a substantial dip without hurting your buddy’s power level. 
Phantom Ally - An extra 4 levels to your Spiritualist’s Phantom progression, capped at character level. People online have suggested homebrewing a similar feat for Eidolons  (kinda bullshit that spiritualist has one but summoner doesn’t)
Favored Prestige Class - this is a prerequisite for another feat. +1 HP or skill point per PrC level is kinda meh on its own, but is great for getting you:
Prestigious Spellcaster - nullify the power dip of a prestige class that doesn’t give +1 spell progression at first level. Shame it takes two feats.
Eldritch Heritage - It takes two feats, but you get a 1st level power from a Sorcerer bloodline at a -2 effective level. While this can technically qualify you for some PrCs most bloodline powers are pretty bland.
Obedience Classes
The “Obedience” feats were first introduced as “Celestial Obedience” for those who worship the Empyreal Lords. Each Lord has a different “obedience” taking 1 hour to perform, and in exchange the player receives a special bonus and access to powerful “Boons” at 12, 16, and 20 Hit Dice. However, the Mystery Cultist but certain prestige classes speed up this progression, starting after 7th level and granting these boons at 10th, 13th, and 16th levels if you take the PrC as soon as possible. The concept was a success and was expanded upon with “Deific Obedience” for the main pantheon of gods. 


There are now feats for Demons, Fiends, Fey, and Monitors each with their own special prestige classes. Deific Obedience works for any deity, so any worshipers can become the main trio of Obedience PrCs. Martial-focused Sentinels, Spellcasting-themed Exalted, and the All-Rounder Evangelist, which can be taken at a minimum of 5th level, and granting Boons at 8th, 11th, and 14th level. PrCs belonging to smaller groups of gods require their specific Obedience feat and can be taken at a minimum of 7th level, granting Boons at 10th, 13th, and 16th level. The quality and effectiveness of each of these feats depends largely on the odiousness of the required Obedience and the quality of the Diety’s Boons. 


For example, Asmodeus requires you to have a ruby-bladed knife, and use it to inscribe symmetrical cuts into the flesh of another creature, drain some of their blood into a bowl of bone made from the skull of a sentient humanoid, use it to draw a large pentagram on the ground, kneel within the pentagram, and pray to Asmodeus. This requires you to either bring or find another creature and to perform this crazy ritual every. frickin’. day. In exchange, you get +4 on saves against fire, and his boons vary widely in quality. Caeden Cailean, by contrast, requires you to compose a little song, and sing it while taking sips of alcohol. In exchange, you get +4 on saves vs poison and on average his boons are pretty good.


Each of these PrCs that require an Obedience feat will be marked with “Obedience” in the title. They will be assessed based on the static abilities granted, as the quality of each God’s boons will heavily impact the effectiveness of the PrC. For a full breakdown of these, I heavily recommend that you check out Walter’s Guide to Deific Obediences.


Core Prestige Classes
The Core Prestige Classes are a mixed bag. Some are excellent and continue to be staples at the gaming table either because they are worth the extra investment (Mystic Theurge), or are so cool that players are drawn to them (Dragon Disciple). However, there are some that just don’t hold up, mostly because they have been left behind by Paizo’s later offerings. 
To me, the Duelist has been surpassed by the Swashbuckler. While the Eldritch Knight hasn’t been replaced by the Magus, due to the fun and unique builds that are still possible, the Magus does a much better job of realizing the “Spellsword” concept.


Arcane Archer: Magic Sniper
Tags:        Martial, Spellcaster, Arcane
Base Class:        While you don’t inherently need to multiclass, the BAB requirements means you’ll either multiclass Martial 6/Spellcaster 1, or you’ll take 8 levels in a 6th Level Caster like Magus or Bard.
Requirement:        BAB+6, 3 Archer feats, and 1st level Arcane Spells.
Martial Power:        d10, Full BAB, and proficiencies with weapons, armor, and shields. Excellent.
Spell Power:         7/10 spell progression would be orange, but the abilities synergize with your spellcasting. If you’re willing to take Prestigious Spellcaster, this can go higher.
Utility:         4+Int and only four skills is ok, but it could benefit a more martial class. The versatility of your Imbued Arrows will constitute most of your utility.
Novelty:         Spell arrows are cool. It's an interesting chance to stack damage, or to extend the range of some spells beyond normal. Shooting an anti-magic field is fun.
Progression:        Spell progression is already limited, and you don’t get any other progression.
Enhance Arrows: This takes care of one of the biggest problems of a normal archer, enchanted weapons. Your arrows are automatically enchanted for free when you fire them. Awesome, and it only gets better with levels.
Imbue Arrow: You cast a spell, imbue it into an arrow, and fire it as one action and you can use this to deliver normally undeliverable spells. Anti-magic Field is the best example.
Seeker Arrow: Shoot around obstacles, corners, and basically ignore cover and concealment. Because it doesn’t take an action, this can be combined with other abilities to help hit when it matters, but it’s not strong on its own.
Phase Arrow: Standard action shoot and arrow that phases through obstacles, and negaltes cover, concealment, armor, and shields. Although it's more powerful than Seeker Arrow, it can’t be combined with other attacks due to the standard action.
Hail of Arrows: Once per day, shoot every enemy in range, to a max of your PrC level. Given that you get this ability at 8th level, shooting 8-10 enemies in a single round with your automatically enchanted arrows is pretty sweet.
Arrow of Death: The Arcane Archer can spend 1 day to make a special slaying arrow that forces a Fort Save or the target immediately dies. The save is 20+Charisma Mod. While this sounds great, by the time you get here, you’ll be at least level 16, maybe higher, and it is unlikely to work. Sounds great, but unless you can force a failure, it won’t work very well.
Final Thoughts: The Arcane Archer offers a unique combat style of infusing your arrows with magic and spells. I can recommend with the caveat that the Eldritch Archer Magus does the same thing a little more smoothly .While this PrC does offer a lot of combat options, all the best ones are at the beginning or the end. Most people recommend a 2 level dip, or play the Myrmidarch Magus for a mechanically stronger and smoother experience. 


Arcane Trickster: Wizard Rogues
Tags:        Sneak Attack, Spellcaster, Arcane, Non-lawful
Base Class:        The classic combo is Wizard 3/Rogue 3, but you could also find sneak attack in classes like the Vivisectionist Alchemist. The Accomplished Sneak Attacker feat means you could meet this requirement with Wizard 3/Rogue 1.
Requirements:        Any Non-Lawful, 4 ranks in three skills, ability to cast Mage hand plus one 2nd level arcane spell, and Sneak Attack +2d6. 
Martial Power:        d6 HD, ½ BAB, and no new proficiencies is pretty bad, but bonuses to your sneak attack and other abilities means your physical attacks will be pretty effective. 
Spell Power:        10/10 spells per level, and Tricky Spells and Surprise Spells do help your spellcasting power.
Utility:         4+Int and 15 non-knowledge skills is pretty good. The rest of the class is pretty geared towards combat, but maybe you could use Invisible Thief for actual sneaking, but you’ll probably just cast Invisibility. 
Novelty:         I love this one because you don’t see this playstyle too often. A Wizard/Rogue is a tough trick to pull off because you’re so squishy but it feels great when it works.
Progression:         Spellcasting, Sneak Attack
Progressing both spells and Sneak attacks at a normal rate is top tier.
Ranged Legerdemain lets you pick locks from a distance, which is fine.
Impromptu Sneak Attack and Invisible Thief are both great ways to activate your sneak attacks, the 1/day limit until 7th level is rough, and isn’t really effective as a damage source until you get Surprise Spells.
Tricky Spells enables sneaky spellcasting with Silent and Still feats, but only 3/day.
Invisible Thief is Greater Invisibility for 9 or 10 rounds/day which is amazing! Pity you only get this at 9th level.
Surprise Spells lets you add sneak attack damage to spells against flat-footed foes. Its a shame that this doesn’t come earlier, as this would be a great way to play the whole class. A strict DM might say that it is only flat-footed, and not any condition that denies the target’s Dex bonus to AC like a normal sneak attack, so your mileage may vary.
Final Thoughts: This is a rare build … for good reasons. You’re a glass cannon that has to get close to enemies. Your best abilities kick in at 7+ levels in, but full spellcasting progression will see you through.




Assassin: Killer Rogues
Tags:        Sneak Attack, Alignment (Evil)
Base Class:        With such strong Sneak Attack progression, Rogues, Ninjas, Slayers, and other sneak attack classes are perfect. Slayers also use Int for a few Slayer Talents, which would synergize well with the Int DC of your death attack. 
Requirements:        5 ranks in Stealth and 2 in Disguise, Alignment (Any Evil), and kill someone just because.
Martial Power:        You get some proficiencies, but you probably already have these. Average Hit Dice and ¾ BAB are pretty average for a class with these abilities.
Spell Power:        N/A
Utility:         4+Int and a dozen skills. Good list but way less skill ranks than a rogue.
Novelty:         The playstyle of sneaking ahead to prepare your death attack while the rest of the party goes in front to listen to the BBEG monologue, then you 1 shot him mid-sentence is great. If your DM is ok with this happening then it’s a barrel of laughs, if not, you’ll be left feeling mostly useless.
Progression:         Sneak Attack progression is good; continuing to progress uncanny dodge with rogue levels is nice.
Death Attack: This death attack requires you to study your target for 3 rounds, make a successful attack, then the target must make a Fort save, DC 10 + Assassin Level + Int mod. Why Int mod? Both this and Arrow of Death from Arcane Archer have the unfortunate design problem of making their death attack scale with mental ability scores you’re not likely to have. That makes the Death Attack a gamble I don’t associate with deadly planning assassinations.
True Death, Quiet Death, Swift Death, and Angel of Death are all good abilities that synergize with the Death Attack, and adds to the feeling of being an assassin.
Hide in Plain Sight lets you enter sneak without breaking line of sight. It’s a great ability, even if it’s not unique to the Assassin.
Poison Use, Save Bonus Against Poison, Hidden Weapons, and the Uncanny Dodge abilities are nice, but not exciting.
Final Thoughts: The Assassin is good, but only really works in an urban setting like you’d find in a Mystery or Intrigue campaign. Assassinating one demon on the battlefield of Wrath of the Righteous might just not work.


Chronicler: Storyteller of Legends
Tags:        Martial, Bardic Knowledge, Bardic Performance
Base Class:        This PrC has Bard abilities and themes, but theoretically anyone can qualify.
Requirements:        5 ranks in 3 skills, and you must have authored something (not magic) for which someone has paid 50gp or more. 
Martial Power:        d8 HD, ¾ BAB, and no proficiencies. 
Spell Power:        0/10. If you are a Bard, either trade out your spells with an archetype or be prepared to stick with 2nd level spells for the majority of your career.
Utility:         8+Int skills, over a dozen class skills, and all Knowledge skills are outstanding. Many abilities in this PrC are for out of combat situations. Great stuff.
Novelty:         You sing tales of old and call barbarians heroes of old from their resting place in Valhalla to fight your enemies. Very cool.
Progression:        Bardic Knowledge, Bardic Performance
Bards will have progression on their abilities. Bardic Knowledge has full scaling, but Bardic Performance has two levels of no progression before it starts stacking, which is a shame since other abilities in this PrC rely on spending rounds of Bardic Performance.
Deep Pockets is basically a portable shop with 100gp of stock per PrC level. You need to spend money to refill it, but the versatility of buying a potion or scroll in the field is great.
Epic Tales: You can convey Bardic Performance through the written word. While technically you could distribute buffs across the team, who has time to read during combat? 
Greater Epic Tales: Someone else can read your words aloud, thus allowing you both to provide bonuses of Bardic Performance. Much better.
Whispering Campaign: A multi-effect power. Good if using the Enthrall effect to affect a crowd, but the Doom ability is pretty weak except to show you can shake someone with words. 
Inspire Action lets your allies take bonus actions. Move action at 6th and Standard at 9th. This is a fantastic enabling tool for your damage dealers.
Call Down the Legends and Lay of the Exalted Dead both work like a Horn of Valhalla; and since they are different abilities they can be used together.
Final Thoughts: This class has some good abilities, but the great stuff doesn’t kick in until later levels. By the time you get your best stuff, the regular Bards are unlocking Bardic Masterpieces. It does work very well as team support, but isn’t strong on its own.


Dragon Disciple: Dragon Sorcerer/Fighter
Tags:        Martial, Spellcaster, Arcane, Multiclass, Bloodline (Draconic)
* Base Class:        A martial focused PrC that uses spells as support. Bard and Sorcerer used to be the best options, but now Bloodrager is the perfect option for this PrC. Bloodrager 1/Sorcerer 4 has been recommended to me for this build.
Requirements:        5 ranks in K. Arcana, Spontaneous Spellcasting with 1st level arcane spells. If you are a Sorcerer or Bloodrager, must have the Dragon bloodline. 
Martial Power:        d12, ¾ BAB, but no good proficiencies. Overall, that's a pretty good base, but this class offers a LOT of support for your martial prowess.
Spell Power:        7/10. Not great, but this is a more Martial class with spells for support. 
You could spend 2-4 feats on Prestigious Spellcaster, but that's a commitment. 
Utility:        2+Int, some unsatisfying skills, and wings at 9th level are the most you get.
Novelty:        Great flavor for a class. Channeling the power of dragons is fun..
Progression:         Great for bloodlines like Sorcerers and Bloodragers. Bad for everyone else.
Ability Boosts and Natural Armor bonuses are great, especially since they are untyped and therefore stack with other enhancements. Little weird that you get Int at level 8.
Blindsense and Bloodline feats are good support for a martial class.
Breath Weapons, Dragon Form, and Wings are all strong dragon abilities that create the feeling of taking the dragon powers for your own.
Final Thoughts: This is a good PrC for a martial character who wants to use a few spells for self-buffs. However, to qualify you really have to plan to take it from the beginning. The martial abilities synergize well with the Bloodrager and smoothly transitions into the PrC.


Duelist: Worse Swashbuckler
Tags:        Martial
Base Class:        Any Weapon Finesse class (Unchained Rogue, Swashbuckler) can work.
Requirements:         BAB+6, 2 ranks in two skills, and three feats. Not bad, but requires planning.
Martial Power:        d10, Full BAB, Martial weapons, and a suite of combat abilities is good.
Spell Power:         N/A
Utility:         4+Int and a handful of skills isn’t great. But the class is also purely for combat.
Novelty:         The Duelist is all in the name. You duel people. But you’re bad at everything else. Just play a Swashbuckler.
Progression:        None.
Final Thoughts: This PrC was released in 2009 and was unique at the time. In 2014, Paizo released the Swashbuckler, that does the same concept, but better. Although all the abilities are fine, they just aren’t strong or special enough to be worth taking a PrC for.


Eldritch Knight: Old Magus
Tags:        Martial, Spellcaster, Arcane
Base Class:        Technically, only the Magus, Skalds and Bloodrager will qualify for this PrC without multiclassing. 9th Level Casters like Wizards or Sorcerers will need to multiclass for the required weapon proficiencies.
Requirements:        Able to cast 3rd level arcane spells, and proficiency with all martial weapons. 
Martial Power:        Full BAB with 9/10 spellcasting and d10 Hit Dice is rare. You need Martial weapons proficiency and you probably won’t be using heavy armor due to the high Spell Failure chance, so the lack of proficiencies doesn’t hurt. Bonus Combat Feats definitely for a more martial build.
Spell Power:        9/10 spell progression can be brought to 10/10 with Prestigious Spellcaster. Spell Critical is your only new feature for spellcasting, and it’s your capstone.
Utility:        Bad skills, but good Int means you’ll have enough ranks to get by. Spells make up your utility cause this class offers nothing there.
Novelty:        Strong stats makes this an optimal class choice, but it’s not very unique.
Progression:         Other than spells there’s no mechanics that get boosted. Your fighter levels stack for qualifying for feats, but you probably only took a dip in fighter at most.
Bonus Feats and Diverse Training: You get combat feats and your levels stack with Fighter and your spellcasting class’s levels for feat prereqs. This helps your lacking martial abilities, but it's pretty weak overall.
Spell Critical: When you crit, you can cast a spell as a Swift Action. The action requirement means it's only one per turn, but it's still great if you can get all the way to level 16…
Final Thoughts: The main difference between Magus and Eldritch Knight is that the magus fills the gish role from level 1 while the EK takes a long time to "blossom". In return, the EK can cast level 9 spells in the end while the magus is always capped at level 6 spells.With 9/10 spell progression, you will still lag behind a bit, but not too much.


Loremaster: Diviner of Ancient Secrets
Tags:        Spellcaster, Arcane, Divine, Bardic Knowledge, Divination
Base Class:        Bard or Wizard will have good synergy with the knowledge stacking.
Requirements: 7 skill ranks in any two Knowledge skills, 4 feats, and 7 different divination spells. So basically be level 7 and devote all your feats to this. Yikes.
Martial Power:        d6, ½ BAB, and no weapon and armor proficiencies.
* Spell Power:        10/10. Spells every level and a few small things to help your spellcasting.
Utility:        4+Int, good skill selection, and bonuses to skill checks. 
Novelty:        This class has a strong concept, but the abilities don’t do it justice. You get bonuses to skills, languages, and some flat bonuses, but no interesting powers. 
Progression:        Bardic Knowledge
Secrets: Every odd level, you can pick one of 10 options. You’ll get 5 overall, but they are all static bonuses that aren’t very exciting.
Lore: A bonus on Knowledge skill checks that stacks with Bardic Knowledge. Boring.
Bonus Languages, Greater Lore, and True Lore are fine, but are just versions of things that already exist.
Special Feat: Secret of Magical Discipline is a special feat just for the Loremaster that once per day lets you use ANY SPELL from ANY SPELL LIST by spending a spell of the same level. 
Final Thoughts: This class has some mechanically good bonuses, but it lacks anything inherently unique or interesting. Well, it looks like Paizo took this personally, and made a special feat for the class that makes them one of the best Deus Ex Machinas ever. Wizards don’t lose very much by taking this, and it might be worth a dip just for the Secret of Magical Discipline feat.


Mystic Theurge: Master of Arcane & Divine Magic
Tags:        Spellcaster, Arcane, Divine, Multiclass
Base Class:        9th Level Casters work best, and you must be both an Arcane caster (Wizard, Sorcerer, Witch, etc.) and Divine caster (Cleric, Oracle, Druid, etc.). Wizards are ideal for multiclassing on the arcane side.
The Mystic Theurge is so popular it has its own guide on rpgbot: (https://rpgbot.net/pathfinder/characters/prestigeclasses/mystic_theurge/)
Requirement:         3 ranks in two skills. The ability to cast both 2nd level Arcane and Divine spells. Needing both 2nd level Arcane and Divine spells requires multiclassing. You’ll need to be at least level 6, even if you go Wizard 3/Cleric 3.
Martial Power:        d8, ½ BAB, and no weapon and armor proficiencies. Low BAB hurts, but that's not why we’re here.
Spell Power:        Double spell progression. You get new spells per day in both your arcane and divine classes. You’ll only get 9th level spells at level 20, but in the meantime you’ll have double the number of the lower level spells.
Utility:        2+Int, limited skills together with no special abilities is a little flat. However, having double the number of low level spells means you have crazy amounts of utility, but bad skills nonetheless. 
Novelty:        Being the Big Kahuna of both Arcane and Divine spellcasting is pretty fun. You don’t get anything unique for the class but having double the spells is great on its own. 
Progression:         Shame there is no class progression for the Clerics domain powers, but the trade-off are worth it.
Combined Spells: You can prepare different spell lists in each other’s slots, but they cost level higher. This is most useful if you are a combo Spontaneous/Prepared Spellcaster. A spontaneous caster can only do this with spells that have been prepared on the prepared spell list. For example, a Cleric/Sorcerer/Mystic Theurge who prepared Bless as a 1st level divine spell, but needs it again, could spontaneously cast Bless with a 2nd level Arcane spell slot.
Spell Synthesis: Once per day, you can cast two spells in one go, one from each spell list, and each is slightly more powerful. A good capstone. 
Final Thoughts: Dual spell progression is inherently powerful, as you have so many more spells to throw around. Unfortunately, you’ll be behind in Caster Level and DCs, so while your utility is sky-high, you’ll be behind your peers. 


Shadowdancer: Magical Shadow Rogues
Tags:        Martial, Rogue Talent, Uncanny Dodge
Base Class:        Fighters, Swashbucklers, Slayers, Vigilante, and other lightly armored Martial classes will benefit from the Shadowdancer’s rogue elements without having to commit to a sneak attack build. While Rogues are a solid option, no Sneak Attack progression and overlapping features means that Evasion and Improved Uncanny Dodge will be wasted. Charisma based saves means that Swashbucklers are actually a good choice.
Requirements: 5 ranks in stealth, 2 ranks in Perform (Dance), and 3 feats. Achievable at level 5 with some planning. 
Martial Power:        d8 and ¾ BAB with a scattering of proficiencies puts you on par with the average Rogue. The abilities of this PrC help you be more effective in combat with some creativity, but are not big number machines.
Spell Power:        These spell-like-abilities are helpful, but you’re no wizard.
Utility:        6 skill ranks less than a rogue, but the skill selection is adequate (why no Disable Device?). More importantly, the abilities you get in this class are top notch.
Novelty:        I personally love the flavor of this class, but it comes down to your taste in shadow powers. 
Progression:         There’s some overlap with Rogue, and if you have Advanced Rogue Talents you’ll get to pick them, but nothing says if you switch back to Rogue later then your levels will stack for the purposes of what Rogue Talents you can select. For other classes, this is basically a dip in Rogue with extra powers.
Hide in Plain Sight: Just like the Assassin, you don’t need to break line of sight before going into stealth. This version is one of the most contentious powers for me as a DM. My players love using it but I have to keep reminding them they’re not in shadow. Then they remind me that this just requires you to be “within 10 feet of dim light” and I facepalm.
Rogue Talents are good on their own, but with the Player Companion book “Blood of Shadows” there are talents designed for the Shadowdancer such as Cloying Shades and Gloom Magic. Highly recommended. 
Shadow Illusions: A Silent Image spell-like-ability at this level is pretty weak.
Summon Shadow: You create an awesome shadow companion, 
Shadow Call: This works like Shadow Conjuration, which is very versatile and eventually upgrades to Greater Shadow Conjuration. 
Shadow Jump: You can teleport between shadows like Dimension Door, but as a supernatural ability this power is a standard action. This means no teleport full attacks, but it's still useful.
Shadow Power: This works like Shadow Evocation, which is pretty flexible and can be a decent nuke, but it uses your PrC level as Caster level, the Wizard got it 6 levels ago, and is otherwise pretty uninteresting.
Shadow Master: Gain DR 10/-, +2 Luck on all saving throws in dim light, and when you crit an enemy who is in dim light they are blinded. As a capstone this is very strong and thematic.
Final Thoughts: I think this is a fun, semi-powerful PrC with some unique abilities. While by RAW it doesn’t stack with Rogue abilities, I’ve seen some DM’s do what later PrCs do, which is to upgrade overlapping features like Evasion to Improved Evasion if you already have it.


Advanced Prestige Classes


Battle Herald: Battlefield Commander
Tags:        Martial, Multiclass
Base Class:        This class is probably the best for a battlefield commander in a war campaign, but pretty out of place in most other campaigns.
Being a battlefield commander is good for Cavaliers leading an army or Bards who want to Bard harder. The requirements mean you may need a level dip in Cavalier and Bard each, but with archetypes your options open up. 
Requirements: BAB +4, 5 ranks in three skills, 2 ranks in Profession (Soldier), and you need both the Challenge (Cavalier) and Inspire Courage (Bard) class features. Luckily, you can find these in other classes' archetypes. 
Inspire courage turns up in evangelist cleric (archetype not PrC), sensei monk, exemplar brawler, storyteller medium and ocean's echo oracle, and possibly martyr paladin or seasoned commander fighter. 
Challenge appears to be Cavalier only, but given that the Samurai is an alternate version of the Cavalier, the alternatively named abilities should be applicable.
Martial Power:        d10 and Full BAB make this good for characters who wanna hit things. The lack of new weapons and armor proficiency don’t really hurt you since you should have already have Cavalier proficiencies.
Utility:        4+Int and over a dozen class skills are good, but you won’t have enough skill points to put in all your new skills. While some of the Inspiring Commands can provide some benefits outside of combat, this is clearly focused on the battlefield.
Novelty:        This PrC is very similar to what Cavalier and Bard already do, but if you’re in large-scale battles then is the MVP.
Progression:         The Battle Heralds levels stack with Bard levels for Inspire Courage, with Cavalier levels for Tactician and Banner abilities.  While the multiclass requirement is rough, the multiple progressions are a wonderful surprise.
Inspiring Command: This is the key feature of the Battle Herald. It has its own pool of uses that gets bigger every level and you can spend rounds of Bardic Performance instead. It has 15 different uses, some of which are outstanding, a few that are generally useful, and the rest are situational. It also has some nice synergy with the Leadership feat, which is cool.
Final Thoughts: The Battle Herald provides some of the best abilities you would have gotten from your base classes, meaning you don’t miss out by taking the PrC. Inspire Greatness is a 9th level Bard ability and Demanding Challenge is a 12th level Cavalier ability, and you get these at about the same level. Overall, you don’t miss out on the best stuff with the PrC and you get some cool other abilities. Worth taking.


Holy Vindicator: Blood for the Gods
Tags:        Spellcaster, Divine, Melee
Base Class:        Clerics, Warpriest, and technically Paladins or a Shaman with the Life Spirit.
Requirements: BAB +5, Channel energy and a Channel feat, and the ability to cast 1st level divine spells. A regular Paladin will have most of this at level 5, but a Cleric or Warpriest will have to wait until 7th level to have BAB +5. 
Martial Power:        d10, Full BAB, plus proficiency in Martial weapons and Heavy armor. Top class.
Spell Power:        7/10 progression, kind of a bummer. Most of the Holy Vindicator’s abilities spend 1st, 2nd, or 3rd level spells to activate, so you want spells but you don’t actually need the high level spells.
Utility:        2+Int and a handful of class skills. Like the name suggests, this is a very fighting focused PrC. Not much to do outside of battle. Maybe you can intimidate people by bleeding on them with your Stigmata. 
Novelty:        This makes a pretty fun not-paladin, but other than Stigmata there isn’t much new here.
Progression:         Channel Energy
Levels stack with levels in any class for Channel Energy progression, which is great because this class relies heavily on it. This means you get both more dice and more uses per day. 
Vindicator’s Shield: Enhances a shield with a bonus equal to the number of dice from your channel energy… unfortunately… it only lasts until the first time you get hit… by anything in combat. If you take advantage of the heavy armor and then get hit by a touch attack or an unlucky crit then it's gone for the whole day.
Stigmata: Deal half your Holy Vindicator level in bleed damage every round to yourself to give an equal bonus to either: Attack Rolls, Damage, CL, AC, or Saving Throws. Faith Healing can help mitigate the constant bleed damage, but the bigger problem is spending a Standard action at 2nd or a Move action at 6th to activate. Changing requires deactivating and reactivating. The bonuses just aren’t high enough to spend an action on, and you don’t want to activate before combat because of the bleed damage. Any of the bonus effects can be done by casting a normal spell. Overall, doesn’t get good until it's a swift action at 10th level.
Faith healing: Empower (and later maximizes) your cure spells on yourself. Doesn’t work for Lay on Hands, or Channel Energy, and you’ll need this to deal with the Stigmata damage.
Versatile Channel: Shape your channel energy into a cone or line.
Divine Judgment: When you kill something, you can spend a 2nd level spell to use Death Knell. Its pretty ok.
Bloodrain: When you channel energy to harm, it deals extra damage, sickens, and bleeds. 
Channel Smite/Bloodfire: When you use Channel Smite, it deasl an extra 1d6 damage, sickens, and deals 1d6 bleed. A slight boost over Channel Smite, but still not great as there are so many other uses for your channel energy.
Divine wrath/Divine Retribution: When you crit and enemy Wrath lets you spend a 1st level spell to give the enemy -2 on rolls. At 10th level, Retribution lets you spend a 3rd level spell to invoke bestow curse. If you are critting something, it’s probably about to die, so many times the debuff will be wasted. Maybe it makes sense for a Cleric, but not for Paladin.
Final Thoughts: This PrC gives a lot of powers, but none of them particularly stand out unless you really build for them. The PrC seems balanced for Paladins or Cleric, but the class doesn’t give enough new things to justify what you’re giving up.


Horizon Walker: Magic for Martials
Tags:        Martial
Base Class:         Rangers get some nice synergy, but no ranger spells. Take an archetype that gives up spells for something more useful in the long run. 
This PrC works well for any melee fighter that wants some cool abilities.
The effectiveness of this PrC depends on the Terrain. Read the Player’s Guide for your campaign.
Requirements: 6 ranks in K. Geography and the Endurance feat. Anyone can qualify.
Martial Power:        d10, Full BAB no new proficiencies.
Spell Power:        N/A. There are some spell-like-abilities, but they are mainly utility focused and not damage focused.
Utility:        6+Int and a good skill selection. You receive bonuses from Favored Terrain, these bonuses stack with a Ranger’s Favored Terrain, and you can give some +2 skill buffs to your whole party. The ability to share these bonuses with your allies is the real shining star of this PrC.
Novelty:        This is a cool variant on the Ranger terrains, but with new, extra fun bonuses for your chosen terrain that can be used anywhere. This can make you Superman in your element and still a magical fighter everywhere else.
Progression:         Ranger abilities work with all of the Terrain powers of this PrC, so if you go Ranger you’ll get a lot of mileage out of this PrC.
Favored Terrain works as the Ranger ability. You get +2 on initiative checks, Knowledge (geography), Perception, Stealth, and Survival skill checks while in this terrain. If I am reading this correctly, every time you get a new Favored Terrain, the bonus is +2 and you get another +2 to increase the bonus of any Favored Terrain. You get 7 Favored Terrains in this PrC plus whatever you had from Ranger, so you could easily have around +12-16 bonuses! 
Note: these large bonuses disappear outside your specific terrain, but if you’re in a location-based campaign, this is awesome.
Terrain Mastery: pick a Favored Terrain and you get two abilities. First, while in your terrain, you can give a +2 on Climb, Stealth, Perception, and survival to all allies within 30ft for a number of rounds equal to your Wisdom (min 1). This only lasts a few rounds, but if you have +2 Wisdom, just spam it every other round for a permanent party bonus.
Second, you get an ability of varying quality that applies everywhere. These are important, because you need to have Terrain Mastery to take:
Terrain Dominance: The real MVP of this PrC and worth a 3 level dip. You must select a terrain that you have Terrain Mastery, and you get the following:
First, you apply your Favored Terrain bonus (which, i must remind you, can be stacked absurdly high) on Bluff, Knowledge, Perception, Sense Motive, and Survival checks as well as Weapon Attack and Damage rolls against creatures native to the terrain. 
Second, you get a really nice ability based on your terrain which works everywhere. Some standouts include: 
* Astral: Dimension Door 3+Wis/day
* Mountain: DR 2/Adamantine
* Plains: +10 movement speed
* Plane of Air: Fly as a SLA 3+Wis/day
* Water: Swim speed 20ft
Master of All Lands: Your Favored Terrain bonus for all terrains goes up by +2, all other terrains are treated like Favored Terrains (+2), you automatically succeed on checks from naturally occurring weather, and all allies get +2 or +4 on such checks.



Master Chymist: Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde
Tags:        Alchemist, Spellcaster, Martial
Base Class:        This is definitely meant for the Alchemist and their archetypes, as the extracts requirement disqualifies other class’s Alchemist-like archetypes such as the Mutation Warrior Fighter.
Requirements: Ability to create 3rd level extracts, the Mutagen class feature, and either the feral mutagen or infuse mutagen discoveries. 
Martial Power:        d10 and Full BAB, and no proficiencies are good. The lack of new weapons and armor sucks but Alchemists get pretty good proficiencies to start.
Spell Power:        7/10 is kind of expected with the amount of power this PrC gives, but you can still take Prestigious Spellcaster to boost your spellcasting. Continued Bomb damage progression still makes you a significant source of ranged damage. 
Utility:        2+Int and few skills are bad, but as an Alchemist you probably have high Int. As a mutagen/combat focused class, all your utility comes from your Alchemist levels rather than this PrC.
Novelty:        Having 2 personalities, each with their own alignment is a fun character idea. I actually wish they took this further, with your alter-ego having bonus skills like the eidolon Skilled Evolution (+8), which would force you to call out your alter-ego when you need their skills. The transformation can be forced by damage or stress (being hit by a crit or failing a Fort save), so other players can technically force your extra personality out by spamming the Flare cantrip, which is hilarious. Giving extra abilities to your mutagen is also a pretty fun build.
Progression:         Your Master Chymist levels stack with Alchemist levels for the purposes of Bomb damage and Mutagen duration, and some of your mutagens count as Alchemist Discoveries of the same name, which means you can take improvements to them as Discoveries. 
Unfortunately, your PrC levels don’t stack for the purposes of prerequisite like Alchemist 12 for Splitting Mutagen. You can see that this was probably their intent, because every Advanced Mutagen that is also a Discovery is available at the same combined level as the normal Alchemist, but they didn’t future-proof it. Paizo has released some amazing support since 2010 when this PrC was released that is technically unavailable to the Master Chymist, but maybe talk to your DM about Rules as Intended. 
Bomb-thrower and Extracts Per Day are your progression tools. 
Mutagenic Form, Mutate, and Advanced Mutagen are your key features and making your Mr. Hyde the best damn monster he can be.
Brutality: Encourages you to go into melee while in your Mutagenic Form, with a small +2 damage (later +4 and +6) with simple weapons and natural attacks. Its okay, but not rocking the boat.
Final Thoughts: Overall, this PrC shifts the Alchemist from a mid-line spellcaster with some mutagens to a front-line fighter that can stand side-by-side with your Melee allies. 


Master Spy: Intrigue before “Ultimate Intrigue”
Tags:        Sneak Attack, Martial, Intrigue
Base Class:        Bards, Rogues, Ninja, or Vigilante all work well.
Requirements:        7 Ranks in two skills, 5 ranks in two skills, and 2 feats. Easy to get by level 7.
Martial Power:        d8, ¾ BAB, and no new proficiencies.
Spell Power:        N/A
Utility:        6+Int and a dozen skills are good, but the real fun is the special abilities. All your abilities go toward making you the best spy out there. 
Novelty:        Abilities like Quick Change were more fun before the Vigilante class came around. But this is still a lot of good abilities that other classes don’t normally have.
Progression:         Sneak attack stacks with any other class that has it. Good synergy, but slow.
Art of Deception, Master of Disguise, Glib Lie, Mask Alignment, Concealed Thoughts, Quick Change, Elude Detection, Slippery Mind, and Hidden Mind are basically all passive buffs to make you a better sneak and preempt many methods to discover spies. They are all useful, but active powers are more exciting.
Sneak Attack stacking is always good.
Nonmagical Aura is a nice ability to sneak magic items to where they aren’t supposed to be. 
Superficial Knowledge synergizes with Master of Disguise and a disguise playstyle in general.
Shift Alignment gives you a choice of what alignment you want to be. Its not as good as effects that lets you count as the “most beneficial” but you can really sell your disguise. 
Death Attack: 8th level is a little late, but if you already have a Death Attack (Assassin PrC gets it from level 1), your levels stack before you hit level 8. Fantastic.
Fool Casting lets you trick spellcasters into thinking they’ve succeeded in dominating your mind. Very cool and potentially critical.
Assumption lets you assume another persona entirely. As a full-round-action you can touch a helpless creature and all divination effects (except for the gods) will detect you as that person. Combine this with your disguises and you can infiltrate anything.
Final Thoughts: This class was better before the Vigilante and “Ultimate Intrigue” existed. As it stands, all your best abilities come very late. Just play a Vigilante.


Nature Warden: Overshadowed by “Ultimate Wilderness”
Tags:        Spellcaster, Divine, Multiclass
Base Class:        Single-class Rangers can take this PrC at level 8, but the spellcasting nature of the class and overlapping Ranger features will mean most who take this will be Ranger/Druids, Ranger/Clerics with the Animal Domain, or Archetypes who “technically” qualify such as Ranger/Oracles with a Bonded Mount.
Requirements: d8, BAB+4, 5 ranks in two skills, and the ability to cast 2nd level Divine spells is normal. However, you also need Animal Companion, Favored Terrain, and Wild Empathy class features, which come from two different classes. 
Martial Power:        d8, ¾ BAB, and no proficiencies. However, your animal companion gets some nice power boosts. 
Spell Power:        7/10 spell progression so consider whether Prestigious Spellcaster is worth it. The abilities of this PrC don’t help your spellcasting, and you’re likely already behind the ball with a dip in Ranger.
Utility:        4+Int and a few skills aren’t great. For a class like this, non-combat abilities are expected, but they are all for the abilities I normally trade away with archetypes. In a certain type of game, I could see all of these powers being useful, but not for an adventuring campaign.
Novelty:        It borrows so heavily from other Nature classes that it doesn’t feel unique. Most of the abilities are improvements or combinations of existing abilities.
Progression:         Animal Companion, Wild Empathy, Favored Terrain 
All these features stack with other classes that grant them. Good multiclass skills.
Companion bond: Progress your Animal Companion levels and give them the benefits of Favored Terrain, which isn’t much until much later.
Mystic Harmony: Gain an Insight bonus to AC equal to half your favored terrain bonus… given that you aren’t a Horizon Walker, this is a small bonus.
Wild Stride: Move through natural hazards. Good, but situational.
Animal speech and Plant Speech: Speak with their respective subjects while in your terrain, or 1/day outside. Not bad, but pretty lackluster.
Silverclaw and Ironpaw: Grant DR equal to your Warden level to your Animal Companion and Nature’s Ally summons, and their natural weapons are considered those metals for overcoming DR. At most, the DR maxes out at DR10, and it takes 1 hour to switch your animal companion’s DR. Good, but not great.
Survivalist and Woodforging: You can make items, with the caveat that some of their effects only work for you. It isn’t clear if Woodforging lets you enchant items as though you had the Craft Arms & Armor feat, but if it does, it is Green.
Companion Walk: Bring your animal companion along when using travel or polymorph spells. But the suggested spells are so high level you probably won’t take them with your slow spellcasting progression, don’t go far, and Transport Via Plants already lets you take passengers. Why is this a 7th level feature?
Guarded lands: Spend 24 hours studying an area to get lackluster bonuses. 
Companion Soul: Give your Animal Companion +8 Will vs Enchantment, you can scry your Animal Companion once per day, and see their location like a scrying focus, and you can revive your Companion for free with an 8 hour ritual. Honestly, this should have been your first level ability, not a capstone.
Final Thoughts: Who is this for? Rangers have better HD, BAB, and skills. Druids lose out on casting and Wild Shape (arguably their best features) to focus on their animal companion. This class is all about connecting with nature and empowering your animal companion. However, Ultimate Wilderness introduced a host of Animal Companion Archetypes and other features that do similar things without drawbacks. With what you’re giving up for this PrC, these lackluster features aren’t worth it.


Rage Prophet: Barbarian/Oracles
Tags:         Divine, Martial, Spellcaster, Multiclass
Base Class:        Oracle/Barbarian seems the main way to get the required class features.
Requirements:        BAB+5, Oracle’s Curse, Moment of Clarity Rage Power, 5 ranks in K. Religion, and ability to cast 1st level Divine Spells. A Barbarian 5/Oracle 1 would work, but its annoying that you need BAB +5 and Oracle, which is ¾ BAB. Makes it practically impossible to take at 5th level.
Martial Power:        d10, ¾ BAB, and no proficiencies. All the powers go into making you a better spell fighter.
Spell Power:         7/10 spell progression. Clarity of Mind isn’t great, as it suppresses all the benefits of rage until the next round. Abilities like Raging Healer and Raging Spellstrength are way better than the 4th level power of Ragecaster.
Utility:         4+Int and eight skills are fine. Just like you would expect for a Barbarian archetype, all the abilities of this PrC are combat focused. 
Novelty:         Bloodrager and even Skald do a lot of what this class does. The Spirit Guide is fun and the divine spell list is an interesting variant, but overall it is probably worse.
Progression:        Rage Powers, Oracle Revelations, Oracle’s Curse
7/10 spell progression is meh, but the Rage Prophet’s levels stack with Barbarian and Oracle levels for Rage Powers, Oracle Revelations, and Oracle’s Curse, but it does not grant additional abilities. So, you won’t get new Revelations and Rage Powers, but what you do have will continue to scale/progress. Even better, you won’t get saddled with a useless curse.
Ragecaster: At 4th level, you add your Barbarian level to caster level “while using moment of clarity” which means it doesn’t apply to healing spells from Raging Healer or Raging Spellstrength.  At 7th level, he adds his Constitution bonus to the save DCs of spells cast while raging. This would have been Blue if it was always on, but otherwise it's situational.
Spirit Guide gives you a little ghost who casts Guidance (+1 on a roll) on you once per Rage. You can also use some cantrips once per day as SLAs… Don’t you already have Orisons? Why not just make these Orisons?
Spirit Guardian increases the Guidance bonus to half your level, but only when fighting Fey, Outsider, Undead, or Incorporeal enemies. As a swift action, you can spend 1 round of rage to get ghost touch for 1 round. Again, why is this bonus so limited? The Ghost Touch is nice, but it would have been better if you kept scaling on how many round of Rage/day you receive, or got more abilities.
Raging Spellstrength: Removes the requirement to use Clarity of Mind when casting spells with a range of “personal.” Why didn’t this come before 8th level?
Spirit Warrior: Increases the Guidance bonus to your Rage Prophet level, but again only while fighting certain enemies, and your weapons and armor can get the Ghost Touch property as an Immediate action. 
Greater Rage: Increases the Strength and Constitution bonuses of your rage. Unqualified good, and presumably can be converted for Unchained Barbarian.
Final Thoughts: A good class with a lot of flavor, but also a lot of extraneous abilities. The fact that you don’t get more rounds of Rage as you level, but your abilities rely on Rage really kneecaps a lot of the fun.


Stalwart Defender: “Hold the Line”
Tags:        Martial
Base Class:         Fighter works best because by 7th level you’ll have your Weapon and Armor training. Cavalier or Paladin could work, but have to give up some abilities. 
Barbarians can’t use defensive stances with Rage, so maybe not them.
Requirements: BAB +7, 3 feats, and proficiency with Light & Medium Armor. Martial classes will qualify easily, but ¾ BAB classes like rogues or a magus won’t qualify until 10.
Martial Power:        d12, Full BAB, AC bonuses, proficiency in martial weapons and all armor (including tower shields) makes this a great dip on its own.
Spell Power:        N/A
Utility:        This is pure combat, no utility here.
Novelty:        While there isn’t much here for out of combat situations, the pure, unadulterated focus on combat is pretty fun on its own. Defensive powers are pretty fun.
Progression:         Your uncanny dodge stacks with other sources, so I guess you armored Barbarians and heavy armor Rogues can rejoice.
Defensive Stance: Kinda works like Rage. You get 4+Con per day (gaining an extra 2 rounds per level) and while in the stance you get +2 Dodge to AC, +4 to Strength and Constitution, and +2 Will! And, it's a free action to activate so you don’t waste your first round of combat.
However, you have to stay in place or it ends and you are fatigued (-2 to Str and Dex) for double the rounds you were in the stance. Vehicles don’t count as moving as long as you aren’t driving.
Defensive Powers are 13 abilities of varying quality. You will be able to pick 5 over the course of this PrC, so find your favorites. Here are some standouts:
* Clear Mind: reroll bad Will saves.
* Halting Blow: stop enemies you hit with an Attack of Opportunity.
* Unexpected Strike: Hit an opponent who moves into a threatened square, even if that movement wouldn’t normally provoke. Take a reach weapon and you can stop enemies from reaching you.
* Increased DR: oh yeah, this PrC also gets DR. Sweet.
* Mighty Resilience: negate a crit or sneak attack. Like Fortification, except it always works and it’s free.
Uncanny dodge/Improved Uncanny Dodge: You know, this actually makes more sense for a big shield man rather than a Rogue.
Damage Reduction 1,3,5/- and it stacks with DR from Armor, so invest in some Adamantine Full Plate ASAP.
Mobile Defense: At 9th level, you can take a 5-foot-step per round without ending your stance. Kinda meh, wish it came earlier.
Last Word: Once per day, when you’re about to get knocked unconscious or die, you get to make one last melee attack with double weapon damage.
Final Thoughts: Overall, the strong defensive abilities make a nice Fighter PrC. Would not recommend it for anyone with more interesting class features.

Other A-B
Other 
A-B
After Paizo released their initial books of Prestige Classes, they continued to release new PrCs in smaller books like the Player Companion and Campaign Setting books. Each of these books had a theme, with some class archetypes and PrCs that would work well in that theme or setting. As time went on, Paizo became much better at design and future-proofing their creations, these PrCs have fewer “traps” of class features and usually present novel ideas that set them apart from the regular classes.
Occasionally, there would be larger books introducing new sets of rules such as “Ultimate Intrigue” and “Ultimate Wilderness” which would also introduce multiple PrCs that work with those new rules. But because these PrCs were released as smaller parts of other books, they are organized alphabetically. I will be applying tags in the Base Class section marking what theme, setting, or mechanics would be good for the PrC. 


Agent of the Grave: How to Become Undead in 5 Steps
Base Classes:        Cleric, Wizard, Sorcerer, Evil, Oracle, Undead
An Undead Lord Cleric would be great at this.
Requirements:        Any Evil, 5 ranks K. Arcana & Religion, must be able to cast animate dead, and you must be a member of an undead-worshiping cult for 1 year. 
If you plan to take this PrC from character creation, I suggest you make your own cult as part of your backstory, because the main undead worshiping cult, The Whispering Way, are the main villains of several adventure paths and that could get awkward at the next adventurer’s tavern. Also, if you join during the campaign you’ll have to wait a year, which isn’t ideal.
Martial Power:        d8, ½ BAB, and no proficiency. Better HD than I’d expect for a caster. 
Spell Power:        4/5 spell progression means you’ll want to take Prestigious Spellcaster. The abilities of this PrC make you a strong necromancer, but at the final level you get some strong spellcasting abilities. 
Utility:        A great little suite of abilities for taking care of your undead minions.
Novelty:        At the 5th and final level, “an Agent of the Grave has prepared his body and mind for the transition into undead” … “if slain by an undead creature with the create spawn ability, the agent of death retains his Intelligence and free will.” This ability alone makes me want to play this build. Also, unless it says you keep your class abilities, you lose them, so try to get killed by a vampire for free vampire powers. 
Progression:        Nothing other than spell levels.
Inspired Necromancy gives you a bigger horde of undead.
Lich’s Touch lets you heal undead minions (and yourself with Negative Energy Affinity)
Unholy Fortitude lets you use your Cha mod for HP rather than Con.
Undead Manipulator lets you use mind-affecting spells on mindless undead. 
Secrets of Death lets you take Necromancy spells from other spell lists.
Undead Initiate lets you turn into an undead, either as a Lich (talk to your DM) or by getting killed by a Vampire.
Final Thoughts: This short PrC does what it sets out to do, help you become an intelligent undead. Check with your DM and party to see if this is compatible with your campaign.


Dawnflower Anchorite (Anchorite of Dawn): Sun Worshiping Hippies
Base Classes:        Spellcaster, Neutral Good, Sarenrae
While all spellcasting classes are eligible, your key ability, Solar Invocation, gets more rounds per day with Charisma. If you have an animal companion, they also get a boost, so a Cleric with the Animal domain would fit perfectly. Nature Oracle even better because of CHA casting.
Requirements:        Any Good, 5 ranks in two skills, and able to cast 2nd level spells. Interesting that it isn’t “divine” spells for a class that is so divinely flavored.
Martial Power:        d8, ¾ BAB. Some of the Credence abilities can help your melee power.
Spell Power:        9/10, might be worth taking Prestigious Spellcaster. There are several abilities that boost your spellcasting, and Solar Invocation provides some small bonuses.
Utility:        4+Int and a lot of skills are good for a spellcaster, but the real fun is the abilities.
Novelty:        Dawnflower Anchorites focus on the nature aspect of their sun worship, but invoking the power of the sun is pretty fun on its own. Combining this with liberal uses of “Praise the Sun!” or “Release the Power of the Sun!” to piss off your DM and some party members. <3
Progression:         Oddly, your animal companion can also get your bonuses from Solar Invocation. I guess a Druid or Ranger could take the PrC, but more likely its for Clerics of the Animal domain.
Also, you can take “Focused Class Feature” Credence to progress certain class features. Might be good for Bards, Druids, Clerics, Rangers, or Warpriests, but given that you only get 5 Credences means you could spend more than half of them on just progression. Wish it was standard, but the flexibility for other classes is neat.
Solar Invocation takes a Standard Action and it only works outside in some kind of light. Bad. It boosts Attacks, Damage, and +1 to DCs against Evil creatures. At 5th it becomes a move action and the bonus becomes +2. You can take a Credence to make it work inside, but it sucks that you must forgo fun options to fix the PrC’s flaws. 
Credences are your flexibility and character options. There are nine options, and they are mostly pretty good, but you only get 5 total, and you’ll likely have to first take ones that fix your weaknesses first.

Lion Blade: Bard/Rogue, Master of Intrigue
Base Classes:        Sneak Attack, Multiclass, Bard
A relic of 3.5, the Lion Blade was known for being a Multiclass PrC of Rogue and Bard that screws both. Luckily, it got a rework in the 2016 “Inner Sea Intrigue.”
Aon: https://aonprd.com/PrestigeClassesDisplay.aspx?ItemName=Lion%20Blade 
Article for build advice: https://kitsunemori.com/build-of-the-week-the-lion-blade/

3 feats is a big ask, especially since they are all skill feats you really don’t need with your two skill-monkey classes.
Martial Power:        d8 and ¾ BAB. All your abilities synergize really as well for a urban adventure or Intrigue focused game. 
Spell Power:         N/A
Utility:         6+Int and 15 skills is good. Several of your DCs are Int based, so you should have even more points for skills. Several of your abilities are designed for non-combat scenarios, and this PrC really helps Disguise as a skill.

* Narrow Miss - Dodge erratically as a swift action, gaining 20% concealment until your next turn. You can use this 1/day for every 2 PrC levels.
   * Swift action for a free 20% miss chance? Yes please!
   * Considering you get this at 8th level, you start with 4 uses per day. Nice.

* Yamaraj-Feather Cloak makes you Immune to Electricity and Death effects. When yoeu get hit by an electric effect, you regain 1/3rd the damage as HP, which can go above your max health as THP (max 50) for 1 hour. Take Shocking Grasp and boostboos your health before a big encounter.

Progression:        Spell progression is nice, but Channel Damage only stacks with Channel Energy for the purpose of damaging undead, not healing, which makes it pretty limited. However, it does keep up the every-other-level progression of Channel, so you don’t fall behind.
Special:
* Channel Damage - You can channel, but only to hurt undead. Pretty limited, and annoying for a healer cleric, but an Inquisitor could get a lot of use out of it, as you get a separate pool of channeling if you don’t have it already.
* Focus Items - You must use this item as a focus for your spells. Cool flavor, but it does create the risk of losing your spellcasting if you get disarmed. 
* Channel Casting - Channel Damage’s other use is getting turned in a limited selection of spells. While the spells you get at 2nd level are pretty trash, from 4th level up they’re pretty good.
* Corpse Whisperer - Add half your level on Charisma based skill checks vs undead, and on checks to assume control of an undead like Command Undead or Control Undead. While hilarious to imagine your “specially trained cadre of anti-necromantic commandos” trying to seduce the undead with high Charisma checks, this is more for Command Undead or offensive abilities.
* Bonus Feats - 3 bonus feats over the course of your career is okay, plus you still have to meet the prerequisites. This is just fine overall.
* Harvest - If you kill an undead with “channel positive energy, channel damage, or a spell with an instantaneous duration,” you regain a use of Channel Damage. While there is a limit of only 1/round and a cap of regaining up to your PrC level of uses per day, it’s still a great way to get extra use out of your abilities and really feel like an “anti-undead commando.” Plus, it also works with some spells, so you can theoretically get uses back even after you’ve run out.
* Undead Imposition - Spend a Channel Damage to take control of an undead. They get a save, but mindless undead usually have trash Will saves. Downside is that this control only lasts for rounds per level, so it’s more of a crowd control ability than a minion ability. Plus, the way I read it, you should be able to add your ½ Charisma bonus from Corpse Whisperer to the DC of this ability, which bumps this to Blue for me.
* Negative Energy Ward - Once a day, ward an area against undead for 1 minute per PrC level. It only stops undead with less HD than your caster level, but higher HD undead still have to make a save and the penalties for failing are pretty high. Cool ability.
* Necrotic Resistance - Take 1 less ability drain or negative levels, which quickly upgrades to 2 less at 10th level. No save required. By the time this kicks in, you’ll be running into those threats quite often so while this is situational I still want to give it a green.
* Life Shell - An Anti-Life Shell ability that gives fast healing 1, and has 10 minutes per day of use. Great ability. 
Final Thoughts:  This PrC synergizes with itself quite well, and the kit seems fun to play. It is great if you want to fight undead, but pretty useless in almost any other context. Only take it in a heavy undead-themed game, but if you are in a game like that then you’ll have a lot of fun.

The first version made it’s debut in the 2011 ‘Campaign Setting: Book of the Damned–Volume 3’ until in 2017 the revised omnibus “Book of the Damned” reworked it into the rules of the greater “Deific Obedience” and “Fiendish Obedience” system. 
Since these work relatively differently, I will review them separately. 


Souldrinker (PZO9239) - Four Horseman Fanboy
Base Classes:        Spellcaster, Wizard, Magus, Bloodrager
Requirements:        5 skill ranks in Knowledge (Planes) and Spellcraft, speak Abyssal or Infernal, Neutral Evil alignment, and then worship the appropriate deity (Four Horsemen).

Utility:         Using the Soul Pool feature of out of combat Item Creation and a solid choice of immunities means you have quite a few features for roleplay opportunities. I know my players would use ‘Energy Drain’ to “pacify” captured enemies.
Novelty:         The Four Horsemen are an under-explored aspect of Mythology due to their association with the End of Days, but Pathfinder works them into a regular feature of their overall cycle of souls, letting us roleplay something new. 
Progression:        8/10 spell progression is all you get. This is really meant for Wizard types who don’t get a lot of new features on the back-end of their class and want to spice it up. 
Special:

* Damned - Your soul is damned when you die. Mechanically, this just makes you harder to revive, but in-universe and RP wise it’s a big deal.
* Energy Drain (combo w/ Soul Pool) - You can drain energy from appropriately strong targets to get points for your Soul Pool to give yourself 5 Temp HP and your enemy a negative level (or two!). In theory, this means you can get close and melee-curse powerful foes, or drain captured enemies to death and use their soul energy to craft magic items for the party. In reality, you’re more likely to get your squishy wizard whalloped, so be careful.
   * By my reading, Energy Drain uses the ‘Universal Monster Rules’ meaning “Each successful energy drain bestows one or more negative levels (the creature’s description specifies how many).” If so, this ability is one of the old “letting the players have access to an OP monster ability” that I really love about the old days, but can be a headache for DMs to balance. 
* Soul Pool - Energy Drain also gives Soul Points are not only a shield, but free refills for spell slots, Staffs, Item Creation, or even to replace your Cacodaemon Familiar. Very thematic to be callous and uncaring about your soul-sucking daemon familiar. 
* Summon Cacodaemon - free summons of annoying mobs, they last for minutes instead of rounds, and as a Sp (spell-like ability) its a standard action instead of a full-round action to summon. 
* Lesser Oblivion - depends on the Horseman, but they are all useful in some way.

   * You can tell they thought the same about Death’s Immunity to aging, because they also threw in immunity to Str and Con penalties to try and balance it, except immunity to Con penalties stops a lot of nasty surprises. 
* Oblivion - New ability usable at-will, so long as you can spend 1 soul point each. 
   * Pestilence - Contagion
   * Death -  Vampiric Touch
   * War -  Rage
   * Famine - Bestow Curse
      * Standouts here are Death’s Vampiric Touch and War’s Rage, which are both useful in-combat, while a Famine’s Bestow Curse is a nice debuff, but I tend not to like Contagion since it allows for a lot of saves, so you rarely get the fullest result, but YMMV.
* Summon Ceustodaemon - A Ceustodaemon is a CR6 monster with large size, DR, and decent HP. By this time, you’ll be level Character Level 13 or higher, so these are useful walls of HP or flank-buddies, but not combat breaking. 
   * HOWEVER, the real fun is “At Will- Dimension Door” with no restriction on additional targets, and “3/day— dispel magic, fly”. That these summons last for 1 minute/level gives a lot of out of combat utility as daemonic servants. 
* Greater Oblivion - As a capstone feature, this has some strong abilities and the “at-will, but costs 3 soul points” should be pretty manageable (and even a benefit if your DM will let you use Cacodaemon Soul Gems to refill your Soul Points.
   * Pestilence - Creeping Doom is a 7th level spell that creates roving swarms of bugs that deal 4d6 damage and a higher save against the poison/distraction. Good, but not a huge upgrade over the spell. 
   * Death - Fast healing 10 for 10 rounds = 100pts of slow healing. Very useful, but you shouldn’t be getting hit. Technically, two hits of Energy Drain would refill your Soul Pool enough to cast this, which is a potentially strong synergy. 
   * War - Greater Magic Weapon, but you get the paladin-like ability to swap part of your bonus for mighty cleaving, unholy, vicious, or wounding. The text arguably locks you at +4 enhancement bonus, but I’d argue it should go up to +5 when your Character Level hits 20. 
   * Famine - Horrid Wilting is an 8th level spell, Long Range, and targets all “living creatures” in a 60ft radius with good damage. Having an extra casting of this in your back-pocket
Final Thoughts:        A great PRC for a spellcaster who wants to get into soul-craft, and it comes in 4 flavors. You’ll fall a little behind in spells, but your ability to dish out negative levels, quantity of spell slots, temp HP, and magic items should make up the difference. 
Also… I can’t get the idea of a melee build with the Horseman of War. Maybe a Magus who can stay alive in melee long enough to debuff? A Bloodrager who wants to torment the very souls of the slain? That would de-center spellcasting, but maybe War or Death would synergize well.

Stargazer: Desna’s Strongest Spellcaster
Tags:        Spellcaster, Familiar, Hex
Base Class:        Witch, Cleric, Wizard, or any Archetypes with the Hex, Familiar, Domain
Requirements:        5 Ranks Knowledge (Geography) & (Planes), and needing to cast 3rd level spells means you can qualify ASAP as a spellcaster. Must also worship Desna, and be within 1 step alignment-wise of her (Chaotic Good). 
Martial Power:        ¾ BAB is good but only d6 HD suggests you should stay in the backline with your shiny new familiar. Coat of Many Colors Stars may give a +4 Armor like Mage Armor, and yes, it scales, but don’t get fooled. You are still squishy.
Spell Power:         10/10 spell progression and a lot of fun abilities to choose from. 
Utility:         The abilities of this PrC are heavily choice dependent, but there are solid powers that are useful out of combat. Look at Hexes, Familiars, Domain Spells, and the Sidereal arcana has a lot to offer. 
Novelty:         Desna is a fun, stress-free Chaotic Good deity. Her domains are solid, including the top tier Chaos, Liberation, Luck, and Travel, and the sheer variety of classes this PrC pulls from leads to some interesting combinations. 
Progression:        10/10 Spell Progression is good, but there are a few extra goodies sprinkled there and there. Witch levels stack for Hexes, Wizard levels stack for Familiars, Cleric levels stack for the Star Domain, Cleric/Oracle levels stack for your Mage Armor equivalent and Commune spell equivalents, and if you really dig, the Mother Sidereal Arcana lets Cleric Channel Energy levels stack.
Special:        
* Guiding Light - Gain a familiar using your “stargazer level as his wizard level” and since the Familiar feats refer to your “wizard level” I’m going to assume it will stack with a preexisting familiar. So either you get a familiar or progress your current one. 
* Mystery Magic 
   * Hex - Choose a Witch Hex (which range in usefulness) at 1st and 9th level, and these levels stack! That means you should qualify for the even better Major and Grand hexes for picking purposes!
      * Added bonus, you can add some neat Hexes from the Heavens Domain Shaman. 
   * Stars subdomain - This includes the +2 insight bonus on mind-affecting saves from the Void Domain, along with the modifications of the Stars Subdomain. 
      * The wording on “The Stars Are Right” is a little weird, but having a list of spells to spontaneously cast is HUGE for a prepared spellcaster, and this has some good ones (1-feather fall, and 3-fly, being the best to have in a pinch).  Also, if you cast them when the stars are visible, you get a little healing. :)
   * Coat of Many Stars - this is like Mage Armor, in that it’s an “armor bonus” so it won’t stack with the “armor bonus” of regular armor and armor enchantments, but unlike Mage Armor, it scales with Cleric/Oracle level, and gets DR5/slashing eventually. Also saves the spell slot you would have used for Mage Armor. 
   * Star Chart - I find the usefulness of “commune” to depend heavily upon the DM, but getting to ask some probing questions once a day for free is fun. 
* Sidereal arcana - at level 2, and every 2 levels you get to pick a power from a list. You’ll only be able to pick 5 total, so choose well:
   * The Bridge: While “cold resistance 5 and darkvision with a range of 30 feet” is useful only if you don’t have it from character creation, being able to “see through darkness created by demons without penalty” is going to be highly situational depending on your campaign.
   * The Daughter: Aura that gives allies a +4 morale bonus on saving throws against fear, and the DC to demoralize them via Intimidate increases by 4. 
      * “The stargazer is immune to fear effects created by demons.” Immunity is good, but again, that’s highly situational.
   * The Follower: +4 bonus on saves against death effects & immune to all death effects, negative energy effects, and negative levels created by demons.
      * Even with immunity, that's a good list of things to be immune to. 
   * The Lantern Bearer: The stargazer’s ability to conjure light increases. The radius of any light source he creates via magic increases by 10 feet, and its spell level is considered to be 2 higher.
   * The Mother: Whenever the stargazer casts a cure spell, casts breath of life, or uses the healing hex, he adds twice his class level to the hit points restored. 
      * Cleric Bonus: “If the stargazer is a cleric with channel positive energy, his stargazer levels stack with his cleric levels to determine its effects.” Healing clerics going crazy in the chat. 
   * The Newlyweds: “The sign of intertwined lovers grants the stargazer a romantic mystique.” Whenever he uses a charm or language-dependent effect against someone who could be romantically attracted to him, the save DC increases by 2. In addition, he gains a +2 bonus on Bluff and Diplomacy checks against such individuals. Finally, the stargazer gains a +2 morale bonus on saving throws against charm effects created by demons.
      * The language “on someone who could be romantically attracted to him” should be setting off GM alarm bells. You’re opening the door to debating the sexuality of every NPC for that +2 bonus. 
   * The Pack: The stargazer gains a +2 bonus on Handle Animal checks. In addition, whenever he casts a summoning spell that conjures multiple creatures of the animal type, he summons an additional animal of that type.
      * Flat bonuses are good, but the real power is adding an extra to your Stampede Squad. Just make sure to cast the “multiple creatures” version. 
   * The Patriarch: The stargazer gains an innate sense of direction. He always knows which way north lies, and gains a +4 bonus on skill checks made to navigate or find his way. The stargazer also becomes immune to maze and similar effects.
      * Immunity is good, but bonuses to navigation can be a little situational. However, navigation is in the Adventurer’s handbook, so it’s not unreasonable to assume this will be needed someday. 
   * The Rider: The stargazer can conjure a steed that deters those who would harm him. Once per day, the stargazer can use phantom steed as a spell-like ability; this spell-like ability has the light descriptor, and only the stargazer can ride the mount. The steed is made of starlight, and sheds light as a torch. While riding his star-steed, he is under the effects of sanctuary heightened to 4th level; if he breaks this sanctuary, he can reactivate it after 1 minute. The caster level of these effects is equal to his character level.
      * Scaling with Character Level means that this power becomes much more useful, floating over land, water, and eventually air. Sanctuary is a unique bonus, especially as a defense while you prepare your buffs. 
   * The Stargazer: The constellation that shares his name warns the stargazer of danger. The stargazer gains a +2 insight bonus on initiative checks and is not considered flat-footed before he acts in combat, although this does not allow him to act if he could not otherwise do so.
      * While not flashy like some of the others here, getting the somewhat rare “insight” bonus to initiative is a solid tool in any adventure kit. 
   * The Stranger: The stargazer learns to blend seamlessly into others’ cultures. He can use cultural adaptation as a spell-like ability three times per day with a caster level equal to his character level. While under the effects of this ability, details of his body’s appearance change as well, making him physically appear as the appropriate race or ethnicity (although his statistics do not change).
      * Cultural Adaptation is a pretty low level spell and the benefits are pretty minor. On the fun side, this practically invites players to do some funny voices to “blend in” with a fantasy culture, but just be careful of making a caricature of IRL cultures. 
   * The Thrush: The stargazer’s voice becomes harmonious. The stargazer gains a bonus equal to half his class level on Diplomacy and Perform (sing) checks.
      * Again, niche. It’s a scaling 1-5 bonus on a skill check. Unless you’ve got a power or ability that key’s off of these skill checks, kinda pointless. 
   * The Wagon: The stargazer’s movement becomes swift and steady. He gains a +10-foot enhancement bonus to his movement speed, and can take 5-foot steps in difficult terrain. In addition, three times per day as a swift action, he can gain the effects of freedom of movement for 1 round (🤩), but only against effects created by demons (😣).
      * Great set of abilities, even if the demon-specific limiter is pretty annoying on that last power. 
Final Thoughts: This PrC offers a lot of features from different classes, with a good way to blend them.You gain a familiar, a witch/shaman hex, a cleric domain, and oracle revelations. All of the features also stack with the related base class features. As if that wasn't enough, you also get a selection of arcana that grant bonuses or SLAs. Hexes can be useful at any level if you pick well, and depending on what your base class was, you’ll get to progress some good abilities.

Winter Witch
Base Classes:        Witch

I highly suggest that you play this in temperate places that have snowy winters, but it feels out of place in deserts or jungles.
