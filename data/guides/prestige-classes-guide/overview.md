When to Prestige Class
All classes offer a variety of abilities that are drip-fed each level. If you rely on one of these class features, like Sneak Attack or Sorcerer Bloodlines, then taking a PrC will stop that feature from getting stronger unless the PrC says it continues to progress or the features stack. 
Wizard is an excellent class to add a prestige class to, as they rely primarily on spellcasting, and don’t get more features later on (although by RAW they don’t get new spells known). Summoners and Sorcerers, by contrast, rely heavily on their spell-like-abilities which only progress with class level, and so generally don’t mix well with prestige classes.
PrCs often require a certain number of ranks in a skill. Since the number of ranks you can put into a skill is capped by your character level, this is basically a level requirement. For example, a Genie Binder requires: “Skills: Diplomacy 4 ranks, Knowledge (nobility) 4 ranks, Knowledge (planes) 11 ranks.” Therefore, you must be level 11 before taking this PrC. I will try to put the highest rank requirement first so you know what level you must be.
Grading system
Sky Blue: Superior option, often more powerful than a base class.
Blue: Fantastic option, great reason to take the class.
Green: Solid option, you won’t be worse for taking it.
Orange: Okay option, or useful only in rare circumstances.
Black: Unrated, either because usefulness varies or because it’s not applicable.
Red: Bad option, useless or extremely situational.


Each class is graded using the following block:
Base Classes:         The classes most likely to qualify at a glance. Non-exhaustive.
Requirements:                How hard it is to qualify and special restrictions.
Martial Power:                Overall quality of BAB, Hit Die, and Weapon/Armor proficiencies.
Spell Power:                 Spellcasting progression and class abilities that boost spellcasting.
Utility:                         Skills per level and non-combat powers.
Novelty:                 How new or exciting the class is to play.
Progression:                Other class or ability progression from regular classes.
Special:                Particularly notable abilities or synergies between class features.
Final Thoughts:         An overall review of the PrC.

Standard Action Buffs are Bad
I have made many characters I thought were “invincible.” I calculated all their buffs stacked together and thought, “no enemies can harm me.” Then, when it came to actual combat, we rolled initiative, enemies were right in front of me, and I had 3 buffs to stack as standard action each. This meant that if I wanted to be strong, I had to spend the first three rounds of combat sitting on my ass while my party-members attacked.
The lesson: if you have to know the fight is coming and stack buffs to be powerful, you’re not powerful. Several PrCs are rated lower due to their main features being limited resources that use a Standard Action to activate at the start of combat, effectively wasting your first turn or  surprise round.


Progression Helpers
Most prestige classes include a power drop in exchange for all the new stuff you get. There are some things you can do to mitigate this power drop, such as taking one of these feats:
Magical Knack - a.k.a. the multiclass trait. +2 caster Level progression capped at your Hit Dice. Also, it's a trait, so effectively free for multiclassing spellcasters.
Accomplished Sneak Attacker - +1d6 to your sneak attack, capped at ½ your character level rounded up. 
Shaping Focus - Wild Shape progression, an extra 4 levels of progression lets a druid take a dip or prestige class without penalty to your best ability. Because you need the Wild Shape feature, you have to already be a 4th level druid to take this.
Boon Companion - An extra 4 levels to your familiar/animal companion progression, capped at character level, means you can take a substantial dip without hurting your buddy’s power level. 
Phantom Ally - An extra 4 levels to your Spiritualist’s Phantom progression, capped at character level. People online have suggested homebrewing a similar feat for Eidolons  (kinda bullshit that spiritualist has one but summoner doesn’t)
Favored Prestige Class - this is a prerequisite for another feat. +1 HP or skill point per PrC level is kinda meh on its own, but is great for getting you:
Prestigious Spellcaster - nullify the power dip of a prestige class that doesn’t give +1 spell progression at first level. Shame it takes two feats.
Eldritch Heritage - It takes two feats, but you get a 1st level power from a Sorcerer bloodline at a -2 effective level. While this can technically qualify you for some PrCs most bloodline powers are pretty bland.
