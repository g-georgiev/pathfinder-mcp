﻿Tark’s Guide To building Tag Team Champions: A summoner guide.


Take a look at the picture below;
  

Note the whimsical smile that suggests it will break your game.  Notice the ironic wreath of flowers around the neck of a beast who likely pounces for ridiculous amounts of damage and has enough natural armor to shrug off a hill giants mightiest blows.  This is a vanilla summoner.  Powerful, optimal, arguably as strong as an entire adventuring group.  We are not going to be talking about this summoner today.
  



We’re going to talk about this guy.  Note the not quite optimal fire immunity.  Note the armor.  Note the sword.  Note the expression on the face that suggests spamming summon monster spells is for limp wristed pansies with pink toenail polish.  This is a combat summoner and he and his big friend define the term curbstomp.






Before I begin let me just start by saying that this is not the most optimal way to play a summoner.  Nope, sorry, you’ll see some crazy tricks, you’ll see some hilarity ensue, and you’ll see plenty more reasons why a GM might feel the need to ban the class entirely from their games.  But, ultimately the conventional method for summoners really is the most optimal.  That being said this guide is meant to turn convention on its head and explore the class in a deeper light and explain why not only is the class a viable melee contender but a fun one to boot. 


However, if what you are looking for is a more cut and dry standard summoner, or if you’ve never played one before I highly suggest you take a look at Saph’s The Summoner Handbook.  In a sense you can say this is a guide for making the Flank Buddy summoner as described in that guide and then using your summoner as the beat stick it supports. In another sense you can say I just straight up ignored the advice never to bother with melee.  Alternatively if you want to try a different form of summoner that’s very different from the Vanilla summoner I suggest looking into Summoning Information - a guide to an alternate Eidolon.


One more thing, I’m not going to talk about synthesists.  As far as this guide and this author is concerned the synthesist does not exist.  Is it great at melee?  Yeah of course it is.  But to me the differences are so stark as to not even be recognizable from the original class.  More than that it feels like a munchkin’s wet dream that’s hard to understand, vague, and essentially tough to play and to DM for.  Let’s also not forget that in terms of action economy and the sheer versatility of having more skill points, feats, and bodies to use, the vanilla summoner laughs in the synthesist's face.


To start with let’s discuss some simple basic rules for making a melee summoner work;


1. Efficiency is your bread and butter: Action economy is one of the many things that make summoners such a powerful class.  A typical summoner will always try to leverage their positioning and actions in order to do as much as possible (usually a spell or 2 full attacks or several full attacks depending on situation).  Melee summoners get the added bonus of being a competent melee combatant on top of these perks.  So not only can they be efficient in terms of action economy but efficient in terms of longevity as well.  Without having to worry about conserving spells as much you can focus on spells that will have a greater impact in a single casting (such as haste) rather than focus on a spell list that will have an answer for every plausible scenario.


2. Remember you can Double Buff: One of the main weaknesses of any gish type fighter is the requirement that they spend a round or two buffing in order to meet the standards of numbers required for rough and ready close combat.  Whether this is boosting AC, Attack, or Damage all gishes, even those from classes designed as such, usually need a round to warm up before wading into the fray.  What makes the summoner special in this regard is a creature capable of buffing him and vice versa.  With access to skills like use magic device an eidolon can easily work to continually buff a summoner as much as the summoner works to buff him.  So through the summoner’s superior action economy they can double up on the buffs per rounds they can throw up.  If you include Improve Shared Spells into the mix you can effectively cast three spells a round without rods or metamagic.  With that kind of action economy at your disposal there’s no reason not to be buffed by the end of the first round.




3. You work best when other support characters are around: You have one of the strongest buff lists in the game.  Combined with your eidolon and summoning that makes you easily one of the best support classes in the game.  That being said by voluntarily choosing a melee route you rob your group of that.  However if, say, someone is already taking a support role than adding on your plethora of buffs will be redundant at least some of the time.  Therefore being able to lever yourself into both positions makes you a bigger boon to the group than going one way or the other 100% of the time which leads me to make next basic tip...


4.  Versatility is your motto: A more subtle feature of the summoner class is their remarkable versatility.  With spells like Evolution surge and abilities like Aspect and Greater Aspect you can custom build your evolutions to fit the needs of your group.  Whether they require you to do more damage, be more mobile, have greater defenses, or provide some form of transportation for the group you can do quite a bit no matter which way you go.  By turning into a more combat oriented character you open up your versatility slightly by helping to mitigate some of your weaknesses.  Removing your spell component pouch only makes you leverage your non-magic bonuses harder, an antimagic field only makes your more cautious (not useless).  On top of this you can take abilities and spells that were previously defensive/utility options and turn them into aggressive tools for bringing the fight to the enemy as quickly as possible.  The final word to all of this is; you can do a lot remarkably well.


5. Positioning is King: Some things a summoner can take advantage of all by himself that few other classes can is the ability to constantly make use of flanking, aid another, and situational teamwork feats.  However this is entirely due in part to careful positioning of Eidolon/Summoner/Summons in order to best find these bonuses and maintain abilities like Shield Ally.  What’s interesting about the summoner is that for a caster class it has a number of abilities that allow it to get the positioning and subsequent modifiers that good positioning provides. Knowing how to place yourself as well as your spells is the quickest route to the most damage in any situation.  Whether it’s fishing for attacks of opportunities, searching for charge lanes, or just looking for ways 


6. Choose Your Spells Wisely: When a lot of people describe the summoner as a spellcasting class I feel a slight urge to cringe.  This is in part due to the very limited spellcasting at the summoner’s disposal combined with a rather stark focus on their admittedly amazing spell list.  As such I feel compelled to advise that any summoner, not just ones focused on getting into the brawl, choose their spells with extreme care not just in adding to their spells known but also in casting.  Comparatively speaking a summoner has no business casting spells every round.  So, instead focus on meaningful spells for your build and high impact spells that can swing fights quickly in your favor when cast.  Use them conservatively and don’t bother casting spells if you are dealing meaningful amounts of damage without them.


7. When in Trouble: Summon Help  So the gm throws a night encounter at you, your character is in his britches and your eidolon is unsummoned.  What now?  Summon crap.  Summon lots of crap.  Your summon monster ability is easily the strongest ability at your disposal in any similar situation.  Use it wisely, use it often,  use it well and it will pull you out of impossible scenarios and the brink of death.


8. Learn to Unsummon Your eidolon fails a will save and ends up dominated.  He fails a fort save and he suffers a deadly poison.  Depending on how much you rely on your eidolon and how badly they were just messed up it may be worth just unsummoning your eidolon as a standard action.  This sucks, to be sure.  But wasting time trying to cure a dominate, fix a slow, or heal off a ton of damage from the eidolon can turn out much worse.  So, instead of wasting actions curing a useless eidolon consider unsummoning the eidolon with a standard action and just summoning in something useful with the next.  This isn’t always practical so some wisdom should be applied when using this option.  But it is an option and often a good one.  So don’t forget it.
