# Brute

Brute


The Basics


        Hulk-esque vigilantes who completely lose control of their ability to distinguish friends from foes during combat. Fun fact: the brute archetype is widely acknowledged to be one of the worst in the game. I’ve done a third-party rewrite of the archetype here, in case you’re interested in what that might look like.


        Versatility: -2
        Power: -2

The Abilities


        (1) Chaotic Vigilante (Ex): Alters dual identity. Blah blah alignment restrictions blah blah. Sure.


(1) Chaotic Vigilante [3PP]: No changes needed. The chaotic alignment restriction is annoying, but not mechanically bad.


(1) Brutish Dualism [3PP]: Although I appreciate the Hulk-like changes that this archetype makes to the vigilante chassis, there’s more than one way to skin a cat, and I think a freakishly speedy, dexterous brute is every bit as interesting as a smashy-smashy brute. Brutish dualism essentially creates two “tracks” for the archetype, one of which will focus more on Dexterity-based combat, while the other is the traditional brute you know and love (to hate).


        (1) Brutish Fortitude (Ex): Alters base saving throws. Ack! You lose two good saves (Reflex and Will) in exchange for one good save (Fortitude). Given that Will saves are far and away the most important save type in the game, and doubly so when you will continue to attack your allies after a fight has concluded unless you succeed at a high Will save, this is absolutely unacceptable. 


(1) Brutish Resilience [3PP]: My third-party rewrites give both wrath and freak brutes a strong Will save progression; the only difference is that wrath brutes get a strong Fortitude save progression, while freak brutes get a strong Reflex save progression. Flavor this however you want: brutes are too angry to be mind-controlled, whatever. They need strong Will save progression mechanically, or else they will start murdering team members once a fight has concluded.


        (1) Weapon and Armor Proficiencies: Alters weapon and armor proficiencies. Ayayay. Brutes already have huge AC problems, thanks to brute form’s -2 penalty, and then add on the loss of medium armor? No way. Martial weapons I’m fine surrendering, but you need your armor or a valid equivalent.


(1) Weapon and Armor Proficiencies [3PP]: My third-party rewrites go one step further than Paizo did and eliminate a brute’s armor proficiencies altogether. Nani…!? Yes, that’s right. Does the Hulk wear armor? No, he does not. So we’re going to slap some natural armor and dodge bonuses on this package in the next ability. You can use a wand of mage armor to supplement, like any other monk.


        (1) Brute Form (Ex): Replaces vigilante specialization. So you wanted to be the Hulk, did you? Well, I hope you’re happy, world, because this is the stinking pile of dog crap that Paizo squeezed out onto the lawn when you clamored for Hulk fan service. To get to my point, brute form is universally loathed, and for excellent reason. Why, I hear you ask? Let’s run through a list of brute form’s many fatal flaws in no particular order before you beg for my third-party rewrites:


      * A permanent -2 to AC. Wasn’t the -1 AC from the size change enough for you, Paizo? You had to kick us while we’re down?
      * Absolutely tanks your out-of-combat utility. Brute form prevents the vigilante from using Bluff, Diplomacy, Use Magic Device, Disguise, Handle Animal, Disable Device, Escape Artist, Sleight of Hand, Stealth, Appraise, Linguistics, Spellcraft, or any Knowledge check while in their vigilante identity. Not to mention the ability proscribes any activities that require “patience or concentration,” which GMs could interpret as liberally as their twisted minds allow. (I 🖤 you, GMs!) Vigilantes in their social identities can’t often participate in combat without risking discovery, but a saving grace of the class is that vigilantes in their vigilante identities can often do most things that their social identities could, including skill use, spellcasting, reading a children’s picturebook, etc. Without those aspects of the vigilante class, you are a weapon and nothing more—and that’s not a compliment.
      * Terrible entry into brute form. Brutes are renowned for their inability to control the shift into their vigilante identities, and it’s a doozy, even in an archetype that’s already a real doozfest. If you fail a Will save as combat starts, you immediately start the transition to your vigilante identity. Allies will know your secret double life, as will any enemy that happens to witness this. More than that, the process takes 1 full round and cannot be shortened with quick change, immediate, change, transformation sequence, or other social talents…plus you’re flat-footed the entire time, making you easy prey for sneak attack damage and bad on-hit rider effects. The worst.
      * You’ll end up attacking your friends. Classic Hulk, right? Yes, except The Avengers goes into great detail about why having an uncontrollable, easily provoked, infinitely powerful smash-monster on your team leads to such volatile situations. The Will save to stop mauling your allies starts at DC 21 at 1st level, when essentially nothing but a natural 20 will pass, and doesn’t get meaningfully better throughout your career, thanks to a poor Will save progression and little support in the class for a strong Wisdom stat. Folks in the Max the Min thread about this archetype have some ideas, but they almost always burn your teammates’ resources, and that’s a major party foul.
      * Your armor and weapons don’t resize. Again, classic Hulk: mild-mannered Bruce Banner wakes up in an impact crater, buck-naked because he hulked out of his clothes. Humorous to watch Mark Ruffalo cosplay; really freaking annoying to have to deal with as a Pathfinder PC. This forces you into the unenviable position of having to either slap magical enchantments on your equipment (a resizing weapon will set you back a cool 4,000 gp, while fitting armor costs 2,000 gp) or else burn a precious vigilante talent on sizing equipment.
      * You become fatigued or exhausted by your transitions. It’s like Paizo built a machine specially engineered to kick this archetype in the metaphorical fruitstand with all the force of Lionel Messi on steroids, over and over again. Yeesh. Immunity to fatigue or exhaustion (e.g., the horizon walker’s desert terrain dominance or the oracle’s lame curse) also make you immune to turning into a brute, and you can’t remove the fatigued or exhausted condition by any means except rest. I have no idea how to fix this except to burn the entire archetype down to the waterline and start over again.


(1) Brute Form [3PP]: Brute form is the ability that absolutely, 100% needs to get completely overhauled by third-party content, so let’s run through the changes I’ve made:


      * Your armor and weapons resize automatically. I have no idea why Paizo punished the archetype like this, but it’s gone now. Enjoy your extra vigilante talent or 6,000 gp.
      * You can use all skills in your vigilante identity, albeit at a penalty. Rather than penalize Dexterity, Intelligence, and Charisma, I penalized Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma, and rather than make you unable to use these skills entirely, I simply added a scaling penalty to some checks.
      * You can choose to transform out of sight, for a price, or transform in full view, for a benefit. I’m a fan of decision points that are meaningful, so I wrote one into the ability. You can choose to transform out of sight, but at the cost of dealing yourself nonlethal damage every round to suppress your murderous urges, or you can transform in full view of enemies, causing them to become shaken or frightened but risking the secrecy of your vigilante identity. At the very least, the brute needs a way not to get made unless they want to get made.
      * You become confused after combat is over, instead of dominated. In addition to changing the scaling of the Will save DC to transform (10 + vigilante level is infinitely more lenient than 20 + ½ vigilante level) I made it so that the brute simply becomes confused when there are no enemies left. You might roll “attack nearest creature,” but you might also harm yourself or babble incoherently. It still lends some urgency to your post-combat actions, but doesn’t make it so that you automatically start dismantling your loved ones when there aren’t any mooks left to be slaughtered.
      * You take mental ability damage for transforming, rather than the fatigued or exhausted conditions. The longer you stay in your brute form, the more ability damage you rack up to your Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma scores as your social identity recoils from the horrors of your vigilante identity. You can still heal it through natural or magical rest, but you can also heal it through murder. The social identity may fear and loathe killing; the vigilante identity revels in it. Anyway, this felt like a nice push-and-pull mechanic that lets you take some penalties for overusing brute form while not shooting you in both kneecaps absolutely every day.
      * Automatic enhancement bonuses to Strength or Dexterity. This should mostly be thought of as a means of compensating brutes (who will tend toward unarmed strike builds) for the amulet of mighty fists that they’re inevitably going to need. The bonuses are enhancement bonuses, rather than alchemical bonuses or somesuch, in order to avoid too steep a power curve.
      * Automatic dodge or natural armor bonuses to AC. Skipping the armor question entirely makes sense for this archetype, which feels like it shouldn’t narratively rely on armor anyway. Instead, wrath brutes get scaling natural armor bonuses to AC, while freak brutes get scaling dodge bonuses to AC. Add on a wand of mage armor, and a 2nd-level brute can have an AC of about 18 or 19. This scales up pretty well into the endgame; accounting for mage armor, the “replacement” AC values for wrath brutes will be about +11 (equivalent to a +5 breastplate) and +9 for freak brutes (equivalent to a +5 chain shirt). The rules text precludes any stat from being added to AC, so GMs fearful of monk dips should rest easy.


        (2) Brute Talents: Alters vigilante talents. Brute talents are actually pretty good! Well, some are, at least. Let’s review:


      * (2) Heavy Punches (Ex): Monk unarmed strike scaling immediately earns its blue rating with aplomb. Especially given that the brute’s size increase automatically increases your damage (to a maximum of 4d8 at 20th level, rather than the standard 2d10 for Medium-sized creatures) this talent is exceptionally worth it. More damage never goes out of style.
      * (8) Awesome Blow (Ex): Awesome Blow is, I would hazard, flirting with a yellow rating. It costs a standard action, forcing you to miss out on a full-attack action, and relies on a successful CMB check against a Medium or smaller creature. If you nail the CMB check, though, you get to damage the opponent, move them against their will, and cause them to fall prone—a tidy little package.
      * (4) Scale Surroundings (Ex): A climb speed is great if a fly speed isn’t available. I’m fine with this.
      * (2) Total Destruction (Ex): Total destruction would be an awesome talent but for one itsy-bitsy problem: it doesn’t allow you to use your Strength modifier on attack rolls with thrown weapons. This is still doable with a lesser belt of mighty hurling, but frankly, a talent needs to come functional out of the box if you intend to spend a precious resource investing in it. If your GM is willing to give you Strength to attack rolls, it becomes much, much better—a cheap and easy ranged option for a mostly melee archetype. This is the change I make in my third-party rewrite of the talent.
      * (2) Sizing Equipment (Ex): Sizing equipment is a talent tax, plain and simple. See my third-party rewrites to brute form if you’d rather not have to suffer through this horse manure.


(2) Brute Talents [3PP]: These should mostly be self-explanatory. The only changes I’ve made to Paizo’s talents are to eliminate sizing equipment and buff total destruction so that it allows you to use your Strength modifier on attack rolls.


        (20) Tear Them Apart (Ex): Replaces vengeance strike. I enjoy tear them apart, both for the undeniably metal name as well as for the fact that it’s more or less a passive damage buff. If you anticipate getting to 20th level, you should almost certainly take the morphic weaponry vigilante talent, as adding a bunch of natural attacks to your normal unarmed iteratives will increase the likelihood that you hit with enough attacks to proc your rend.


Is It Worth It?


        Oh, brute. You know you’re in trouble when you’ve been the subject of a Max the Min Reddit thread. It’s a pity, because I actually quite like some of the brute talents, and I love the idea of a Hulk/Mr. Hyde kind of vigilante. But brute form, the loss of a strong Will save, and the loss of your armor and weapon proficiencies are so hard in the red that there’s truly no way for the archetype to recover. Go with the third-party rewrites, or don’t go at all.
