# Cabalist

Cabalist


The Basics


        Dark, nefarious, half-magus/witch vigilantes who use blood, shadow, and necromancy to disable opponents.


        Versatility: +2
        Power: -1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Class Skills: Alters class skills. Despite losing more class skills than you gain, this is probably still a net positive for the archetype. Knowledge (Engineering) and Survival are mostly useless for the class, and Disable Device won’t be everyone’s jam. The only truly useful skill you lose is Perception, and that’s often easily reacquired through alternative racial traits like Fey Thoughts. Knowledge (Arcana) and Spellcraft, meanwhile, are always pretty useful.


        (1) Skill Ranks per Level: Alters skill ranks per level. Losing skill ranks doesn’t matter, because you’re Intelligence-based now, and Intelligence-based classes never have trouble with skill ranks.


        (1) Weapon and Armor Proficiencies: Alters armor proficiencies. Since the archetype strips away medium armor permanently—you can’t ever cast in it without arcane spell failure chance, even at later levels—cabalists will necessarily be high-Dexterity builds, likely taking the lethal grace vigilante talent at 2nd level to make ends meet.


        (1) Spellcasting: Replaces 4th-, 8th-, 10th-, 14th-, and 16th-level vigilante talents. The cabalist’s spellcasting class feature means that it will functionally only ever have three vigilante talents in the course of a normal campaign, which is…*checks notes*...yeah, not many. Lethal grace should almost certainly be your 2nd-level vigilante talent, but the rest are more or less up to you: cunning feint and perfect vulnerability are the most useful for proc’ing bleed damage from spill blood, but there are a number of cabalist-exclusive talents that will also be competing for your attention. Familiar or tattoo chamber are excellent picks at 6th level, and 12th could be shadow jump or any other dance partner who tickles your fancy.
        With regard to spellcasting…yeah, it’s great to have a spellcasting vigilante. Especially in conjunction with your bloodbound spell class feature, the cabalist can muster some nasty save DCs on big debuffs. Some cherries from the witch list:


      * 1st Level
      * Hermean Potential/Ill Omen: Probably your most valuable 1st-level spells, notable in both cases for the lack of a save. A lesser quicken metamagic rod + ill omen is a recipe for an enemy’s disaster, and the same rod + hermean potential is a wonderful buffing opportunity for a teammate.
      * Long Arm: Cabalists do best when enemies are bleeding, and your spill blood class feature or a wounding weapon is the quickest way to get that bleed damage flowing. The longer your reach, the more attacks of opportunity you have available for blood.
      * 2nd Level
      * Tears to Wine: A lovely spell that, while a bit more expensive at 2nd level on the witch list, is still a workhorse. A +10 bonus to all Intelligence- and Wisdom-based skills for 10 minutes/level is…really good.
      * Blood Transcription: Kill a caster, steal their spells. It’s evil-aligned, of course, but cabalists are in a great place to take advantage of this effect.
      * Mortal Terror: This spell has some interesting overlap with bleed damage, because as long as a target keeps taking even 1 point of damage, they keep having to save against fear conditions. An easy one to slap on a melee bruiser and stand back for the fireworks.
      * See Invisibility: Your spill blood class feature doesn’t work against creatures with total concealment. Don’t let enemies get away with being unseen.
      * Spectral Hand: Any bad touch build loves spectral hand, and cabalists are no exception. You’ve got bestow curse, touch of idiocy, mathematical curse, curse of befouled fortune, vampiric touch, fleshworm infestation, baphomet’s blessing, and more on your spell list. The fact that most of these also happen to be necromancy spells is icing on a very nice cake.
      * 3rd Level
      * Bestow Curse: You know it! You love it! Give it up foooooor bestow curse! The standby debuff that puts all other debuffs to shame…at least until greater bestow curse shows up. You’ve got a lot of save DC support from class features, too, which is lovely.
      * Accursed Glare: Similar to pugwampi’s grace, but technically a werebat-exclusive spell and saddled with an unfortunate 1-round casting time. It’s still phenomenal, but you might have to resort to pugwampi’s grace. About the only difference is duration (days/level vs. rounds/level), which won’t make much of a difference in a pitched battle anyway.
      * Pugwampi’s Grace: Think of pugwampi’s grace like ill omen’s older, hotter cousin who went off to college a few years ago. A Will save now negates the effect, but if you manage to stick the landing, every d20 roll for rounds/level becomes a roll-twice-take-the-worse jawn. A particularly nasty combo, in fact, is ill omen → pugwampi’s grace, where the former’s lack of a save opens an enemy up to a failed save against the far deadlier effects of the latter.
      * Siphon Might: Most cabalists will use lethal grace to attack in melee range, and that’s actually aces, because lethal grace forces you to use your Strength modifier for damage rolls. If you grab Weapon Finesse and then use siphon might to buff your damage numbers, you’ll be in really great shape. It’s a good spell, plain and simple.
      * Twilight Knife: What a lovely spell. It flanks with you, deals sneak attack force damage, and is impervious to nearly all forms of attack. Think of this like the best possible version of spiritual weapon.
      * 4th Level
      * Dimension Door: You know you’ll need it, so pick it up.
      * Enervation: One more tool in your debuffing toolkit. Pugwampi’s grace + enervation + siphon might = enemies having a bad time.
      * Masochistic Shadow: Masochistic shadow is basically a fast-moving death sentence for any target without rock-solid Will and Reflex saves, especially when paired with pugwampi’s grace or bestow curse. Slap this on a fighter and their Strength score will drain away so quickly they’ll begin buckling under the weight of their own armor.
      * Spite: A very solid witch spell that binds touch-range debuffs onto your skin for hours/level until they’re discharged by a melee aggressor. Bestow curse is the classic example, but others (see my rating for spectral hand) will also do excellent work.
      * 5th Level
      * Mind Fog: An absurd -10 penalty to Will saves makes enemies putty in your hands. My recommendation would be to throw this at a crowd of enemies, see which failed their saves, and then go for possession, dominate person, etc. for a fun time.
      * Possession: Want an easy way to completely take control of an enemy? Because possession is quite a bit better than magic jar.
      * Wracking Ray: Wracking ray deals an average of 12 points of Strength and Dexterity damage on a failed Fortitude save, and between your excellent buffs to necromancy save DCs and all the spells you have to force enemies to reroll saves, it should be a cinch to get that failed save.
      * 6th Level
      * True Seeing: Anyone who can get true seeing must get true seeing. That’s the rule. It counters blur, displacement, mirror image, deeper darkness, invisibility, greater invisibility, mislead, and all manner of illusion and polymorph spells—in short, some of the deadliest tools that high-level spellcasters will leverage against your party. Being able to see the enemy for what and where they really are can easily make the difference between a cakewalk of an encounter and a TPK. Don’t be the person who skips this spell.
      * Greater Heroism: Despite all appearances to the contrary, the cabalist is still an archetype that is expected to perform decently in melee combat in order to spread bleed damage. Greater heroism is one of the best vehicles for ensuring that you can do so.
      * Unwilling Shield: Taking half damage by any means (see, e.g., fire shield) is always a sweet proposition, and the fact that unwilling shield synergizes with your overall focus on necromancy is even sweeter. The duration is long enough for one combat, and barring the somewhat expensive material component, it has no other factors that would detract from its usefulness.


        (1) Spellbook: Overall, casting spells from a spellbook is more powerful than casting spells through a familiar. A book can be stolen, of course, but then again, a familiar can be killed, so on that front there’s no inherent advantage either way. No, the real advantage of a spellbook is that it’s far easier for cabalists, magi, wizards, etc. to learn new spells than it is for witches. While both witches and cabalists can—and should, as often as possible—learn spells through scrolls, witches can only learn spells from the larger witch list by having their familiar commune with the familiar of another witch (or by using more niche effects like blood transcription). This presupposes that you have access to a willing witch’s familiar, a condition that won’t be met in a large majority of cases. Cabalists and other spellbook users, on the other hand, can borrow a spellbook from a willing spellcaster or take spellbooks off of deceased or imprisoned enemy spellcasters; thanks to the larger proportion of wizards and magi in the world of Golarion, you’re likely to meet these conditions much more regularly.


        (1) Spill Blood (Ex): Replaces vigilante specialization. I feel like it’s a little disingenuous to say that spill blood replaces your vigilante specialization, since cabalist is essentially a specialization of its own. Either way, it’s a great ability for the archetype, if a bit finicky to use. First, just to check its power level, it’s one of the strongest sources of bleed damage I know of in the game, beating even Shax’s third-tier sentinel boon. Bleed damage is an odd beast: it works on fewer types of creatures than the precision damage of hidden strike, which is the closest thing we have to an opportunity cost for the class feature, but can be more powerful if the circumstances are right. Assuming you’re fighting just one enemy, hidden strike will always win, especially if you can land multiple blows per round for the full d8 damage. Bleed damage continues to tick even when you’re not attacking, however, and when stacked on multiple enemies can boast competitive damage per round. When you get right down to brass tacks, though, the cabalist is a spellcaster first and foremost, and it’s your 5th-level bloodbound spell class feature that makes spill blood worth your while. Any enemy who’s bleeding takes a -1 penalty to save against your spells—or a hefty -2 for necromancy spells. Combine that with the shaken condition from bloody horror, Spell Focus + Greater Spell Focus, effects like ill omen or pugwampi’s grace, and you’re well on your way to becoming an unstoppable debuffing machine.
        Now the bad part: it’s unreasonably difficult to proc your bleed damage. Unlike the stalker specialization, cabalists don’t deal bleed when they flank a foe, including with something like twilight knife. That’s bad. What’s worse is that witches don’t have invisibility or greater invisibility on their spell list! There are other routes to denying an enemy their Dexterity bonus to AC, of course, like feinting (with the cunning feint vigilante talent) or Shatter Defenses, but invisibility is by far the easiest. Beg, borrow, or steal that effect from your teammates. Since spill blood can’t always be relied upon to get an enemy bleeding, you’ll most likely want to invest in a wounding weapon. The effect is expensive at a +2 bonus, and the bleed damage from wounding and spill blood won’t stack, but wounding weapons do at least get the process started in order to trigger bloodbound spell, which you want up and running as much as humanly possible.


        (2) Cabalist Talents: Alters vigilante talents. While not every cabalist talent is a slam dunk, some are quite good! Let’s get into it.


      * (2) Familiar (Ex): This won’t come as a surprise to anyone. Familiars grant good bonuses, the Alertness feat, the ability to deliver touch spells for their masters—and that’s all without touching the flexibility of familiar archetypes or the Improved Familiar feat. If you have any spare room in your build, you want this talent. My recommendation is to make either familiar or tattoo chamber your 6th-level vigilante talent.
      * (10) Shadow Jump (Su): A short-range, tactically optimal dimension door. Mind the lighting restrictions, but otherwise enjoy teleporting around like Nightcrawler. Since cabalists trade away their 10th-level vigilante talents, you’d have to wait until 12th level to pick up shadow jump.
      * (2) Tattoo Chamber (Su): Tattoo chamber is a great talent whose primary purpose is to allow you to use spell trigger items without actually holding them. What counts as a spell trigger item? Wands, for one, and staves, for another. It’s unclear whether metamagic rods count, because while they’re use-activated items, not spell trigger items, they don’t need to be either donned or activated with a command word, only held in the hand while casting a spell. My recommendation would be to store predominantly wands in your tattoo early on, transitioning to staves at later levels and adding in a quicken metamagic rod or a persistent metamagic rod as your budget (and GM!) allows. In all cases, prioritize spells that you’ll want at your fingertips in combat without having to devote spell slots to them.
      * (12) Living Shadow (Sp): At 12th level, living shadow would earn a red rating because of its criminally short duration and your inability to cast spells or attack. (What is it useful for at this level? Scouting?) At 14th, it finally becomes functional, giving you some nice defensive and mobility options while also allowing you to continue casting spells. Pick up Eschew Materials if you foresee yourself using this power regularly.
      * (2) Bond of Blood (Sp, Su): Gaining a few temporary HP every hour isn’t what I’d call the pinnacle of effectiveness. Especially when you stack bond of blood up against other cabalist or vigilante talents, there’s simply no competition. Skip it.
      * (2) Necromantic Focus (Ex): A cleverly disguised trap. Feats are much less valuable to cabalists than vigilante talents are! Never forget that. With the exception of blindness/deafness and bestow curse (which you’d be picking up with spell slots anyway) none of the other spells are worth your investment, and Spell Focus is decidedly better as a normal feat pick.


        (5) Bloodbound Spell (Ex): Replaces startling appearance. Assuming you can get enemies bleeding, which, again, should be accomplished with a wounding weapon as a backup in case spill blood doesn’t work, bloodbound spell is an amazing class feature—the equivalent of Spell Focus (Everything) and Greater Spell Focus (Necromancy). Save DCs are a perennial problem for ⅔ spellcasters, but bloodbound spell is your ticket to the big time. Try never to target an enemy with a debuff (especially a necromancy debuff) unless they’re bleeding.


        (11) Bloody Horror (Ex): Replaces frightening appearance. Bleed damage and spellcasting are, like, the top two things that cabalists are best at, so sure! Intimidation tactics never hurt. Shatter Defenses is one of the better routes you can take to consistent damage from spill blood, and there’s a great argument to be made for Signature Skill (Intimidate) as your 11th-level feat so that you can start escalating fear conditions.


        (17) Shadowy Appearance (Sp): Replaces stunning appearance. At a lower level, shadowy appearance would easily earn a blue rating, but by 17th level, practically every enemy you face will have some means of detecting invisible creatures or seeing through concealment. Blindsight, tremorsense, true seeing, echolocation—there’s no shortage of ways that this ability could fail to work for you. If enemies aren’t packing those abilities, though, it’s an amazing defensive ability. The 1/day greater invisibility is particularly nice for spreading bleed damage using spill blood without having to resort to Shatter Defenses, blindness/deafness, feinting, etc.


Is It Worth It?


        There’s some debate over whether the cabalist or the warlock is the best spellcasting vigilante archetype, but I think both have lovely niches to fill. Cabalists are excellent at doling out ticking bleed damage, demoralizing enemies, and laying down hard-hitting debuffs, all without surrendering any of the versatility that social talents confer. The loss of vigilante talents hurts, of course, but you can’t get everything in a build without making sacrifices. I quite like it!
