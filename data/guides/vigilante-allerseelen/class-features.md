# Class Features

Class Features


        (1) Dual Identity (Ex): Dual identity isn’t so much a ratable class feature as it is just a fact of life for the vigilante class. As with any good superhero, you’re living a double life: by day, you’re a mild-mannered citizen, and by night, you run around the streets beating up bad guys (or good guys—I won’t judge) with your bare fists. Assuming that you don’t want people to associate your vigilante identity with your social identity, managing the tension between these two alter egos will be at the core of your playstyle, because the two skillsets are, for the most part, mutually exclusive. If you want to use social skills, well, you’d better be in your social identity, and if you want to use vigilante skills, you’d either better be in your vigilante identity or else really good at Disguise checks. If you’d rather take the Iron Man approach and just broadcast to the whole world that your vigilante and social identities are one and the same, well, you can just stay in your social identity most of the time and ignore dual identity. Let’s hop into the rules text.


A vigilante hides his true identity, allowing him to move about social circles and nobility without carrying the stigma of his ruthless actions. In effect, the vigilante has two identities: one is a polite member of society while the other is a skilled and cunning warrior. To keep up this charade, the vigilante usually has two names: his true name, used in polite company, and his vigilante name, used to strike fear in the hearts of those who oppose him. Knowledge checks about one do not reveal information about the other, unless the vigilante’s true identity revealed to the world at large.


        Sure, yes, Knowledge checks don’t reveal information about the one from the other. It’d be pretty weird if someone said, “Hey, you know Bruce Wayne?” and all of a sudden the other person was like, “Bruce Wayne? Sure, I know…hey, wait a minute! He’s Batman!” It’s hilarious to imagine how the plot of The Dark Knight would have unfolded had that been the case, but fortunately, that’s not the story here.


The vigilante can start each day in either of his identities, referred to simply as social or vigilante. Changing from one identity to another takes 1 minute and must be done out of sight from other creatures to preserve the vigilante’s secret. Changing identities is more than just changing outfits and clothing (although that is certainly a part of it); the process often also involves applying make-up, altering his hair, and adjusting other personal effects. Furthermore, the change is as much a state of mind as of body, so items such as a hat of disguise and similar spells and effects that change the user’s appearance do not reduce the time required to change identities. Most social talents require the vigilante to be in his social identity, but a vigilante who uses vigilante talents in his social identity risks exposing his secret.


        Changing between your identities takes a full minute, which, while only mildly annoying in normal play, becomes a real time crunch when you need to hop into an unexpected combat. Ironically, this makes vigilantes somewhat weaker at intrigue play than other classes, because they can’t go from social interaction to sudden but inevitable betrayal without hiding behind a tapestry for a minute while they get themselves in the appropriate Uta Hagen headspace to start living their double life. For this reason, I highly, highly recommend that you take the quick change social talent at 7th level, and possibly the immediate change social talent at 13th level, as well. Quick change reduces your changing time to a full-round action, while immediate change will further reduce it to a move action. You can’t reliably use the two halves of the vigilante class together if you want to maintain secrecy, so the best that can be done in most cases is to make the transition between the two halves more streamlined or to ignore the delineation altogether and play the whole game in your social identity.


Despite being a single person, a vigilante’s dual nature allows him to have two alignments, one for each of his identities. When in an identity, he is treated as having that identity’s alignment for all spells, magic items, and abilities that rely on alignment. For the purpose of meeting a qualification for a feat, class, or any ability, he is only eligible if both of his alignments meet the requirements. A vigilante’s two alignments cannot be more than one step from each other on a single alignment axis. For example, a vigilante with a lawful neutral social identity could have a vigilante identity that is lawful good, lawful neutral, lawful evil, neutral, neutral good, or neutral evil. If a vigilante is the target of an effect that would change his alignment, it changes both of his alignments to the new alignment.


        Vigilante multiclass builds can be a nightmare when they interact with alignment-locked classes such as barbarian, monk, cleric, paladin, antipaladin, etc., because you have to think about how each of your two identities is going to interact with those classes. Some vigilante archetypes (cabalist, zealot, warlock, psychometrist, etc.) also turn you into a spellcaster of some stripe, and spellcasters can occasionally mess up their alignments with good, evil, lawful, or chaotic spells. That’s pretty rare, fortunately, but still worth keeping an eye on.


Any attempts to scry or otherwise locate the vigilante work only if the vigilante is currently in the identity the creature is attempting to locate (or if the creature knows that the two identities are the same individual). Otherwise, the spell or effect has no effect, revealing nothing but darkness, as if the target were invalid or did not exist.


        The final paragraph in dual identity’s rules text simply outlines some of the defenses that vigilantes have against high-level divination effects. Under no circumstances would you want spellcasters to be able to use greater scrying, locate creature, etc. to find you in your social identity based on your vigilante identity, or vice versa.


        (1) Seamless Guise (Ex): Seamless guise is the backstop that ensures that most enemies (barring, I don’t know, inquisitors who are 10 levels higher than you are) can’t figure out your little dual identity ruse. Circumstance bonuses stack with everything except other circumstance bonuses from the same source, so there shouldn’t be any overlap issues with feats or buffs that affect Disguise, but you do need to remember one crucial bit of information: seamless guise does not protect you if you use vigilante talents in your social identity. How exactly your GM interprets the clause (found in the vigilante talent rules text) will determine a lot—if you have the magical familiarity vigilante talent as a gnome, do you automatically lose your seamless guise bonuses just for casting a cantrip, when everyone would know that gnomes have some racial magic? Or if you have the shadow’s sight vigilante talent but can see in the dark as a human, would that trigger a Disguise check? You’ll probably want to communicate with your GM about how often they’re going to call for Disguise checks, and how ruthlessly they’re going to interpret seamless guise, as that might inform how much you invest in certain social talents. If you’re going to ignore the social/vigilante identity divide, then seamless guise doesn’t do much for you, as you’re not attempting to conceal the fact that you’re the vigilante everyone’s been looking for.


A vigilante knows how to behave in a way that appears perfectly proper and normal for his current identity. Should anyone suspect him of being anything other than what he appears to be while either in his social or vigilante identity, he can attempt a Disguise check with a +20 circumstance bonus to appear as his current identity, and not as his other identity.


        (1) Social Talents: Social talents are your bread and butter abilities for skills, social interaction, utility, disguise, and downtime. One thing that you’ll notice if you do a quick search on the Archives of Nethys feat page is that there is no means short of the halfling favored class bonus of acquiring more social or vigilante talents. Nope, no “Extra Social Talent” feat, and no “Extra Vigilante Talent” feat—they would be far too powerful for that. Instead, all you have to work with is the progression that you’re given at every odd level, and you can’t go back and revise choices that you’ve made. Consider every option carefully, and make every level count!


        (1) Vigilante Specialization (Ex): Your vigilante specialization determines which class your vigilante is going to draw inspiration from. If you choose to be an avenger, that class is going to be the fighter; if you choose to be a stalker, that class is going to be the rogue/ninja. Both have different complements of vigilante talents available to them that encourage different styles of play, so the choice of specialization is one of the biggest decisions you’ll ever make when building a vigilante.


At 1st level, a vigilante must choose to be either an avenger or a stalker. An avenger gains a base attack bonus equal to his vigilante level instead of using those listed on Table 1–1. He adds this value to any other base attack bonus gained from other classes or racial Hit Dice as normal.


        Woo, full BAB for avengers! To my knowledge, trappings of the warrior occultists are the only other characters that can get full BAB as a d8 Hit Die class, so you should take this class feature as the blessing that it is. Full BAB means faster access to many combat feats, better chances to hit with attacks, more damage from Power Attack or Deadly Aim, better CMD to resist combat maneuvers, and more.


A stalker gains an ability called hidden strike, which allows him to deal an extra 1d8 points of precision damage on melee attacks (or ranged attacks from within 30 feet) against foes who are unaware of his presence, who consider him an ally, or who are made flat-footed by startling appearance (see page 17). This extra damage increases by 1d8 at 3rd level and every 2 vigilante levels thereafter. A stalker vigilante can also deal hidden strike damage to a target that he is flanking or that is denied its Dexterity bonus to AC, but in these cases, the damage dice are reduced to d4s. A stalker can deal hidden strike damage against targets with concealment (but not total concealment).


        Stalkers, meanwhile, get a pseudo-clone of the rogue’s sneak attack class feature. You deal a bit more damage under limited circumstances, including (typically) attacking a foe at the start of combat, but a bit less damage against foes who are already aware that you’re there. Stalker vigilantes will probably deal slightly less damage than rogues in the long run, but the difference isn’t huge. Of note, the final sentence of the paragraph indicates that stalkers get the equivalent of the Shadow Strike feat for free, enabling them to use hidden strike on creatures who are under the effects of blur, concealed by obscuring mist or fog cloud, shrouded in dim light, etc.; the strike the unseen vigilante talent will let you use hidden strike on targets with total concealment, as well.


Once this choice is made, it can’t be changed. While many vigilante talents are usable by both specializations, some are unique to each specialization. A stalker vigilante can apply only one talent marked with an asterisk (*) to a given hidden strike, and only when that hidden strike is dealt against a foe that is unaware of the stalker vigilante’s presence (or who considers him an ally), unless otherwise noted.


Unlike rogues, who get to apply sneak attack talents every time they sneak attack an enemy, stalker vigilantes can only apply their special hidden strike talents when they’re attacking someone from stealth or betrayal. This isn’t huge, but it does make vigilante talents that modify hidden strike much less worthy of your purchase than comparable talents would be for a rogue.


(2) Vigilante Talents: Now we’re talking. Vigilante talents comprise all of the abilities that relate directly to combat, and y’all, let me tell you, these puppies are mean. Some vigilante talents will grant you between two and four feats’ worth of value, while others will poach class features from different classes or even grant effects that are available nowhere else in the game. There’s a reason that no effect in the game apart from the Past Your Limits alternate capstone allows you to gain more vigilante talents—they would be too over-the-top powerful to hand out willy-nilly.


Starting at 2nd level and every 2 levels thereafter, a vigilante gains a vigilante talent. Unless otherwise noted, a talent can be selected only once. Some talents require the vigilante to meet prerequisites before they can be chosen, such as possessing another vigilante talent, possessing a particular specialization, or attaining a minimum level. Once a talent has been selected, it can’t be changed.


        You can’t swap once you’ve chosen. Choose wisely.


If the vigilante uses any of these talents while in his social identity, he must succeed at a Disguise check against the Perception checks of all onlookers (without the +20 circumstance bonus from seamless guise) or the onlookers will realize that he is more than his social identity appears to be and perhaps discover the social and vigilante identities are one and the same. If a talent calls for a saving throw, the DC for that save is equal to 10 + 1/2 the vigilante’s level + the vigilante’s Charisma modifier, unless stated otherwise.


If you intend to keep your identities separate, you’ll want to check with your GM about how they intend to rule on which vigilante talents would trigger a Perception check. Unless you’re extraordinarily skilled at Disguise, Perception checks will regularly beat your DCs, and once the secret’s out, the music stops. This paragraph is a good argument for saving one of the slots you get with the 9th-level skill familiarity social talent for Disguise—you don’t want to accidentally roll a natural 1 and give yourself away when low-level commoners are watching you get ambushed in your social identity. Of course, the option is always there to let your social identity predominate, in which case feel free to go all John Wick on people with no preamble.


(3) Unshakable (Ex): Cue the Kimmy Schmidt theme song, eh? Anyway, increasing the DCs for enemies to Intimidate you is horribly, horribly underpowered. I can think of…yeah, nope, zero times that this has ever happened to me or any of the players I know.


(5) Startling Appearance (Ex): Stalker and avenger vigilantes are going to care about startling appearance for entirely different reasons. Stalker vigilantes will care about startling appearance because it lets them trigger their hidden strikes for an entire round, dealing a bunch of precision damage; avenger vigilantes will mostly care about forcing enemies to take a -4 penalty to attack them in the first round of combat. In either case, it’s a pretty good class feature—nothing amazing, but also not worthless.


At 5th level, a vigilante learns to use the element of surprise to his advantage, startling foes that are unaware of his presence. Whenever a vigilante with this ability attempts an attack against a foe that is completely unaware of the vigilante’s presence (usually due to Stealth or invisibility), the foe is treated as flat-footed for the rest of the vigilante’s turn (uncanny dodge or a similar ability prevents this effect unless the vigilante is at least 4 levels higher than the foe with uncanny dodge). The foe also takes a –4 penalty on attacks made against the vigilante until the start of the vigilante’s next turn. 


The important thing to notice about startling appearance is that an enemy must be completely unaware of your presence to use it. I don’t know of any official ruling about what happens if you use hide in plain sight, vanish, invisibility, etc. to go back into invisibility or stealth, but I would think that an enemy knows you’re there at that point—they just can’t locate you. Talk to your GM about their interpretation, though.


(11) Frightening Appearance (Ex): Frightening appearance is kind of like a miniature Dazzling Display. It’s fine, but the radius is pretty small, you’re not guaranteed to have built for Intimidate, enemies become immune after you use it once, and it’s a mind-affecting fear effect, which means that a large-ish group of enemies (constructs, oozes, vermin, undead, etc.) will be flat-out immune. A good rule of thumb for vigilante archetypes is that surrendering anything other than social and vigilante talents won’t impact the build much.


At 11th level, whenever a vigilante with this ability makes an attack against a foe that is unaware of the vigilante’s presence (see startling appearance above), the vigilante can, as a free action, attempt an Intimidate check to demoralize the target of his attack and any enemies within 10 feet who can see the attack. This check is attempted before the attack roll against the foe is made and resolved. The vigilante rolls only one Intimidate check and applies the result to all the targets. If the check succeeds against the target of the vigilante’s attack, that foe is also frightened for 1 round, in addition to being shaken as normal, unless the foe succeeds at a Will save (DC = 10 + 1/2 the vigilante’s class level + his Charisma modifier).


Free action, 10-foot radius, and only usable against foes that are totally unaware of your presence. The Will save negates only the frightened condition, not the shaken condition that accompanies a successful use of the Intimidate skill to demoralize a foe.


Once a creature has been the target of this ability (either as the target of the attack or as a nearby creature, regardless of whether or not it was successful), it is immune to that vigilante’s frightening appearance for 24 hours. This effect is in addition to the bonuses gained from the startling appearance ability. This is a mind-affecting fear effect. 


Mind-affecting and fear tags limit your target selection, but them’s the breaks with Intimidate. And since you can’t target the same enemy with frightening appearance multiple times, it doesn’t truly matter whether startling appearance can be triggered multiple times in the same combat.


(17) Stunning Appearance (Ex): The stunned condition is brutal, of course, especially at this high of a level. Any enemy that fails their save will be dead before the status condition ever wears off. On the flip side, nearly every enemy is guaranteed to have more HD than you by this point, which essentially grants a blanket +4 to any enemy you use stunning appearance on. You’re unlikely to get a failed save except on a natural 1 from most enemies.


At 17th level, a vigilante can leave his foes unable to respond when he appears. Whenever a vigilante with this ability makes a successful attack against a foe that is unaware of the vigilante’s presence (see startling appearance), the foe must make a successful Will save (DC = 10 + 1/2 the vigilante’s class level + his Charisma modifier) or be stunned until the end of the vigilante’s next turn. If the creature has more HD than the vigilante’s class level, it receives a +4 circumstance bonus on this save. This ability is in addition to the effects of startling appearance and frightening appearance. Once a creature has been the target of this ability (regardless of whether or not the saving throw is successful), it is immune to that vigilante’s stunning appearance for 24 hours. This is a mind-affecting fear effect. 


(20) Vengeance Strike (Ex): Vengeance strike is fun, and definitely plays into the whole Batman feel of the class. I’d gladly trade it out for Perfect Body, Flawless Mind or Past Your Limits, though.


At 20th level, a vigilante can study a target to strike more viciously. He can spend up to 5 standard actions, which all must be in consecutive rounds. On the following round, he can make a vengeance strike against the studied creature. The target must remain unaware of the vigilante’s presence (or not see him as a threat) until the attack. For each round spent studying the target, the vigilante grants himself one of the following benefits on his vengeance strike: gain a +4 circumstance bonus on the attack roll, deal an additional 3d6 points of precision damage if he hits, or treat the attack roll as if it were 2 higher for the purposes of determining whether the attack hit or threatens a critical hit (to maximum effective roll of 20). Each round of study can be spent in a different way or the same way (they stack with each other), but each round of study must be allocated before the attack roll is made. If the vigilante is an avenger, he automatically confirms any critical hits threatened by his vengeance strike. If the vigilante is a stalker and would normally be able to add a special effect from a vigilante talent with an asterisk (*) to this attack, he can instead add two special effects to his vengeance strike.


Alternate Capstones: But wait, there’s more! Chronicle of Legends introduced alternative capstones that you can take instead of your normal capstone. Each class has one class-exclusive capstone, and then several others that are more general for all characters, all spellcasters, etc. Do note that you can’t select an alternative capstone if your archetype replaces the vengeance strike class feature. Here are my ratings:


* Past Your Limits (Ex): You can’t get more vigilante talents by any means short of this capstone, and you can’t get more social talents by any means short of this capstone or…being a halfling? Favored class bonuses are weird, is the moral of the story. Anyway, being able to grab talents outside of your specialization is a wonderful idea.
* Perfect Body, Flawless Mind (Ex): PBFM is usually a fantastic capstone, and there’s no denying that grabbing a +8 to your Strength or Dexterity scores (whichever is your main combat attribute) will leave you far deadlier than you were previously. You’ll need to weigh the benefit of these stat increases with the benefit of getting more social and vigilante talents with Past Your Limits in order to arrive at a decision that’s best for your character—there’s not really a one-size-fits-all answer.
* With This Sword (Ex): A good runner-up to PBFM, but still inferior to it.
* The Boss (Ex) and Won’t Stay Dead (Ex): More story points than anything else—fitting sunsets for your characters. They won’t have overwhelming effects in combat.
