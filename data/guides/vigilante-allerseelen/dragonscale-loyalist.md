# Dragonscale Loyalist

Dragonscale Loyalist


The Basics


        Pro-monarchy Brevoyan vigilantes who pretend to be nobility. The archetype loses relatively little, but also doesn’t gain much.


        Versatility: -1
        Power: -1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Ruby Courtier (Ex): Alters social talents and replaces 1st-level social talent. Quite beyond the fact that I believe swapping Exotic Weapon Proficiency for a 1st-level social talent is a bad trade, ruby courtier locks you out of some of your most powerful social talents, including the many guises line. Why all the mundane identity hate, Brevoy?


        (3) Reflexive Reaction (Ex): Replaces unshakable. Reflexive reaction is better than unshakable, but frankly, that’s a laughably easy bar to clear. You don’t come out ahead in the action economy at all, since you’re still staggered on the first round of combat, but stalker vigilantes in particular would be able to take advantage of a full-attack while enemies are flat-footed.


        (5) False Allegiance (Ex): Replaces startling appearance. Oh boy, you know how I love class features that are actually seven class features standing on top of each other’s shoulders in a trenchcoat! We’ll get into the nitty gritty below, but the value of the class feature depends on whether you’re a stalker or avenger. Avengers care less about startling appearance than stalkers do, and even then, only about four of the seven possible houses are worth anything.


      * House Lebeda: One of the top houses for dragonscale loyalists. It’s sure as shootin’ not because of Skill Focus: Appraise, either, which is just as poor as it sounds. No, it’s for the extra 10% commission you get on all equipment you sell! Combine four PCs’ wealth by level, add 10% on top, and then tell me that this isn’t worth surrendering startling appearance for. Heck, if you have a crafter buddy, they can craft goods for 50% of their listed cost, and you can then sell them for 60%. Talk about a mercantile wombo combo.
      * House Surtova: Persuasive supplies some sound bonuses to interaction skills. Plus, it’s not like using Sense Motive to discern when someone is lying about being your friend or ally is uncommon. Pretty solid, overall.
      * House Garess: Garess isn’t terrible, but it’s a long way from good, too. Climbing should come up less and less as campaigns progress, making Sure Grasp dubious, and stonecunning (while nice for the automatic roll to notice unusual stonework) is often reliant on a GM remembering to roll it.
      * House Lodovka: Fair is fair: in an aquatic campaign, the Lodovka bonuses would be pretty great, giving you some well-rounded skill bonuses while also allowing you to ignore armor check penalties for most armor while swimming. Everywhere else? Yeah, not so much.
      * House Medvyed: Endurance is widely considered a weak feat used only to clear away the prereqs for Diehard. Resist nature’s lure, likewise, is very weak. Not a good package.
      * House Orlovsky: Call Truce is a bad feat with a ludicrously long “casting” time, a sky-high Diplomacy DC, and a decent chance of failing altogether due to Intelligence restrictions or enemy hostility. CMD bonuses, likewise, are worth almost nothing. Probably the weakest house bonuses of all.
      * House Rogarvia: Knowledge (History) rarely comes up in campaigns, and Diplomacy checks to gather information are pretty niche, too. I’m comfortable calling this red.


        (11) Dragonscale Vigilance (Su): Replaces frightening appearance. Dragonscale vigilance is an NPC ability, if it’s anything, and even then, perceive betrayal is a staggeringly non-specific spell for what it costs. Again, an easy red.


        (17) Conqueror’s Wrath (Ex): Replaces stunning appearance. Conqueror’s wrath is technically more versatile than the mad rush avenger vigilante talent, since you don’t have to charge to use it, but it comes with some hefty attack and damage bonuses that enemies get to leverage against you. Watch your opponents carefully for attacks of opportunity, because if they attack you, chances are excellent that they will hit, and hit hard. Still a great class feature.


Is It Worth It?


        Not truly, no. Conqueror’s wrath is the best argument for the archetype, but unless you’re playing at the very end of a six-book Paizo AP, you’ll never see that class feature. You’re left with a limited repertoire of social talents and some mediocre abilities otherwise. If you’re looking to trade away startling, frightening, and stunning appearance, experimenter is probably the superior archetype.
