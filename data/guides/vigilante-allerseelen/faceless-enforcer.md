# Faceless Enforcer

Faceless Enforcer


The Basics


        Hellknight vigilantes who specialize in the use of heavy armor and helms to conceal their identities.


        Versatility: -1
        Power: +1


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Weapon and Armor Proficiencies: Alters weapon and armor proficiencies. Sure, free heavy armor proficiency. Is there anyone who wouldn’t appreciate this?


        (1) Dual Identity (Ex): Alters dual identity. Here’s the bad news: on its own, with normal armor, trying to keep your identities separate, this version of dual identity is abysmal. Like, truly abysmal. Are you really going to spend 4 minutes donning heavy armor with a friend’s help while they beat you senseless? No, no, a thousand times no. You’ve got two options: first, if you want to keep your identities separate, cheat! And by that, I mean find items, spells, and other effects that let you just skip right past the armor donning rules. Faceless enforcers may not ever be able to pick up the quick change or immediate change social talents, but with the right gear loadout, they can actually change identities as fast as a 13th-level vigilante with immediate change—right at 1st level! (Armored coats. The solution is always armored coats, my friends.) The second option is to ignore your vigilante identity altogether. Who cares if people know that you’re a faceless enforcer? They’re just going to have to deal with it.


      * Armiger’s Armor: Action cost: Standard action, 22500 gp. In my opinion, armiger’s armor is the best unique heavy armor you have available to you: it’s decently cheap, has DR 3/— and the light fortification enchantment baked in, gives the best armor bonus of any item on the list, and can be donned or removed with a single command word issued as a standard action. If you’re not enchanting an armored coat from scratch, this would be my vote for armor in the late books of a Paizo AP.
      * Armored Coat/Erutaki Coat: Action cost: Move action, 50 gp. Armored coats and Erutaki coats have the distinction of being the only mundane armors I know of that ignore armor donning rules. Make friends with these items, because you will be unable to don or remove other armors this swiftly for a very, very long time.
      * Clockwork Armor: Action cost: Full-round action, 33350 gp. Want to be Iron Man? Now you’re Iron Man. Built-in guns, bonuses to Strength and Constitution, and more! Suiting up is slower than with an armored coat, armiger’s armor, or flowing fortress armor, but you may decide that the attribute bonuses and ranged attack options are worth it.
      * Flowing Fortress Armor: Action cost: Move action, 30350 gp. Although flowing fortress armor clocks in as one of the most expensive items on this list, you can don and remove it as a move action from 10 feet away, and the melt and gush options are surprisingly powerful for exploration and dungeon crawls. If you’re looking for a specific set of enchantments not offered by this armor, though, the armored coat is still the superior option.
      * Wand of Serren’s Swift Girding: Action cost: Swift + standard action, 750 gp. While Serren’s swift girding is practically useless in the Pathfinder meta, it’s actually quite good for this archetype, especially in the mid levels when you may want to move away from an armored coat but can’t yet afford unique armors. Basically, you would carry around your armor and a wrist sheath loaded with a wand of this spell, then teleport your armor onto yourself once combat started. The DC 20 Use Magic Device check needed to activate a wand that’s not on your spell list is going to be a constant struggle here, so pump UMD in every way you can and invest in the skill familiarity social talent at 9th level like a good little Pathfinder player. You could also have an ally cast this spell, but that’s a waste of critical actions for a spellcaster at the beginning of combat.
      * Restful Armor: Action cost: none, 4500 gp. Restful armor solves the same problem that a wand of bed of iron does, but better. You only need to sleep 2 hours per night now, and can sleep in heavy armor without penalty. Again, good for nighttime ambushes, but not a solution for daytime encounters.
      * Wand of Bed of Iron: Action cost: Standard action, 750 gp. Bed of iron is a decent spell if you want to sleep in heavy armor, and credit it where credit is due, it will prevent you from having to don anything if you get ambushed in the middle of the night. It does nothing if you’re just in your social identity and get attacked, though, which is why it falls down the ratings quite a bit.
      * Raiment of Chains: Action cost: Move action, 6250 gp. The raiment of chains is a +1 chain shirt that can be donned or removed as a move action, plus some other junk. This gives you a slightly better maximum Dexterity bonus and movement speed than an armored coat, but the cost 125 times higher. You want my advice? Skip it and just upgrade the armored coat.
      * Wand of Guardian Armor: Action cost: Swift + immediate action, 750 gp. I can’t really think how this would work unless you had a very good friend who didn’t need armor and was willing to burn a spell slot to teleport their armor onto you. Like, a warpriest friend with a level in unchained monk: they probably prefer to wear no armor, but they can still clomp around in borrowed armor until initiative gets rolled, at which point they just zip it on over to you. It’s a very clunky solution, and probably not feasible in most circumstances.


        (2) Armored Juggernaut (Ex): Replaces 2nd-level vigilante talent. I was all set to trash armored juggernaut, but after rereading a few vigilante talents, this class feature is actually the armor skin and heavy training vigilante talents smooshed into the space of one vigilante talent. And that’s actually pretty good, especially considering how integral armor is to your overall kit! I wish it had been pushed back a bit to make room for other low-level vigilante talents, but that’s the way the cookie crumbles.


        (5) Faceless Infiltrator (Ex): Replaces 5th-, 11th-, and 17th-level social talents. Again, I was entirely prepared to mercilessly browbeat faceless infiltrator! Much to my surprise, however, it’s another solid ability that essentially grants you all three social talents from the many guises line (many guises, everyman, and any guise) with some helpful tweaks. Many guises specializes in generic, mundane identities that are utterly unremarkable but also lacking any social clout; you don’t gain the ability to assume a form particularly suited to infiltration until any guise comes along at 17th level. While faceless infiltrator doesn’t permit faceless enforcers to change identity nearly as often as many guises (once per month with this class feature, as opposed to once per use of many guises) what it does do is lend much-needed depth and variety to your infiltrations. Your identities, for example, are no longer restricted to common laborers, peasants, and farmers; now you can look like a noble, or a cultist, or a law enforcement officer, or a banker. You have papers—identification cards, visas, written orders from a superior, letters of introduction—to prove you are who you say you are; you get huge bonuses to Disguise and Bluff to maintain the ruse. Many Paizo APs see the PCs infiltrating a specific organization, but it’s rare that you would need to shift an identity rapidly between two organizations. So who cares whether you need to take a month recharge the ability?


        (6) Enforcer’s Wrath (Ex): Replaces 6th-level vigilante talent. I’ve tried to see the good in faceless enforcer, and I’ll admit that there’s more good in the archetype that I suspected at the outset. Enforcer’s wrath, however, is every bit as bad as you would expect. Surrendering a vigilante talent in exchange for a +4 bonus to Intimidate—in only a specific set of circumstances, mind you!—is the poorest of poor trades. Now, without access to many guises, everyman, any guise, quick change, and immediate change, you can probably guess that most of your remaining social talents will go toward the renown social talent line, which also grants Intimidate bonuses. Faceless enforcers should therefore invest in Enforcer, Cornugon Smash, Hurtful, Signature Skill: Intimidate, a cruel weapon, etc. to fully leverage demoralization in their builds. You’ve got to find some way to make hay here.


Is It Worth It?


        There aren’t too many ways to build a heavily armored stalker vigilante, but since faceless enforcer grants heavy armor proficiency, armor skin, and heavy training without surrendering your vigilante specialization, you can definitely make that concept work here. I like faceless infiltrator as an alternative to many guises, and the armor restrictions are actually a lot less onerous than I had anticipated. Remember: armored coats.
