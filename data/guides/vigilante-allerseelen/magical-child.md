# Magical Child

Magical Child


The Basics


        Anime-inspired vigilantes who gain unchained summoner spellcasting, a familiar, and the ability to do Sailor Moon-esque transformation sequences.


        Versatility: +1
        Power: -2


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Class Skills: Alters class skills. The magical child archetype skews much more toward arcane caster than rogue-like—and that’s no trouble, it’s just that you have to get used to it. If you were planning on taking on trap spotting and scouting duties, this ain’t the archetype to do it with; if you were planning to be more of a knowledge jockey, then this is the archetype to do it with.


        (1) Skill Ranks per Level: Well, 4 + Intelligence is obviously worse than 6 + Intelligence, but it’s in keeping with the design decision to make the archetype more like arcane casters, which generally don’t get many skill ranks per level.


        (1) Weapon and Armor Proficiencies: Alters weapon and armor proficiencies. Again, totally par for the arcane caster course. You’ll prioritize CHA > DEX, CON > WIS, INT > STR at point buy and stick it out in light armor.


        (1) Spellcasting: Replaces 4th-, 8th-, 10th-, 14th-, and 16th-level vigilante talents. I’m torn on this one. On the one hand, spellcasting is always good! It’s good for the cabalist, good for the warlock, and good for pretty much any other archetype in the game that can learn to cast spells. On the other hand, the unchained summoner spell list is a pale shadow of the chained summoner spell list, and without an eidolon to cast some of its most powerful spells on, you end up with this strangely mediocre spell selection. (Don’t mistake me: you’ve got invisibility, greater invisibility, see invisibility, fly, haste, heroism, dispel magic, magic circle against alignment, dimension door, stoneskin, dismissal, teleport, true seeing, and a bevy of wall, pit, and fog spells to work with, so even with a limited selection, you’ll still have enough to work with. It’s just that it’s suboptimal, relative to other spellcasting classes. You can refer to my resource The Spell Codex 2.0, sort by Unchained Summoner (A to Z) → Rating (Z to A) → Name (A to Z) to see a well-organized list of how I would rate the entire spell list.) I’ve heard suggestions from other GMs to swap the unchained summoner spell list for the bard spell list, which seems like a sensible suggestion. The bard spell list has the same support flair that I think Paizo was going for, but with a more eidolon-agnostic approach.
Regarding what to do with your three vigilante talents, there isn’t any vigilante talent support for spellcasting, so magical children will need to take perhaps one offensive vigilante talent—lethal grace would be my recommendation—and then focus on defensive and mobility options to ensure you don’t get pinned down and beaten to death. Shadow’s speed, sure-footed, and steely resolve are good on this front.


        (1) Transformation Sequence (Su): Prepare for trouble! And make it double! Transformation sequence is another one of those abilities that makes it almost entirely impossible to keep your vigilante and social identities separate. After all, it’s not an easy task when you’re shooting light from every orifice and “Immigrant Song” is playing in the background. The best remedy for this problem is simply to loudly proclaim, “I’m a magical child!” and let the world know that your social identity is your vigilante identity. Let your familiar be the one who lurks in the shadows, defending justice. You’re just a kid…with magic.


        (1) Animal Guide (Ex): Replaces vigilante specialization. So…animal guide is a really weird ability. Awesome, but weird, weird, weird in terms of how it interacts with normal familiar rules. Things don’t start out weird: you get a familiar with DR/magic equal to your vigilante level, and staunch ally gives it the option to use startling appearance, etc. So it’s a combat familiar, right? Just slap the mauler archetype on there and call it quits. But wait! Your animal guide transforms into a creature from the Improved Familiar list at 3rd level, and some of the best familiar archetypes (valet, mauler, protector, etc.) don’t stack with Improved Familiar, since both options replace the speak with animals of its own kind ability. When your animal guide picks up two more forms at 5th and 7th level and then the ability to shapeshift between any of its four forms at 9th level, you can start to see what a headache this class feature becomes.
        Originally, people just applied different sets of archetypes to the different forms. The base familiar identity kept mauler, protector, or whatever familiar archetype you wanted to give it, and the Improved Familiar forms got either a compatible archetype (sage, e.g.) or else no archetype at all. Then along came Ultimate Wilderness, which quietly sent animal guides into the Big Sleep with the nerfhammer:


Shapechanging Familiars: Familiars that can take various forms, such as the magical child’s animal guide, imps, and quasits, must have the same archetype for each form, and it must be legal for all of those forms (meaning if any form is an improved familiar, it can’t take archetypes that don’t stack with improved familiar).


        So if you take a sage familiar, it always has to be a sage, regardless of current identity; if you take a mauler familiar, it always has to be a mauler—and can’t be, because mauler doesn’t stack with Improved Familiar. Personally, I think Paizo is vastly overrating the power level of familiars, even with archetypes. Especially since the next class feature grants your familiar some level of combat efficacy, you want it to be decent in a scrap. That means mauler is a must. If you want my recommendation, simply ignore the Ultimate Wilderness errata and allow different familiar forms to have different archetypes—the base form gets mauler, another form gets sage, a third gets emissary, etc. I always thought it was lunacy that “improved” familiars couldn’t use most of the archetypes that, ya know, improve familiars. RAW, however, animal guide is a bad ability that deserves a red rating. Gaining a familiar does not compare favorably with losing full BAB, scaling hidden strike dice, or the slew of vigilante talents available to the base specializations.


        (5) Staunch Ally (Ex): Alters startling appearance, frightening appearance, stunning appearance, and vengeance strike. Yeah, see, this is just one more reason why the magical child truly needs a familiar with the mauler archetype. Normally, familiars are pretty bad at combat, barring things like using wands or delivering touch spells, and if you want them to be actually fighting and using all of these class features, they definitely need more power. GMs, just let your players have their multiple familiar archetypes.


Is It Worth It?


        RAW? No. Magical child is an unfortunate errata victim that got done dirty by Paizo when it really didn’t need to be. Swap the spell list for the bard’s and allow multiple familiar archetypes as originally written, and you’ve got a pretty decent archetype, though.
