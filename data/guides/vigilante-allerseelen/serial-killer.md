# Serial Killer

Serial Killer


The Basics


        Well, the name couldn’t be more obvious: serial killers butcher their enemies, plain and simple, gaining features from the slayer class, as well as the assassin prestige class. Obviously evil-aligned.


        Versatility: -1
        Power: +2


        Stacks With: 


The Abilities


        (1) Alignment: Uhhh…yeah, I think an evil alignment makes sense. Although one could argue that most PC adventuring parties are also in the business of murdering for fun, at least they (mostly) don’t harm innocents.


        (1) Vigilante Specialization: Alters vigilante specialization. Fun fact: I’ve heard from deep, dark corners of the Paizo forums that, early in the playtests for the vigilante class, hidden strike counted as sneak attack for everyone, not just for serial killers. I’m actually still in favor of that ruling—hidden strike has nearly no feat or item support, while sneak attack has tons. Anyway, this is pretty great. Flensing Strike alone makes this version of vigilante specialization worth your time.


        (3) Thwart Pursuit (Ex): Replaces unshakable. Both unshakable and thwart pursuit give bonuses to truly irrelevant DCs and checks. Both should leave you with a bad taste in your mouth, so…no harm done?


        (4) Studied Target (Ex): Replaces 4th- and 14th-level vigilante talents and 9th- and 19th-level social talents. Uhhh, since when did we start handing out insane combat bonuses in exchange for social talents, Paizo!? Studied target, even the slightly weakened version you get here, is hugely powerful, and cuts your vigilante talent budget in the low-level build space—where things are always tightest—by only one talent. Studied target improves the DCs of your class abilities, too, which might supercharge abilities such as mighty ambush. Don’t forget that you can apply studied target as an immediate action anytime you deal your hidden strike damage!


        (5) Charming (Ex): Replaces startling appearance. RAW, I’m pretty sure this ability is broken, and not in a good way. The duration of the witch’s charm hex is a number of rounds equal to their Intelligence modifier, yet no provision is made in the ability text for the vigilante’s Charisma modifier. If you can convince your GM to give you this hex with both the save DC and the duration keyed off of Charisma, it can be an excellent ability, helping you make friends in unlikely places, charm your way past guards, or buy a temporary reprieve from combat in order to parlay. Heck, it even works in antimagic fields! As is, however, the duration is broken.


        (6) Death Attack (Ex): Replaces 6th-level vigilante talent. This ability receives a yellow rating because not every enemy will be susceptible to death attacks or paralysis, and because enemy Fortitude saves tend to be exceptionally high in the endgame. There's no guarantee that even after three rounds of study you'll make them fail that save; if you do fail, you've wasted those three rounds, and the enemy is alerted to your presence. It's far easier instead to simply purchase one of the hidden strike vigilante talents such as throat jab or mighty ambush and use those in place of death attack. More or less identical effects, but less fuss.


        (7) Calling Card (Ex): Replaces 7th-level social talent. As bizarre as it seems, calling card is actually quite a good class feature. The defining weakness of the renown social talent line is that it takes an unconscionably long time to establish renown in new areas. Once you can Joker someone to become known and feared, however, it’s a walk in the park (albeit a gruesome walk) to renown’s mechanical bonuses.


        (11) Grisly Murder (Ex): Replaces frightening appearance. Dreadful Carnage comes functional right out of the box, which is lucky because stalkers wouldn’t be taking Power Attack and Furious Focus most of the time. You’ll almost certainly want Signature Skill: Intimidate so that one merciless kill shot will send enemies into a blind panic. Anyway, better than frightening appearance, for my money.


        (12) Quiet Death (Ex): Replaces 12th-level vigilante talent. Even though you take a -5 penalty to your Stealth check when using the silent dispatch vigilante talent, I’d still rather have that than quiet death. Why? Quiet death only functions when you make a death attack, whereas silent dispatch functions regardless of whether you use death attack or simply rely on studied target and hidden strike to put an enemy into the ground. The versatility of silent dispatch outweighs the slight increase in power from quiet death, easily.


        (17) Swift Death (Ex): Replaces stunning appearance. Finally, at 17th level, you can kill people without having to stare at them for 18 seconds! What a time to be alive.


Is It Worth It?


        Throw away the death attack stuff, and I’d say you had a winner—certainly, counting hidden strike as sneak attack and tossing studied target onto the stalker vigilante chassis can only do good things for the class. It’s not that the archetype loses thematic focus after that point, but it does feel like it loses mechanical focus. The biggest barrier to a PC playing one of these is that it’s a laughably evil archetype; as a terrifying NPC antagonist, though? *Mwah.* Perfection.
