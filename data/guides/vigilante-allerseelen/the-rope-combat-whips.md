Strategy


The Rope is a fun little build that I’ve had cooking in the back of my mind for a while. I’m sure there are more efficient ways to do it with several multiclasses—a friend of mine came up with a lore warden fighter 3 / empiricist investigator 3 / hangman vigilante X build—but the builds I post in my guides tend to be simpler so that beginning players don’t have to juggle too much complexity. So, this build is meant to be good at roughly three things: first, dealing hidden strike damage from stealth; second, using the grappling rules to lock down one particularly dangerous opponent—usually a spellcaster—in a combat; and third, upping the Calistrian “sting factor” of the build with a poisoned whip. Of note, this build works best in campaigns against predominantly humanoid opponents due to its reliance on combat maneuvers and poisons. If you take this build into a game full of Huge-sized constructs or undead, you’ll get no sympathy from me!
While the Rope won’t ever deal as much damage as builds like the Murderer or the Reaver, it can still hold its own fairly well. You’ll almost always want to begin combat from invisibility (segueing into Skill Focus: Stealth, animal patron: scorpion, Dampen Presence, and hide in plain sight at higher levels to cut down on spell slots) and hit the squishiest, deadliest enemy (read: spellcasters) before they get the chance to act. You could choose to flurry with unarmed strikes or your hangman’s noose/whip/net, of course, but that’s not primarily what the build does. No, you want to grapple your enemy. Weapon Finesse does in fact allow you to use Dexterity in place of Strength for combat maneuvers if you’re using your weapon to perform the maneuver, and grappling with a whip definitely counts. You’ll open against an unaware opponent with a grapple + Bushwhack + Chokehold, immediately moving them from “free and fully functional” to “pinned and choking” before the surprise round ends. (Against martial enemies, the pinned option for Bushwhack is probably best, but against spellcasters, the verbal component denial portion of Chokehold definitely reigns supreme.) To bolster your CMB score, you’ve got Improved Grapple (+2) and Greater Grapple (+2), and animal patron: scorpion (+6). My back-of-the-napkin math puts your CMB bonus to grapple around +29 (11 BAB + 8 Dexterity + 2 Improved + 2 Greater + 6 animal patron) by 15th level. This isn’t quite as competitive as I’d like against the median CMD of 42 for CR 15 creatures, but barring a few wondrous items, it’s as good as we’re going to get. Like I said, it’s best in campaigns where you’re fighting other Medium-sized creatures. If you try to grapple a storm giant, you’re gonna have a bad time.
Finally, we come to poisons, the most controversial part of this build. This is almost certainly not an optimized poison build—I’d recommend you check out /u/Stargazer5781’s excellent guide The Long Goodbye for that—but it’s good enough for most purposes against humanoid enemies. The linchpin of your poisoned potency is Calistria’s Poisoned Lash, a divine fighting technique that allows you to apply up to three doses of poison to a whip at one time, and deliver contact poisons on successful combat maneuver checks. (Remember, you’re going to be grappling a lot!) Your poisons of choice, which you can choose to craft or purchase, should most likely be quicklime or Count Ambras’ punishment; since both cause the nauseated condition, enemies will simply be unable to take the standard action to attempt either a Strength check or an Escape Artist check to escape the grapple! Yikes. Count Ambras’ punishment has a listed price, indicating that you could potentially buy it to synthesize with the poisoner vigilante talent, but you’d want both Toxicological Timing and its Improved version to eliminate its onset time and change its frequency to 1/round. If you can use quicklime, Daggermark Lore and Treacherous Toxin are better investments. Either way, you’ll stockpile as many of these poisons as possible, then use the poisoner vigilante talent to synthesize new venoms every day, free of charge. You can always burn one of your skill familiarity slots on Craft (Alchemy) if you want to ensure that you can crush your crafting checks without breaking a sweat. Finally, you’ll pick up the signature poison or deadly cocktail rogue talent at 15th level. With a +4 virulent weapon (toxic is also good), deadly cocktail, Daggermark Lore, Treacherous Toxin, and some quicklime, you can easily hit DC 24; add toxic and signature poison, and you’re looking at a DC 28 poison with three consecutive saves to negate that you can synthesize all day. Just sit back, hold the grapple, and drain the life out of them while they can do nothing about it! There’s something very Calistrian about that, right? Don’t forget about all the polymorph and venom milking shenanigans you can get into with a full caster friend who can work some transmutation spells into their lineup! Most animal venoms are injury poisons, but the fact that you can milk so many at a time and use the DC of the polymorph spell as the DC of the poison just steamrolls over any objections you might have. And hey, the poisoner vigilante talent lets you synthesize those, too!

Strategy


Oberyn Martell, GRRM did you dirty. This is the apology build that would have won you that duel against the Mountain. Like the real Red Viper, the Viper build is founded on a simple precept: why hack and hammer away at an enemy when you could present a veneer of weakness, wait for your enemy to make an overly confident attack, and then shiv them with a polearm full of poison? Mechanically, the Viper’s bread and butter is attacks of opportunity. Attacks of opportunity will tend to occur without any “user input” as enemies move into and out of range of a reach weapon, but since we want a more consistent source of damage, we’re going to pair the wonderful Ascetic and Snake style feat lines. The master of many styles monk, a classic in the world of style feats, lets us maintain two styles at once, and even gives us the ability to grab two bonus style feats at 1st and 2nd level without satisfying prerequisites. Snake Fang lets us strike back at enemies when they miss us with attacks of opportunity, while Ascetic Style allows us to use a naginata with the versatile design modification instead of unarmed strikes when we hit back. This is the heart of the build: you’ll voluntarily trigger as many attacks of opportunity as possible in a round, usually by running blindly through the battlefield, taking an attack of opportunity yourself every time an opponent misses you.
Since attacks of opportunity have to miss you in order to trigger your attacks of opportunity, though, and since you need to actually hit with your attacks of opportunity to deal any damage, we need to address a few more components of the build. First, how are you going to get more attacks of opportunity? Taking one per round doesn’t do you much good, so Combat Reflexes at 5th level will bump you up to three; further Dexterity boosts from wondrous items or the vigilante’s reflexes talent at 12th level will continue stacking more attacks of opportunity. Second, how are you going to avoid getting splattered by so many enemy attacks of opportunity? Snake Style takes care of one hit per round, as it allows you to substitute a Sense Motive check for your AC. With 15 (ranks) + 3 (class skill) + 4 (Wisdom) + 6 (Skill Focus) + 2 (Snake Style) + 5 (inquisitor’s monocle) + a guaranteed roll of a 10 (skill familiarity), you can easily clock a 45 AC for one hit per round. (It even works against ranged touch spells, so keep that in mind if you’ve got a spellcaster on the field!) For all other hits, you have Dodge, Mobility, mage armor or bracers of armor, a ring of deflection, an amulet of natural armor, a shield bonus to AC from shield of blades, Wisdom to AC, a good Dexterity score, and the snake animal patron, which will eventually grant a +6 bonus to your AC against attacks of opportunity. Back-of-the-napkin math puts your regular AC around 38-42 vs. attacks of opportunity at 15th level. Third, how are you going to hit? An attack of opportunity-focused playstyle already helps immensely, since you’ll always be attacking at your full BAB, but you’ve still got the Heirloom Weapon trait for a permanent +1 on attacks of opportunity, Weapon Focus, Deceitful Incompetence, and animal patron: snake for about a +8 to hit on top of your normal BAB, Strength score, and weapon enchantments. Not too shabby. Fourth, how are you going to deal damage? With ease, actually. Ascetic Strike already scales base weapon damage with monk levels; add a monk’s robe, and you have a fully scaling naginata that will eventually attain 2d10 base damage. Power Attack is relatively harmless, given that you’ll almost always be attacking at full BAB. The real wombo combo happens with the vital punishment vigilante talent, though, which tags Vital Strike or its older cousins onto an attack of opportunity once per round. With a single size increase, a 15th-level Viper’s naginata will deal about 9d8 + 28 damage on a vital punishment or Improved Vital Strike. A 100% valid strategy is to provoke attacks of opportunity from every enemy you can as you move, provoke a vital punishment from your intended target, then end your movement within range of that target and take one final Vital Strike as a standard action. You can also double move, withdraw, or perform any other movement-based action without losing out on much damage at all. And if you crit with that 19-20/x4 naginata? Well, you’re looking at an average of about 275 damage on an Improved Vital Strike at 15th level. Fifth, you have poison? That’s not a question. You do. Synthesize as much as you like with your poisoner talent, because it’s a proper capstone for the most BAMF that Game of Thrones ever served up. Rest in peace, sweet prince.

Strategy


The Chevalier is not my most subtle build, but in fairness, it doesn’t have to be: mounted combat with a lance can deal sickening amounts of damage, and that’s usually what parties care most about. So, since that’s the main attraction, let’s get into it. The Chevalier’s primary damage source is going to be a lance paired with Spirited Charge for a good portion of their career; lances are ideal for most mounted combat builds because they triple the damage dealt—note that that’s not the damage dice, as in Vital Strike, but the damage itself! This is key for multiplying Power Attack, take ‘em alive, and other static damage sources. Ride-By Attack, Wheeling Charge, and the Lightning Rider trait will also give you fairly unprecedented movement in the early levels, permitting turns, charges through allied creatures’ spaces, and attacks at any point along the charge line. All the damage in the world doesn’t count unless you can reliably reach enemies to unload it, and those two feats help skirt you around areas of difficult terrain, obscuring mist, deeper darkness, and the like. In later levels, you’ll probably want to talk with your resident spellcasters about preparing air walk (perhaps supply a pearl of power so that it’s not detracting from their spell slots) so that you have effective counters against flying enemies, difficult terrain, battlefield control spells like hungry pit, etc.
Levels 7-11 open up a fairly standard demoralization build, with Cornugon Smash to make the initial demoralize check, Hurtful to take a second strike at an enemy that you successfully demoralize, skill familiarity to guarantee a roll of a 10 on every Intimidate check, and finally Signature Skill: Intimidate at 15th level to escalate fear conditions from shaken to frightened or panicked, depending on your degree of success. Mounted furies don’t particularly gain anything against flat-footed enemies, which is why I haven’t built for Shatter Defenses, but it could be an option if you were willing to give up the next component of the build that comes online in the mid-levels: natural attacks. Malleable flesh, seamless shapechanger, morphic weaponry, and Multiattack all line up exceedingly neatly in your 8-11 slots, granting some much-needed damage when you’re forced to full-attack, rather than charge.
If building for full-attacks with natural weapons seems like a strange left turn for a build that I stated at the outset was focused on charging, you’re not wrong—it looks strange. But all the pieces finally come together at 14th level, when mounted furies can finally take the avenger specialization’s mad rush talent. (Remember, mounted furies can select talents as avengers, even though they lose the full BAB of avengers!) Mad rush states that whenever you charge, you can make a full attack. Spirited Charge states that when mounted and using the charge action, you deal double damage with melee weapons and triple damage with lances. Thus, a 15th-level Chevalier will charge an enemy, unloading a minimum of three iterative attacks with a lance, each dealing triple damage on a hit, followed by four natural attacks made at full BAB - 2, each dealing double damage on a hit. All seven of these attacks will receive the +4 charge bonus to hit from your furious charge class feature, which additionally mitigates the AC penalty from charging/mad rush. All seven of these attacks will also receive a doubled critical threat range, thanks to your mighty charge class feature. (The Hurtful attack, I’m sad to report, will not receive any bonuses from charging, since it’s not technically a part of the charge, but rather a separate action.) Add in Power Attack, take ‘em alive, weapon enchantments, and your respectable Strength score, and you’re looking at easily 100+ damage per lance hit and almost 50 damage per morphic weaponry hit. This is, frankly speaking, more damage than a ¾-BAB class should be able to reliably dole out, but there you have it: mad rush is incredible.

Strategy


        One of the archetypal builds that Pathfinder actually struggles to realize is the commando build. You know the type: a Solid Snake-esque warrior who strikes from the shadows, killing instantly and leaving no trace. Various prestige classes like shadowdancer, assassin, and red mantis assassin have attempted to fill the void over the years, but they all suffer from some key vulnerabilities—vulnerabilities that stalker vigilantes can actually address, thanks to the enormous power of vigilante talents. First of all, though, let’s get our theme down: historical ninja were predominantly espionage specialists who had great skill at observing enemies and collecting intelligence for their employers; rather than focusing on being unseen, they often focused on hiding in plain view, disguising themselves as ordinary farmers, laborers, or craftsmen. When it came time for a quick spot of assassination, sabotage, or arson, they didn’t shrink from that, but their first mandate was always to elude capture. That’s what we’re working with in this build, where a staggering number of abilities go toward denying enemies knowledge of your presence, even as you carve a bloody path through them.
        The foundational building block of the Ninja’s combat chassis is the shuriken. Teisatsu have access to ninja tricks, so flurry of stars combined with the usual Point-Blank Shot, Precise Shot, Rapid Shot, Clustered Shots, and Improved Precise Shot feat progression will net you about four un-hasted attacks by 4th level. You can more or less disregard the penalties you’re taking to these attacks—the goal is quantity, not necessarily quality, since you’ll almost always be targeting an enemy’s flat-footed AC and dealing your full hidden strike damage while startling appearance is still in effect. Whenever possible, begin an encounter with a surprise round from Stealth. One hit with a shuriken will trigger throat jab, silencing your enemy temporarily, and your first full-attack action will then silence them permanently. What if you can’t get the drop on an enemy, though? This build has answers. Dampen Presence will negate blindsight and blindsense, two of the most common hard counters to stealthy approaches; hide in plain sight will allow you to use Stealth even while there’s no concealment or cover nearby; a cheap wand of negate aroma (or a custom CL 1 magical item similar to the hat of disguise that grants at-will negate aroma) will counter scent; fly from an ally will counter tremorsense; even true seeing can’t penetrate mundane Stealth. If there’s truly no stealth approach possible, you can approach in disguise, whether as your social identity (seamless guise gets a bunch of bonuses in this build from morphic mask and the human favored class bonus) or a mundane identity from many guises and everyman, or use deception abilities like mockingbird to lure enemies away from chokepoints to dispatch them somewhere quieter.
        Okay, but when it comes to combat, won’t enemies hear you? The Perception DCs to notice the sounds of an ongoing battle are criminally low, right? Well…no. Not in this build, at least. The linchpin to all of this is the silent dispatch vigilante talent, which allows you to substitute your Stealth check result for the Perception DC to notice the sounds of battle. Know what this build really rocks at? Stealth. You’ve got Skill Focus: Stealth, greater shadow armor, and skill familiarity to take 10 on Stealth checks even in the middle of combat. With skill ranks, Skill Focus, and greater shadow armor alone, my napkin math shows a +46 Stealth bonus at 15th level (7 Dexterity + 3 class skill + 6 Skill Focus + 15 ranks + 15 greater shadow); even accounting for the -5 penalty you take on silent dispatch attempts, this build will still hit a Perception DC of 51 while taking 10 on Stealth. In other words, no one will know. Period. Now, you still need to beat an enemy’s initiative check to get the most out of both your hidden strike damage and to have the best chance of killing an enemy before the Perception DC returns to -10, so maybe try to work in Improved Initiative to this build at some point. Expert Sniper would be my recommendation if you’re looking to trim fat.
        Even in the event that everything goes wrong—your shuriken miss, the enemy survives long enough to outlast throat jab, the hue and cry gets raised—you should still be able to deal quite a bit of damage! The 5-rank clause in Signature Skill: Stealth reduces the penalties for sniping by 10, and Expert Sniper reduces that penalty by a further 10. What you’re left with is consequence-free sniping: you can continue to attack once per round while enemies struggle to beat your Perception DC of 56, concealed all the while by hide in plain sight. And lest you think this combo doesn’t work, let me throw some Stealth language at you: “Your Stealth check is opposed by the Perception check of anyone who might notice you. Creatures that fail to beat your Stealth check are not aware of you and treat you as if you had total concealment.” The phrase “not aware of you” is key, because this actually lets you continue to trigger your full hidden strike damage! “Your Stealth immediately ends after you make an attack roll, whether or not the attack is successful (except when sniping as noted below).” So sniping is the exception to Stealth breaking after attacking. We scroll down to the sniping section, and… “If you’ve already successfully used Stealth at least 10 feet from your target, you can make one ranged attack and then immediately use Stealth again. You take a –20 penalty on your Stealth check to maintain your obscured location.” Hide in plain sight takes care of the former problem (can’t make Stealth checks while observed) and your two sniping powers take care of the latter problem (huge penalties to Stealth). Of course, you’d only need to do this while operating completely solo—in most cases, with a team behind you, greater invisibility, etc., you’ll be more than happy simply to full-attack over and over while dealing your reduced hidden strike damage. Either way, a deadly, deadly build, if a little unconventional.

Strategy


First of all, there’s an alternative build for the Throttler that involves taking Unusual Heritage (1st) → Bushwhack (3rd) → Blood Drinker (5th) for true vampiric flavor: you can work your secondary bite attack into a normal full-attack routine, use the fangs racial trait to suck blood when you grapple, and generally keep your enemies nicely drained of Constitution while you stay nicely topped up on temporary HP. However, Blood Drinker has a lot of weaknesses, notably that it applies to only one specific subtype of humanoid (why, Paizo?) so I went for a much more conventional grappling build here. I GM an Ironfang Invasion game in my spare time, though, and I can’t help but think how much fun PCs would have in some books with Blood Drinker applied to hobgoblins.
Anyway, this build is founded on trying to grapple, pin, and ultimately kill enemies as quickly and quietly as possible. The “quiet” part is fairly easy, and will come from various wondrous items, the scorpion animal patron, skill familiarity: Stealth, and the estimable silent dispatch vigilante talent. The “quickly” part comes from a few key feats: Bushwhack lets you pin someone in a single standard action if you’re attacking out of Stealth, or even pin + chokehold once you pick that feat up at 11th level, Greater Grapple and Rapid Grappler give you move and swift action grappling, respectively, and Kraken Style and its various upgrades will start to deal static damage every time you succeed at a grapple check. If you want to just end an opponent, you can always use Bushwhack (standard action to grapple, then free action to pin) → Greater Grapple (move action to maintain the grapple and drag an enemy back into the shadows) → Round 2 Throat Slicer (standard action, attacking with an unarmed strike). (You can’t always rely on Throat Slicer, since enemies that are immune to critical hits are immune to coups de grace.) Because Chokehold prevents an enemy from speaking or calling out and enemies will need to beat your Stealth check to even figure out that combat is happening, it should be quite easy to kill foes without alerting anyone. 
Besides, your grapple bonuses are insane: +2 from Improved Grapple, +2 from Greater Grapple, +6 from the scorpion animal patron, +2 to maintain from the Final Embrace racial trait, +2 to maintain from Kraken Style, plus whatever bonuses you get from wondrous items, full BAB, and a good Strength score. You can even grapple flying creatures with leap and bound! So start chowing down, amigo.

Strategy


Jeez, just looking at that mess of multiclasses gives me a headache. I promise, though, there’s a method to that madness, and that method is to turn you into an unholy flurry of pocket sand and kicks in the testicles. Why pocket sand and kicks in the testicles, you ask? Because it’s a dirty trick build! Although combat maneuvers get a lot of guff, dirty trick is so flexible and so debilitating (when done right) that you can actually turn a vigilante into a very dangerous all-day debuffing machine. Much like with demoralize builds, though, dirty trick builds need some sprucing up before they really hit their stride. Let’s go over what improvements this build needs to thrive:


      * Better numerical bonuses. No surprises here: if you can’t beat an enemy’s CMD, you’re going to get CM-Destroyed. Dirty Fighting, Improved Dirty Trick, Greater Dirty Trick, and the Dirty Trickster trait all add numerical bonuses, but a good portion will have to come from wondrous items. I recommend gauntlets of the skilled maneuver and a burglar’s buckler to start, but the dueling enchantment placed on your kukri will account for most of your bonuses as you level up. A +5 dueling kukri would be ruinously expensive, but grants a +15 bonus (5 enhancement, 10 luck) to your CMB score. You Want That.™
      * Better action economy and more opportunities to dirty trick. Unlike feinting or grappling, dirty tricks never get any feats that outright reduce the action cost of the maneuver. What they do get are plenty of feats that can tag dirty tricks on as free actions or attacks of opportunity! 
      * With Cloak and Dagger Style, you get a free action dirty trick anytime you use an attack action like Vital Strike during a surprise round, plus an attack of opportunity whenever an enemy tries to remove one of your conditions.
      * With Cloak and Dagger Subterfuge, you get to add a dirty trick and a steal maneuver onto every attack of opportunity as a free action.
      * With Cloak and Dagger Tactics, you get a 1/round free action dirty trick when flanking, hitting an enemy denied their Dexterity to AC, or Vital Striking.
      * With Kitsune Style, you can substitute a dirty trick for an attack at the end of a charge. (It is possible to sustain both Cloak and Dagger and Kitsune Style at the same time due to the fuse styles feature of the master of many styles monk.) While charges may not always be your jam, the addition of mad rush at 14th level lets you perform as many dirty tricks as you have iterative attacks on a charge, potentially slapping every status condition known to man on foes.
      * More status conditions. Kitsune Tricks conveniently allows you to inflict two status conditions with a single dirty trick, rather than one. Rejoice! Blinded + sickened is probably the strongest out-of-the-box combo, although deafened or entangled can have places in an anticaster routine.
      * Nastier status conditions. Cloak and Dagger Tactics allows you root enemies in place or cause the exhausted, confused, or staggered conditions when flanking, hitting an enemy denied their Dexterity to AC, or Vital Striking. Given that you can blind enemies fairly easily with dirty tricks, it should be simple to deny Dexterity, but even if not, you have Vital Strike ready to go. Dirty Trick Master also upgrades existing status penalties to dazed, pinned, frightened, or nauseated. Nauseated is particularly mean to toss on enemies: Greater Dirty Trick makes your tricks removable only through a standard action, but nauseated permits you only a single move action every round! Stun-locked. Finally, the turnabout and expose weakness vigilante talents give you additional options for ranged combatants, spellcasters, and creatures with DR. Those can be situationally very useful.
      * Longer-lasting penalties. Base dirty tricks last for 1 round, plus 1 round for every 5 by which you beat the creature’s CMD. Greater Dirty Trick amps this up to 1d4 rounds, plus 1 round for every 5 above CMD. Remember that as enemies take status conditions like entangled, pinned, or exhausted their CMD scores drop, too, giving you a better shot at longer durations on your tricks.
      * Greater action cost to remove penalties. Greater Dirty Trick forces enemies to spend a standard action (instead of a move action) to remove any of your dirty trick conditions, including both conditions imposed by Kitsune Tricks. With the addition of Superior Dirty Trick, however, one of your conditions (it should most likely be blinded, although I can also see an argument for sickened if you’re planning to use Dirty Trick Master extensively to upgrade sickened → nauseated) costs a full-round action to remove. This is the best of all worlds, as you now have multiple conditions (dazed, nauseated, staggered) that make it impossible for targets to pay the full-round action cost to remove your worst condition.


        Of note, I tried to tack on a little crit-fishing to the end of this build with Staggering Critical. Kukris threaten on an 18-20, so it wouldn’t be difficult to get that party rolling with the keen enchantment. Alternatively, you could eliminate Weapon Focus at 1st level by taking the signature weapon vigilante talent at 6th level; that would allow you to shift Kitsune Style and Kitsune Tricks up to 1st and 3rd, subbing in Improved Critical later in your rotation. Many of the Cloak and Dagger feats let you keep attacking while making dirty tricks as free actions, so it’s useful to be able to add yet another layer of debuffing through Staggering Critical.
        As a final note, some enemies are just going to be immune to this style of play. Larger monsters have higher CMD scores, make dirty tricks less effective overall, but enemies like constructs and undead will simply shrug off these status conditions with no ill effects. Conditions like pinned, blinded, and rooted in place will still work, but nauseated, frightened, confused, dazed, etc. will all bounce off ineffectually.

Strategy


        If you’re going to build a bunch of vigilantes, at least one of them has to focus on the twisting fear vigilante talent, right? I mean, c’mon, it’s just too cool to scare a bunch of enemies so badly that their bodies start to break down. Heck, enemies can’t even scream, they’re so frightened of you! In many ways, the Terror build is an offshoot of the Gauntlet build: both focus on Intimidate and demoralization tactics, and both will generally traffic heavily in nonlethal damage through the take ‘em alive vigilante talent and Enforcer feat. Beyond that, though, the two builds diverge: Gauntlet is an avenger, while Terror is a stalker; Gauntlet focuses on beating single enemies into a pulp, while Terror focuses on dealing massive nonlethal AoE damage with repeated uses of Dazzling Display. Let’s get into it.
        At its heart, the Terror build is designed to intimidate, and specifically to intimidate with nonlethal damage. You are not going to be at your most effective against constructs, undead, and other creatures immune to mind-affecting effects or nonlethal damage. Make your peace with this, because it ain’t gonna change. Good? Cool. Let’s talk turkey: Intimidate builds need a few things to survive. First, they need a better action economy than standard actions to demoralize one enemy. Enforcer takes care of single-target demoralization whenever you deal nonlethal damage through take ‘em alive, likely starting around 4th level; Dazzling Display handles AoE demoralization, which you can perform as a full-round action starting at 7th level. Second, Intimimancers need better effects than just the shaken condition. Enforcer already lengthens the duration of shaken by a huge margin, and a cruel scimitar will give you the sickened condition on top of that. Shatter Defenses causes fear-stricken enemies who get hit by your attacks to become flat-footed, allowing you to deal your reduced hidden strike damage with alarming regularity—alarming for the enemies, that is. Signature Skill (Intimidate) ratchets up the severity of fear conditions you can inflict, so long as you can absolutely crush the Intimidate DCs…and this brings us to our third point: Intimimancers need static Intimidate buffs. The Fiery Glare ifrit race trait permits you to take 10 on Intimidate checks, which is great for ensuring consistency; the incredible renown social talent will grant a +8 circumstance bonus to Intimidate; the animal patron (lion) vigilante talent will grant a +8 competence bonus to Intimidate; gravelly tonic is a cheap 50 gp item that grants a +5 alchemical bonus (of all things!) to Intimidate for 1 hour; draconic perfume grants another +2 circumstance bonus; if you really wanted to, you could even take Skill Focus (Intimidate) for more shenanigans. This leaves you with an average Intimidate check of around 55 (Base 10 from Fiery Glare + 15 ranks + 3 class skill + 6 Charisma + 8 renown + 8 animal patron: lion + 5 gravelly tonic) which by 15th level should let you trigger cowering or panicked with absolute confidence against creatures that aren’t outright immune to fear effects.
        Have we gotten to the best part yet? No, we have not. Twisting fear is the best part. We’ve already established that you can absolutely knock Intimidate checks out of the park, leading to terrifying debuffing via Signature Skill (Intimidate). Once per round when you demoralize someone, however, you also deal your reduced hidden strike damage! If it’s only one target that you’re demoralizing with Enforcer, think of this like a free hidden strike every round. If it’s multiple targets that you’re demoralizing with Dazzling Display, well, that’s a different story entirely! Stalkers cap out at 10d4 hidden strike damage, which isn’t what I’d call massive, but that’s still an average of 25 damage per round dealt to every enemy—and where casters can’t maintain that kind of AoE damage output all day, the Terror can. And remember: this is on top of the crowd control that Dazzling Display brings to the table. Every round an enemy spends frightened, panicked, or cowering is a round that they don’t spend murdering your allies, buffing themselves, or calling for friends. You’re obviously going to need to fight the right kind of enemies for this build to work, but in humanoid-centric campaigns, there’s very little that will present more than a speed bump to the Terror. Oh, and one last thing: I envisioned this as a Sarenrae build, I guess? It comes online very late, but Sarenrae’s Divine Fighting Technique lets you heal 2d6 HP once per round when you deal nonlethal damage to an enemy with a scimitar. Take ‘em alive + Enforcer + surprise strike + Shatter Defenses + hidden strike + the equivalent of a cure light wounds every round creates a surprisingly potent and resilient melee striker. Very useful when you don’t need to mow down dozens of enemies with the power of the sympathetic nervous system and can instead focus your tender ministrations on one enemy.

Strategy


What a wacky, wacky build. I feel like I always create at least one Knowledge jockey in every guide of mine, and they’re always shabti, and they always involve the cheesy Charisma- or Wisdom-skill-to-Intelligence traits, and they always take Deific Obedience (Irori or Magdh), but then again…they always rock. There are good ways to build characters and bad ways. I didn’t make these rules; Paizo did. I just follow the breadcrumbs. Anyway, the Scholar build is designed to crush Knowledge checks primarily, with secondary emphases on skirmishing, non-magical healing, poison use, and light crafting. Starting with the secondaries, lethal grace and fantastic stride are about all we need to build a combat-effective Dexterity vigilante; non-magical healing is taken care of by Healer’s Hands, Incredible Healer, Signature Skill (Heal), Pathologist, and Acupuncture Specialist, all of which do excellent things for your party’s HP and longevity in the face of diseases, poisons, and curses without you ever having to mess with caster levels; poison use we get from the poisoner vigilante talent; and crafting comes to us from Brew Potion and Craft Construct, the experimenter’s twin crafting feats. (Keep in mind that these run off of Craft [Alchemy] and Knowledge [Engineering] in the experimenter archetype, rather than Spellcraft, and invest your skill ranks accordingly.) Healing the team, poisoning enemies, crafting potions, and engaging constructs to fight on your behalf are all well and good, especially since this build won’t be abysmal at dealing damage. (It will leave much to be desired, but it won’t be abysmal.) Where it shines, however, is in telling you absolutely everything you need to know about enemies and the world around you. Let’s dive in.
Starting with an empiricist 2 dip is clichéd beyond belief at this point, but dang it all if ceaseless observation and inspiration aren’t amazing class features! It’s difficult to beat +1d6 on most Intelligence-based skill checks for free plus Intelligence to many skills. Perception? Intelligence. Disable Device? Intelligence. Sense Motive and UMD? I’ll give you three guesses, and the first two don’t count. Taking into account inspiration, Irori’s Deific Obedience, and the shamelessly amazing shabti-exclusive Servant in the House of Truth feat, this build should enter 4th level with typical Knowledge checks in the low 30s (13.86 average from Servant in the House of Truth + 4 skill ranks + 3 class skill + 3 Intelligence + 4 Irori Deific Obedience + 3.5 inspiration), with opportunities for even higher checks, depending on the luck of your rolls. Ancestral enlightenment and the lore master bard ability clock in at 7th level, for a grand average of about 42 on Knowledge checks (13.86 average + 7 skill ranks + 3 class skill + 4 Intelligence + 4 Irori DO + 4 ancestral enlightenment + 3.5 inspiration + 2 tears to wine). Social grace can boost your most-needed Knowledge checks, of course, although you may wish to save those for out-of-combat skills like Craft (Alchemy), Knowledge (Engineering), Heal, etc., since you won’t get the bonuses when you identify monsters unless you happen to be in your social identity when you do so. Skill familiarity, while it’s as good as ever, is also not best suited to Knowledge skills, since you can already take 10 as part of the lore master ability and roll twice and take the better result through Servant in the House of Truth. Heal, Perception, Stealth, UMD, etc. are all better uses of that vigilante talent in this instance. Finally, of course, the cognatogen discovery line adds alchemical bonuses to our mental attributes, including Intelligence. With ceaseless observation and our various traits keying so much off of Intelligence, even modest increases in Intelligence result in huge skill check boosts: we end the build at 15th level with an average Knowledge check of about 60 (13.86 average + 15 skill ranks + 3 class skill + 6 Intelligence + 4 Irori DO + 6 animal patron: dolphin + 3 greater cognatogen + 4 ancestral enlightenment + 3.5 inspiration + 2 tears to wine). You’re more than welcome to flex up to a 66 or so with lore master’s ability to take 20, or even variant multiclass with bard to pick up bardic knowledge if you feel that’s really necessary, but I feel like this is more than enough to be getting on with!

Strategy


        I’ll be honest: I think the Shackled is as close to a perfect aid another build as you’re ever going to find. I know, I know, people have their favorite ways to play one, but the vigilante class brings such a lot of other fun tools to the table that I’d be hard-pressed to find a better concept. To begin with, you’re going to be playing as a halfling using the weapon of his oppressors—the slavemaster’s whip—against them. The whip is a conceptually fantastic weapon for a halfling abolitionist to use in a campaign, but it’s also fabulous for aid another builds because it permits you to threaten enemies up to 10 feet away while remaining adjacent to your melee allies. (You’ll remember that the aid another action specifies that you must be in a position to make a melee attack against an opponent, and most of the core of this build dictates that you must also be adjacent to your allies. Park your three biggest bruisers in a line of scrimmage between you and your enemy, and the rest will become clear.) While the start of the build is a bit rocky due to feat and vigilante talent crunches, it has huge parabolic inflection points at 6th and 9th level that make it a great pick for a mid-campaign backup character. I feel like the easiest way to break down the build is in terms of which actions it’s going to use every round, so let’s start with that and see whether any questions remain at the end, shall we?
First, your standard action. This is going to be used for one of two things: at the start of a combat from 9th level onward, your standard action will be used in conjunction with tend the garden to share Harrying Partners with your bellflower crop. This is not negotiable. Harrying Partners is perhaps the single most important feat in this build, and you positively must have it running on any melee ally you plan to support with aid another actions. Before that point, however, and after the initial tend the garden action from 9th level onward, you’ll be using the team player vigilante talent to perform the aid another action on all adjacent allies. While using Swift Aid (even with the support of team player) would normally grant only a +1 bonus, the crop vigilance class feature simply replaces the scaling of all aid another attempts, giving you a flat +4 by the time 6th level unlocks most of the build’s potential. You will use this standard action to boost attack rolls only! Aid another checks to boost AC will be handled by the Bodyguard feat.
Second, your move action. The Shackled has almost no incentive to attack enemies himself, so it makes no sense in this build to focus on anything that will improve the damage output of your full-attack actions. Helping others maximize their DPR, however, is exactly on point for this build, and that’s where the vigilante talent cunning feint comes in at 10th level. By this point, the 8th-level upgrade to cunning feint will have taken effect, so any successful feint attempt will result in the enemy losing their Dexterity bonus to AC against all attacks until the vigilante’s next turn. In addition to layering on yet more obscene bonuses to hit enemies, this is the ability that will make allied rogues and slayers adore you, since they often need to waste precious actions flanking, feinting, blinding with dirty tricks, casting vanish, etc. before they’re ready to deal sneak attack damage. With you around, it just kinda happens. Bluff should obviously be one of your 9th-level skill familiarity choices, both for its synergy with your obsequious class feature and its crossover hit status with feinting.
Third, your attacks of opportunity. This is an easy one: Bodyguard, Bodyguard, and oh look, Mr. Frodo, more Bodyguard! Bodyguard is the reason why you can improve your team’s offense and defense at the same time. Standard actions with team player will have attack rolls more than covered—to the tune of about a +13 bonus to all attacks by 17th level—but that still leaves AC untouched. Bodyguard patches that pothole by allowing you to use attacks of opportunity to reactively aid an ally’s AC. Between Combat Reflexes and vigilante reflexes, this build should comfortably be sitting on about 12 attacks of opportunity per turn at 20th level, and with the full-turn coverage of Harrying Partners, you can afford to protect three allies from four enemies, two allies from six enemies, or any other combination you can think of. (Remember, aiding another is specific both to the ally being aided and the enemy being aided against. The credits don’t transfer.) Even if AC is one of the lesser defense metrics as time rolls on, no enemy is ever going to be able to laugh off a +12 AC bonus to each one of your melee allies, especially if those same allies are hitting like freaking trucks the whole time. And vastly improved AC means fewer on-hit rider effects: fewer saves against negative levels, poisons, curses, grapples with the grab universal monster ability, and more—and that’s worth the price of admission all on its own.
Fourth and finally, your immediate actions. I’ve reserved a late-game feat slot for In Harm’s Way, a nice “sacrificial” power that keeps your allies healthy at your own expense. While I don’t normally advocate for this kind of ability, it’s powerful in this build because it continues to extend the runway that your sturdier melee colleagues have before they start to get worn out. Even if an enemy survives long enough to attack, and even if they punch through your huge AC bonuses from Bodyguard, you’re there to suck up one successful hit per round. What’s more, you have the ability to heal yourself without sacrificing any of your defensive tools! Healer’s Hands + Incredible Healer + Signature Skill (Heal) have enormous non-magical healing potential, especially when paired with your guaranteed take-10 mechanic from skill familiarity. If you tank enough hits that you start to flag, you can take a turn off from buffing attack rolls, use a full-round action to dump a bunch of HP in your pool, and continue bolstering AC with attacks of opportunity as normal.
With Bodyguard, vigilante’s reflexes, team player, and whip of vengeance, this build has everything it needs to provide a large number of melee allies both attack and AC bonuses of about 12-13 by the end of a PC’s career: 6 (crop vigilance) + 5 (benevolent weapon and armor) + 1 (ring of tactical precision) + 1 (Battlefield Disciple trait, for attack rolls only). Thanks to Harrying Partners and tend the garden, these bonuses extend for an entire round, not simply the single attack that the base aid another ability provides. This build can also provide skill bonuses of +7 in addition to being able to heal allies non-magically and act as an incredible infiltrator and party face. Best of all, to quote Captain America, he can do this all day. Aid another is not a finite resource, and with the exception of healing limitations from Healer’s Hands, this build has nothing to expend. Attack rolls, AC, skills, HP, feinting—I’m honestly worried that this build is too strong. Save-based effects and non-melee attacks are still problematic, but find me a team that doesn’t struggle at least a little with high-level spellcasters anyway and I’ll eat my own hat.
