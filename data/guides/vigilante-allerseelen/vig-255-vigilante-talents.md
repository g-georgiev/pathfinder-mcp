# VIG 255: Vigilante Talents

VIG 255: Vigilante Talents


Vigilante talents are unquestionably why this class has such a reputation for power and versatility. They often function as thematic “bundles” of abilities, perhaps granting multiple feats, poaching limited version of features from other classes, or even granting benefits that are found nowhere else in Pathfinder. Often one talent is all you need to become good at a particular theme, which is what makes them so appealing. Want to build a Weapon Finesse build? Lethal grace is all you need. Want to be good at feinting? Cunning feint. Nonlethal damage? Take ‘em alive. They’re dead simple to use, modular enough to complement or supplant feats, and powerful enough to make even 1e veterans perk up. One important thing to note is that there is no way to get more vigilante talents. No Extra Vigilante Talent feat, no favored class bonus that grants them every 6 levels, no training weapon enchantment shenanigans, no wondrous items—nothing. You get what you get at the levels the vigilante class says you get them. Choose even more wisely here than you do for social talents, because what you choose will define what your vigilante is capable of.




Universal


Offense


(2) Lethal Grace (Ex): Lethal grace opens up so, so many interesting build options for Dexterity-based vigilantes. Unarmed strike and natural attack builds, Two-Weapon Fighting builds, run-of-the-mill Weapon Finesse builds…all will benefit considerably from adding this much damage to each attack. And you even get to buy back Weapon Finesse, if you took it at an earlier level! Isn’t that a daisy?


(5) Animal Patron (Ex): Minor shifter aspects are essentially juiced-up animal foci from the hunter class, and offer everything from scaling skill or attribute bonuses to free feats. Only you will know exactly what’s right for your build, but consider the attribute-boosting aspects (Bull for Strength, Tiger for Dexterity, Bear for Constitution, Peafowl for Charisma), combat maneuver-boosting aspects (Crocodile or Scorpion for grapple, Elephant for bull rush and overrun), skill-boosting aspects (Dolphin for Knowledge, Falcon for Perception, Lion for Intimidate, Lizard for Acrobatics, Owl for Stealth), save-boosting aspects (Giant Wasp for mind-affecting effects), attack-boosting aspects (Snake for attack of opportunity builds), and initiative-boosting aspects (Deinonychus) very strongly.
(2) Morphic Weaponry (Ex): Requires shapechanger subtype. Morphic weaponry is one of the premiere vigilante talents for natural attack builds, as it eventually grants you four primary natural attacks that can all be enhanced with Strength bonuses or the lethal grace vigilante talent. All vigilantes can gain the shapechanger subtype by taking the malleable flesh vigilante talent.
(2) Take ‘Em Alive (Ex): This talent is notable for two reasons. First, nonlethal damage is just as effective as lethal damage in a wide variety of scenarios, and jeezy pete, those are some big attack and damage bonuses. Second, nonlethal damage is the necessary tie-in to the Enforcer feat, which is, bar none, the most effective single-target demoralization tool in Pathfinder. If you’re intending to use Intimidate heavily in melee range, you should absolutely consider getting this vigilante talent. One final note: I don’t know what to make of the scaling. I looked up the text in The Spymaster’s Handbook, and it does indeed say “starting at 5th level, and every 3 levels thereafter,” which doesn’t make a lot of sense. If they meant every 5 levels thereafter, it would cap out at 20th, like Paizo wrote; if they meant every 3 levels, it caps out at 14th level. The latter is obviously more powerful than the former.


(2) Strike the Unseen (Ex): This is a prime example of how vigilante talents are, in fact, often three or four feats standing on each other’s shoulders in a trench coat, asking to be admitted to the R-rated movie. Let’s see: we’ve got Blind-Fight, Improved Blind-Fight, Greater Blind-Fight, and even a feat that doesn’t technically exist in Pathfinder (let’s call it “Improved Shadow Strike”) that allows you to deal precision damage to targets with total concealment. Avenger vigilantes can benefit from the first three feats as much as any melee character, of course, but stalker vigilantes are the intended audience, since they need to be able to deal hidden strike damage to enemies that are invisible, under the effects of displacement, concealed by supernatural darkness, etc.
(2) Surprise Strike (Ex): On balance, I think that surprise strike really hits its stride with stalker vigilantes. Not only do stalkers have a harder time hitting foes than avengers thanks to their ¾ BAB progression, they’re also way more incentivized to deny enemies their Dexterity to AC so that they can deal their reduced hidden strike damage. If you plan to attack from invisibility or greater invisibility, build toward Shatter Defenses, use dirty tricks to blind foes, feint, or employ any other tactics that would leave an enemy without a Dexterity bonus to their AC, then surprise strike is for you.
(2) Vigilante’s Reflexes (Ex): Vigilante’s reflexes is a lot of fun, and quite powerful in the right hands. Whose hands? Why, Strength-based avenger vigilante builds using reach weapons, naturally. You get to keep your Dexterity low, wear heavy armor, and still get to take a bunch of attacks of opportunity every round with your longspear, glaive, ranseur, etc. Dexterity-based vigilantes will likely want to skip vigilante’s reflexes and instead just purchase Combat Reflexes—the extra attacks of opportunity from this talent are overkill when you’re already adding four to seven attacks of opportunity per round with the feat, and vigilante talents are 450% more valuable than feats. That’s just science, baby.
(6) Vital Punishment (Ex): Vital Strike as a bonus feat, plus the ability to add Vital Strike to one attack of opportunity per round, in the same vein as Diabolic Judgment or the advanced benefit of Torag’s Divine Fighting Technique. It’s good stuff. Full-attack actions are never guaranteed in Pathfinder.


(8) Aquatic Action (Ex): Mega-useful in aquatic campaigns like Ruins of Azlant, but pretty much nowhere else.
(8) Perfect Vulnerability (Ex): Hmmm. Perfect vulnerability guarantees that you’ll hit any enemy—touch AC without a Dexterity bonus is effectively AC 10, if not lower—but the fact that you can do it to an enemy only once per day makes it a bit limited. I’d sooner just pump my attack rolls and deal with things that way.
(2) Poisoner (Ex): Poisoner solves one of the main problems of any poison build—namely, how do I get my hands on more poisons without having to buy or craft them? So now your poisons pay out a 20% dividend daily! How’s that for yield farming? All of poison’s usual weaknesses—widespread immunities, low save DCs matched against enemies’ strong Fortitude saves, etc.—apply, but this is a step in the right direction.
(2) Returning Weapon (Ex): Returning weapon will be absolutely indispensable for thrown weapon builds, and pretty lousy for everyone else.
(2) Shield of Fury (Ex): Absolutely de rigeur for avenger vigilantes going with sword-and-board builds. One of the subtlest—but most interesting—parts of shield of fury is that it satisfies the Dexterity prerequisite for any feat that lists Two-Weapon Fighting as a requirement. This change single-handedly opens up heavily armored sword-and-board builds where Dexterity is a dump stat and Strength is king. Lovely not to have to split point buy.
(14) Signature Arrows (Ex): In essence, a 50% discount on magical ammunition for ranged builds. It comes online so late that I’m inclined to think it won’t be of much use.
(2) Silent Dispatch (Ex): Under normal combat circumstances, the Perception DC to notice the sounds of battle is -10, which is so low that most enemies will beat it even if they’re far away and freaking asleep. Silent dispatch simply gives you the opportunity to roll a Stealth check for it at a -5 penalty; whatever you get, that’s the Perception DC. With Skill Focus (Stealth), shadow armor, skill familiarity to take 10, etc., stalker vigilantes really can start to murder enemies without anyone noticing, especially if they have greater invisibility up and running. A good way to continue reaping the benefits of hidden strike.
(2) Tiger’s Claws (Ex): The malleable flesh + morphic weaponry combo will grant more natural attacks than tiger’s claws in the long run, but also requires more investment than tiger’s claws unless you already have the shapechanger subtype from your race. The phrasing in the morphic weaponry rules text suggests to me that the two powers won’t stack, so you’ll have to consider which route you want to take.
(2) Turnabout (Ex): I really love turnabout, flavor-wise, but it’s hard to parlay into combat effectiveness as a vigilante. Truthfully, what you want is a fighter chassis—Step Up, Following Step, Step Up and Strike, Disruptive, and Spellbreaker being the key feats, along with any dirty trick feats you need—that allows you to follow ranged combatants and spellcasters, impede defensive casting, and take an attack of opportunity whenever your target fails to cast defensively. It’s a powerful ability, and not one available anywhere else in the game, but it seems to be more fitting for a magehunting fighter dipping vigilante. Even with the combat skill vigilante talent, single-class vigilantes can’t qualify for Spellbreaker until 20th level, and I think you’d probably want to get the build online a bit faster than that.
(2) Unexpected Strike (Ex): Quick Draw isn’t really an essential feat in most builds. Unexpected strike is fine, but it can’t compete in the same tier as other vigilante talents like lethal grace or take ‘em alive.
(2) Volatile Arrows (Ex): Call me crazy for taking a pass on alchemist bombs, but I don’t like volatile arrows that much. Although it’s fun to see ranged combat styles get some love, vigilantes are not intended to be built with high Intelligence modifiers, and the number of vigilante talents you get doesn’t allow you to invest much in extra iterations of volatile arrows. If you want my opinion, you should just build a grenadier alchemist if you’re seriously considering volatile arrows as your main mode of attack.
(2) Whip of Vengeance (Ex): It will surprise no one that whip of vengeance is obligatory for whip builds, which can be built pretty easily on a vigilante, especially with the hangman archetype. The final benefit, using your vigilante level instead of your BAB to qualify for whip feats, is clearly intended for stalker vigilantes—and it’s a nice touch.


(2) Environmental Weapon (Ex): Improvised weapon builds are certainly a thing, but they tend to fall into “intentional improvised weapon builds” that utilize Shikigami Style to deal big damage or “unintentional improvised weapon builds” that use feats like Chairbreaker to continually grab and smash weapons from the environment. I honestly don’t see that environmental weapon is of great benefit even to this latter style of build. Just get Catch Off-Guard and call it quits.




Defense


(2) Shield of Blades (Ex): Shield of blades is best used by avenger vigilantes wielding two-handed weapons. Not only do they receive the greatest damage bonuses from Power Attack, they’re also the most likely to need the extra AC that they’re passing up by not using a shield. The scaling is great, giving you a full +5 AC by 20th level, and doesn’t require you to do any more than you would already have been doing. Note that you can in fact use Furious Focus with shield of blades, but that you’ll have to wait until BAB +6 and your first iterative attack to do so.
(2) Steely Resolve (Ex): Frankly speaking, companion to the lonely is more mechanically powerful than steely resolve, and, critically, doesn’t require a vigilante talent slot. Where steely resolve has an edge, however, is that it doesn’t require you to engage in…*hem,* consensual activities with anyone to receive the bonuses. Mind-affecting effects, it goes without saying, are the worst of the worst: charm person, fear, geas, feeblemind, dominate person, synaptic pulse, color spray, mindwipe, song of discord, confusion, oneiric horror, phantasmal killer, you name it. You absolutely want to have every tool at your disposal available to avoid them.


(2) Armor Skin (Ex): I quite like armor skin—it’s like a fighter’s armor training class feature, but better for a vigilante’s purposes. Acrobatics, Escape Artist, and Stealth are perpetually useful skills for escaping grapples, avoiding attacks of opportunity, moving quietly ahead of the group, etc., so you want bonuses wherever you can get them instead of penalties. Avenger vigilantes get even more bonuses with the heavy training and steel soldier vigilante talents.
(2) Mockery (Ex): Antagonize is one of the only feats in Pathfinder that allows heavily defended PCs to truly “tank” in the MMORPG sense of the word. Antagonize, however, is limited by both its duration and the number of times per day that you can use it on a creature. Mockery solves both of these problems. If you’re a heavily armored avenger vigilante who wants to keep their team safe, you can’t do better than mockery.


(2) Another Day (Ex): In general, you shouldn’t be purchasing abilities that rely on unconsciousness to work. More than that, though, appearing to be dead will confuse your team more than it will confuse your enemies. If your team prevails, they need to waste time determining whether you’re really dead or not, and if your team all falls, you walk away with intense survivor’s guilt—assuming that the monster doesn’t just eat you and really make you dead.




Mobility


(2) Close the Gap (Ex): Monsters with huge reach are a fact of life in high-level Pathfinder combat, and melee-focused PCs typically lack the ability to dance in under the guard to deal damage. No longer! Close the gap makes attacks of opportunity far, far less common. You Want That.™ Plus, eliminating the penalty for charging is pretty perky, too. Maybe consider some charge-based style feats, or the mad rush avenger vigilante talent?
(6) Fantastic Stride (Ex): Spring Attack has always been a good feat, but most people are put off (justifiably so) by the oodles of prerequisites that it has. With those gone, it’s actually okay!
(2) Sure-Footed (Ex): The initial benefits of sure-footed are certainly nice, especially for stalker vigilantes, but what really clinches the talent’s blue status is the 8th-level upgrade. No more difficult terrain, whether magical or natural? Huge score.


(10) Leap and Bound (Ex): Haha, that’s a lot of fun. Between armor skin, leap and bound, and possibly heavy training, even heavily armored vigilantes can start parkouring around the battlefield like they’re in the opening of Casino Royale. The bit about being able to grapple flying creatures is particularly interesting for grapple builds, who may want to investigate the talent as a means of snagging flyers and bringing them back down to earth.
(5) Monkey’s Paws (Ex): Requires rooftop infiltrator. Whether anyone will actually spend two vigilante talents to acquire a climb speed is doubtful, but that’s not to say that monkey’s paws is a bad ability—not at all. Bonuses to Escape Artist checks are always relevant, too, especially for ¾-BAB stalker vigilantes.
(2) Shadow’s Speed (Ex): Increases to base speed improve your Acrobatics checks to jump, and stack with just about any other kind of movement speed increase—longstrider, slipstream, haste, and the like. I certainly wouldn’t kick shadow’s speed out of bed for eating crackers.


(2) Rooftop Infiltrator (Ex): Even a slow climbing speed is better than none at all. Rooftop infiltrator will double the speed at which you can climb unaided and quadruple the speed at which you can climb a rope, not to mention that the Climb skill bonuses will ensure that you can almost never fall.




Combat Maneuvers


(2) Expose Weaknesses (Ex): This talent is a really nice addition to a dirty trick build. Although the ideal is that every member of a team has tools for dealing with DR of all types, the reality will often be that only one person—or no one—does. Expose weaknesses therefore acts as a force multiplier for the team in a way that simple status conditions don’t. You could probably save it for 8th or 10th level, though, as DR isn’t prevalent before that point.


(2) Cunning Feint (Ex): Cunning feint is essentially Improved Feint and Greater Feint packaged as one vigilante talent, plus a little bit of Two-Weapon Feint. The option to feint as part of a full-attack is particularly nice, as it ensures that high-level avenger vigilantes can get two or three attacks off after an opponent has already been denied their Dexterity bonus to AC. Pairs nicely with the vital punishment vigilante talent so that you can feint as a move action and then hit someone with a standard action Vital Strike.
(2) Gator Wrangle (Ex): Gator wrangle is part defense, part offense, part combat maneuver, but when you put all the parts together, it’s actually not bad! Grapples are most characters’ worst nightmare, so getting huge bonuses to avoid grapples, reduce common damage from grapples, and punish those who grapple you will ensure that your grappled experience is much nicer. Heck, you might even try to get grappled, if it keeps a creature from grappling squishier allies.
(2) Living Shield (Ex): Because you lose your grapple-specific CMB bonuses, living shield won’t always work the way you want it to, even for very specialized grapplers. When it does, though, it’s hilarious, both saving you some damage and forcing your enemy to eat that same damage.
(2) Shackle Smash (Ex): You’d be surprised at how relevant shackle smash really is—not necessarily for its in-combat applications with the sunder maneuver, unless you’re into that kind of thing, but for its applications in smashing down doors, walls, floors, and any other obstacles you might need to get through. Quiet and efficient are the watchwords here.


(2) Brutal Maneuver (Ex): I won’t say that combat maneuvers are totally useless options in Pathfinder, only that they’re often heavily outclassed by other available options. Brutal maneuver might boost your damage a little, but the penalty to attack rolls is simply not worth it in many cases. Probably viable only for avenger vigilantes, thanks to their higher BAB.
(6) Concealed Strike (Ex): Intended to be a “curse your sudden but inevitable betrayal!” talent that lets you backstab unsuspecting foes before they can react. Hard to see why a feint would be useful here—if you’re getting a surprise round, you’ve already made the enemy flat-footed.
(2) Deceitful Trick (Ex): Deceitful trick does the same basic thing for the dirty trick maneuver that cunning feint does for the feint maneuver, but where cunning feint gives you the Improved and Greater versions of its combat maneuver for free, deceitful trick does not. Still nice to be able to inflict two status conditions and perform a combat maneuver as part of a full-attack action.
(2) Favored Maneuver (Ex): Repeat the refrain with me: vigilante talents are worth more than feats. You want an Improved [Maneuver] feat, my recommendation is that you pick it up in your free time, not with vigilante talents. If, in spite of my recommendation, you would still like to use a vigilante talent, the option is here.
(2) Pull into the Shadows (Ex): AKA the Batman Begins move. It suits stalker vigilantes thematically, but mechanically fits only avenger vigilantes. Hard to get excited when only totally unaware enemies can be affected.


(2) Combat Expertise (Ex): Always, always remember this: vigilante talents are worth way more than feats. If they’re doing the same thing as a feat, they’re not worth your time. Combat Expertise in particular is a heinous feat tax that no one should spend a vigilante talent on. Heck, I typically play with Elephant in the Room feat tax rules at my tables, and that just gives everyone Combat Expertise for free. Perhaps the one exception here is for bellflower harvesters, who need Intelligence 13 and Combat Expertise to qualify for Swift Aid, Team Up, Open Up, and Got Your Back.
(2) Vortex Splash (Ex): Being able to feint or dirty trick every adjacent target is a great benefit, but you can’t possibly expect to be fighting in rain or waist-deep water regularly enough to merit a vigilante talent.




Spellcasting


(2) Magical Familiarity (Sp): Ugh, magical familiarity might as well be a rogue talent, it’s so bad. If you want cantrips, buy something like a hand of the mage. Don’t waste your precious, non-refundable vigilante talents.
(4) Major Magic (Sp): A bad upgrade to a bad vigilante talent, major magic has nothing to offer players that couldn’t be better accomplished by the vigilante class’ many spellcasting archetypes.
(2) Minor Magic (Sp): Somehow even worse than magical familiarity. And you thought it couldn’t be done!




Utility


(2) Malleable Flesh (Su): Malleable flesh is wacky, weird, and I love it. In addition to getting an at-will, non-magical disguise self, malleable flesh lets you squeeze through extremely narrow openings and opens up the morphic weaponry and seamless shapechanger vigilante talents. There’s really a lot to like here, despite the minor loss of seamless guise bonuses.


(2) Inspired Vigilante (Ex): Without investigator talents or the ability to use inspiration for free on certain skill checks, there’s a certain je ne sais quoi missing from inspired vigilante. It’s not terrible, just not nearly as useful as the investigator class feature.
(2) One with the Wild (Ex): Requires environmental weapon vigilante talent. The prereq is kind of a stinker, and only gives you a limited form of the ranger’s favored terrain. I dunno. I like the skills getting bonuses, but competence bonuses are common, especially for skill checks, and can be easily duplicated with cheap wondrous items.
(2) Racial Paragon (Ex): I’m going to rate this talent yellow, since its utility will vary wildly depending on which race your vigilante is, which racial feats are available to that race, and which situations those feats are useful in. For some races, getting the equivalent of the brawler’s martial flexibility class feature for racial feats could be extremely powerful; for other races, it could be laughably weak.
(2) Shadow’s Sight (Ex): If you already have darkvision, you can probably skip shadow’s sight. If you don’t have darkvision, though, you’d normally have to pay 12,000 gp and take away your eye slot with goggles of night in order to get it. I can think of much worse things to spend a vigilante talent on.
(2) Team Player (Ex): Swift Aid isn’t a particularly powerful feat, but bellflower harvesters can take advantage of the archetype’s bonuses to become superb at aid another actions.


(2) Chase Master (Ex): Please name for me the last time you were in a chase. I’ll wait.
(2) Instant Plan (Ex): Eh. The morale bonuses are easily superseded by heroism, and they don’t apply to really critical checks even when you don’t have duplicate morale bonuses floating around.
(2) Perfect Fall (Ex): Boots of the cat cost 1,000 gp and do a better job of preventing lethal fall damage than this talent does. And anyway, it’s not like flight is uncommon once you get past about 7th level.
(2) Swamp Concoctions (Ex): Only 2/day, and with a bad prereq. Yawn.
