﻿Owl’s Guide to Witches
The witch is an intelligence-based, prepared arcane caster with a familiar in place of a wizard’s spellbook. The witch’s spell list is primarily a subset of the wizard’s with a handful of cleric & druid spells mixed in.


The witch gains hexes, which are roughly spell-like abilities, in addition to their spells. These hexes are broadly the defining feature of the class.

Roles
Control
Any witch can be capable at Control. You have a measure of battlefield control (web, sleet storm, black tentacles), but lack the wall and pit spells of a wizard/sorcerer. Once you combine those with your other area effect spells (euphoric cloud, glitterdust, confusion) you can wildly shift the balance of an encounter, while targeting individual threats with your hexes.
Healing
Some build considerations are needed to be your best at healing, notably the Healing patron. Other than cure light wounds, you gain healing later than clerics/oracles. You do have key condition removal spells in delay poison, remove blindness, remove disease, and with the Healing patron you gain lesser restoration. The Herb Witch adds general purpose condition removal remedies, or the Hedge Witch adds spontaneous healing. At low levels the healing hex is great, but should be retrained once the party can afford a wand of cure light wounds.
Buffing
A witch must specifically choose her hexes to be effective at buffing. While you have a few good spells (heroism, etc) for buffing, the key here lies in a few hexes:
* Protective Luck, Soothsayer (and Cackle) – Protective Luck is not limited to 1/day/target, and Soothsayer allows it to be applied in advance.
* Fortune – Take Fortune after the above suite. A weapon with a high crit range (18-20), the feat improved critical, and the Fortune hex will get a critical hit on half of its attacks. With Soothsayer to apply it in advance, and Cackle to sustain it through an encounter, this is a massive buff.
Debuffing
The core, best known witch hexes are built for debuffing. This is the role where the witch exceeds any other class. Importantly, hexes are a supernatural ability, and so they ignore spell resistance. Note that the area-of-effect spells mentioned above under Control overlap heavily with debuffing.
* Slumber can end encounters
* Misfortune is a solid Plan B vs constructs, etc.
* Swine is brutal past 8th level
* Split Hex simply doubles the power of each of the above.
* Ice Tomb adds a fort save, though a major hex does not work with Split Hex.
Blasting
You must make build decisions to be a competent blaster. This is certainly not a core strength of the witch, as it is for a sorcerer. But the Winter Witch is a competent blaster, and simply taking the Elements patron adds key spells (notably fireball) to your list.
Races
Most races have the same favored class bonus: 1 spell below your highest level. This can be compelling a few times in the mid to upper levels. I will only note other favored class bonuses, rather than listing this one repeatedly.
Elf
You have good stats (+2 int & dex, -2 con) and tremendous racial traits. 
Standard Traits
* Elven immunities: immune to sleep spells, +2 vs enchantments
* Keen senses: +2 perception
* Elven magic: +2 caster levels vs spell resistance, +2 spellcraft to identify magic items
* Weapon familiarity: bows, longswords, etc. Trade this away.
* Low light vision
Alternates
* Blended view: Dark vision, replaces keen senses.
* Dreamspeaker: +1 DC for sleep effects (e.g. your slumber hex), replaces elven immunities. 
* Fey thoughts: Gain 2 class skills, replaces weapon familiarity. Take perception and one other.
* Fleet footed: Gain run as a class skill, +2 initiative as a racial bonus. Because this is a “racial bonus” to initiative it stacks with traits such as reactionary that grant a “trait bonus”. Replaces weapon familiarity.
* Keeper of Secrets: Gain +1 on enchantments vs humanoids and +2 on bluff & linguistics. Replaces elven magic. Many witch spells are enchantments, will many of your foes by humanoids?
* Overwhelming magic: Gain the feat spell focus, replaces weapon familiarity.
Half Elf
Good stats (+2 int) and solid racial traits. Significantly, only half elves can learn the 3rd level spell paragon surge, giving you access to all of the situational hexes below. The reason half elf is top-tier is the spell paragon surge. It’s that good.
Standard Traits
* Elven immunities: immune to sleep spells, +2 vs enchantments
* Adaptability: Gain the feat skill focus. You’ll trade this away.
* Keen senses: +2 perception
* Low light vision
* Multitalented: Two favored classes. Trade this away.
Alternates
* Blended view: dark vision, replaces multitalented.
* Drow heritage: Gain the drow prerequisite for feats, traits, etc. Modifies languages known. Consider the arcane prodigy trait.
* Drow magic: dancing lights, darkness & faerie fire 1/day as spell-like abilities. Replaces adaptability & multitalented. Since you’re a witch, these spells aren’t that significant. But this and drow heritage qualify you for the drow nobility four feat chain, culminating in noble spell resistance.
* Dual minded: +2 will saves, replaces adaptability.
* Fey thoughts: Gain 2 class skills, take perception and one other. Replaces multitalented.
Half Orc
Good stats (+2 int), and solid racial traits. Strongly consider the scarred witch doctor archetype. 
Favored class bonus: +1 skill level for your familiar.
Standard Traits
* Intimidating: +2 intimidate
* Orc ferocity: Can fight for 1 more round at 0 hp. 
* Weapon familiarity: Great axes, etc. 
* Dark vision
Alternates
* Fey thoughts: Gain 2 class skill (likely perception and one other), replaces weapon familiarity.
* Projection: +1 DC on fear or pain spells. Replaces orc ferocity & weapon familiarity.
* Sacred tattoo: +1 luck bonus on saves, replaces orc ferocity.
* Shadowplay: +1 caster level for light, dark & shadow spells. Replaces intimidating. Complements the shadow patron.
Halfling
The stat bonuses are fine but don’t help int, which is what you really want (+2 dex & cha, -2 str). There are a couple interesting traits.
Favored class bonus: +¼ caster level for patron spells. If you take the elements patron you have an extra 1d6 or 2d6 on your shocking grasp, fireball, etc. Not bad.
Standard traits 
* Size: +1 AC, +1 attack, -1 CMB & CMD, +4 stealth
* Slow speed: base speed 20
* Fearless: +2 vs fear
* Halfling luck: +1 saving throws
* Sure footed: +2 acrobatics & climb
* Weapon familiarity: slings, etc
* Keen senses: +2 perception
Alternates
* Caretaker: +2 sense motive, and your familiar gains +2 to one ability stat – probably +2 con or +2 dex to keep them alive. Replaces halfling luck, sure footed, and weapon familiarity.
* Creepy doll: stealth w/o concealment, and no size penalty to intimidate larger humanoids. Replaces keen senses and sure footed. This could be worthwhile if you add the social trait bruising intellect.
* Festive: grant a +2 luck bonus 1/day, replaces sure-footed.
* Fey thoughts: gain 2 class skills, replaces fearless.
* Fleet of foot: base speed of 30,replaces sure footed.
* Halfling jinx: Combine with the feat malicious eye for an additional -1 evil eye. Replaces halfling luck. 
* Shadowplay: +1 caster level for light, dark & shadow spells. Replaces weapon familiarity. This is better if you took the shadow patron.
* Small quarter ally: Grants +1 luck bonus vs fear for all small allies within 60’. Replaces fearless. For a party with enough halflings & gnomes this is more attractive. 
Human
Your stats are great (+2 int).
Standard traits
* Bonus feat: consider extra hex
* Skilled: +1 skill level/character level. Your int is high enough that you’re already getting lots of skills.
Alternates
* Fey magic: Four low level druid spells, 2 class skills, and low-light vision. Replaces skilled.
* Heart of the Fey: low-light vision, +1 reflex & will saves, perception becomes a class skill. Replaces skilled.
Changeling
Your stats are fine (+2 int & cha, -2 con), assuming you take the witchborn trait. You gain access to dreamweaver archetype, which could be quite nice in an intrigue-style campaign.
Standard traits
* Natural armor: +1 natural armor
* Claws: two 1d4 natural attacks
* Darkvision: to 60’
* Hag racial traits: one from a list of underwhelming options. Some of the better choices are:
      * Gaze blindness: +2 saves vs gaze attacks
      * Heartstone Heritor: +2 vs disease
      * Inscrutable: +5 DC vs sense motive
Alternates
* Hag magic: +1 enchantment DC, gain a cantrip spell-like ability. Replaces natural armor & claws.
* Object of Desire: +1 caster level for charm person & charm monster. Replaces the Hag racial trait.
* Witchborn: Bonus stats are (+2 int, +2 cha, -2 con) instead of the standard (+2 wis, +2 cha, -2 con). It’s assumed you take this.


Goblin
Your stats are a net positive, but don’t boost int (+4 dex, -2 str, -2 cha). You get the 1st level spell mudball, one of the best at its level.
Standard traits
* Size: +1 AC, +1 attack, -1 CMB & CMD, +4 stealth
* Skilled: +4 ride & stealth
* Fast movement: +10 base speed
* Darkvision
Alternates
* Over-size ears: +4 perception, replaces skilled.


Ifrit: Lavasoul
There are a variety of “subraces” of Ifrits. The lavasoul has the stats we want for a witch (+2 con, +2 int, -2 dex)
Standard traits
* Darkvision 60’
* Spell-like ability burning sands 1/day (CL = character level)
* Fire resistance 5
* Fire affinity: Sorcerer fire spells have +2 cha, Fire domain spells have CL +1
Alternates
* Wildfire heart: +4 initiative (replaces fire resistance)
* Desert mirage: +2 stealth (replaces fire affinity)
* Fire in the Blood: +2 fast healing for 1 round on fire damage (replaces fire affinity)
* Fire Insight: summon monster/summon nature’s ally last 2 rounds longer when the summoned creature has the fire type.
* Efreeti Magic: enlarge person or reduce person 1/day (replaces spell-like ability)
* Forge-Hardened: +2 craft (weapons/armor), +2 vs fatigue/exhaustion (replaces spell-like ability)
* Hypnotic: +1 DC for fascinated effects (e.g. hypnotism, euphoric cloud, babble; Aurora patron: hypnotic pattern, rainbow pattern), and force a re-roll on a successful save vs fascination 1/day. (replaces fire affinity)
Kitsune
While the standard stats (+2 dex, +2 cha, -2 str) aren’t ideal, the keen kitsune (+2 dex, +2 int, -2 str) gives a great stat spread. It is assumed your witch takes that trait. Also, see the kitsune-only feat Magical Tail, for which you have the FCB ⅙ of this feat. Lastly, because so many witch spells are enchantments, the kitsune magic trait is quite strong.
Standard traits
* Low-light vision
* Change shape: appear human with +10 disguise, as alter self
* Agile: +2 acrobatics
* Kitsune Magic: Enchantment spell DC +1, when cha 11 or higher dancing lights 3/day
* Natural weapons: bite 1d4 in fox form
Alternates
* Keen Kitsune: +2 dex, +2 int, -2 str – it’s assumed you take this
* Superior Shapeshifter: gain fox shape, replaces kitsune magic.
Ratfolk
Optimal stat bonuses (+2 dex, +2 int, -2 str), and the small size is an overall win.
Favored class bonus: +5 ft hex range, except touch range hexes. This is a huge win over other races.
Standard Traits
* Size: +1 AC, +1 attack, -1 CMB & CMD, +4 stealth
* Tinker: +2 craft, perception & UMD
* Rodent empathy: +4 handle animal
* Swarming: Two ratfolk in the same square flank the opponent.
* Darkvision
Alternates
* Cornered fury: +2 attack & +2 AC when below ½ hp, replaces swarming
Samsaran
You have good ability stats (+2 int, +2 wis, -2 con) and excellent traits. 
Standard traits
* Lifebound: +2 vs death effects, negative energy, +2 fort saves vs negative levels
* Shards of the past: Gain 2 class skills with +2 in each
* Low-light vision
* Samsaran magic: three 1st level spell-like abilities 1/day each
Alternates
* Mystic past life: Add 1 plus your starting int bonus arcane spells from another class to your spells known. Your DM should probably tell you to choose another race unless your choices are thoughtful & interesting, as opposed to just adding raw power (e.g. haste, wall of stone, greater invisibility, etc).
Sylph
You have good ability stats (+2 dex, +2 int, -2 con) and solid traits. Consider the cloud-gazer feat.
Favored class bonus: +½ stealth & perception for your familiar.
Standard traits
* Energy resistance: electrical resistance 5
* Spell-like ability: feather fall 1/day
* Darkvision
* Air affinity: a sorcerer-only bonus that we want to trade out
Alternates
* Air insight: +2 rounds when summoning creatures with the air subtype, replaces air affinity.
* Breeze kissed: +2 AC vs non-magical ranged attacks, replaces air affinity.
* Like the Wind: +5 movement, replaces energy resistance.
* Secretive: +2 vs enchantment & divination, replaces energy resistance & spell-like ability.
* Whispering Wind: +4 stealth, replaces spell-like ability.
Tiefling
Good stats (+2 int, +2 dex, -2 cha)
Favored class bonus: +1 cold, fire, or electricity resistance/level for familiar.
Standard traits
* Darkvision 60’
* Skilled: +2 bluff/stealth (varying by bloodline)
* Spell-like ability: darkness 1/day (varying by bloodline)
* Fiendish resistance: 5 cold/fire/electricity
Alternates
* Prehensile tail: replaces a sorcerer bonus you can’t use
* Darklands guide: +2 initiative & when underground perception vs traps, replaces darkness.
* Scaled skin: +1 natural armor and a single energy resistance, replaces fiendish resistance.
Wayang
Good stats (2 dex, +2 int, -2 wis) and FCB that gives access to shadow spells from the wizard/sorcerer list. You should probably take the shadow patron and read Iluzry’s guide on shadow spells. The invoker increases your shadow DC or decreases their SR. The hex witch knife does too, as does your shadow magic trait. Lastly, the item shadow stencil gives you another +1 DC for shadow spells.
Standard traits
* Small size
* Slow speed
* Dark vision 60’
* Light & Dark: 1/day swap how negative/positive energy affects you
* Lurker: +2 stealth & perception
* Shadow magic: +1 DC for shadow spells, at cha 13 gain spell-like abilities: ghost sound, pass without a trace, ventriloquism 1/day
* Shadow resistance: +2 saves vs shadow spells
Alternates
* Scion of Shadows: Miss chance is 50% in dim light, replaces light and dark & shadow resistance.
* Shadow Inheritor: CL +1 for shadow spells, replaces shadow magic. You lose some spell-like abilities, but increase both DC and SR rolls. 
* Shadow Speaker: Ability, attack, CL, concentration, saving throw, or skill check +2 3/day. Replaces shadow magic.
Traits
Before you choose any traits you’ll want to nail down your race and its standard/alternate racial traits. After that, start with this thorough guide.
A guide to Traits


Traits of note for a witch include:
* Balloon-headed (race: goblin): +1 perception, and it becomes a class skill
* Born Under the Green Sun: +1 CL for mind affecting spells & SLAs (not slumber, as hexes are mostly supernatural abilities). Improves duration, targets, SR rolls.
* Bruising intellect: Use your int bonus for intimidation.
* Clever wordplay: Use int bonus for one cha-based skill.
* Deft Dodger: +1 reflex, a weak save for witches
* Magical Lineage: Best choices are a quickened Ill Omen, or with the Elements patron a dazing Fireball.
* Mediator: +1 diplomacy, +1 DC of charm/compulsion effects that do not explicitly control, e.g. a slumber hex.
* Omen: a 1/day swift action demoralize is welcome in a class with few swift actions. Probably only worthwhile if you’ve taken bruising intellect.
* Pragmatic activator: Use your int bonus for UMD. Of the three key skills for arcane spellcasters (Knowledge Arcana, Spellcraft & UMD), this is the only one that isn’t keyed off of your int. Let’s fix that.
* Reactionary: +2 initiative, the whole party does better if your evil eye or misfortune land before their turn.
* Resilient: +1 fortitude, unless you’ll be taking the gift of consumption & greater gift of consumption hexes.
* Student of philosophy: Use int bonus for diplomacy and bluff
* Wayang Spellhunter: This is identical to magical lineage, above. Taking both of these traits may be restricted by your DM. (E.g. taking both traits to reduce the metamagic cost of a single spell by 2 levels is not something you’ll necessarily be allowed.) Note that it is a regional trait, so may not be available to you in any case.
Feats
Accursed Hex -- Reusing a hex that an opponent successfully saved against can turn the tide of an encounter. This has enormous synergy with split hex. 
Additional Traits – Two more traits can be a massive benefit. Consider Bruising Intellect & Pragmatic Activator to give you a significant bonus to two key skills.
Amplified Hex — A 30’ range or +1 DC is very solid, once you’re no longer using all of your 1st/2nd level spells daily.
Augment Summoning – A significant boost to your Summon Monsters, which requires Spell Focus: Conjuration.
Combat Casting – Increase the odds you can still cast your spells when someone is in your face.
Deific/Fey Obedience – See the guides linked to below under the Evangelist prestige class. Unless you play to 12th level, you get no boons. So focus on the obedience. Is it easy to perform, and how valuable is the benefit Consider:
* Deific
   * Abadar: +4 sacred bonus to saves vs chaotic
   * Desna: +1 luck to initiative, +4 sacred to perception
   * Irori: +4 sacred to all knowledge checks
   * Nethys: +4 sacred to concentration
* Fey
   * Magdh: +4 untyped on int-based skills
   * Ng: +4 sacred vs ability damage
   * Shyka: +2 sacred bonus to initiative
Elven Spirit (half-elf) — an improved spell penetration, and a racial bonus that stacks
Evolved Familiar (skilled: UMD +8) -- If you’ve taken an improved familiar that can use wands then this is worth considering.
Extra Hex -- At lower levels this is amazing, getting you key hexes earlier than you’d otherwise have access to them.
Improved Familiar -- This adds more utility than power. I recommend a few choices, below.
Improved Initiative -- A +4 bonus to initiative increases the odds that you’ll get off slumber or misfortune before your party members take their turn.
Knowledgeable Spellcaster: You should be nailing knowledge rolls. If you’re consistently facing enemy corresponding to a particular area of knowledge then this is superior to spell penetration.
Magical Tail (kitsune): gain 1 spell/day, with increasing power with each feat. Also ⅙ of this feat as a FCB. Since these spell-like abilities are charisma-based, prefer a Seducer or Feytouched Hexer.
Mother’s Gift (changeling): Spell resistance of your character level + 6.
Ritual Hex (4th) – After 2 successful checks of both Know. Arcana & Know. History (and 150 gp), gain 1 new hex for the next 24 hours. With a DC 20 (Major Hex DC 30, Grand Hex DC 40), you won’t want to take this before mid levels. By RAW a skill check does not include critical successes nor failures, however your GM may. On 4 rolls the odds of at least one of them coming up a 1 is 18% (100% - 95%^4).
Spell Hex -- (10th level) You can cast a 1st level spell 3/day with your hex DC. Note that this is cast as a spell-like ability, so unlike other hexes it is subject to spell resistance. And of course split hex applies.
* Web bolt – Drow only, no SR, targets reflex saves
* Sow thought – Fetchling only, permanent, requires subtlety & creativity
* Ear-piercing scream – Modest damage, but dazes target for 1 round
* Charm person – 1 hour/level
* Ray of enfeeblement – up to 1d6+5 str penalty
* Chill touch – hexcrafter
* Touch of blindness – hexcrafter
Spell Focus & Greater Spell Focus -- A bonus to your most used school’s DC is nice, if unspectacular. Most witches benefit from Enchantment.
Spell Penetration & Greater Spell Penetration -- at mid to higher levels you’ll encounter enough spell resistance that this becomes increasingly valuable/essential.
Spirit Talker (6th) – Once per day, with 10 minutes preparation, gain a single Shaman hex for the next hour. The breadth of utility this grants is massive: restoration, clairvoyance, learn a wizard spell, see through stone, teleportation. Here, have a cheat sheet.
Split Hex (10th level) -- You literally double the value of each offensive hex and each defensive hex. You do not gain a feat at 10th level, so consider re-training another feat at 10th level so you don’t have to wait for 11th.
Split Major Hex (18th casting level) -- If your major hex is: agony, ice tomb or retribution then this is a terrific choice. As with split hex you will want to re-train an existing feat now rather than wait until 19th level to get this. Alternately, this is keyed off of casting level, and so a 17th level witch with an orange prism ioun stone would qualify.
Witch Knife — Add +1 DC to your patron spells. The benefit varies significantly by patron, but it is solid with the elements or shadow patrons. It’s also well worth considering for the Winter Witch.
Crafting Feats
Witches are usually somewhat starved for feats, so finding room for a crafting feat is challenging. That said, taking a single crafting feat can fill in nicely for important items you’re just not finding nor able to afford otherwise.


Craft Magic Arms & Armor (5th level): Only consider this if you have at least one party member who uses a fairly uncommon weapon, or if specific weapon/armor abilities are important to your party.
Craft Rod (9th level): Lesser rods are quite cheap to craft, and even standard rods become somewhat affordable. On the other hand, the marquee rod of voracious hexes and rod of abrupt hexes get spendy.
Craft Staff (11th level): You’re effectively buying spell slots and putting them in a walking stick. The main argument against this feat is that you’ll likely only ever craft a very few of them.
Craft Wand (5th level): If your familiar uses wands regularly this becomes a reasonable choice for a feat. 
Craft Wondrous Item (3rd level): This is easily the best choice to take among the crafting feats, purely because you & your party will perpetually wish they had additional items of one sort or another.
Forge Ring (7th level): Rings seem to me a bit less essential than some other items. 
Scribe Scroll -- Ensure that you have remove blindness or neutralize poison available when you need it. Round out your utility by incrementally adding scrolls for spells that you otherwise wouldn’t have prepared in advance.
Metamagic Feats
The goal here is to construct improved versions of your spells that are: 1. worth the higher spell slot they require, and 2. worth investing a feat. I’ll generally try to use examples to indicate why I think these feats are or perhaps are not worth considering.


From there we need to determine whether a metamagic rod is a better choice. A lesser rod for a +1 level metamagic is only 3,000 gp, so at mid-levels this is often the way to go.


Bouncing (+1 level): Bouncing blindness or hold person are very worthwhile 3rd level spells, particularly as you encounter spell resistance around 8-10th level and above.
Dazing (+3 levels): If you’ve taken the elements patron then this is worth considering for a 6th level dazing fireball. Note that a lesser dazing rod is 14,000 gp and leaves that fireball in a 3rd level spell slot.
Ectoplasmic (+1 level): This is too situational to justify a feat, but could well be worth the price of a lesser rod.
Empower (+2 levels): At higher levels this gives a big boost to spells like touch of idiocy or enervate. In general, prefer this over maximize because it brings your average d6 up to 5 or d4 up to 3.5 without costing the extra level. But of course it can be stacked with maximize at higher levels.
Extend (+1 level): Spells with a saving throw each round (hold person/monster, charm person/monster) don’t benefit much from this. A longer-term buff such as mage armor does. An extended suffocation is almost certain death if the initial saving throw is missed.
Familiar (+3 levels): If your familiar can cast spells (speaks, has hands) then this is an improved Quicken Spell.
Heighten (+N levels): Simply increasing the spell level to increase its DC is a great strategy at higher levels to broaden your effective spell choices. E.g. there are no good single-target 4th level spells with a fortitude save, but you could heighten blindness to a 4th level spell, increasing its DC. At mid-to-lower levels you’re likely better off with the power of your higher level spells.
Intensify (+1 level): At mid-levels a 10d6 snowball is a nice 2nd level spell. Given the limited number of direct damage spells on the witch’s list you’re probably fine with a lesser rod.
Maximize (+3 levels): You’re probably not a blaster, but a maximized touch of idiocy gives you -6 int/wis/cha in a 5th level spell slot, or 6th with reach. While empower is a more efficient metamagic feat on average, this has the virtue of being a consistent effect. With an empowered spell you might roll a 1, add 50%, round down, and still have a 1.
Persistent (+2 levels): Persistent blindness is a 4th level spell that now requires 2 successful saves against its base 2nd level. Persistent hold person, also 4th level, requires 2 saves each round. Unlike bouncing this does not apply to spell resistance.
Piercing (+1 level): By mid levels you begin to run into SR, and by higher levels it becomes a predictable impediment. 
Quicken (+4 levels): Quickened ill omen can take out a key opponent in 1 round, definitely worth a 5th level spell slot at higher levels. Consister the trait magical lineage to reduce this to +3 levels.
Reach (+1 level/range increase): Your main use here is to increase touch-range spells at close range, a +1 level usage. The key touch spells for a witch are: touch of blindness, touch of idiocy & bestow curse, so a lesser rod is a great choice. (Note that a lesser rod leaves touch of blindness in its 1st level slot, while the reach feat itself would put it in a 2nd level slot where you’d of course prefer the 2nd level spell blindness/deafness.)
Rime (+1 level): Give this serious consideration as a winter witch, but otherwise you’re likely fine with a lesser rod for snowball, frost fall & ice spears.
Silent (+1 level): This works well with invisibility, another reason to consider the deception patron. But it’s still a tradeoff to determine whether you’d prefer to cast summon monster iv silently or summon monster v.
Still (+1 level): It’s hard to justify this as a feat, given that 1. you’ll also require silent for spells with verbal components, and 2. casting spells in secret doesn’t come up with great regularity. 
Threnodic (+2 levels): By mid-levels it’s worth having a lesser threnodic rod because undead are impervious to so many of your spells.
Widen (+3 levels): There are scenarios where an enormous sleet storm or black tentacles is worth a 6th or 7th level spell slot (resp.). But usually not.
Blood Hexes
Blood hexes are generally a poor fit for a witch because they require that you do damage (not your strong suit), commonly a specific type of damage. File these under, “you should be a half-elf so that you can cast paragon surge to get one of these when/if they’re relevant.”


Alternatively, a hexcrafter or sylvan trickster might dip into a level or two of witch to get access to abeyance or hinder. A rod of abrupt hexes could even make them quite good.


Here are the three best of them.


Abeyance: Do damage with a metal weapon to shut down an opponent’s spell-like abilities for 1 minute. This is a pretty powerful effect, however, your BAB is terrible so you’re doing no real damage when you do hit. And it’s rare that you’ll want to spend 2 rounds (one to hit, another to hex) on this. 
Falter: If you’ve done (any) damage in the last minute then their movement is halved and they cannot take a 5 foot step. This is not enough of an effect to merit investing a feat, and even given the feat it’s not likely your best use of a standard action.
Hinder: If you do melee damage then they cannot make a full attack. I rate this just the same as abeyance, above.
Familiars
The best familiars fly and/or add a bonus to initiative or perception. Saving throw bonuses to reflex or fort saves are worth considering too.


Note that a number of witch spells allow you to use your familiar in additional interesting and useful ways. Any of these options that put your familiar in combat, or generally in any jeopardy, are a bad idea.


Lastly, a familiar gains all of your levels in various skills.


* Bat: flight, low-light vision & blindsense, +3 fly skill
* Cat: stealth & scent, +3 stealth skill. It’s not the best choice, but it’s iconic.
* Compsognathus: swims, low-light vision & weak poison, +4 initiative
* Dodo: +4 initiative. All of the other familiars that grant an initiative bonus are better.
* Fox: stealth, +2 reflex saves
* Hawk: flight, +3 perception in daylight
* Jerboa: low-light vision, scent, diminutive size, +4 initiative
* King crab: +2 grapple CMB, only for white haired witches in a nautical campaign
* Monkey: has hands, +3 acrobatics
* Owl: flight & stealth, +3 perception in low light. Prefer a hawk if your campaign features more daylight.
* Rat: stealth, climb & swims, +2 fort saves
* Rabbit/Arctic Hare: stealth, +4 initiative
* Rhamphorhynchus: flight, low-light vision, scent, +4 initiative
* Raven: flight & speech, +3 appraise. The thrush is a bit better with its diplomacy bonus, though a raven is more iconic.
* Scorpion, greensting: weak poison, +4 initiative
* Thrush: flight & speech, diminutive size, +4 diplomacy
* Weasel: stealth & climb, +2 reflex saves
Improved Familiars
These are available via feat improved familiar. Familiars with hands, spoken language, and skill with use magic device (UMD) can use (rather than just wear) magic devices, including wands. Because a familiar gains your skill levels, it suffices for the witch to have a high UMD when a given familiar does not.
* Agathion, silvanshee (7th, NG): flight, stealth, lay on hands, gaseous form, dimension door
* Angel, cassian (7th, NG): flight, darkvision, constant detect evil, true speech, perfect memory
* Azata, lyrakien (7th, CG): flight, truespeech, darkvision, constant detect evil & detect magic, UMD, remove exhaustion & fatigue 1/target/day.
* Elemental, small air (5th, N): the best flight
* Faerie dragon (7th, CG): flight, telepathy, greater invisibility, 3rd level sorcerer spell casting, UMD. This is the marquee wand-using familiar, with access to all wizard/sorcerer spells and good UMD.
* Imp (7th, LE): flight, at-will invisibility, (limited) beast shape I
* Imp Consular (8th, LE): as Imp, above, with telepath and beast shape II as any small or tiny creature.
* Impundulu (7th, NE): “These some of the strongest familiar options for witches, as they have the starting stats of an imp EXCEPT it keeps its own subtype, alignment and most importantly, damage reduction, which is DR/10 Magic AND cold iron which vastly improves its survivability. On top of that, thank you Witchcraft, if it serves as a witch's familiar, it grants you bonus spells as if you had the Agility, Elements, or Transformation patron, which is amazing.” -- IluzryMage. I’ll only add that if you die this guy eats your soul and you are dead beyond resurrection/reincarnation/etc.
* Psychopomp, nosoi (7th, N): flight, stealth, at will invisibility, darkvision, spiritsense
* Pseudodragon (7th, NG): flight, darkvision, blindsense, telepathy, UMD
* Rakshasa, raktavarna (7th, LE): change appearance, modify memories, darkvision, constant detect magic
* Ratling (7th, CE): climb, swim, burrow, invisibility, dimension door, can use any scroll
* Sprite (5th, CN): flight, light, detect evil/good -- best choice for wand use at 5th level

Hexes
Hexes have key benefits over spells:


* Most hexes that have a target can be used once per day per target. That is, you can attempt to Slumber each opponent once per day, or you can Heal each party member once per day. 
* Using a hex does not provoke an attack of opportunity.
* The DC of a hex scales with your level & intelligence, so that even common hexes remain effective at higher levels.
* They are supernatural abilities, which means that they ignore spell resistance and cannot be countered.
* Further, as supernatural abilities they do not require verbal/somatic/material components unless otherwise specified. 


Many witches will want to start with largely the same hexes: slumber, misfortune, cackle, evil eye and flight. But starting each encounter with: evil eye, misfortune, slumber gets old, regardless of how effective it is. Fortune or protective luck give your front-line characters a substantial buff, and soothsayer increases their power significantly. For the creative player, charm and discord are nice effects once you reach 8th level. Prehensile hair opens up safer touch spells as well as the utility of high strength.
Common Hexes
Ameliorating -- Each party member can benefit from this hex daily. It could be very useful if you are headed toward an encounter where you can expect the party to face, e.g. sicken. But in most cases it’s just too situational.
Aura of Purity -- At minutes/day, with a 10’ radius this hex is reasonably effective, but regardless pretty situational.
Beast of Ill-Omen -- Bane is too minor/weak an effect to justify a hex.
Blight -- This hex has great flavor, but is far too situational.
Cackle -- This is a key hex, extending the hexes below for rounds as a move action.

Cauldron -- At lower levels preparing potions in advance allows for more spells per day and can broaden the spells you have available. But the cost in gold ultimately limits potions’ overall benefit.
Charm -- Only consider this after 8th level when you get a 2 step improvement in attitude, and you’ve taken enough of the preferable hexes that it looks better.
Child Scent -- Interesting in terms of flavor, but still terrible.
City Sight -- Too situational.
Combat Hypnosis -- The 2d4 HD limit on your target will quickly make this hex obsolete. Slumber is almost completely superior.
Congeal -- Too situational.
Coven -- In combat you’ll probably have something better to do than boost another witch’s DC by +1, but out of combat this is useful. You may also gain access to the hag coven’s spell-like abilities. See the Covens section, below.
Cursed Wound -- Only for NPCs.
Dark Apothecary -- Only if you’re a Veneficus Witch.
Deathcall – NPC only
Discord -- The opposite of Charm, you lower the attitude of a target. Only consider this after 8th level when you get a 2 step decrease in attitude. Nice flavor for a witch, though the benefits of charm are more often of value than sowing dissent among your foes.
Disguise -- The utility here is largely dependent on the campaign and the player’s interest in using disguises in the first place.
Disrupt Connection -- Confusion for a single, summoned target, with a chance at controlling the creature. Only consider this after 8th level.
Distraction -- You’ll usually want slumber instead. The only advantage of distraction is that the target can’t be awakened by an ally.
Enemy Ground -- Too situational, even after 8th level.
Evil Eye -- You’ll usually want to apply this to saving throws and sustain it with cackle. 
Feral Speech -- Pretty situational, but interesting flavor.
Flight -- Good at 3rd level, amazing at 5th. Per the fly spell you get your level/2 as a skill bonus.
Floating Lotus -- Flight is better in most situations.
Fortune -- In general it’s a fine buff where a 50% chance to hit becomes 75% (since the chance of missing twice is 50% x 50% = 25%), and can be extended with cackle. It can be overpowered when applied to a player whose weapon has a high crit range as well as keen or the feat improved critical. In that case, talk to your GM before you take it. (Some math -- a weapon with an 18-20 crit range and improved critical has a 15-20 crit range. Applying fortune, the odds of not getting a crit is then 70% x 70% = 49%, i.e. just over half your attacks are a critical hit.)
Gift of Consumption -- Every fort save you have to make becomes a free attack, provided there is a new target within a 30’ range. Note however, that monsters with poison are often immune to poison, the same with disease, and so forth. The feats accursed hex and split hex are of course applicable.
Gift of Consumption, Greater -- Until you run out of targets (once each/day, twice with accursed hex) you are effectively immune to fortitude save failures. The feats accursed hex and split hex are applicable.
Healing -- This is terrific at low to mid levels. You can apply it to each party member 1/day, which is consistently useful. Once the party can reliably afford a wand of cure light wounds (50 charges for 750 gp) you’ll want to re-train it. 
Heralding Bloom -- Too situational.
Iceplant -- A +2 to AC is always welcome. Note that this is a natural armor bonus, which is different from an enhancement bonus to natural armor, and hence stacks with e.g. an amulet of natural armor.
Leshy Summoning -- The leshy lotus is an interesting target for summon monster iv.
Minor Prophesy -- An additional 2nd level spell (augury) 1/day is nice at earlier levels. Talk to your GM to make sure this is compatible with their campaign.
Misfortune -- This significantly disables an opponent when they fail their save, and all but ensures they will fail successive saves. Extend it with cackle.
Mother’s Eye -- Unless your campaign takes place in an overgrown forest this is too situational.
Mud Witch -- You’re probably not playing a goblin.
Murksight -- An interesting hex once you’ve taken enough of the preferable ones, maybe 10th level or later. Alternatively, consider playing a sylph and taking the feat cloud gazer.
Nails -- You never want to be in melee range, and if you are this is still a modest attack at best.
No Place Like Home -- Too situational.
Peacebond -- Slowing down the start of hostilities can buy your party time.
Poison Steep -- If you’re a veneficus witch and you took the cauldron hex then this is kind of fun.
Poison Touch -- You grant an ally str poison with a good DC. This has potential if your party contains a monk or other combatant with at least one free hand.
Pollute Water -- Too situational.
Polluting Glance -- Better than polluting water, but not worth the 2 hexes to get here.
Prehensile Hair -- The 10’ reach is nice for touch spells and attacks of opportunity. The intelligence-based strength on top of the reach adds lots of utility. And if you don’t have any other attacks (put away that dagger), you have a primary natural attack.
Protective Luck -- A very solid buff for a front-line melee character, as long as you can sustain it with cackle. Note that critical hits against the target will almost never happen, and critical failures will beset their attackers nearly 10% of the time. Unlike most hexes, it does not have a limit of once per day per target, though this could well have been an oversight rather than a design decision. Another item to discuss with your GM before you take it rather than after.
Scar -- Extend your buff hexes (Protective Luck and possibly Fortune) beyond 30’ by scarring your allies. And scarring an enemy is fun.
Seduction -- I’m not that interested in the sexy witch trope, and slumber is preferable in most situations.
Sink -- Too situational outside of a nautical campaign.
Slumber -- Unless your opponent has someone to awaken them, if they fail their will save this ends the encounter. It can be overpowered, even boring, if used too often.
Soothsayer -- Buffing every party member with a protective luck and perhaps fortune hex before an encounter and sustaining it with cackle during the encounter can have a huge effect. The same for applying evil eye & misfortune to an opponent before hostilities commence -- at least when the opportunity presents itself. 
Summer’s Heat -- Modest damage and fatigued, with a fort save.
Swamp Hag -- Too situational.
Swamp’s Grasp -- Decent battlefield control. It’s not as good as the “wall” spells, but it’s at-will with the area increasing with your level. And most of the wall spells aren’t on the witch’s spell list anyway.
Swine -- The target suffers a -2 to will saves, and at 8th level melee characters can’t hold a weapon and casters probably can’t perform somatic gestures. Note that this lasts for a number of rounds equal to your int bonus, and 4+ rounds means most/all of a combat encounter. Significantly, this is one of the witch’s only tools vs undead or constructs. It also does not have the 1/target/day restriction we see on most other hexes. It is flatly bad before 8th level though.
Tongues -- Nice, if unremarkable, utility throughout the campaign.
Unnerve Beasts -- Too situational, unless your campaign features lots of mounted encounters or perhaps guard dogs.
Verdant Familiar -- “Makes your familiar immune to mind-affecting effects, paralysis, poison, polymorph, sleep effects, and stunning. Also removes its need for sleep, allowing it to keep watch all night long.” -- Derklord
Ward -- A nice buff that you can always have on a single party member before an encounter starts. At higher levels your party members likely have items that duplicate its effect.
Water Lung -- Situational, and must be cast once per minute per target.
Witch’s Bottle -- Soothsayer is almost always superior.
Dice-affecting Hex Overview
The witch has a variety of common hexes that undermine opponent rolls (evil eye, misfortune, protective luck) and improve the rolls of your allies (fortune). Let’s look through how these overlap and how they stack. Note that these are each extended by cackle, and because hexes are “supernatural” abilities they are not subject to spell resistance.
* Evil eye -- A -2 to all saves (or AC or attacks or skill checks), increased to -4 at 8th level, with a will save.
   * Pro: This always hits for 1 round, and hence can be extended indefinitely via cackle whether the target saves or not.
   * Con: This is mind-affecting, and hence does not apply to undead, constructs, etc. It can only target any given target 1/day.
* Misfortune -- The target has to roll every d20 twice, taking the lower roll, with a will save.
   * Pro: This debuffs the target’s attacks as well as their saves. It is not mind-affecting and hence applies to any opponent.
   * Con: A successful slumber is often preferable to a successful misfortune.
* Fortune -- The target gets to reroll their d20 for 1 round, increased by 1 round at 8th & 16th levels.
   * Pro: This is a buff, and so does not depend on a failed saving throw. This buffs both the target’s attacks and their saving throws (and skill & ability checks). A target with a large critical hit range (e.g. scimitar’s 18-20 plus the feat improved critical yields 15-20) will get a critical hit on literally half their attacks, and confirm almost every critical hit.
   * Con: Nothing of note.
* Protective luck -- The target’s attackers must roll their d20s twice and take the lower roll for 1 round, increased by 1 round at 8th & 16th levels.
   * Pro: This is a buff, and so does not depend on an opponent’s failed saving throw. It does not have the 1/target/day restriction that each other hex has.
   * Con: Your DM might well add the 1/target/day restriction, considering it an oversight.
* Soothsayer -- You can apply any of the above hexes in advance, and do not need to begin cackling until they are triggered -- i.e. typically until combat begins.
   * Pro: This is a massive benefit in terms of the witch’s action economy. When combat does not immediately begin the witch can silently debuff the opponents. Allies can be buffed well in advance of combat.
   * Con: The 1/day/target restriction can trigger fortune on an unimportant skill check or saving throw.
So then how do we evaluate these hexes? Consider opponents who are genuine threats. We’ll say that they have a 70% chance to hit and a 70% chance to make their saving throws, while your party only has a 50% chance to hit them and a 50% chance to save vs their spells/effects.
* Evil eye: Now they make their saves 60%, at 8th level 50% of the time.
* Misfortune: A single opponent will hit & save 49% of the time, and if stacked with evil eye this is reduced to 36% or 25% at 8th level.
* Fortune: One ally has a 75% chance to hit and save vs all opponents.
* Protective luck: One ally has the above benefits of misfortune vs all opponents (excluding the opponents’ saving throws of course, those are still their own). Further, this can always be prepared in advance for the entire party when coupled with soothsayer. In point of fact, it is the combination of protective luck & soothsayer that is likely to result in your DM applying the 1/target/day restriction.
Lastly, consider split hex both when applying evil eye & misfortune to opponents, as well as when applying fortune & protective luck to allies.

Grand Hexes
Abominate -- A curious hex. You can turn an opponent into a CR 1 aberration (n.b. A sagari is only ½ CR), or an ally into a powerful aberration. I think the most reasonable interpretation is that a failed will save (per baleful polymorph) gives your target the mental stats & alignment of the aberration in question, though only the abilities listed under monstrous physique IV. In case an ally fails their will save, ensure they are under a slumber hex or in a circle of protection, and have dispel magic ready. Survey of aberrations
Animal Servant -- A remarkable combination of baleful polymorph and dominate monster, though restricted to humanoid targets.
Curse of Nonviolence -- Eliminate a powerful threat 
Death Curse -- Too bad they get multiple saves.
Death Interrupted -- Unlimited resurrection and speak-with-dead.
Dire Prophecy -- Every effect from evil eye combined.
Eternal Slumber -- Too bad it’s at touch range or via food/drink.
Forced Reincarnation -- The target loses 2 levels, half of their spells, and is some sort of humanoid (e.g. a dragon reincarnated as a gnome, albeit a fire-breathing gnome). Notably, the target reincarnates in a new body, one that does not have all of their gear/equipment.

Life Giver -- Depends on how often party members are at significant risk of death.
Natural Disaster -- Tons of power, but hard to aim/control.

Witch’s Hut -- A traveling, self-defending and hiding hut is a fun effect with reasonable utility.
Covens
This will be a rather rarely used feature, and the rules are a bit thin and require some inference. Here’s my best reading:
* A hag’s coven must contain exactly 3 hags. The hags gain spell-like abilities, which require the entire coven as a full round action to cast. Note that there is no daily restriction on these spell-like abilities, so they can be cast at will.
* The Coven hex, above, allows a witch to join a hag’s coven, but a coven must contain at least one actual hag. I.e. not simply a witch with the Coven hex.
* A sorcerer with the Accursed bloodline similarly counts as a hag for the purpose of joining a hag’s coven. But again, it must contain at least one true hag.
* The Shawl of the Crone also allows its wearer to count as a hag for the purpose of joining a hag’s coven. And yes, it must still contain at least one true hag.
* A changeling with the feat Awakened Hag Heritage might also count, but as always ask your DM.
* The neck slot item, Iron Collar of the Unbound Coven, allows a witch who is not a hag to fill the role of a hag for the purpose of creating a coven. However, at least one of the witches must be 9th level. Note that this item costs 46,000gp, and the wealth-by-level for a 9th level character is 46,000gp. That is, it would require ⅓ of the combined wealth of three 9th level witches.
* The Grand Coven ritual allows the size of a coven to be expanded beyond 3.
* All of the above is unambiguous. There remains only the question whether a coven of witches (one of them with the iron collar), but containing no true hags, gains access to the spell-like abilities listed under the hag coven. Consider the cost of an item that allows Mind Blank to be cast 3/day: 8th level spell x 15th level caster x 1,800 gp x 3 castings per day / 5 default castings per day = 129,600 gp. And that far exceeds the cost of an Iron Collar of the Unbound Coven, so I have to conclude that no, these spell-like abilities should not be gained.
   * Animate dead: presumably to the combined coven hd limit?
   * Baleful polymorph (DC 18): out of combat this can simply be cast until the target rolls a critical failure on its saving throw
   * Blight: no great risk to the campaign
   * Bestow Curse (DC 17): again, cast until a critical failure on the save
   * Clairaudience/Clairvoyance: scout everything within approx 800’ at will.
   * Charm Monster (DC 17): again, cast until a critical failure on the save
   * Commune: unlimited yes/no answers without the 500gp cost
   * Control Weather: a 7th level spell, though with infrequent value
   * Dream: not a significant risk to the campaign
   * Forcecage: a powerful 7th level spell with limited range and a short duration
   * Mind Blank: a very powerful 8th level protective buff, available at will
   * Mirage Arcana: hide the party behind/within any image you like, whenever you like, change the perceived environment at will
   * Reincarnate: skip the 1,000 gp cost, but probably not a great risk
   * Speak with Dead: probably not a risk
   * Veil: completely change the party’s appearance at will
   * Vision: receive the answer to any question, limited only by your DM’s patience
Patrons

The spells are ranked based on how valuable they are to a witch. E.g. summon nature’s ally is a great spell, but witches already have summon monster, making this largely redundant.


The level of your campaign can shift the rating quite a bit, e.g. fate offers nothing of note below 8th level, but is very solid after that. Note that witches’ 9th level spell list is a weak spot compared with other 9/9 spellcasters. If your campaign will go that far then you may want to prioritize a patron that includes a premier spell such as: wish, miracle, or time stop.

Spells
Spells will be ranked a bit differently for a witch than for another class with the spell. E.g. lightning bolt isn’t a great spell overall, but if a witch needs to do damage to an enemy it’s one of their better choices.


This overview is not a replacement for reading the witch’s spell list a time or three as you gain each level. There are likely spells that are relevant to your specific campaign or your specific witch that aren’t broadly useful enough to list here.


When you learn new spells, you’ll almost always want to choose from the blue, or occasionally green ones. As you look for scrolls to buy you’ll also want to prioritize these spells, but some orange ones may be worth including -- depending on your character, interests and campaign. The breadth & variety of these spells highlights the utility of the scribe scrolls feat.


Also note that I ignore lots of spells. There are numerous spells that are either flatly bad or highly situational. Maybe cantrips like Arcane Mark, Bleed, Detect Poison, Grasp, Putrefy Food & Drink, Resistance, Spark, or Touch of Fatigue make sense for your witch.
Combat Tactics
Witch spells include many single-target compulsions, along with a few other single-target spells such as blindness. A solid, general-purpose strategy for a witch is to:
1. Keep yourself safe, often with your flight hex.
2. Cast an area effect spell to shift the entire encounter, e.g. glitterdust, web, euphoric cloud, sleet storm, black tentacles or confusion.
3. Use single-target hexes/spells to mop up remaining targets.

* Light -- The chief advantage over dancing lights is that you can cast this on a stone and throw it into a cave or down a well. Lasts longer too.

* Marionette possession (will) -- A willing target allows you to possess them. You could possess a party member and cast a spell on them with a “personal” target, and then go back to your own body.
* Mind maze (will) -- Remove an opponent from combat for rounds/level, with a chance they stumble into greater peril (though this gives them another save).
* Paragon surge (half-elf) -- Take extra hex to gain access to any hex for minutes/level. Going into a key social encounter you can add charm or discord. Take gift of consumption when faced with a medusa, or aura of purity when faced with a green dragon. Choose spell hex to cast sow thought with your hex DC. Throw a guard post or cavalry unit into disarray with unnerve beasts. At 10th level you can choose from among Major Hexes, and at 18th from among Grand Hexes. Lastly, consider Additional Traits to gain an int-based bonus to a skill you need now: Pragmatic Activator for UMD, Bruising Intellect for intimidation, Student of Philosophy for diplomacy & bluff, or Clever Wordplay for disguise, handle animals, or perform.
* Penumbral disguise -- A significant enough bonus to disguise and stealth to overcome the fact that you’re likely bad at these. Only in normal light, or lower.
* Phase step -- Short range teleportation, within line of sight.
* Pierce disguise -- See through 3rd level or lower illusion or polymorph disguises.
* Pillow talk (will) -- Interrogate a sleeping target, 1 question/2 levels.
* Planar inquiry -- A flavorful augury-style spell.
* Polymorph familiar -- A beast shape of increasing level for your familiar. Primarily useful for disguise or to add a movement type such as flight at lower levels. Revisit this spell when you reach 9th (beast shape iii) & 11th (beast shape iv) levels, respectively.
* Pugwampi’s grace (will) -- A spell-based misfortune for rounds/level, though completely countered by any luck bonus.
* Qlippoth appearance (will) -- Cast at the beginning of or prior to combat, this allows 1 target per round for rounds/level to be paralyzed. This gives your martial party members a series of coup de grace opportunities, 1 each round. May be cast on your familiar via share spells.
* Quell energy (will) -- Only useful if an opponent is capable of repeated area effect attacks of a single, specific energy type.
* Rain of frogs (no SR) -- Only superior to vomit swarm at 5th & 6th levels. But it’s a fun spell to cast.
* Ray of exhaustion (fort, ranged touch) -- A significant debuff to strength & dexterity.
* Remove blindness/deafness -- Most of the time you’ll just want this on a scroll.
* Remove curse -- Most of the time you’ll just want this on a scroll.
* Remove disease -- Most of the time you’ll just want this on a scroll.
* Riversight -- Scout along a river for minutes/level.
* Screech (fort) -- Grant your party attacks of opportunity to opponents within 30’.
* See beyond -- An all-day bonus to perception, particularly vs invisibility. Consider casting this daily at higher levels.
* Seek thoughts (will, no SR) -- Gain key information from a reluctant source.
* Sepia snake sigil (reflex, no SR) -- With the right setup this is a very powerful spell, and one of your few with a reflex save. 
* Share senses -- For minutes/level you can see & hear through your familiar.
* Shroud of Innocuity (will, no SR) – At mid to higher levels this provides a lot of utility. Hours/level of most/all of your party becoming ignored by anyone who’s not already unfriendly/hostile to you. Only these unfriendly/hostile creatures get a will save.
* Silver darts (reflex) -- A 15’ cone of silver darts, inflicting 1d6/level to the unarmored.
* Siphon might (fort) -- Transfer about 5-8 str from an opponent to a party member for rounds/level, halved if the target saves. Or transfer between party members if great strength is required in a particular scenario.
* Sleet storm (none, no SR) -- Blocks all sight, restricts movement to ½ speed, and even then requires an acrobatics check to move. Compare with stinking cloud.
* Speak with dead -- From time to time you’ll wish you learned just a bit more from someone before your party killed them.
* Stinking cloud (fort, no SR) -- Area control that cripples opponents’ offense. Compare with sleet storm.
* Storm step (reflex) -- Moderate damage & short range teleport. Probably a better spell for a magus to launch himself into melee range than for you to get out.
* Suggestion (will) -- A spell that requires more of a deft touch, but which can yield real benefits for the thoughtful.
* Summon monster III -- The first summon spell that gives you competent combatants: crocodile, lantern archon & leopard.
* Thorny entanglement (reflex, no SR) -- Good area control and moderate damage, though only for an outdoor campaign.
* Tongues -- Speak/understand any language. Worth having on a scroll, and in some scenarios worth memorizing.
* Tough crowd -- A solid buff for your party vs. illusions, intimidation, bluffing & diplomacy.
* Trial of fire & acid (touch, fort, no SR) -- The target takes 2d6/round of fire & acid for rounds/level, with a save only halving the damage for that round. Countered by dousing or submerging the target in water. This is the most damage you can do, perhaps averaging 5 hp/level, though over several rounds.
* Unadulterated loathing (will) -- Undermine your opponents’ coordination, or keep an opponent away from you.
* Unhallowed blow, greater -- Buff undead, for gravewalker witches.
* Vampiric touch (none, touch) -- Moderate damage & moderate healing. A good choice for spell storing armor or a spite spell.
4th
* Absorb toxicity -- The key is that you’re immune to poison and disease for 10s of minutes/level, provided you never absorb any poison/disease.
* Aggressive thundercloud, greater (reflex & fort) -- At best you stun a target for 1 round (fort) and do 6d6 for rounds/level (reflex). However, the reflex save negates damage rather than halving it, and you must use your move action to maneuver it to a target.
* Anti-incorporeal shell -- Complete protection from incorporeal foes for minutes/level within 10’.
* Arcane eye -- The ability to examine 2000’+ of terrain in advance can give your party a real tactical advantage.
* Bit of Luck (catfolk) – A significant boost to touch/ranged-touch attack spells and skill checks for 10 minutes/level.
* Bite the hand (will) -- Turn a summoned creature against its summoner.
* Black spot (will) -- A modest debuff, and daily constitution drain (fort). Like cup of dust, this is not a tactically effective spell, but rather a flavorful, long-term threat.
* Black tentacles (none, CMB check, no SR) -- Martial/melee opponents will exceed this spell’s CMB after a few levels, but prior to that it is all but over-powered. 
* Charm monster (will) -- With a bit of creativity this can resolve single-opponent encounters rather nicely.
* Complex hallucination (will) -- A robust, purely mental illusion with 1 target/level. Discuss this spell with your DM, their interpretation & preferences will greatly affect its utility.
* Conditional curse (will) -- You combine bestow curse with a task or condition to remove the curse, significantly motivating your target to fulfill the task. Notably, has a 55+’ range, unlike bestow curse at touch range. Good general-purpose conditions include: unless you fully surrender; until you meet me on this spot 1 year from now.
* Confusion (will) -- Targeting a single opponent is useful, but if you can catch 2 or more it becomes a very good spell indeed.
* Conjuration foil (will) -- A summoning debuff, causing 1d6/spell-level to summoned creatures within a 20’ radius circle. Compare with bite the hand.
* Curse of burning sleep (will) -- Cause the target to burst into flame after 1 hour of sleep. 
* Curse of magic negation (will, no SR) -- The target suffers a roughly 50% chance of spell failure for 10’s of minutes/level. 
* Daze, Mass (will) -- Useful to stop/delay the villagers without killing anyone.
* Death ward -- A very important protection when you need it.
* Deathless – A solid spell to combine with spite, once you’re high enough level to spend two 4th level spells on contingency plans.
* Detect scrying -- Not a common need, but useful in the right circumstances.
* Dimension door -- Medium range teleportation, 700-1,200 feet.
* Discern lies (will, no SR) -- Evil eye & misfortune make this more useful.
* Divination -- With an 80-90% accuracy rate this is consistently useful, subject to your DM’s preferences of course. 
* Enchantment foil -- A nice anti-enchantment buff for hours/level.
* Enervation (none, ranged touch) -- Losing 1d4 levels with no save is a modest debuff, but maximized and/or empowered it becomes a significant hit.
* False Live, Greater – The max is increased to 20 hp, over false life’s 10 hp. A nice option once 4th level spells are no longer a valuable resource, maybe by 13th level.
* Familiar melding -- Take possession of your familiar for hours/level. 
* Fear (will) -- Opponents flee & cower on a failed save.
* Fey form I -- Your BAB is terrible, so you’re taking this for utility, movement, and the ability to cast a few of your spells as uncounterable SLAs. Your options.
* Fleshworm infestation (fort, touch, evil) -- Ongoing modest damage and dex loss for rounds/level.
* Geas, lesser (will) -- Have a captured henchman lead you to their lair. The 7HD limit & 10 minute casting time limit the applicability.
* Ghost brand -- Hide an object in someone’s body for days/level.
* Hallucinogenic smoke (fort) -- A nice area effect nauseated, or even on save sickened. 
* Harvest knowledge (will, no SR) -- Get answers to questions for minutes/level. Since each question requires 1 minute, consider slumber for an unwilling subject.
* Ice storm (none) -- Modest damage and a large area of difficult terrain.
* Inveigle person (will) -- At
*  hours/level, more effective charm person.
* Locate creature -- Find your familiar.
* Masochistic shadow (will, reflex, evil) -- For rounds/level the target takes 1d4 str each turn they fail a reflex save.
* Minor Creation – A ladder, poison, flammable oil, layer of ironwood thorns on the floor.
* Mirror transport -- Travel between 2 mirrors within 500’ of each other, with 1 traveler/level.
* Neutralize poison -- Good to keep on a scroll or two. Occasionally worth memorizing.
* Purge spirit (will) -- Incorporeal or ethereal target takes 1d6/level, half with a will save.
* Rags to Riches – Increase the bonuses of a variety of items, particularly skill-increasing items get +1 insight bonus/4 levels. Or add +1 to a weapon or armor bonus.
* Ride the waves -- Swim and breath underwater for hours/level
* Scrying (will) -- Spy on a target for minutes/level.
* Secure shelter -- For 2 hours/level you conjure a reasonably secure hut for your party.
* Shadowform – A remarkable defensive buff for rounds/level.
* Share Shape – Take your familiar’s form with beast shape ii attributes. This does not apply if you have taken an improved familiar. The utility is of course dependent on your choice of familiar. A bird gives you flight, a rat gives stealth. All give a great disguise for scouting, infiltration or escape. A monkey may be able to cast spells, at least ones without verbal components – check with your DM.
* Spite -- touch of blindness, bestow curse, trial of fire & acid, vampiric touch
* Summon monster IV -- giant scorpion, hound archon, grizzly bear, tiger
* Threefold aspect -- The elderly aspect grants +4 int & wis, well worth the -2 dex & str.
* Vermin shape II -- giant queen bee, giant scorpion, giant wasp, giant black widow
* Wall of blindness/deafness (fort) -- The witch has few “wall” spells, and this one is quite effective.
* Wandering star motes (will) -- This will leave one or another opponent dazed for the majority of an encounter.
* Web cloud (reflex, no SR) -- A 20’ radius cloud akin to web, moving away from you continuously. Unless you specifically want to target reflex saves, black tentacles is usually preferable.
5th
* Arid refuge -- A reasonably secure underwater hut for 2 hours/level.
* Awaken the devoured (will) -- Daemons (1 per 4 levels) take 1d8/level plus 1 round/level of confusion.
* Baleful polymorph (fort, will) -- Transform the target into a 1HD creature, on a missed will save they have its mental capacities. Compare with suffocation.
* Banish seeming (touch) -- Remove illusions and transformations for rounds/level.
* Break enchantment (no SR) — Remove enchantments, curses & transmutations of 5th level or lower.
* Callback, greater -- Retrieve your injured familiar from miles/level away. 
* Charm person, mass (will) -- As charm person vs 2x your level in HD.
* Claim Identity, Greater (fort) -- Turn the target into a mask that you can wear to perfectly imitate them for hours/level, a 6th level spell for druids/sorcerers/wizards
* Cloudkill (fort, no SR) -- Below 6 HD, death, at or above that, 1d4 constitution/round.
* Cold iron fetters (reflex, no SR) -- Reduce the target’s speed and prevent teleportation.
* Commune with plane -- Learn about the plane you’re on, if you’re traveling.
* Curse of unexpected death (fort, touch) -- Over the next minutes/level the target takes about 8d6 every other minute. While not useful in combat, the total damage is massive.
* Curse, major (will) -- As bestow curse, but at range and with a significantly higher DC to remove the curse.
* Debilitating pain, mass (will) -- Stun 2 or more creatures for 3 or more rounds, or they are dazed for 1 round on a save.
* Dominate person (will) -- Control the target for days/level, with no additional save unless you ask them to do something “against their nature”.
* Draconic suppression (fort) -- Remove a dragon’s breath weapon and reduce them one age category.
* Duplicate familiar -- For 10 minutes/level you have a duplicate of your familiar. It doesn’t matter if the duplicate dies.
* Feeblemind (will) -- Completely debilitate any int-based or cha-based caster, permanently.
* Hex glyph, major -- As hex glyph, but can store a major hex. Kind of expensive at 400 gp each.
* Hold monster (will) -- As hold person, but without the humanoid restriction.
* Hungry earth (none, CMB check, no SR) -- a cousin of black tentacles that will drag you under the earth and suffocate you. The downside is that it must compete with other 5th level spells and that your opponents are 2 levels/HD closer to exceeding its CMB.
* Magic jar (will) -- For hours/level you can control various targets in rotation. The trick is keeping your inert body safe all the while. Compare with the mechanically simpler possession.
* Major creation -- Create almost any mundane item, with a craft check for e.g. a copy of a key or a carved item.
* Mind fog (will) -- Within a 20’ radius fog you all but preclude any future will saves.
* Overland flight -- Fly all day
* Pain strike, mass (fort) -- One target/level takes 1d6/round for rounds/level. And they’re sickened.
* Pernicious pranksters (no SR) — Fey spirits perform combat maneuvers against an opponent for rounds/level.
* Possession (will) — Control the target for hours/level. Be sure your body is safe while you’re away. Compare with magic jar. 
* Possession trap (will) — An attempt to possess you requires a save by the caster. On failure they are trapped in a gem you possess for rounds/level.
* Prying eyes — Flying eyes with a range of 1 mile for hours/level.
* Reincarnate — Bring back the dead with a random race and -2 levels.
* Release the hounds — A swarm that does 3d6/round with distraction and trip.
* Rest eternal -- Prevent the target from being brought back to life, as well as speak with dead efforts.
* Suffocation (fort) -- On an initial save the target is staggered for 1 round. Otherwise they must make each of 3 successive saves to avoid unconsciousness. Failing all 3 results in death. Compare with baleful polymorph.
* Summon monster V -- ankylosaurus, large elemental, salamander
* Surface excursion — Teleport from underground to the surface with 3-6 additional targets for hours/level. Effectively, get a safe night’s rest.
* Telepathic bond — Allow telepathy among yourself and 3-6 targets for 10’s of minutes/level.
* Teleport — Teleportation over 100’s of miles/level.
* Wreath of blades (reflex, no SR) — Prevent almost all attacks of opportunity against yourself due to spell casting, and modest damage within 5’.
6th
* Age resistance -- The cumulative decline from middle-age and old-age are -3 to str/dex/con. If that applies to you, then a total 9 point ability score gain/recuperation is worth a spell slot.
* Analyze dweomer — a detailed, informative detect magic for rounds/level
* Animate objects — A spell with raw power, as well as one that rewards creativity. Read up on animated objects’ construction points in advance, particularly additional movement. 
* Balance of suffering (will) -- Transfer perhaps 45-50 hp from one target to another, half that on a save.
* Baleful shadow transmutation (will, fort) — A will-based polymorph that removes spells and spell-like abilities. Compare with flesh to stone, or the 5th level baleful polymorph.
* Besmara’s grasping depths (will) -- Make an area of deep, open water liable to drown any who enter it. 
* Cloak of dreams (will) — For rounds/level adjacent creatures are put to sleep for 1 minute. 
* Cone of cold (reflex) -- Good cold damage in a 60’ cone. 
* Curse of the outcast (will) -- Reduce everyone’s attitude toward the target and cripple their charisma-based skill. More an enjoyable spell than a tactically sound one.
* Curse terrain, greater -- Make a 5 mile radius of land hostile and all but impassable for 24 hours.
* Dispel magic, greater (no SR) -- As dispel magic, but affecting multiple spells on a single target or within a 20’ radius.
* Dust form -- Akin to gaseous form, but you can cast spells, use items, and still take 50% damage. Lasts rounds/level.
* Eyebite (fort) -- A solid spell vs 9HD or lower opponents, but they’re no longer your primary concern.
* Fey form II -- Your options include the overpowered Whisperer. Note that special abilities have the DC of this 6th spell itself.
* Find the path -- Determine the best route to a “prominent” location.
* Flesh to stone (fort) -- Does what it says. 
* Geas/Quest (none) -- The casting time is 10 minutes, but given that it is a powerful inducement to follow your request.
* Guards & Wards -- Render a large building difficult to enter or explore, effectively all day.
* Hellfire ray (ranged touch, none) -- Low damage for a spell of this level, until you get 2 rays at 15th level and then 3 rays at 19th. Note that banishing the subject to hell isn’t inherently an evil act if the target is itself evil.
* Heroism, greater -- A solid, if unexciting buff for a martial character, of note it conveys immunity to fear.
* Invoke deity -- Discuss with your DM which aspects correspond to your patron. The knowledge & magic aspects are attractive.
* Janni’s jaunt -- Your first planar travel, limited to the inner planes. 
* Legend lore -- At this point any person of object of interest to you likely passes the “legend” threshold, potentially learning information never otherwise revealed. Ultimately though, this is entirely at your DM’s discretion.
* Mage’s decree -- Primarily of use if you can effectively restrict the recipients, e.g. to good recipients, or elves, or worshipers of a specific deity.
* Neutralize poison, greater -- Worth use if a party member has taken significant damage from poison.
* Plague storm (fort, no SR) -- Default this to blinding sickness and consider it a minutes/level, 20’ radius cloud of blinding. Unfortunately the DC is the disease’s+2, not the spell DC. 
* Prognistication -- See a year into the future, receive correspondingly cryptic information.
* Raise dead -- The target comes back to life with -2 levels.
* Sarzari shadow memory (none, no SR) -- Choosing the character category gives your victim -2 will saves and gives you +4 levels vs their SR for a month.
* Source severance (no SR*) -- Shut down all arcane magic or divine magic (but not both) within 5’. You can still cast spells, though with a ~40% failure rate. *A summoned monster with SR can use that SR to prevent being unsummoned.
* Spectral saluqi -- With 10 minutes/level this is a fine target for alter summoned monster. Discuss with your DM whether the resulting monster “shares your alignment and can converse with you telepathically”.
* Stone to flesh — When you need it, you need it.
* Suggestion, mass (will) -- Even with a 50% success rate, this can dramatically reduce the number of opponents you confront. But you still have to choose a suitable suggestion.
* Summon flight of eagles -- You have 2-5 giant eagles, likely to use as mounts for 10 minutes/level.
* Summon genie, greater -- A shaitan has terrific SLAs and is capable in combat. 
* Summon laborers -- Sometimes you just need manual labor, lots of it.
* Summon monster VI-- Shadow demon, succubus, triceratops
* Swarm skin -- Turn into a varying number of swarms of varying strength for as long as desired, leaving your skeleton behind until you return. 
* Transformation -- You become a moderately competent martial character, but lose your spell casting for rounds/level. Note that you still have your hexes.
* Treacherous teleport -- You may redirect some of your targets to a different destination than your own.
* True seeing -- For minutes/level the subject can see through all illusions and transmutations.
* Unconscious agenda (will) -- Direct the subject to perform a specified action in a specified situation within the next weeks/level. “When you see X, tell her you’ve been stealing from her for months”.
* Vengeful outrage (will) -- Can be used as a sizable boost for a martial party member, or occasionally to turn one enemy against another (non-allied) enemy. Note the 2nd saving throw if you try to use it to turn an enemy against one of their own.
* Wither limb (fort) -- Render one of the target’s limbs permanently unusable.
7th
* Age resistance, greater -- You counter a total of -6 to each of str/dex/con, a massive gain if you are in fact of venerable age.
* Arcane sight, greater -- You can see and recognize the enchantments & effects of and on creatures as well as items.
* Artificer’s curse (will) -- Nearly or completely shut down any single magic item other than an artifact.
* Bestow curse, greater (will, touch) -- Reducing a key ability score by 12 will cripple most casters and significantly limit martials. Acting only 25% of the time is likely worse. Finally, curse of the ages combines nicely with casting age resistance to increase your int/wis/cha without suffering the accompanying str/dex/con losses.
* Bite the hand, mass (will) -- Turn summoned creatures (1/level) against their summoner.
* Bloodstone mirror -- Redirect any mind-affecting spell targeting you to another target within 30’ for hours/level.
* Chain lightning (reflex) -- Sorcerers/wizards get this at 6th level, but (somewhat sadly) it remains one of the witch’s best direct-damage spells.
* Control weather -- Destroy the crops, or end a drought. Create any number of hostile environments, or end them.
* Create demiplane, lesser -- Create your own extra-planar retreat for days/level, which can be extended in perpetuity casting the spell again within that period. Your wee personal dimension is better yet if you have a wizard to cast permanency on it. You will almost certainly need Plane shift to travel to/from your demiplane.
* Demanding message, mass -- This is effectively mass suggestion, targeting creatures/level with a 200-300’ range.
* Fairy ring retreat -- Create an extra-dimensional retreat for 2 hours/level. This does not last as long as a lesser demiplane, nor can it be extended, however it comes with food, water, and unseen servants. For ~2 days you can rest & recover with safety.
* Fey form III -- Your options now include diminutive fliers (e.g. mockingfey) and the huge tunche.
* Harm (will, touch) -- Inflict 10 hp/level, though not below 1 hp, will all but kill almost any level-equivalent target.
* Heal -- This removes a number of higher level maladies that exceed break enchantment’s capacity, and it adds 10 HP/level. Unless your patron adds wish or miracle to your spell list, this is the strongest such spell the witch has access to.
* Hold person, mass (will) -- As hold person, but on targets/level.
* Ice body -- You gain DR 5/magic, quite a few immunities, and the ability to travel through ice & snow for minutes/level.
* Insanity (will) -- The target is permanently subject to confusion, as per the spell. A significant effect that is difficult to remove, though in combat you have better options.
* Inveigle monster (will) – For days/level you have a new friend.
* Legendary proportions -- Grant significant bonuses to str, con, DR and natural armor to the target for minutes/level.
* Maddening Oubliette (will) – Remove a threat until it successfully saves, and when it returns it is confused for that many additional rounds. Compare with insanity.
* Mydriatic spontaneity, mass (will) – Debilitate any number of opponents for rounds per level: nauseated and blind/dazzled.
* Object possession, greater -- The ability to possess & control a gargantuan object for 10 minutes/level includes targets such as ships or buildings.
* Particulate form -- An assortment of physical protections and moderate healing for rounds/level.
* Phase door -- Create a 30-40’ long extra-dimensional tunnel that can accommodate 1 traveler per 2 levels.
* Planar refuge -- Transform a 50’ radius within another plane into an area roughly equivalent to the material plane for days/level. 
* Plane shift (will, touch) -- Reliable planar travel for up to 8 willing passengers. Can also be used offensively as a touch-range spell.
* Power word blind (none) -- Only lasts for a few rounds vs significant threats (up to 200 hp), but has no saving throw.
* Primal regression (will) – Incapacitate a caster for minutes/level.
* Regenerate -- Regrow missing limbs, with moderate healing.
* Scrying, greater (will) -- Spy on a target for hours/level.
* Shadow body -- For minutes/level you are incorporeal, have concealment except in direct light, can walk up walls, and can still cast spells.
* Soul transfer (will) -- Capture an incorporeal creature in a gem.
* Summon monster VII -- Bone devil, dire crocodile, Greater elemental, Roc
* Teleport, greater -- As teleport, but with no range restriction and arrival on target.
* Teleport object (will) -- As teleport, but for a single object and destinations include the ethereal plane.
* Temporal divergence -- An interesting spell. Cast one spell with a will save and another with a fort save, keeping the one that succeeds. Cast a high level spell and a cantrip, expending the cantrip if the high level spell fails. However, because it occupies a coveted 7th level spell slot it is primarily of use for very high level characters.
* Waves of ecstasy (will) -- Within a 30’ cone, targets are stunned for a round and staggered for rounds/level. If you stun half your opponents for a round, and then leave them unable to make a full attack afterward you’ve given your party a significant advantage. Compare with mydriatic spontaneity, mass.
* Waves of exhaustion (none) -- Within a massive 60’ cone all targets are exhausted (-6 str/dex, ½ movement) with no save. A solid debuff vs martial opponents.
8th
* Charm monster, mass (will) -- Gaining good will from perhaps 60% of the potential opponents in a room should shift an encounter in your favor.
* Clone -- A variant of raise dead that you can have prepared entirely in advance.
* Create demiplane -- You’re building your own little extra-dimensional world. It’s kind of galling that you have to go find a wizard to cast permanency on it.
* Cure serious wounds, mass -- Cure about 30 hp for one target/level.
* Curse of night -- You plunge a 1 mile radius into permanent darkness.
* Curse terrain, supreme -- Create a wildly hostile and impassible land with a 5 mile radius for 1 day.
* Death clutch (fort) -- Remove the target’s heart (200 hp or lower), leaving them dead the next round. Above 200 hp they take 1d4 con and bleed another 1d4 con/round.
* Demand (will) -- Cast suggestion on one target who is familiar to you, no matter where they currently are (5% failure across planes).
* Destruction (fort) -- Inflict 10hp/level on a target, or 10d6 if their save succeeds. For targets below 200 hp death clutch is more effective.
* Discern location (no SR) — Find anyone or anything, with almost no limitations.
* Fey gate -- Create a portal to/from a random location in the fey First World.
* Frightful aspect (none, no SR) -- A few buffs for you, opponents within 30’ are shaken, and lastly if they strike you they become frightened for 1d4 rounds.
* Heart of the mammoth — A massive buff for a melee party member.
* Horrid wilting (fort) — Deal 1d6/level to every living opponent within a 30’ radius.
* Irresistible dance (will, touch) -- For 2-5 rounds (1 round on a save) the target is dazed and significantly debuffed.
* Maze (none) -- Trap an opponent until they succeed on a DC 20 intelligence check.
* Mind blank -- Complete protection from divination & scrying, significant protection vs mental/emotional spells. Lasts 24 hours.
* Moment of prescience -- Gain a  +1/level bonus on one d20 roll that day.
* Nine Lives (catfolk) – Prevent almost anything from going wrong for hours/level.
* Possession, greater (will) -- Same as possession, but your body vanishes and hence you do not need to worry about keeping it safe.
* Prediction of failure (will) -- Target is sickened & shaken permanently (15+ rounds on a save), and gains a random spellblight if they’re a caster.
* Prying eyes, greater -- As prying eyes, but with true seeing and +25 perception, for 1 hour/level.
* Quintessence mastery -- Make an inner plane more habitable, with the more potent shape realm variant lasting for days/level.
* Resurrection -- Bring the dead back to life with 1 negative level.
* Rift of ruin (evil) -- The ultimate “pit” spell. End it early to instead summon demon(s) per summon monster vii.
* Soulseeker -- Find a soul, wherever it may be in the afterlife.
* Storm bolts (fort) -- Significant area effect, selective damage, with a 1 round stun.
* Summon monster VIII -- barbed devil, elder elemental
* Sympathy (will) --- attract an alignment or type of creature to an object or location for 2 hours/level.
* Temporal regression -- Mark a “save point” in time that you can personally revert to within the next 1 round/level. 
* Trap the soul (will, can avoid SR) -- Permanently trap the target’s soul in an (expensive) gem.
* Wandering weather -- As control weather, but it follows you.
9th
* Astral projection -- Nearly unlimited planar travel for 9+ subjects.
* Create demiplane, greater -- Create your own plane with your own rules for magic, time, etc. Still need a wizard to cast permanency though.
* Curse of fell seasons -- Create permanent awful weather within a 2 mile radius.

Archetypes
The defining characteristic of a witch is the hex. Archetypes will commonly offer features in lieu of one or more hexes, so the simple calculus is: would I prefer the hex(es)?


For any archetype that trades out one or more hexes, look at how early they are. The first 3-5 hexes you get are far more important than successive ones. If your hex progression will be stunted more than you’re comfortable with, consider a human character. This gives you an additional starting feat, i.e. extra hex, to offset the loss.


Another point to note is that your starting level matters. I have rated them as if you’re playing from level 1. However, if you’re starting at level 10 or 12 then missing a couple early hexes isn’t nearly so painful.


Conversely, archetypes that trade out or modify your familiar aren’t typically as painful. 
Alley Witch
The skills gained are inferior to the ones lost, the patron spells are subpar, and hex trader isn’t worth losing a hex. But Speak to the City could be good for a campaign set in a single city.
Ashiftah
You lose your familiar and 2 hexes (2nd, 6th). The abilities you gain in trade are worth breaking out individually, simply because you’re gaining more than the typical “one useful feature.”
* Ghostwalk (2nd) -- Vanish and take a 5’ step as a move action after you use a hex. This gets you away from the inevitable melee opponent who has closed on you. It could also be worth dipping 2 levels for a rogue or slayer. If so, consider evil eye for your 1st level hex so you can spam it, and look at the feat hex strike for even more action economy.
* Deliver Touch Spells (3rd) -- Deliver touch spells at a 20’ range as a full turn action. The witch has a few genuinely good touch spells, notably touch of blindness & bestow curse, but often needs to use her move action to cackle or simply to stay safe.
* Fog of War (6th) -- Barrow haze at will as a standard action. You can see through it but others cannot, allowing you to simply center it on yourself when convenient.
Beast-Bonded
You can add feats to your familiar rather than yourself, a rare option. The key attraction to this archetype is the 10th level twin soul, and this is a power-house. If your familiar dies its soul inhabits your body until you decide to use magic jar to take over another body. Per the spell magic jar, “The body retains its Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, hit points, natural abilities, and automatic abilities.” I.e. you can have the physical power of almost any creature that fails the relevant will save. Likewise, if you die you inhabit your familiar’s body, and from there can take over any other body via magic jar. Unless your campaign will spend significant time at/beyond 10th level, this is probably not an attractive choice.
Bonded Witch
Half elves only. You lose the utility of a familiar, but gain a bonded object per a wizard’s arcane bond. Notably, you gain the relevant craft item feat with respect to this item. In addition to your prepared spells, you can cast one of the bonded object’s spells once per day. Overall, wand & ring provide the best spell choices, followed perhaps by staff. The combination of patron spells and bonded object spells gives a Bonded Witch the broadest spell selection of any witch.
* Amulet: These are predominately melee buffs, most with a target of “you”. This is a poor fit for a ½ BAB witch.
* Ring: A pretty good selection of protective spells. Consider a patron that grants you invisibility so that you can craft a ring of invisibility.
* Staff: Mostly druid spells, none of them on the witch list. While you won’t be able to craft until 8th level, a staff extends your spells available both in variety and daily quantity.
* Wand: A solid spell list containing offensive wizard spells and restoration spells. Even at 1st level you can craft a wand of cure light wounds (or infernal healing).
* Weapon: An interesting bag of spells, though not the best overall. And crafting a weapon won’t serve you particularly well.
Bouda
An evil archetype. Using a fetish to cast touch of blindness or bestow curse at range is very nice. Bouda’s eye is superior to evil eye. And hours/level as a dire hyena with natural spell is a reasonable trade for a major hex.
Cartomancer
A harrow deck doesn’t have the utility of a familiar, but it can’t be killed in a fireball. The ability to deliver touch spells (touch of blindness, bestow curse) as ranged touch spells is worth a hex.
Coral Witch
Your familiar has no utility, but can still be killed. You gain access to a few interesting (shaman, waves) hexes and lose your 8th level hex for an odd skill.
Dark Sister
While Hag crony is a very nice feature, you don’t get it until 12th level and lose 3 hexes in total.
Demon Sworn
The patron spells are a net loss unless you’re evil, the damage from cruel hex is a modest bonus, and while the quasit familiar comes a level early it’s not a top tier choice. The overall utility is a mix, but for role play this could be pretty enjoyable.
Dimensional Occultist
For the vast majority of campaigns the planar travel features of this archetype are not worth the loss of 3 hexes, though that shifts as you reach 8th and then 12th level. The CL boost for out-of-combat spells from Dimensional Augmentation is welcome. Commune with Familiar gives you Contact Other Plane with reduced penalties for failure and a level early. Lastly, Dimensional Waypoints make extra-planar travel safer and more reliable.


In terms of the patron spells though, planar binding without a magic circle of protection (none of which are on the witch’s spell list) is dangerous/foolhardy. Only consider this if your DM will add these spells to the witch’s list (e.g. swap it out with blink from your patron list) as a house rule.
Dreamweaver
Changling only. The Dreamweaver gains interesting, and by 10th level quite powerful, abilities vs victims of its slumber hex. This is in trade for 3 hexes, and is the rare case where you come out reasonably well. Consider your campaign though. The benefits of a Dreamweaver only apply if you’re not more interested in simply killing off any victims of your slumber hex. Look through the changeling alternate racial traits. You’ll want witchborn, and should consider hag magic, mist child & object of desire.
Feytouched Hexer (3rd party)
I will not cover any other 3rd party material, primarily because it is so very broad, and not liable to be available in your campaign in any case. I make an exception for the Feytouched Hexer because it gives us a spontaneous charisma-based witch without the baggage of the Seducer. Beyond that we trade out one hex for faerie wings at 5th level, and this is essentially a re-flavored flight hex.


Note that you will want a race with a charisma bonus rather than the ones listed here. And further, as a prepared caster, you will want to prioritize spells that are of value every day. To that end, I suggest giving a sorcerer guide a read.
Flood Walker
Evil. If you’re playing an evil, nautical campaign then this is mediocre. Otherwise it’s bad.
Gingerbread Witch
A colorful, but 100% NPC archetype.
Gravewalker
Create undead, control undead, and take direct control of one of your undead. You lose three hexes: 1st, 4th & 8th. Note that you cannot even take the extra hex feat until you have your first hex at 2nd level.
Hagbound
Give up nearly all of your hexes for claws and strength. And a bestow curse hex at 10th.
Havocker
Give up all your hexes for a moderate blast.
Hedge Witch
You get spontaneous healing in trade for 2 hexes, note however that after cure light wounds you gain healing spells later than a cleric/oracle. This can give some parties just what they need without a cleric.
Herb Witch
Trade 1 hex for cures to poison, disease, blindness, nausea, etc, and must take cauldron at 2nd level. That pushes your first hex choice out to 4th level. Note the spells delay poison (2nd), remove disease (3rd), and remove blindness (3rd) are on the witch’s list. Your remedies are mostly of value because they add general-purpose cures in place of having the relevant scrolls on hand or spells memorized.
Hex Channeler
You give up your 2nd level hex for 1d6 of cleric-style energy channeling, and can give up additional hexes for additional +1d6 as desired. In almost every case hexes are better than 1d6 of channeling, and I do not recommend you ever choose to increase your channel d6. Instead, note that the feats Turn Undead (and then Blazing Channel) or Command Undead are available to you at an effective cleric level equal to your witch level, which is certainly worthwhile in a campaign that features undead. Finally, if your DM allows you to have a deity as your patron then variant channeling becomes available.
Invoker
You trade four hexes for a variety of bonuses, notably to spell/hex DC and to overcome SR. You’ll likely want extra hex to offset the loss. Consider the shadow patron, since higher DCs are particularly useful with shadow spells.
Jinx Witch
Buff your wisdom and constitution, consider iron willl. At 10th level this is a formidable anti-caster, though subpar below 10th. Compare with the hexes gift of consumption and greater gift of consumption.
Ley Line Guardian 
If you really want to be a spontaneous caster, this is the route. Where sorcerers gain bloodline powers compared with wizards, and oracles gain mystery/curse abilities compared with clerics, you just become a spontaneous caster without any compensating abilities.


You gain a situational ability, conduit surge, in trade for 2 hexes. The CL boost is quite nice, but the risk of being staggered for minutes/level if you fail a fort save (your worst) means that this is almost exclusively an out-of-combat ability. 
Medium
In a campaign featuring a good number of incorporeal opponents this could be worthwhile. Compare with buying a rod of ectoplasmic spell.
Mirror Witch
You lose the utility of a traditional familiar, but gain a +2 saving throw bonus per day each odd level. Add in bonuses to knowledge checks, clairvoyance & scrying and it’s a net positive. The scar hex complements scrying.
Mountain Witch
At the cost of one hex you get access to the shaman’s stone spirit hexes, a couple of which are pretty good. Note that the mountain patron gives you comparable spells without costing a hex. 
Pact Witch
You get an improved familiar in trade for your 6th level hex, and a decent patron spell list. Unfortunately the familiar retains its int/wis/cha stats. You’re likely better off with the feat improved familiar.
Putrefactor
Trade six hexes for a variety of swarm abilities.
Rhetoricians
Trade away a hex for an odd anti-diplomacy skill, with subpar patron spells.
Scarred Witch Doctor
Orc or half-orc only. The int bonus is remarkable. If you start with an 18 int, put your half-orc bonus into int for 20, and then add this effective +2 for spells & hexes, you’re playing with an int 22 witch at 1st level. The natural armor is almost worth the cost of a hex, mostly notable for delaying your first hex to 2nd level. Finally, the craft wondrous item option for your mask offsets the loss of your familiar.
Sea Witch
Trade away one hex for know direction near the sea and a nautical wild empathy. The patron spells aren’t great either.
Season Witch
Each Season Witch is associated with a single season, and must choose that patron. Note that none of the season patrons have particularly good spell lists. You then get a +1 DC bonus for a single type of elemental damage and most importantly a bonus hex, chosen from the 2 hexes associated with each season.
* Spring: charm & disguise, +1 lightning DC – your bonus hex is 2nd tier, but you can lean into lightning, lightning arc and chain lightning, with time stop as your 9th level spell.
* Summer: fortune & misfortune, +1 fire DC – a great choice of bonus hexes and a few nice spells, notably: good berry, wall of fire (a level late), and sirocco.
* Autumn: blight & slumber, +1 acid DC -- slumber is a terrific hex, though the patron list is pretty weak past create pit.
* Winter: healing & ward, +1 cold DC --  in lower-level campaigns both of these hexes are useful, and the witch’s spell list include a number of decent cold spells
Seducer (with addendum)
You’re a charisma rather than intelligence based caster, i.e. more diplomacy, bluff & intimidation, but fewer skills. The charm hex isn’t very good until 8th level, and seducer’s kiss is generally inferior to the slumber hex. I also think the sexy witch trope is just too liable to take over this character to the detriment of overall role play.


All that said, garden of delight grants a potential +2 morale save bonus to any party member. My best recommendation is that you and your GM re-work this “mechanic” if you’re intent on playing a seducer. Caveat emptor.


Addendum: In discussion on the forum I’ve heard from multiple Paizo forum members who feel that the Seducer can be re-worked to leave its sexy (and sexual) aspects aside. And frankly, the 8th level ability to add a +2 morale bonus to all saves for the next 24 hours is wildly over-powered. The shift from an int-based caster to a cha-based one means that your choice of races shifts to ones that would typically be taken for a sorcerer. The gathlains in particular have been called out to me as a fey option with a lot of flavor for such a witch, and a FCB that adds druid spells to your list.
Spellspy
A fairly interesting idea, but in most campaigns anti-divination just isn’t going to be worth the loss of a hex.
Synergist
With a pseudodragon familiar you get flight and blindsense, at 11th level the flight is unlimited. This costs 3 hexes. Compare with just taking the flight hex.
Tatterdemalion
Until 16th level when you get unravel you’re probably better off just taking prehensile hair. Even then it’s not clear this is worth 4 hexes, even with web & lipstitch as hexes.
Vellemancer
If you’re focusing on buffing your party (fortune, protective luck & ward, e.g.) then this is worth a look, but that’s not really the witch’s strength. You’ll likely want soothsayer as well.
Veneficus Witch
Lowering saving throws and poisoning opponents has a nice synergy, but for most encounters it’s just too slow. Consider learning the spell vermin shape II to create your own poison with your 4th level spell DC, if you’re intent on going this route.
Venom Siphoner
At 6th level you can add dex poison with a good DC to your single-target spells with a fort save (ear-piercing scream, blindness, lipstitch). This costs you 3 of your first 4 hexes. It’s a nice bonus on top of some good 1st/2nd level spells, but 1d2 dex/round isn’t usually going to be an effect that swings the encounter one way or another.
White-Haired Witch
I really want this to be good, but the witch’s low BAB just cripples the archetype. You have to treat this as a full caster with a fallback plan of grappling and various combat maneuvers. You get no hexes. Dear reader, hexes are better than grappling.
Winter Witch
Taken by itself (i.e. without the prestige class of the same name) this is a decent choice. You give up 1 hex, and gain +1 DC on cold spells and scaling endure elements/cold resistance. The loss of fire spells isn’t significant for a witch. The familiar & patron lists are subsets of the witch’s, but include good choices.
Witch Watcher
At the cost of 1 spell slot at each spell level you can give a single target/day a number of defensive buffs. 
Wyrmwitch
You choose from among some new patron spell lists, though none are top tier. Instead of a familiar, you carry around a hoard (likely some gems/jewelry) that also allows you to cast any 1 spell known 1/day like a wizard’s bonded object. You can also learn spells directly from a wizard’s spell book after it’s been in your hoard for days/spell-level. A bonded witch is probably better because it does not limit your choice of patrons.
Prestige Classes
To get the most out of a witch you really need to retain progression in spellcasting and hexes. Most prestige classes do not include hexes, so failing that they have to add something at least roughly comparable. That limits our list to:
Agent of the Grave
Almost exclusively an option for a Gravewalker, though you could also take the Plague patron. You lose 1 level of spell progression (which can be offset via prestigious spellcaster), as well as hex progression (and if you take this as 6th level Gravewalker when you gain animate dead from your patron you only have a single hex from 2nd level). You gain a lot of undead HD to control. And you must be evil, making this option moot for most campaigns.
Arcane Trickster
Compared with a wizard, you gain a familiar and 2 hexes, but your spell list just doesn’t support the arcane trickster as well as the wizard’s. I.e. generally you’d rather have invisibility and scorching ray than cure light wounds and bestow curse. However, the ashiftah witch at 2nd level vanishes and takes a 5’ step after using a hex, which sets up a sneak attack nicely. The choice of hex here will likely be evil eye because it can be used up to 5 times per target. At 10th level you can add spell hex with touch of blindness for more hex/vanish combinations.


Lastly you open up nice stealth opportunities such as, targeting a guard with evil eye vs skill checks so that you both lower their perception and vanish.


You will almost certainly want the magical knack trait to regain 2 caster levels.
Brewkeeper
You really only want a 1 level dip into this class, likely with prestigious spellcaster to offset the loss of spell casting progression. You gain the ability to prepare a potion for any spell that qualifies as a potion without the 3rd level restriction. The key efficiency gain here is that any party member can drink their potion at any time without requiring the witch to cast the given spell that round (and perhaps be within touch range).
Cyphermage
You lose hex progression but keep spellcasting. A one level dip allows you to cast a spell from a scroll at your CL (enhanced scroll) 1/day, or increase a scroll spell to your DC (insightful scroll) 1/day.
Evangelist
This looks rather niche, but I’m not familiar enough with all of the features to give this a clear-eyed rating. The increase to 6+int skills/level, ¾ HD, and ¾ BAB is clearly a win. But the loss of spell casting and hex progression is a big hit. From there I’ll just link to a couple of guides for your further research.
Walter’s Guide to Deific Obediences
On Bended Knee: A Guide to Pathfinder's Obedience Feats
Loremaster
For a 1 level dip into the Loremaster you gain a Secret, and with that prerequisite you have access to the feat Secret of Magical Discipline. Now you can cast any spell by expending a prepared spell of the same level 1/day. You do not lose a level of spell casting progression, but you do lose a level of hex progression. You should discuss the parameters of this with your DM.
* The spells of half-casters or warrior casters can be more powerful for their spell level.
* Access to spells is often earlier for some classes than others. E.g. a Druid can cast each version of Fey Form as a lower level spell than a Witch.
Magaamyan Arcanist
You incrementally become capable of casting a subset of your spells as if you were a spontaneous caster. However, you lose hex progression. This class mentions adding spells to your spellbook or familiar, indicating that it applies to witches, however it requires the feat spell mastery which is only available to wizards. So check with your DM. Finally, if you’re going to lean into spell casting rather than hexes, consider whether you’re better off choosing a wizard in the first place.
Pathfinder Savant
You lose a level of spell progression, which you’ll likely want to make up via the feat Prestigious Spellcaster. From 2nd level onward with each level you gain access to an additional spell from any class. You must prepare it into a spell slot that is 1 level higher if it is not on your spell list. One shenanigan would be to add a spell from another list that is already on your list, but at a higher level. E.g. you could take the Druid’s 3rd level spell Fey Form I. Since it’s on your list (albeit as a 4th level spell), you can now prepare it in a 3rd level slot. 
Stargazer
You gain hexes more slowly, and notably no major nor grand hexes, however your hexes do scale up with stargazer levels. And notably you gain access to shaman hexes, in particular: intimidating display and heaven’s leap. The mystery magic and sidereal arcana you gain instead offer utility if generally not power.


Consider taking a single level of stargazer, purely for access to a specific shaman hex that you have your eye on. 


Open question: does a witch/stargazer have two familiars?
Veiled Illusionist
You maintain your spell progression, but lose hex progression. With each level of Veiled Illusionist you can add 1 spell from the Illusion school (invisibility, mirror image, and significantly, the various shadow spells) to your spell list. 
Winter Witch
You must have the winter witch archetype. You lose one level of spell casting, which you can offset with the feats favored prestige class followed by prestigious spellcaster. In all you get higher DC, caster level, and bonuses vs SR for cold spells, as well as some cold/ice utility. You cannot learn fire spells, and have a limited list of major hexes. Take the feat elemental focus for another +1 cold DC.
Multiclass
A witch just doesn’t want to slow her spell & hex progression. The advantages of a level of another class are just not worth the loss of a witch level. However, another class might want to dip into a level or two of witch.
* White-Haired Witch 2/Magus, Hexcrafter X – The “defiler” build with two levels of white-haired witch, followed exclusively by hexcrafter levels is a very solid debuffing melee build.
* White-Haired Witch 4/Sohei 1/Eldritch Knight 10/WHW 5 – The “bride of hell” is a cousin to the defiler. You give up spell strike, but nearly keep full spell progression while gaining a respectable BAB. Consider a 2nd level of monk if you want to deflect arrows, but I’d rather have the spell casting.
* Ashiftah Witch 2/Unchained Rogue, Sylvan Trickster X – A 2 level dip into an Ashiftah witch gives you ghostwalk. Consider hex strike to allow you to both strike & deliver a hex, triggering ghostwalk, setting you up for sneak attack the following turn. Note that hex strike is keyed to a specific hex, where good choices are: 
   * slumber – Completely debilitating when it succeeds, can only be used 1/target
   * evil eye – Can be used on the same target repeatedly, e.g. -2 to AC, then -2 to attacks, then -2 to saves, etc. Increasing to -4 at 8th level.
Note that ghostwalk is triggered when you use a hex, regardless of whether the target saves against the hex. Since hex strike is triggered by an unarmed attack, consider the feat extra hex to gain prehensile hair.
* Witch 1/Unchained Monk X – A single level dip into witch to gain the slumber hex is then followed up by monk levels. You gain improved unarmed strike as a free feat, so you can take hex strike at level 1. A human can even take accursed hex as their bonus feat. This allows you to use flurry of blows as a 1st level monk (2nd character level), though only the first strike each round triggers slumber because hex strike requires a quick action. With ascetic style you can apply hex strike with a monk weapon. Note that your slumber DC does not progress, but at lower levels this build is fearsome. At mid-levels a witch 1/monk 9 still has DC 12 or perhaps 13 with a +2 int headband:
   * 10th level fighter has a will save of +3, perhaps +5 with a +2 cloak of resistance, giving a 30% chance of triggering slumber. 
   * 10th level paladin has a will save of +7, +10 with a +3 cloak, only a 5% slumber rate. At this point you’re only succeeding on the intermittent natural 1.
